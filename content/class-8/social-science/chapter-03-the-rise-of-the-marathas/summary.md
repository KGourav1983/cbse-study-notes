# The Rise of the Marathas

Have you ever wondered how a small chieftain's son from Pune grew up to found an empire that eventually became the single biggest obstacle to British control of India — bigger even than the mighty Mughals? That's the story of this chapter. Let's build it up piece by piece.

## Who are the Marathas?

Before we talk about empires, let's get the basics right — an examiner will expect you to identify *who* the Marathas actually are, not just what they did.

- The **Marathas** are a group of people native to the **Deccan plateau**, specifically present-day **Maharashtra**.
- They are identified by the language they speak — **Marathi** — which has had a rich, continuous **literary history** since the 12th century.
- Politically, the region passed through the **Yadava dynasty** (capital at Devagiri, present-day Daulatabad) until it was overthrown by the **Khilji Sultanate** in the early 14th century.

### The cultural groundwork: the Bhakti movement

Here's a common misconception: students often think Maratha power sprang up purely from military genius. Actually, the chapter is clear that the *cultural* foundation came first.

- Between the 7th and 17th centuries, **bhakti** (devotion to the divine) spread across India as an alternative to external ritualism.
- Maharashtrian saints like **Dnyaneshwar, Namdev, Tukaram, and Ramdas** translated texts like the **Upanishads** and the **Bhagavad Gita** into Marathi.
- This gave society political awareness and a shared cultural identity — the seedbed that later let the Marathas organise into a political force.

> **Exam trap:** Don't confuse "who created Maratha political power" (Shivaji) with "who created the cultural foundation for it" (the Bhakti saints). Examiners often ask you to link the two.

## Foundation of Maratha Power and the Rise of Chhatrapati Shivaji

### Early life

Think about growing up mostly without your father around, in a region torn apart by constant infighting — that was Shivaji's childhood.

- Born in **1630** into the **Bhonsle clan**, to **Shahji** and **Jijabai**.
- Shahji served the Deccan sultanates and was frequently away; Shivaji grew up in his father's **jagir** at Pune under Jijabai's guidance.
- **Jagir**: a piece of land given by a king to a noble/soldier as reward, from which the holder could collect taxes.
- At just **16**, Shivaji launched military campaigns, starting by capturing neglected forts around Pune. His vision of a sovereign kingdom, or **Swarajya**, grew from here to cover political, economic, and cultural life.

### Building a navy and mastering guerrilla warfare

Ever notice how underdogs in history often win not by being bigger, but by being smarter about terrain? That's exactly Shivaji's approach.

- As his kingdom reached India's west coast, Shivaji recognised the need for a **navy** to secure coastal resources — a genuinely revolutionary move, since neither the Bijapur Sultanate nor the Mughal Empire maintained a serious full-time naval force.
- He relied on **guerrilla warfare**: small, fast-moving groups using surprise and knowledge of terrain to defeat much larger armies.
- **Afzal Khan episode**: Bijapur sent General Afzal Khan against Shivaji. At a one-on-one meeting near Pratapgad, Shivaji killed Khan (famously using the **wagh nakh**, a tiger-claw weapon), and hidden Maratha forces routed Khan's army.

### Clashes with the Mughals

- **Shaista Khan**: This Mughal nobleman invaded for three years; Shivaji raided his camp at night, and Khan fled after losing a few fingers — a raid the chapter compares to a modern **surgical strike**.
- **Sacking of Surat**: In retaliation, Shivaji sacked the wealthy Mughal port of Surat, seizing about **one crore rupees** — but deliberately avoided religious places and spared the charitable Mohandas Parekh.
- **Treaty of Purandar**: Aurangzeb sent Rajput general **Jai Singh**, forcing Shivaji to concede defeat at Purandar Fort, surrender territory, and send his son Sambhaji into Mughal service.
- **Agra escape**: Shivaji was insulted at Aurangzeb's court, placed under house arrest, and famously escaped by hiding himself and Sambhaji in gift-baskets meant for holy men and generals.

### Coronation and the conquest of the South

- In **1674**, Shivaji was crowned with full Vedic rites at **Raigad**, taking the title **'Shri Raja Shiva Chhatrapati'** and starting his own era, the *Rajyabhisheka shaka*.
- He then undertook the **dakshina-digvijaya** (conquest of the South), extending into northern Tamil Nadu and parts of Karnataka — territory that later gave the Marathas strategic depth against Mughal attacks.
- While in the South, he **forbade the Dutch from trading slaves**, a rare stance among Indian powers at the time.

### Death and immediate legacy

- Shivaji died of fever at **fifty**. Europeans compared him to Alexander; the Bundela prince **Chhatrasal** was inspired to found Bundelkhand; the Hindi poet **Bhushan** composed poems praising him.

## The Marathas after Chhatrapati Shivaji

### Sambhaji and Rajaram

- **Sambhaji** succeeded Shivaji. Aurangzeb conquered **Bijapur (Adil Shahi)** and **Golconda (Qutb Shahi)**, then captured, tortured, and executed Sambhaji, later seizing Raigad.
- **Rajaram** became the next Chhatrapati and fled to **Gingee** (Tamil Nadu), spreading the conflict to south India. The Marathas held their fortresses well, and Aurangzeb died in the Deccan without subduing them.
- **Tarabai**, Rajaram's queen, then organised large-scale Maratha counter-attacks into Mughal territory — she was the architect of the northward Maratha expansion.

### The rise of the Peshwas

> **Exam trap:** Students often assume the Chhatrapati stayed the real power-holder throughout Maratha history. After Shivaji's centralised system, actual power shifted.

- The state became **decentralised**: still nominally centred on the Chhatrapati, but real power moved to regional chiefs, especially the **Peshwa** (Persian for 'prime minister').
- **Peshwa Bajirao I** and his son **Nanasaheb Peshwa** drove the pan-Indian expansion.
- This decentralisation had a downside: regional chiefs sometimes acted with cruelty, such as the **ten-year Bengal campaign**, which caused much suffering to common people — a sharp contrast to Shivaji's own values.

### Northward expansion and the end of Maratha power

- The Marathas briefly controlled **Lahore, Attock, and Peshawar** in the north.
- They suffered a disastrous defeat at the **Battle of Panipat (1761)** against the Afghans but recovered under **Peshwa Madhavrao I**.
- **Mahadji Shinde (Scindia)** recaptured **Delhi in 1771**, held until the British took it three decades later.
- The Marathas became the **chief rivals of the British** in the late 18th century. Three **Anglo-Maratha Wars** (1775–1818) were fought; internal Maratha disunity plus superior British organisation and technology ended Maratha power.

> **Exam trap:** A frequently tested line from the chapter — "**the British took India from the Marathas more than from the Mughals or any other power**." Be ready to explain *why* this is true using evidence (largest empire before the British, chief rivals, three wars).

## Maratha Administration

### Civilian administration

Shivaji ran a relatively **centralised** administration:

- Abolished **hereditary posts** and land assignments (common under Sultans/Mughals); officials were salaried from the state treasury instead.
- Officials were **periodically transferred** so no one accumulated enough power to challenge the king.
- Gave **pensions to soldiers' widows** and military posts to their sons.
- Maintained the **ashta pradhana mandala** — a council of eight ministers:

| Title | Role |
|---|---|
| Pradhan | Prime Minister |
| Amatya | Finance Minister |
| Sachiv | Land Revenue Minister |
| Mantri | Intelligence Minister |
| Sumant | Minister for Foreign Affairs |
| Senapati | Commander-in-chief |
| Nyayadhish | Chief Justice |
| Panditrao | Head of Religious Affairs |

- Levied **chauth** (25%) and **sardeshmukhi** (an additional 10% on top of chauth) from provinces not directly under Maratha rule, in exchange for protection and non-interference — arrangements the Mughals themselves approved through treaties.

### Military administration

- Forces were divided into three parts: **infantry, cavalry, and navy**.
- Cavalry had two types: **bargirs** (horses/equipment paid for by the state) and **shiledars** (paid for by the soldiers themselves).
- By the 18th century, Marathas recruited **European-style disciplined troops**; Mahadji Shinde had a large such army.
- Preferred weapons were **swords and lances**, alongside guns; **rockets** were used from Shivaji's time, with **metal tube rockets** by 1770.
- **Forts** were, in the words of finance minister **Ramachandrapant Amatya** (in the *Adnyapatra*), "the core of the state" — they let the Marathas control key routes and shelter guerrilla forces even against enemies as powerful as Aurangzeb.

### Maritime supremacy

- **Kanhoji Angre** led the Maratha navy to many victories in the 18th century through clever tactics, despite less advanced ships than the Europeans.
- Europeans forced Indians to buy naval trade passes (**cartaz**, Portuguese) — the Marathas defied this by demanding passes from Europeans themselves, which is why Europeans frustratedly branded Angre a "**pirate**."

### Judicial system

> **Exam trap:** Don't confuse the Maratha-era **panchayat** (a local gathering of officials and prominent men that delivered justice) with the modern-day Panchayat, a government body — the chapter explicitly warns about this.

- Known for **moderation in using capital punishment**.
- Appeals against panchayat verdicts could go to a Maratha chief.
- The **kotwal** (police) maintained law and order in prominent towns like Pune and Indore.

### Trade networks

- Shivaji personally participated in maritime trade, sending ships as far as **Mocha (Yemen), Muscat (Oman), and Malacca (Malaysia)**, carrying goods like gold and textiles.
- Roads, bridges, and (in 18th-century Odisha) a **ferry network** were built and maintained for transport.

## Cultural Revival

Shivaji's contribution wasn't only military — think about how a ruler's choice of language or coinage can be a political statement in itself.

- His **seal** carried a Sanskrit inscription (a break from the usual Persian seals), proclaiming his authority reigned "for the welfare of the people."
- He commissioned the ***Rajya-Vyavahara-Kosha***, replacing common Persian administrative terms with Sanskrit equivalents.
- A devout Hindu who **respected other religions**, he adopted a **saffron flag**, rebuilt desecrated temples, and promoted Sanskrit and Marathi literature and traditional arts.
- His greatest cultural contribution, arguably, was simply proving that mighty empires *could* be defeated and that Indians could build and run one of their own.

### The mighty Maratha women

- **Tarabai**: ruled after husband Rajaram's death; organised the large-scale Maratha invasions of Mughal north India, becoming the architect of the northward expansion.
- **Ahilyabai Holkar**: of the Holkar dynasty (Indore region); governed for thirty years after losing her husband and son; built/restored temples, ghats, wells, and roads across India (including rebuilding the **Kashi Vishwanath temple** and **Somnath temple**); promoted the **Maheshwar weaving industry**.

### In focus: Thanjavur

- **Ekoji**, Shivaji's half-brother, conquered **Thanjavur** (Tamil Nadu) in the late 17th century, starting Maratha rule there.
- The Thanjavur Marathas created a **syncretic, multilingual culture** blending Tamil, Telugu, and Marathi influences.
- **Serfoji II** was the most notable ruler: wrote the Marathi play *Devendra Kuravanji*; patronised music and dance during the formative period of modern **Carnatic music** and **Bharatanatyam**; founded the **Dhanwantari Mahal** (free medical treatment, Indian and Western methods); started India's first printing press by a native ruler; inscribed Bhonsle family history on the **Brihadishwara temple** walls.

## The Maratha Legacy

The Maratha rule challenged Mughal dominance and built the **largest Indian empire before the British**, ruling much of central and northern India with an efficient administration and religious tolerance. Driven by the ideal of **Swarajya**, their resistance to foreign and oppressive rule later inspired Indians to believe in self-governance — planting early seeds for India's freedom movement.

## Quick Recap

- The Marathas are a Marathi-speaking people of the Deccan plateau; the Bhakti movement gave their society its cultural foundation before political unity came.
- **Chhatrapati Shivaji** (b. 1630) founded Maratha power, built a navy, used guerrilla warfare, defeated Afzal Khan and Shaista Khan, sacked Surat, escaped Agra, and was crowned at **Raigad in 1674**, later conquering the South.
- After Shivaji, sons **Sambhaji** and **Rajaram** led the Marathas through brutal Mughal conflict; **Tarabai** drove the northward counter-expansion.
- Power decentralised to the **Peshwas** (Bajirao I, Nanasaheb); expansion reached Lahore/Attock/Peshawar; defeat at **Panipat (1761)**; recovery and recapture of **Delhi (1771)** under Mahadji Shinde.
- Three **Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818)** ended Maratha power — the British effectively took India from the Marathas more than from the Mughals.
- Shivaji's administration used a **salaried, transferable bureaucracy** and the **ashta pradhana mandala** (eight ministers); taxes included **chauth and sardeshmukhi**.
- Military strength rested on **forts**, a capable **navy** (Kanhoji Angre), and a mixed **infantry-cavalry-navy** force.
- The **panchayat** delivered local justice; **kotwals** policed towns.
- Cultural revival included Sanskrit seals and terminology, temple rebuilding, and religious tolerance; **Ahilyabai Holkar** and **Tarabai** were key women leaders; **Thanjavur** under **Serfoji II** produced a syncretic culture linked to Carnatic music and Bharatanatyam.
- Legacy: largest pre-British Indian empire, inspiring the ideal of self-rule that later fed into the freedom movement.

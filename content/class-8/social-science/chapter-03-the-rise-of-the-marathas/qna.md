### Q1: Who are the Marathas?
The **Marathas** are a group of people native to the **Deccan plateau**, specifically present-day **Maharashtra**, identified by their language, **Marathi**, which has had a continuous literary history since the 12th century.

### Q2: What is a jagir?
A **jagir** was a piece of land given by a king or ruler to someone (usually a noble or soldier) as a reward for service. The holder of the jagir could collect **taxes** from the land to meet their needs or assist the king when required.

### Q3: Define guerrilla warfare as used by the Marathas.
**Guerrilla warfare** is a tactic that uses small groups of people, moving with speed, surprise, and detailed knowledge of the terrain, to defeat much larger and more powerful armies. Chhatrapati Shivaji used this tactic to protect his people from stronger enemies such as the Bijapur Sultanate.

### Q4: What were chauth and sardeshmukhi?
**Chauth** and **sardeshmukhi** were taxes levied by the Marathas on provinces not directly under their control.
- **Chauth** was **25 per cent** of the revenue.
- **Sardeshmukhi** was an **additional 10 per cent** on top of chauth.
In return, the Marathas protected these provinces and did not interfere in their internal administration; the Mughals approved this arrangement through treaties.

### Q5: What was the ashta pradhana mandala?
The **ashta pradhana mandala** was Chhatrapati Shivaji's **council of eight ministers** who assisted him with administration: the **Pradhan** (Prime Minister), **Amatya** (Finance), **Sachiv** (Land Revenue), **Mantri** (Intelligence), **Sumant** (Foreign Affairs), **Senapati** (Commander-in-chief), **Nyayadhish** (Chief Justice), and **Panditrao** (Religious Affairs).

### Q6: Who were the bhakti saints, and why are they important to this chapter?
Bhakti saints such as **Dnyaneshwar, Namdev, Tukaram, and Ramdas** promoted devotion to the divine over external ritualism between the 7th and 17th centuries. They translated texts like the **Upanishads** and **Bhagavad Gita** into Marathi and encouraged social organisation and political awareness, giving Maharashtrian society a **solid cultural foundation** that later helped the Marathas unite as a political power.

### Q7: When and where was Chhatrapati Shivaji crowned, and what title did he take?
Chhatrapati Shivaji was crowned in **1674** at the mountain fortress of **Raigad** with full Vedic rites. His formal title was **'Shri Raja Shiva Chhatrapati'**, and he started his own era, the *Rajyabhisheka shaka*.

### Q8: What was the panchayat in the Maratha judicial system, and why must it not be confused with a modern institution?
The **panchayat** was a local gathering of officials and prominent men that formed the main body delivering **justice** in Maratha administration; an appeal against its verdict could be taken to a Maratha chief. It must **not be confused** with the current-day Panchayat, which is a governmental body, since the term refers to a very different institution in this historical context.

### Q9: Name the two types of cavalry soldiers in the Maratha army and explain the difference between them.
The Maratha cavalry had two types of soldiers:
- **Bargirs**, whose horses and equipment were paid for by the **state**.
- **Shiledars**, whose horses and equipment were paid for by the **soldiers themselves**.

### Q10: What were forts, and why were they described as "the core of the state"?
**Forts** were strategic strongholds that helped the Marathas control important routes and shelter their army during guerrilla warfare. Finance minister **Ramachandrapant Amatya**, in the *Adnyapatra*, called forts "the core of the state," explaining that without them the land would be devastated by invasion, and that it was due to forts that the Maratha state survived decades of onslaught even against an enemy as powerful as **Aurangzeb**.

### Q11: Who was Kanhoji Angre, and why did Europeans call him a "pirate"?
**Kanhoji Angre** led the Maratha navy to many victories in the 18th century using clever geography and battle tactics, despite Maratha ships being less advanced than European ones. Europeans forced Indians to buy naval trade passes (**cartaz**), but the Marathas defied this and began demanding passes from Europeans themselves — a challenge to European naval dominance that led frustrated Europeans to brand Angre a "**pirate**."

### Q12: Describe the key events of Chhatrapati Shivaji's conflict with Shaista Khan.
The Mughal nobleman **Shaista Khan** invaded Shivaji's territories with a large army and occupied them for three years. Shivaji responded with a small force, raiding Shaista Khan's camp at **night**; the Khan barely escaped, losing a few fingers, and promptly left Maharashtra. The chapter compares this daring raid to a modern-day **surgical strike**.

### Q13: Explain the circumstances and outcome of Chhatrapati Shivaji's escape from Agra.
Shivaji was persuaded by Jai Singh to visit the Mughal court at **Agra**, where **Aurangzeb** insulted him by making him stand before a general he had defeated. Shivaji stormed out and was placed under **house arrest**. To escape, he began distributing gift baskets of fruits and sweets to holy men and generals; once the guards stopped checking the baskets, Shivaji and his son **Sambhaji hid inside them** and escaped. Aurangzeb was never able to capture Shivaji again.

### Q14: How did the sacking of Surat demonstrate both Chhatrapati Shivaji's military strength and his moral principles?
Shivaji sacked **Surat**, a wealthy Mughal port city, obtaining treasure worth almost **one crore rupees** — showing his military reach and the vulnerability of Mughal territory. At the same time, he was careful **not to attack religious places** and spared the house of **Mohandas Parekh**, a charitable man, showing that his campaigns followed deliberate moral limits even in retaliation.

### Q15: What structural transformation did the Maratha state undergo after Chhatrapati Shivaji's death?
After Shivaji's death, the earlier **centralised** state gave way to a more **decentralised** structure. Though still nominally centred on the Chhatrapati, real power shifted to regional chiefs, especially the **Peshwa** (Persian for 'prime minister'), who wielded influence even over the Chhatrapati himself — Peshwa **Bajirao I** and his son **Nanasaheb Peshwa** were key figures in this pan-Indian expansion.

### Q16: Describe the role of Tarabai in Maratha history.
**Tarabai** was a fearless Maratha warrior queen who ruled after the death of her husband **Rajaram** in the early 18th century. Recognising that north India was left unprotected while the Mughal army was tied down in the Deccan, she organised large Maratha armies to invade Mughal territories in the north, making her the **architect of the northward Maratha expansion** and preserving Maratha independence during a critical period.

### Q17: Describe the role of Ahilyabai Holkar in Maratha history.
**Ahilyabai Holkar**, of the **Holkar dynasty** based around present-day Indore, governed her state wisely for **thirty years** even after losing her husband and son. A devout patron of religion, she built and restored hundreds of **temples, ghats, wells, and roads** across India, most famously rebuilding the **Kashi Vishwanath temple** (Varanasi) and the **Somnath temple** (Gujarat), and promoted the **Maheshwar weaving industry**.

### Q18: What were the main contributions of Serfoji II to Thanjavur's cultural life?
**Serfoji II**, the most notable Thanjavur Maratha ruler, was fluent in several Indian and European languages and wrote the Marathi play *Devendra Kuravanji*. He patronised musicians during the era when modern **Carnatic music** and the early stages of **Bharatanatyam** took shape, founded the **Dhanwantari Mahal** for free Indian and Western medical treatment, started India's first native-run printing press, and inscribed the Bhonsle family history on the **Brihadishwara temple** walls.

### Q19: How did Chhatrapati Shivaji demonstrate cultural and religious revival through his seal and administrative reforms?
Shivaji's **seal** carried a **Sanskrit** inscription — a break from the prevalent Persian seals — declaring his authority "reigns for the welfare of the people." He commissioned the ***Rajya-Vyavahara-Kosha***, providing Sanskrit equivalents for Persian administrative terms, which reduced foreign loanwords in Maratha diplomacy. As a devout Hindu who respected other religions, he rebuilt desecrated temples, promoted Sanskrit and Marathi literature, and adopted a saffron flag used by all Marathas thereafter.

### Q20: Analyse how geography — particularly mountains and coastlines — shaped Maratha military strategy and state formation.
Geography was central to Maratha strategy:
- **Mountains and forests** (e.g., near Pratapgad) allowed the Marathas to hide, ambush, and use **guerrilla warfare** effectively against larger armies, as seen against Afzal Khan.
- Numerous **hill forts** let the Marathas control key routes and shelter their army, functioning, as Ramachandrapant Amatya wrote, as "the core of the state."
- The **western coastline** gave access to valuable trade resources, prompting Shivaji to build a **navy** — a step neither the Bijapur Sultanate nor the Mughals had seriously taken — enabling coastal defence and challenges to European naval dominance.
In short, difficult terrain protected the Marathas defensively, while coastal access enabled trade and naval power, together allowing a smaller regional power to resist and eventually rival much larger empires.

### Q21: "The British took India from the Marathas more than from the Mughals or any other power." Explain this statement with evidence from the chapter.
This statement reflects the fact that by the late 18th century, the **Marathas**, not the Mughals, were the **largest and most powerful Indian empire**, controlling much of central and northern India and even briefly holding **Delhi**. They were the **chief rivals of the British** in this period, and it took **three Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818)** — not a single decisive Mughal confrontation — for the British to end Maratha power, owing to increased Maratha internal disunity alongside superior British organisation and technology. Since the Mughal Empire had already weakened considerably by this time, it was effectively Maratha territory and power that the British had to overcome to gain control of the subcontinent.

### Q22: Compare how Chhatrapati Shivaji and the later, decentralised Maratha chiefs treated their subjects and administered territory. Use evidence from the chapter.

| Aspect | Chhatrapati Shivaji | Later Maratha chiefs (post-decentralisation) |
|---|---|---|
| Administrative structure | Centralised; salaried, transferable officials | Decentralised; regional chiefs held greater autonomy |
| Treatment of subjects | Issued strict instructions against mistreating subjects (e.g., his letter on not cutting mango/jackfruit trees); gave pensions to soldiers' widows | Occasional indiscipline and abuse, e.g., the cruel ten-year campaign in Bengal |
| Religious approach | Respected other religions while upholding his own; rebuilt desecrated temples | Broadly continued religious tolerance (e.g., Bhonsles of Nagpur supporting Jagannath worship) |

Overall, while Shivaji's personal values emphasised restraint and care for subjects, the increased autonomy of regional chiefs after his death sometimes led to a stark contrast with these founding values.

### Q23: Compare how Chhatrapati Shivaji and later Marathas treated religious places and people of different faiths, using evidence from the chapter.
Chhatrapati Shivaji consistently combined religious devotion with tolerance: he was a **devout Hindu** who respected other religions, rebuilt **desecrated temples**, promoted Sanskrit and Marathi religious literature, and even **forbade the Dutch from trading slaves** while campaigning in the South. His successors carried this forward in various ways — the **Bhonsles of Nagpur** revived the interrupted worship of **Jagannath at Puri**, and **Ahilyabai Holkar** rebuilt major temples like **Kashi Vishwanath** and **Somnath** that had been destroyed earlier. Together, this shows a continuing Maratha tradition of reviving and protecting Hindu religious institutions while, per the chapter, ruling "without religious discrimination."

### Q24: Trace the sequence of events from Chhatrapati Shivaji's death to the decline of Maratha power, showing how each stage led to the next.
After Shivaji's death, **Sambhaji** became Chhatrapati but was captured, tortured, and executed by **Aurangzeb**, who then took the capital **Raigad**. **Rajaram** fled to **Gingee**, spreading the conflict to the south, where the Marathas held firm until Aurangzeb died without subduing them. His widow, **Tarabai**, then led a large-scale counter-expansion into Mughal territory, marking the start of Maratha dominance. This expansion brought **structural decentralisation**, with the **Peshwas** (Bajirao I, Nanasaheb) gaining power over regional chiefs and driving pan-Indian growth, including brief control of **Lahore, Attock, and Peshawar**. A crushing defeat at **Panipat (1761)** was followed by recovery under **Peshwa Madhavrao I** and the recapture of **Delhi (1771)** by **Mahadji Shinde**. However, growing internal disunity among Maratha chiefs, combined with superior British organisation and technology, led to Maratha defeat across **three Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818)**, ending Maratha power.

### Q25: "The Marathas' most important contribution to Indian history was not just military success but the demonstration that Indians could build and administer their own empire." Do you agree? Support your answer with examples from the chapter.
This is a valid interpretation, and the chapter supports it. Beyond his military victories, Shivaji contributed to a broader **cultural revival**: he used a **Sanskrit seal** instead of the prevalent Persian ones, commissioned the *Rajya-Vyavahara-Kosha* to reduce Persian loanwords in administration, rebuilt desecrated temples, and promoted Marathi and Sanskrit literature. The chapter explicitly states that "perhaps he contributed most to the revival of ancient Indian culture and values by giving people a demonstration that mighty kingdoms and empires could be defeated, and the Marathas could maintain, expand and administer an empire of their own." This example later inspired figures like **Chhatrasal** to found Bundelkhand, and the broader Maratha achievement — the largest Indian empire before the British — is credited with planting "the early seeds for India's freedom movement," showing that the psychological and cultural impact of Maratha rule extended well beyond battlefield victories.

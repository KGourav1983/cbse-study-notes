## Answer Key

1. The Marathas are a group of people native to the **Deccan plateau**, specifically present-day **Maharashtra**, and are identified with the language **Marathi**.

2. A **jagir** was a piece of land given by a king or ruler to someone (usually a noble or soldier) as a reward for service; the holder could collect taxes from the land to meet their needs or help the king when needed.

3. The two types of cavalry soldiers were the **bargirs** (whose horses and equipment were paid for by the state) and the **shiledars** (whose horses and equipment were paid for by the soldiers themselves).

4. After his coronation, Chhatrapati Shivaji took the title **'Shri Raja Shiva Chhatrapati'**.

5. Chhatrapati Shivaji was coronated at the mountain fortress of **Raigad**.

6. Chhatrapati Shivaji's kingdom expanded to India's west coast, and to secure access to the resources of the coastal regions, he felt the need to establish a **navy**. This was a revolutionary step at the time, since the **Bijapur Sultanate** only maintained merchant ships without a full-time naval force to protect the coast, and even the **Mughal Empire's** use of a navy was very limited. As a result, the Maratha Navy was born and went on to achieve legendary success, later led effectively by figures like Kanhoji Angre.

7. **Chauth** and **sardeshmukhi** were both taxes levied by the Marathas on provinces not directly under their rule. **Chauth** was a tax of **25 per cent**, while **sardeshmukhi** was an **additional 10 per cent** charged on top of chauth. In exchange for these taxes, the Marathas protected the paying provinces and did not interfere in their internal administration — an arrangement the Mughals themselves eventually approved through treaties, and one that over time helped some of these provinces become part of the Maratha Empire.

8. Ramachandrapant Amatya, the finance minister of Chhatrapati Shivaji, explained in his work *Adnyapatra* ('The Royal Edict') that forts were the core of the Maratha state. He wrote that in the absence of forts, land gets devastated in the face of invasion, and that "all the former kings secured the country by building forts." He credited the survival of the Maratha kingdom through decades of onslaught — even against a "great enemy like Aurangzeb," who had conquered great empires like Bijapur and Bhaganagar — to the strength of its forts. This was because forts allowed the Marathas to strategically control important routes and shelter their army while engaging in guerrilla warfare, giving them a defensive advantage that larger, better-resourced enemies could not easily overcome.

9. After being insulted at the Mughal court in Agra and placed under house arrest by Aurangzeb, Chhatrapati Shivaji devised a clever strategy to escape. He began sending out large gift baskets containing fruits and sweets to holy men and Mughal generals on a regular basis. The guards checked these baskets carefully at first, but over time grew careless and stopped checking them. Anticipating this, Shivaji and his son Sambhaji hid themselves inside the baskets and were carried out of the palace, successfully escaping. Aurangzeb was never able to capture Chhatrapati Shivaji again after this episode.

10. Chhatrapati Shivaji had an **ashta pradhana mandala**, or council of eight ministers, to assist him with administration, structured around the Chhatrapati (the sovereign king) at the centre:
    - **Pradhan** — Prime Minister
    - **Amatya** — Finance Minister
    - **Sachiv** — Land Revenue Minister
    - **Mantri** — Intelligence Minister
    - **Sumant** — Minister for Foreign Affairs
    - **Senapati** — Commander-in-chief
    - **Nyayadhish** — Chief Justice
    - **Panditrao** — Head of Religious Affairs
    
    This council reflects the relatively **centralised** nature of Shivaji's administration, in which he abolished hereditary posts and land assignments common under the Sultans and Mughals, instead paying every official a **salary** from the state treasury and periodically **transferring** officials so that no single individual could accumulate enough power to challenge the king. Together with his care for soldiers' families — such as giving pensions to the widows of soldiers who died in battle — this council system shows a deliberate, well-organised approach to governance rather than one relying purely on military conquest.

11. This statement reflects the historical reality that, by the second half of the 18th century, the Marathas — not the weakened Mughals — had become the **largest Indian empire** and the **chief rivals of the British** in India. The Marathas controlled much of central and northern India, briefly held territories as far north as Lahore, Attock, and Peshawar, and recaptured **Delhi in 1771** under Mahadji Shinde, holding it for three decades. Ending Maratha power was not a single event but required **three Anglo-Maratha Wars**, fought between **1775 and 1818**. The Marathas were far from an easy target — they won the First Anglo-Maratha War (1775–1782) — but over time, **increased internal disunity** among Maratha chiefs, combined with the **superior organisational and technological abilities of the British**, allowed the British to finally defeat them in the **Third Anglo-Maratha War**, ending Maratha power by 1818. Since it was Maratha territory, resources, and armies that the British had to overcome step by step to gain control of the subcontinent — far more so than any confrontation with the already-declining Mughals — the chapter concludes that "the British took India from the Marathas more than from the Mughals or any other power."

# Natural Resources and Their Use

Look around you right now. Your notebook, the chair you're sitting on, the electricity powering the light above you, even the cotton in your shirt — every single one of them started out as something in nature. This chapter is about that journey: how "nature" becomes a "resource," why some resources run out and others don't, and why something as basic as water can start wars.

## When Does Nature Become a Resource?

Here's a thought experiment: is a diamond sitting 3 km under the Antarctic ice a "resource"? Technically it's valuable — but if nobody can reach it, mine it, or use it, does calling it a "resource" mean anything?

This is exactly the distinction the chapter draws. **Nature** is everything around us that exists independently of humans — forms of life and non-life we didn't create. A **resource** is what nature becomes *once humans can actually use it*.

For something to count as a resource, three conditions need to line up:
- **Technologically accessible** — we need the know-how or tools to reach and use it.
- **Economically feasible** — extracting or using it shouldn't cost more than it's worth.
- **Culturally acceptable** — a community has to be willing to use it (for instance, some communities treat certain groves or trees as sacred and won't cut them, regardless of their timber value).

> **Exam trap:** Don't confuse "part of nature" with "resource." A tree standing in a forest is nature. The moment we cut it and turn it into furniture, it becomes a resource. Examiners often test this exact distinction.

## Three Jobs Natural Resources Do For Us

Once something *is* a resource, it usually falls into one of three roles:

| Category | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Resources essential for life** | Keep us alive — we can't manufacture these from scratch | Air, water, soil (for food) |
| **Resources for materials** | Get transformed into objects we use or admire | Wood, marble, metal ores |
| **Resources for energy** | Power our homes, transport, and industry | Coal, petroleum, sunlight, wind, flowing water |

Notice that a single resource can sometimes wear more than one hat — water is essential for life *and* a source of energy (through hydropower).

## Renewable vs Non-Renewable: It's Not Just About "Running Out"

Nature has a built-in repair system. When something gets damaged, nature can **restore** it — bring it back to a healthy state, the way a scraped knee heals. Nature can also **regenerate** — go a step further and actively create new life and new conditions for things to thrive, like a burnt forest slowly growing back with new saplings, insects, and birds.

This is the real difference between the two categories of resources:

- **Renewable resources** (sunlight, wind, flowing water, timber) keep coming back *as long as we don't use them faster than nature can restore or regenerate them.*
- **Non-renewable resources** (coal, petroleum, metal ores like iron, copper, and gold) took nature millions of years to create. At the rate we're using them, they simply can't be replaced in any human timeframe.

> **Common mistake:** Students often treat "renewable" as a permanent label — as if solar energy or timber can *never* run out. That's not quite right. A renewable resource stays renewable only if we respect its natural pace of regeneration. Push past that pace, and a "renewable" resource can behave exactly like a non-renewable one. You'll see a real example of this later in the Punjab groundwater case.

**Worked example — is it renewable or not?**
Ask yourself: *if I stopped using this resource today, would it come back on its own, and how fast?* Sunlight: yes, constantly. A forest: yes, but over years or decades — and only if it isn't cleared faster than it regrows. Coal: no — not on any timescale that matters to us. That question is your quickest way to sort any resource into the right category in an exam.

## Ecosystem Functions vs Ecosystem Services (Don't Mix These Up)

This pair sounds similar but means two different things:

- **Ecosystem functions** are things nature does automatically, with no humans involved — a tree releasing oxygen, a forest holding soil in place with its roots.
- **Ecosystem services** are the *benefits humans get* from those functions — us breathing that oxygen, farmland staying fertile because the forest nearby prevented erosion.

Think of it like a factory and its output: the function is the machine running; the service is what you receive because it ran.

**A number worth remembering:** a mature tree produces roughly **275 litres of oxygen a day**, while a human needs about **350 litres a day** — a handy way to appreciate just how many trees are quietly working to keep us breathing.

## Where Resources Are Found Changes Everything

Natural resources aren't spread evenly across India or the world — coal, oil, iron ore, and bauxite are all concentrated in particular regions. This unevenness has consequences far beyond geography:

- Regions near resource deposits often see new industries, jobs, and townships spring up.
- But the same development can displace local communities or threaten places they consider sacred — creating real tension alongside the economic benefit.
- Because rivers ignore state and international borders, water sharing becomes a constant point of negotiation — the Kaveri river's water is shared (often uneasily) between Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Puducherry, and the Brahmaputra flows through multiple countries before reaching India.

## The "Resource Curse": Why More Isn't Always Better

Here's a genuinely surprising idea: some of the most resource-rich regions in the world are *not* the wealthiest. Economists call this the **natural resource curse** (or the "paradox of plenty") — simply having lots of natural wealth doesn't automatically translate into economic prosperity, especially if a country never builds the industries needed to turn raw resources into higher-value products.

India has largely avoided this trap by investing in exactly those industries — turning raw materials into finished, valuable goods rather than just exporting them raw.

## Stewardship: Using Resources Responsibly

"Stewardship" is the chapter's word for using nature's gifts responsibly enough that they're still around for the future. Two real situations show what happens when we don't:

**Case study — Punjab's groundwater crisis:** In the 1960s, farmers switched to high-yielding wheat and paddy varieties that needed far more water. Combined with free electricity (which encouraged non-stop pumping) and heavy fertiliser/pesticide use, groundwater was pulled out far faster than rain could refill it. The result: in large parts of Punjab, water now sits inaccessible at depths of around 30 metres, and chemical residue has contaminated what's left. Roughly 80% of Punjab's area is now officially classified as "over-exploited." Food security was won in the short term — but at a long-term cost that will take years to reverse.

**Case study — Sikkim's organic turnaround:** Individual farmers, and eventually the entire state, faced the same problem in miniature — expensive chemical inputs and declining soil health. Sikkim's government pushed a switch to organic farming statewide. The transition was genuinely painful at first (yields dropped as soil recovered), but by 2016, Sikkim became India's first fully organic state. The payoff: healthier soil, returning wildlife, a boost in eco-tourism, and average farmer incomes rising by about 20%.

**The cement problem:** Cement is everywhere — houses, schools, roads, airports — but producing it is one of the most polluting industrial processes around, releasing fine dust that damages lungs, crops, and water. This is pushing a revival of older, gentler building materials (like the mud used in Rajasthan's centuries-old Jaisalmer Fort, and in modern earthen buildings in Auroville) alongside newer plant-based and recycled materials.

**Old wisdom, still useful:** Long before "sustainable agriculture" was a phrase, texts like *Vṛikṣhāyurveda* (an ancient Indian science of plant care, formalised around the 10th century CE) were already describing crop rotation, mixed cropping, natural pest repellents, and soil-friendly ploughing — practices we're now returning to.

**Looking ahead:** India and France jointly launched the **International Alliance for Solar Energy (IASE)** in 2015 to help sunlight-rich countries invest in solar power — a sign of the shift toward using more renewable, less extractive resources going forward.

## Quick Recap

- A part of nature becomes a **resource** only when it's technologically accessible, economically feasible to use, and culturally acceptable to exploit.
- Resources serve three broad roles: **essential for life**, **materials**, and **energy**.
- **Renewable** resources regenerate naturally — but only if we don't use them faster than nature can restore them; overuse can make a renewable resource behave like a non-renewable one.
- **Non-renewable** resources (fossil fuels, metal ores) formed over millions of years and can't be replaced on any human timescale.
- **Ecosystem functions** are what nature does automatically; **ecosystem services** are the benefits we get from those functions.
- Uneven distribution of resources drives trade, industry, and — sometimes — conflict and displacement.
- The **natural resource curse** shows that resource wealth alone doesn't guarantee prosperity; building industries around those resources does.
- Punjab's groundwater crisis and Sikkim's organic farming turnaround are two real, opposite examples of what happens when we ignore — or respect — nature's pace of regeneration.

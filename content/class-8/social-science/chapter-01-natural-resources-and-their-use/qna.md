# Top 25 Q&A — Natural Resources and Their Use

### Q1: What is a natural resource?
A natural resource is any material or substance that occurs in nature and is valuable to humans — something that exists independently of human creation but that people use for sustenance or to make new things.

### Q2: What three conditions must be met for something in nature to be considered a resource?
It must be **technologically accessible** (we have the means to reach or extract it), **economically feasible** to exploit (the cost of extraction is worth the value gained), and **culturally acceptable** to use.

### Q3: Give an example of something that is part of nature but is not yet a usable resource, and explain why.
Petroleum trapped deep beneath the ocean floor is part of nature, but where we lack the technology to extract it, or where extraction costs are too high, it does not yet qualify as a resource — it only becomes one once it becomes accessible and economically worthwhile to extract.

### Q4: Name the three categories of natural resources based on their use, giving one example of each.
**Resources essential for life** (e.g. air), **resources for materials** (e.g. wood), and **resources for energy** (e.g. coal).

### Q5: Why are air, water, and soil described as "resources essential for life"?
Because life cannot exist without them, and humans cannot manufacture them — we depend entirely on the atmosphere for air, on rivers and groundwater for drinking water, and on soil (combined with cultivation) for our food supply.

### Q6: What is meant by "resources for materials"? Give two examples.
These are natural resources that humans convert into physical objects for utility or beauty — for example, **wood** made into furniture, and **marble** carved into sculptures or used in construction.

### Q7: Name two natural resources used for energy other than fossil fuels.
**Sunlight** (solar energy) and **flowing water** (hydropower) are both natural energy resources that do not rely on fossil fuels.

### Q8: Differentiate between renewable and non-renewable resources, with one example of each.
**Renewable resources** (such as sunlight, wind, and timber) can regenerate naturally over time, provided they are not used faster than nature can restore them. **Non-renewable resources** (such as coal and petroleum) formed over millions of years and cannot be replenished within any meaningful human timeframe.

### Q9: What is the difference between "restoration" and "regeneration" in the context of nature?
**Restoration** is nature returning something to its original healthy state after damage (like a wound healing or a forest recovering after a fire). **Regeneration** goes further — it is nature's ability to actively create new life and new conditions for things to thrive, not just repair what existed before.

### Q10: Under what condition can a renewable resource stop behaving like one?
If a renewable resource is harvested or extracted faster than nature's natural rate of restoration and regeneration, it becomes effectively depleted — behaving like a non-renewable resource even though it is, in principle, renewable.

### Q11: Why is timber usually considered a renewable resource, and what could change that?
Timber is renewable because forests can regrow the trees that are cut. However, if trees are harvested faster than the forest can regenerate them, the forest will be depleted over time, making the timber supply effectively non-renewable in practice.

### Q12: Name two non-renewable resources found in India, and state roughly how long India's coal reserves are estimated to last at current usage.
**Coal** and **petroleum** are two of India's key non-renewable resources. India's coal reserves have been estimated to last for around **another 50 years** at present rates of use.

### Q13: Differentiate between ecosystem functions and ecosystem services, with an example of each.
**Ecosystem functions** are natural processes that occur without human involvement, such as a tree producing oxygen or a forest holding soil together with its roots. **Ecosystem services** are the benefits humans receive as a result of those functions, such as breathing that oxygen or farmland remaining fertile because nearby forests prevented soil erosion.

### Q14: Roughly how much oxygen does a mature tree produce per day, and how does this compare with a human's daily oxygen requirement?
A mature tree produces approximately **275 litres of oxygen per day**, while a human requires roughly **350 litres of oxygen per day** — showing how many trees' worth of output it takes to sustain just one person.

### Q15: How can the uneven distribution of natural resources affect trade and international relations?
Since natural resources are concentrated in specific regions rather than spread evenly, countries and regions must trade with one another to access resources they lack. This dependency can strengthen economic ties, but it can also create tension, competition, or even conflict over access and control of valuable resources.

### Q16: Name two rivers whose water-sharing has caused disagreement between different states or regions, as mentioned in the chapter.
The **Kaveri river**, shared between Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Puducherry, and the **Brahmaputra**, whose waters are shared across international borders, are both examples where water-sharing has required careful negotiation.

### Q17: What benefits and costs can arise for local communities when industries are set up near natural resource deposits?
**Benefits** include new jobs, the growth of townships, expanding economic opportunities, and improved access to modern facilities. **Costs** can include displacement of communities from their homes, and in some cases, threats to sacred or culturally significant sites, which can lead to conflict.

### Q18: What is meant by the "natural resource curse" (or "paradox of plenty")? How has India generally avoided it?
The **natural resource curse** describes how some resource-rich regions or countries experience slower economic growth rather than prosperity, often because they fail to develop industries that convert raw resources into higher-value products. India has largely avoided this by investing in the development of such industries rather than only extracting and exporting raw resources.

### Q19: Explain the case of groundwater overexploitation in Punjab. What caused it, and what were its effects?
In the 1960s, Punjab's farmers adopted high-yielding wheat and paddy varieties that required much more water, leading to heavy groundwater extraction. This was worsened by free electricity, which encouraged excessive pumping, along with heavy use of chemical fertilisers and pesticides. As a result, groundwater in large parts of Punjab is now inaccessible at depths of around **30 metres**, and chemical residues have contaminated the remaining groundwater, creating health hazards.

### Q20: What percentage of Punjab's area has been classified as "over-exploited" in terms of groundwater, and what does this classification mean?
Around **80% of Punjab's area** has been classified as "over-exploited," meaning groundwater has been extracted at a rate far greater than the rate at which it can naturally be replenished.

### Q21: Why is cement production considered harmful to the environment, and what alternatives are being explored?
Cement production releases fine dust that damages the lungs of humans and animals, reduces plant yields when it settles on leaves, and contributes to soil and water pollution. Alternatives being explored include traditional materials like **stone and mud**, along with newer **plant-based materials** and materials **recycled from waste plastic**.

### Q22: What is Vṛikṣhāyurveda, and name two sustainable practices it recommends.
**Vṛikṣhāyurveda** is an ancient Indian botanical science focused on the care of plants and trees, formalised in texts such as Surapala's work around the 10th century CE. It recommends practices such as **crop rotation and mixed cropping** to maintain soil health, along with pest management using natural repellents rather than chemicals.

### Q23: Describe how Sikkim transitioned to becoming a fully organic state, and name two benefits that followed.
Sikkim's government promoted organic farming statewide, encouraging farmers to replace chemical inputs with compost and natural pest repellents. Although yields initially dropped as soil recovered, by 2016 Sikkim had become a **100% organic state**. Benefits included the **return of local biodiversity** (such as beneficial insects and birds) and an **average 20% rise in farmers' incomes**, along with a boost in tourism.

### Q24: What is the International Alliance for Solar Energy (IASE), and why is it significant for India?
The **International Alliance for Solar Energy (IASE)** was launched by India and France in 2015 as a coalition of sunshine-rich countries working to expand solar power use. It is significant for India because the country has used the alliance to channel investment into solar projects, share technical expertise, and demonstrate leadership in the transition toward renewable energy.

### Q25: Suggest three actions that individuals or communities can take to use natural resources more responsibly.
Possible actions include: **conserving water** through practices like rainwater harvesting and avoiding wasteful use; **supporting sustainable farming methods**, such as organic farming and crop rotation, instead of over-relying on chemical inputs; and **reducing dependence on polluting materials** like cement by choosing traditional or recycled alternatives where possible.

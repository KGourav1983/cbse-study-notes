## Answer Key
1. A natural resource is any material or substance that occurs in nature and is valuable to humans.
2. Any two of: **coal, petroleum, iron, copper, gold** (any non-renewable resource named in the chapter).
3. Regeneration is nature's ability to go beyond simple repair and actively create new life and the conditions needed for that life to thrive.
4. **Vṛikṣhāyurveda**.
5. **Punjab**.
6. The resource must be **technologically accessible** (we have the means to extract or use it), **economically feasible** to exploit (the cost of extraction is worth the value gained), and **culturally acceptable** to a community.
7. **Ecosystem functions** are natural processes occurring without human involvement, such as a tree producing oxygen. **Ecosystem services** are the benefits humans gain from those functions, such as breathing that oxygen or farmland staying fertile due to a nearby forest preventing soil erosion.
8. The natural resource curse describes how resource-rich regions can experience slower economic growth rather than prosperity, usually because they fail to build industries that convert raw resources into higher-value products. A country can avoid this by investing in developing such industries rather than relying solely on raw extraction and export.
9. Any two of: traditional fishing restrictions during spawning season to protect fish populations; using compost and natural pest repellents (like neem and garlic) instead of chemical fertilisers/pesticides; crop rotation and mixed cropping as recommended by Vṛikṣhāyurveda; using traditional building materials like mud and stone instead of cement.
10. A renewable resource remains renewable only if it is used at a pace nature can restore or regenerate. For example, timber is renewable as long as trees are not cut faster than forests can regrow them — if harvesting outpaces regrowth, the forest becomes depleted, and the resource behaves as though it were non-renewable, similar to what has happened with groundwater in Punjab.
11. In the 1960s, farmers in Punjab shifted to high-yielding wheat and paddy varieties that required significantly more water. This, combined with free electricity that encouraged continuous pumping and heavy use of chemical fertilisers and pesticides, led to groundwater being extracted far faster than it could be naturally replenished. As a result, groundwater in large parts of Punjab is now inaccessible at depths of around 30 metres, chemical residues have contaminated the remaining water causing health hazards, and roughly 80% of Punjab's area is now classified as "over-exploited."
12. Cement production is one of the most polluting industrial processes — it releases fine dust that damages the lungs of humans and animals, reduces crop yields when it settles on plant leaves, and contributes to soil and water pollution. Two alternatives being explored are the use of traditional materials such as stone and mud (as seen in structures like Jaisalmer Fort and modern earthen buildings in Auroville), and the use of plant-based or recycled materials, such as those made from waste plastic.
13. Sikkim's government promoted a statewide shift to organic farming, encouraging farmers to replace chemical fertilisers and pesticides with compost and natural alternatives like neem and garlic-based repellents. Although yields dropped initially as the soil recovered from years of chemical use, by 2016 Sikkim became a fully organic state. Ecologically, local biodiversity flourished as beneficial insects and birds returned; economically, tourism increased and average farmer incomes rose by around 20%, making Sikkim a model for sustainable agricultural transition.

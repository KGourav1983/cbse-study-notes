# The Colonial Era in India

Have you ever wondered how a trading company from a small island nation ended up ruling a subcontinent that was once one of the two richest economies on Earth? That's the puzzle this chapter unpacks — and the answer says a lot about strategy, exploitation, and resistance.

## The Age of Colonialism

Before diving into India specifically, let's get the big term right, since examiners love asking for a clean definition here.

- **Colonialism** is the practice where one country takes control of another region, establishes settlements there, and imposes its political, economic, and cultural systems.
- It isn't new — it can be traced to the great empires of the 1st millennium BCE, and to the spread of Christianity and Islam in the 1st millennium CE.
- But the **'Age of Colonialism'** usually refers specifically to Europe's expansion from the **15th century** onward, led by Spain, Portugal, Britain, France, and the Netherlands, who colonised Africa, Asia, the Americas, Australia, and Pacific islands — often through military campaigns involving massacre or **enslavement** (turning someone into a slave).

### Why did European powers do this?

Think of it as a mix of greed, ambition, and self-justification:

- **Political competition** between European powers for territorial expansion and global influence.
- **Economic advantages**: access to new natural resources, markets, trade routes, and plunder.
- **Religious conversion**: spreading Christianity to indigenous populations.
- **Scientific inquiry**: a lesser motivation — exploring unknown lands to gather geographic and natural knowledge.

> **Exam trap:** Colonisers claimed a **'civilising mission'** to bring 'progress', often **demonising** (falsely portraying negatively) colonised peoples as 'savage' or 'primitive'. The chapter is clear that the *reality* was loss of independence, resource exploitation, destruction of traditional life, and imposition of foreign values — don't confuse the colonisers' stated justification with the actual impact.

Colonialism declined in the mid-20th century, especially after World War II, leading to widespread decolonisation. This chapter, however, focuses on the special case of **India**.

## Europeans in India

Here's something that might surprise you: India wasn't a poor, backward land waiting to be 'civilised' — quite the opposite.

- India traded with the Greeks and Romans over two millennia ago, exporting spices, cotton, ivory, gems, sandalwood, teakwood, and **wootz steel**.
- Until the 16th century CE, India was a vibrant economic and cultural powerhouse. Economist **Angus Maddison's** estimates suggest India contributed at least **one-fourth of world GDP** throughout this period — one of the two largest economies globally, alongside China.
- This very prosperity is exactly what made India a target for European colonial ambitions.

### The Portuguese: commerce and atrocities

- **Vasco da Gama** arrived at Kappad (near Kozhikode, Kerala) in **May 1498**, paving the way for European colonisation in India.
- Though initially well received, his aggressive methods — seizing, torturing, and killing Indian merchants, and bombarding Calicut — damaged relations with local rulers.
- The Portuguese captured **Goa in 1510**, making it their colonial capital, plus trading posts along the Malabar and Coromandel coasts.
- They enforced the ***cartaz*** (pass) system, requiring all ships in the Arabian Sea to buy Portuguese navigation permits — ships without one were seized. This let them monopolise the **spice trade** for nearly a century.
- Alongside commercial exploitation came **religious persecution**: the **Inquisition**, established at Goa in **1560**, severely persecuted Hindus, Muslims, Jews, and suspect Christian converts, alongside forced conversions and temple destruction. (The Goa Inquisition was only abolished in **1812**.)
- **Rani Abbakka I** and her successor **Rani Abbakka II**, rulers of the port town of Ullal (southern Karnataka), resisted repeated Portuguese attempts to take over their territory — their stories are remembered today through **Yakshagana**, a traditional dance-drama form.

### The Dutch: commerce and competition

- Arrived in India in the early 17th century, focusing primarily on **commercial dominance**, especially the spice trade, through the **Dutch East India Company**.
- Established trading posts at Surat, Bharuch, Cochin (west coast) and Nagapattinam, Masulipatnam (east coast), with their strongest presence in Kerala's **Malabar region**.
- Their power declined sharply after the **Battle of Colachel (1741)**, where **King Marthanda Varma of Travancore** decisively defeated them on land and sea — a rare instance of an **Asian power successfully repelling a European colonial force**.

### The French: colonial ambitions

- Established their first trading post at Surat (1668), then at **Pondicherry (1674)**, where they set up their East India Company (*Compagnie des Indes Orientales*).
- **Dupleix**, Governor-General from 1742–1754, pioneered strategies later copied by the British: training Indian soldiers in European techniques (creating **sepoys**), and ruling indirectly through puppet Indian rulers installed via succession disputes.
- French ambitions were checked during the **Carnatic Wars (1746–1763)** against Britain; despite capturing Madras in 1746, the French colony was reduced to Pondicherry and a few enclaves.
- Unlike the Portuguese, the French rarely intervened in Indian social/religious life — a rare exception being the 1748 destruction of Pondicherry's Vedapurishwaran temple.

> **Exam trap:** Students often mix up which power did what. Use this quick table to keep them straight.

| European Power | Key Trait | Major Setback |
|---|---|---|
| Portuguese | Religious persecution (Inquisition), *cartaz* system, spice trade monopoly | — |
| Dutch | Focused purely on commerce/spice trade | Defeated at Battle of Colachel (1741) by Travancore |
| French | Trained sepoys, indirect rule via puppet rulers | Lost ground to British in Carnatic Wars (1746–63) |
| British | Combined trade, diplomacy, and military conquest | — (ultimately dominant) |

## Enter the British

### From traders to rulers

This is one of history's most remarkable transformations — a trading company becoming an empire.

- The **English East India Company** received a royal charter from **Queen Elizabeth I**, granting special powers like raising a private army — but its agents kept up a pretence of being mere traders.
- This allowed them to establish coastal footholds in the 17th century — **Surat, Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta** — with minimal resistance, since local rulers welcomed foreign trade.

### The strategy of 'divide and rule'

- While appearing as traders, Company agents cultivated political relationships with local rulers, offered military support against rivals, and exploited succession disputes and religious tensions — the **'divide and rule'** policy.
- The **Battle of Plassey (1757)** is the classic example: **Robert Clive** conspired with **Mir Jafar**, the Nawab of Bengal's military commander, promising to install him as Nawab in exchange for betrayal. Mir Jafar's forces stood aside during battle, ensuring British victory despite smaller numbers.

> **Exam trap:** Remember — even today, **'Mir Jafar'** remains a synonym for 'traitor' in India. Examiners like this cultural detail as evidence of the battle's lasting legacy.

### Doctrine of Lapse and Subsidiary Alliance

- The **Doctrine of Lapse**: any princely state would be annexed if its ruler died without a natural male heir — deliberately disregarding the legitimate Hindu tradition of adoption. This created resentment that contributed to the 1857 Rebellion.
- A **princely state** was a region ruled by an Indian prince/maharaja/nawab that had accepted British protection while keeping internal autonomy — over **500 princely states** existed at Independence, covering about **40 per cent** of the Subcontinent.
- **Subsidiary alliance**: British 'Residents' were installed in Indian courts to 'protect' rulers, who in exchange had to maintain British troops at their own expense and conduct foreign relations only through Britain. This created what was called an **'empire on the cheap'** — Hyderabad was among the first to enter such an alliance, in 1798.

## From Paradise to Hell?

### Devastating famines

Here's a chilling contrast: Robert Clive called Bengal "the paradise of the earth" — yet under Company rule, it became a graveyard.

- After Plassey, the Company gained revenue collection rights in Bengal, Bihar, and Odisha, extracting maximum revenue while investing minimally in governance.
- The **Bengal Famine of 1770–1772**, worsened by harsh cash-tax demands regardless of harvest, killed nearly **one-third** of Bengal's population — an estimated **10 million people**. The Company even *increased* land tax during the famine.
- The **Great Famine of 1876–1878** killed up to **8 million** Indians, mostly in the Deccan — even as grain was exported to Britain (about **one million tonnes of rice per year** during the famine) and Viceroy **Lord Lytton** ordered no government interference in food prices, while hosting a lavish week-long durbar feast for 68,000 officials.
- Estimates of total famine deaths across British rule range from **50 to 100 million** — nearly the number of deaths caused by World War II.

> **Exam trap:** Don't say famines were unique to colonial India — the chapter notes famines occurred throughout Indian history from droughts/floods, but **never on this scale**; colonial policy (harsh taxation plus 'free market' non-intervention) is what made them catastrophic.

### The drain of India's wealth

- U.S. historian **Brooks Adams** (1895) argued Britain's Industrial Revolution (beginning around 1760) was fuelled by "Bengal plunder."
- **Dadabhai Naoroji** (first Indian elected to the British **House of Commons**, 1892) wrote *Poverty and Un-British Rule in India* (1901), compiling evidence of wealth drained from India; **Romesh Chunder Dutt** did similar work in *Economic History of India*.
- A recent estimate by **Utsa Patnaik** puts the wealth extracted from India (1765–1938) at **$45 trillion** in today's value — about **13 times Britain's 2023 GDP**.
- This wasn't just taxes — Indians were even charged for British expenditure on **railways, telegraph, and wars**.

### Reading the painting: 'The East offering its riches to Britannia'

- This painting (1778, by Spiridione Roma) was made for the East India Company's London headquarters.
- Symbolism: **Britannia** sits higher than the colonies (India, China), reflecting Britain's claimed superior power; the darker-skinned Indian figures are shown offering pearls, jewels, and cotton in a bent, lower posture — reflecting era-typical beliefs in white racial superiority, not a genuine 'offering'.

## Changing Landscapes

The British reshaped nearly every aspect of Indian life, convinced their systems were 'superior'.

### Decline of India's indigenous industries

- Before the 18th century, India was renowned for **textile manufacturing** — cotton, silk, wool, jute, hemp, coir — with intricate designs and fine muslins in high global demand.
- British policy imposed **heavy duties on Indian textile imports into Britain** while forcing India to accept British goods with minimal tariffs, and Britain controlled sea trade and exchange rates.
- Result: India's textile exports collapsed while British imports into India soared. Governor-General **William Bentinck** observed in 1834: **"The bones of the cotton weavers are bleaching the plains of India."**
- India's share of world GDP fell to hardly **5 per cent** at Independence — from one of the richest lands to one of the poorest in under two centuries.

### Dismantling traditional governance structures

- India had well-organised local self-governance: village councils managed community affairs, disputes, and public works.
- **Charles Metcalfe** (acting Governor-General, 1830s) called village communities "**little republics**" that survived dynasty after dynasty.
- The British dismantled these, replacing them with a centralised bureaucracy focused on **tax collection**, not public welfare, and imposed British law codes that were expensive, slow, and conducted in a foreign language.

### Transforming Indian education: creating 'brown Englishmen'

- Pre-colonial India had diverse educational traditions: ***pathashalas***, ***madrasas***, ***viharas***, apprenticeships — hundreds of thousands of village schools existed (100,000–150,000 in Bengal and Bihar alone, up to 1830).
- **Thomas B. Macaulay's** 1835 **'Minute on Indian Education'** was the turning point: despite admitting no knowledge of Sanskrit or Arabic, he declared "a single shelf of a good European library was worth the whole native literature of India and Arabia," aiming to create Indians "**Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect**."
- This policy won out over Orientalist objections, and traditional schools declined while English became the language of prestige — creating a lasting divide between English-educated elites and the masses, and supplying cheap clerks for colonial administration.

### Reshaping economic structures to serve imperial needs

- India's economy was transformed from self-sufficient (agriculture plus craft/manufacture) into a supplier of **raw materials** for British industry and a forced market for British goods.
- The **railway network**, though it did integrate India's internal market, was designed mainly to move raw materials to ports for export, distribute British goods, and move armies to crush rebellions — not to serve Indian needs.
- Crucially, most railway and telegraph construction was **paid for by Indian tax revenue** — as were the administrative costs of colonial rule itself. In short: **Indians funded their own subjugation**.

## Early Resistance Movements: Challenging Colonial Authority

Even as the British called India "the jewel in the crown" of an empire "on which the sun never sets," resistance began almost immediately.

### The 'Sannyasi-Fakir rebellion'

- Began in Bengal after the terrible **famine of 1770**; groups of **sannyasis** (Hindu ascetics) and **fakirs** (Muslim ascetics), whose free movement was restricted by new land/tax policies, attacked British treasuries and tax collectors over three decades.
- Inspired **Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay's** novel *Anandamath* (1882), which contained **'Vande Mataram'** — later a rallying song of the freedom struggle and, after Independence, India's national song.

### Tribal uprisings

- British expansion into forests and hills disrupted tribal life: restricted forest access, imposed cash taxes, debt traps, replaced tribal councils with British law, and classified hundreds of tribes as 'criminal tribes'.
- **Kol Uprising (1831–1832)**: in Chota Nagpur (Jharkhand), against land policies favouring outsiders; the Kol tribes (including Mundas and Oraons) briefly controlled significant territory before defeat.
- **Santhal Rebellion (1855–1856)**: led by brothers **Sidhu and Kanhu Murmu** against moneylenders and landlords seizing ancestral lands with British support; Santhals declared their own government and vowed to "fight to the last drop of blood," but the British brutally crushed the uprising, burning villages and killing thousands.

### Peasant uprisings against economic exploitation

- **Indigo Revolt (1859–1862)**: European planters forced Bengal peasants to grow indigo instead of food crops; peasants were trapped in **debt slavery** and faced imprisonment or torture for refusing. Supported by educated Bengalis and the Bengali press, it forced the British to restrict some of the worst abuses.

## The Great Rebellion of 1857

> **Exam trap:** The British called this the **'Sepoy Mutiny'**, but post-Independence historians rejected this term, preferring **'Great Rebellion of 1857'** — know both names and why the terminology matters.

### Background

- Earlier warning sign: the **Vellore Mutiny (1806)**, sparked by uniform regulations violating Hindu and Muslim sepoys' religious practices (banning religious marks, forcing beard-shaving); sepoys seized Vellore fort before being crushed.
- Most sepoys came from **agricultural families** suffering under British land revenue policies.

### The spark and spread

- In 1857, rumours spread that new rifle cartridges were greased with **cow and pig fat**, offending Hindu and Muslim sepoys.
- At **Barrackpore**, sepoy **Mangal Pandey** attacked British officers; his execution spread further discontent.
- At **Meerut**, sepoys killed British officers and marched to **Delhi**, proclaiming the elderly, politically powerless Mughal emperor **Bahadur Shah Zafar** as their leader.
- The revolt spread across northern and central India, with sepoys capturing **Kanpur, Lucknow, and Jhansi**. At Kanpur, rebel forces under **Nana Saheb** massacred over 200 British civilians after promising safe passage.

### British response and outcome

- The British response was **systematic and brutal**: house-to-house massacres in recaptured Delhi, mass executions at Kanpur to strike terror, and burned villages/destroyed crops in a long punitive campaign — causing far more deaths than the rebels inflicted.
- The rebellion failed, partly due to the sepoys' **lack of unified command and consistent strategy**.
- Consequences: in **1858**, the **British Crown** took direct control from the East India Company, starting the **British Raj**; the Indian Army was reorganised to prevent future unified resistance.
- Despite failing militarily, the rebellion sowed the seed for the idea that foreign domination was unacceptable — a seed that grew into the 20th-century freedom struggle.

### Two heroines

- **Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi**: fought to save her kingdom from annexation, escaped besieged Jhansi with help from **Tatia Tope**, conquered Gwalior fort, and was killed in battle on **18 June 1858**. A British officer called her "the best and bravest of the rebels."
- **Begum Hazrat Mahal of Awadh**: led the defence of Lucknow after her kingdom's annexation, rejected British offers of safe passage, and took refuge in Nepal. She issued a counter-proclamation warning Indians not to trust Queen Victoria's 1858 promises of reform.

## The Legacy of European Colonialism in India

The chapter is explicit: this was **not** a genuine 'civilising mission' — India's civilisation predated Europe's. It was systematic subjugation and exploitation, with brutal repression when needed.

### Unintended consequences

- Colonial rule (mostly British, and the French to a lesser extent) opened (or re-opened) India to the world and vice versa, through **meticulous surveys**, documentation of monuments, and the founding of **archaeology** as a discipline — though ethnic classification lists were flawed by unscientific notions of 'race'.
- At the same time, colonisers **stole thousands of statues, paintings, jewels, and manuscripts**, sending them to European museums/collections — a profound cultural loss, now the subject of ongoing repatriation debates.
- British scholars began publishing translations of Sanskrit texts into European languages (e.g., **Charles Wilkins'** 1785 English translation of the **Bhagavad Gita**), followed by the French, Germans, and others. German philosopher **Georg Hegel** compared this to the 'discovery of a new continent' — showing that while political domination flowed from Europe to India, **cultural influence sometimes flowed the other way**.

## Quick Recap

- **Colonialism**: one country controlling another region and imposing its systems; Europe's 'Age of Colonialism' began in the 15th century, driven by political competition, economic gain, religious conversion, and scientific curiosity — masked by a false 'civilising mission'.
- Pre-colonial India contributed roughly **one-fourth of world GDP**, making it an attractive target.
- **Portuguese** (from 1498): commerce plus religious persecution (Goa Inquisition, 1560); defied by Rani Abbakka I and II.
- **Dutch** (from early 1600s): pure commerce; defeated by Travancore at the **Battle of Colachel (1741)**.
- **French** (from 1668/1674): Dupleix pioneered sepoys and indirect rule; checked by Britain in the **Carnatic Wars (1746–63)**.
- **British**: rose from traders to rulers via the East India Company, using **'divide and rule'** (Battle of Plassey, 1757), the **Doctrine of Lapse**, and **subsidiary alliances**.
- Colonial exploitation caused devastating **famines** (Bengal 1770–72; Great Famine 1876–78) and a massive **wealth drain** (estimated $45 trillion, 1765–1938).
- British policy **deindustrialised** India (destroying the textile industry), dismantled traditional governance and law, and reshaped education (Macaulay's 1835 Minute) to create English-educated elites.
- **Railways and telegraphs** served British strategic/commercial interests and were largely funded by Indian taxes — "Indians funded their own subjugation."
- Resistance built steadily: the **Sannyasi-Fakir rebellion**, **tribal uprisings** (Kol, Santhal), the **Indigo Revolt**, culminating in the **Great Rebellion of 1857** — brutally crushed, but leading to the end of Company rule and the start of the **British Raj** (1858).
- Colonial rule's legacy includes both destruction (deindustrialisation, cultural theft, famine deaths) and unintended consequences (documentation, archaeology, and the flow of Indian classical knowledge—like Sanskrit texts—back into European thought).

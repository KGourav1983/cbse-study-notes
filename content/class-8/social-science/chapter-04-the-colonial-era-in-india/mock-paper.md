## Answer Key

1. **Colonialism** is the practice where one country takes control of another region, establishing settlements there, and imposing its political, economic, and cultural systems.

2. Vasco da Gama arrived at **Kappad (near Kozhikode, Kerala)** in **May 1498**.

3. **Siraj-ud-daulah**, the Nawab of Bengal, was defeated at the Battle of Plassey.

4. The **Doctrine of Lapse** stated that any princely state would be annexed by the British if its ruler died without a natural male heir, disregarding the Hindu tradition of adoption.

5. The sepoy **Mangal Pandey** attacked British officers at Barrackpore in 1857.

6. The Battle of Colachel (1741) is historically significant because it was fought between the **Dutch** and the forces of **Travancore** under **King Marthanda Varma**, who decisively defeated the Dutch both on **land and at sea**. This battle marked a sharp decline in Dutch power and influence in India. Most importantly, it is considered significant because it was a **rare instance of an Asian power successfully repelling a European colonial force** at a time when most Asian regions were losing ground to European conquest — making it an exceptional example of successful resistance rather than colonial expansion.

7. The Dutch and Portuguese differed considerably in their approach to India:
    - The **Portuguese** combined commercial ambitions with deep religious and cultural intervention. They established the **Inquisition** at Goa in 1560, which severely persecuted Hindus, Muslims, Jews, and suspect Christian converts, and was accompanied by **forced conversions** and the destruction of many Hindu temples.
    - The **Dutch**, in contrast, focused primarily on **commercial dominance**, particularly in the spice trade, through the Dutch East India Company, and did **not much intervene** in Indians' social and religious life.
    
    In short, while both powers sought trade dominance, only the Portuguese systematically imposed religious transformation on the Indian population, whereas the Dutch largely limited themselves to economic competition.

8. A **subsidiary alliance** was a system whereby the British installed a British 'Resident' in the courts of Indian rulers, ostensibly to protect them against internal or external threats. In exchange, the rulers had to **maintain British troops at their own expense** and conduct their **foreign relations only through the British**. While this arrangement appeared to preserve the sovereignty of princely states, it effectively transferred real power to the British while burdening Indian rulers with the costs of their own subjugation. This benefited the British enormously, as it allowed them to control vast territories without bearing the administrative costs of direct rule — creating what was described as "an empire on the cheap." The ruler of Hyderabad was among the first to enter such an alliance, in 1798, and once a state entered the system, exiting it was virtually impossible due to the threat of overwhelming British military response.

9. Three factors, other than military campaigns, that drew European powers to India were:
    - **Economic advantages**: access to new natural resources, new markets, and new trade routes, along with the prospect of plunder.
    - **Religious conversion**: converting indigenous populations to Christianity was a powerful motivation, as seen especially in the Portuguese approach.
    - **Scientific inquiry**: a lesser but significant motivation was the desire to explore unknown lands to accumulate knowledge of the planet's geography and natural history.
    
    Additionally, political competition between European powers created a race for territorial expansion and global influence, though this is closely tied to the economic motive above.

10. British economic policy significantly worsened the severity of famines in colonial India, as illustrated by two major famines. During the **Bengal Famine of 1770–1772**, which came on top of two years of crop failure, the East India Company imposed **harsh revenue collection targets**, requiring farmers to pay a high rate of cash taxes on their produce regardless of harvest conditions. Remarkably, the Company **increased the land tax even during the famine**, and the resulting catastrophe killed nearly one-third of Bengal's population, an estimated 10 million people. British official W.W. Hunter later described how "the husbandmen sold their cattle... they sold their sons and daughters... they ate the leaves of trees and the grass of the field" as the government continued to demand increased land tax. Over a century later, during the **Great Famine of 1876–1878**, up to 8 million Indians died, mostly in the Deccan plateau. This famine was worsened by the British economic policy of the '**free market**', which left commodity prices free to fluctuate without government intervention; Viceroy **Lord Lytton** explicitly ordered that "there is to be no interference of any kind on the part of the Government with the object of reducing the price of food." Compounding this, the British administration continued to **export grain to Britain** — about one million tonnes of rice alone per year during the three years of the famine — even as people starved, and some Indian traders held onto stocks in hope of price rises, creating artificial scarcity. Famine relief camps were opened but were too few and inadequately supplied; the Famine Commission of 1878–80 even argued that famine relief should be kept deliberately low. Together, these examples show that it was not natural crop failure alone, but deliberate revenue and market policies, that turned droughts into mass-mortality events on a scale India had never previously experienced.

11. The **Great Rebellion of 1857** — called the 'Sepoy Mutiny' by the British — arose from a combination of long-standing and immediate causes. Most sepoys came from **agricultural families** who had suffered great hardship under British land revenue policies, and earlier incidents like the **Vellore Mutiny of 1806** (sparked by uniform regulations violating religious practices) showed longstanding discontent. The immediate spark in 1857 was the spread of rumours across northern and central India that new **rifle cartridges were greased with cow and pig fat**, offending both Hindu and Muslim sepoys' religious sensibilities. At **Barrackpore**, sepoy **Mangal Pandey** attacked British officers, and his execution spread further unrest. At **Meerut**, sepoys killed their British officers and marched to **Delhi**, where they proclaimed the elderly, politically powerless Mughal emperor **Bahadur Shah Zafar** as their symbolic leader, though actual military decisions were taken by the rebel commanders. The revolt spread rapidly across northern and central India, with sepoys capturing key cities including **Kanpur, Lucknow, and Jhansi**. At Kanpur, rebel forces under **Nana Saheb** initially promised safe passage to British civilians but then massacred over 200 men, women, and children. Notable resistance leaders included **Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi**, who escaped a besieged Jhansi with the help of **Tatia Tope** and later died fighting at Gwalior, and **Begum Hazrat Mahal of Awadh**, who led the defence of Lucknow and later took refuge in Nepal rather than trust British assurances. The British response was systematic and extremely brutal: they conducted house-to-house massacres upon recapturing Delhi in September 1857, carried out mass executions at Kanpur to spread terror, and burned villages and destroyed crops in a long punitive campaign, causing far more deaths than the rebels had inflicted. The rebellion ultimately failed, largely because the sepoys **lacked a unified command and a consistent strategy** despite some heroic leadership. Its consequences were significant: in **1858**, the British Crown took direct control of India from the East India Company, beginning the period of the **British Raj**; British policy shifted from aggressive territorial expansion to consolidation of control, and the Indian Army was reorganised to prevent future unified resistance. Although it failed militarily, the rebellion sowed an important seed — the idea that foreign domination was unacceptable — which would grow into a full-fledged freedom struggle in the 20th century.

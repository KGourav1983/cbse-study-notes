### Q1: Define colonialism.
**Colonialism** is the practice where one country takes control of another region, establishing settlements there, and imposing its **political, economic, and cultural systems** on it.

### Q2: What was India's approximate share of world GDP before the colonial period, and according to which economist's estimates?
According to estimates by economist **Angus Maddison**, India contributed at least **one-fourth of the world GDP** during the period before European colonisation, making it one of the two largest economies globally alongside **China**.

### Q3: Define the term 'enslavement' and explain its relevance to European colonial conquests.
**Enslavement** means turning someone into a slave. Many European conquests during the Age of Colonialism were achieved through military campaigns that involved the **massacre or enslavement** of native populations.

### Q4: What was the 'cartaz' system introduced by the Portuguese?
The **cartaz** was a pass system requiring all ships in the Arabian Sea to purchase **Portuguese permits** for navigation; ships without these permits were seized. This naval dominance allowed the Portuguese to **monopolise the spice trade** between India and Europe for nearly a century.

### Q5: What was the Goa Inquisition?
The **Inquisition** was a tribunal set up by the Roman Catholic Church to judge heretics. Established by the Portuguese at **Goa in 1560**, it severely persecuted **Hindus, Muslims, Jews, and suspect Christian converts**, accompanied by forced conversions and the destruction of many Hindu temples. It was only abolished in **1812**.

### Q6: Who were 'sepoys', and who first trained them in this role?
**Sepoys** were Indian soldiers trained in **European military techniques** to serve as disciplined infantry. This practice was pioneered by **Dupleix**, Governor-General of French India (1742–1754), and was later adopted by the British.

### Q7: What was the 'divide and rule' policy?
**'Divide and rule'** was the British strategy of cultivating political relationships with local Indian rulers, offering military support to some against their rivals, and exploiting succession disputes or existing tensions (including between religious communities) within Indian society, so as to benefit from these conflicts and expand their own control.

### Q8: What was the Doctrine of Lapse?
The **Doctrine of Lapse** was a British policy stating that any princely state would be **annexed** if its ruler died without a natural male heir. This deliberately disregarded the legitimate **Hindu tradition of adoption** as a means of succession, and led to the annexation of numerous states, causing much resentment.

### Q9: What was a 'subsidiary alliance'?
Under a **subsidiary alliance**, the British installed a 'Resident' in the courts of Indian rulers to protect them against threats; in exchange, the rulers had to maintain British troops at their own expense and conduct foreign relations only through the British. This transferred real power to the British while making Indian rulers pay for their own subjugation, creating what was called an "**empire on the cheap**."

### Q10: What was the Battle of Colachel (1741), and why was it significant?
The **Battle of Colachel (1741)** was fought between the **Dutch** and the forces of **Travancore** under **King Marthanda Varma**, who decisively defeated the Dutch both on land and at sea. It was significant as a **rare instance of an Asian power successfully repelling a European colonial force**, and it led to a sharp decline in the Dutch presence in India.

### Q11: Describe the events and significance of the Battle of Plassey (1757).
Tensions arose between **Siraj-ud-daulah**, the Nawab of Bengal, and East India Company officials led by **Robert Clive**. Clive identified disgruntled elements in the Nawab's court and conspired with **Mir Jafar**, the Nawab's military commander, promising to install him as the new Nawab in exchange for betrayal. During the battle at Palashi, Mir Jafar's forces — the majority of the Nawab's army — stood aside, ensuring a British victory despite their smaller numbers. This exemplifies the 'divide and rule' strategy and marked a turning point in British territorial control, as the Company gained revenue collection rights over **Bengal, Bihar, and Odisha** soon after. Even today, **'Mir Jafar'** remains a synonym for 'traitor' in India.

### Q12: What were the main causes and consequences of the Bengal Famine of 1770–1772?
The famine was caused by **harsh revenue collection targets** imposed by the East India Company in Bengal — requiring farmers to pay high cash taxes regardless of harvest conditions — coming on top of two years of crop failure. The Company even **increased land tax during the famine**. As a result, the famine killed nearly **one-third of Bengal's population**, an estimated **10 million people**. Such famines recurred throughout British rule, with the **Great Famine of 1876–1878** killing up to **8 million** more, partly due to British 'free market' non-interference policies and continued grain exports during the crisis.

### Q13: Explain what is meant by the 'drain of India's wealth' during colonial rule, with reference to specific estimates.
The 'drain of wealth' refers to the systematic **extraction of India's resources and revenue** by British colonisers. U.S. historian **Brooks Adams** noted that the 'Bengal plunder' following Plassey coincided with the start of Britain's Industrial Revolution in 1760. Indian nationalist **Dadabhai Naoroji** (in *Poverty and Un-British Rule in India*, 1901) and historian **Romesh Chunder Dutt** documented billions of pounds extracted from India, not just through taxes but by charging Indians for British expenditure on railways, telegraphs, and wars. A more recent estimate by **Utsa Patnaik** puts the total extraction between **1765 and 1938 at $45 trillion** (in today's value) — about **13 times Britain's 2023 GDP**.

### Q14: How did British policy lead to the decline of India's indigenous textile industry?
British policy imposed **heavy duties on Indian textiles imported into Britain**, while forcing India to accept British manufactured goods with **minimal tariffs**. Britain also controlled most sea trade and exchange rates, making it harder for Indian traders to export. As a result, India's textile exports fell sharply in the 19th century while British imports into India rose sharply, ruining communities of skilled artisans and forcing them into subsistence agriculture on overtaxed land. Governor-General **William Bentinck** observed in 1834 that "**the bones of the cotton weavers are bleaching the plains of India**." India's share of world GDP fell to hardly **5 per cent** by Independence.

### Q15: How did the introduction of railways in India serve British colonial interests rather than Indian needs?
Although railways brought people closer together and integrated India's internal market, they were designed primarily to **move raw materials from the interior to ports for export** and to **distribute British manufactured goods** throughout India, while also allowing armies to move quickly to suppress rebellions. Railway routes largely **ignored existing trade patterns**. Moreover, most of the construction was **paid for by Indian tax revenue**, meaning Indians funded infrastructure that primarily served British strategic and commercial interests rather than their own needs.

### Q16: What was the significance of Thomas Macaulay's 1835 'Minute on Indian Education'?
Macaulay's Minute marked a turning point in Indian education. Despite admitting he had "no knowledge of either Sanskrit or Arabic," he claimed "**a single shelf of a good European library was worth the whole native literature of India and Arabia**," and argued Indians needed British education aimed at creating a class of people "**Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect**." This policy won out over Orientalist objections, leading to the decline of India's traditional schools (*pathashalas*, *madrasas*, *viharas*) and creating lasting divisions between English-educated elites and the masses, while providing cheap clerks for the colonial administration.

### Q17: What was the Santhal Rebellion (1855–1856), and how did the British respond?
The **Santhal Rebellion** was a widespread uprising of the Santhal people across present-day Jharkhand, Bihar, and West Bengal, led by brothers **Sidhu and Kanhu Murmu**, against moneylenders and landlords who were seizing their ancestral lands with British support. The Santhals declared their own government and vowed to "fight to the last drop of blood." The British response was brutal: after initial losses, they burned entire villages and killed thousands of Santhals, including eventually the rebel leaders. Despite its defeat, the rebellion inspired other tribal communities to resist colonial rule.

### Q18: What was the Indigo Revolt (1859–1862)?
The **Indigo Revolt** arose when European planters forced peasants in northern Bengal to abandon food crops and grow **indigo** instead, as indigo dye was in high demand in Europe. Peasants were poorly paid and trapped in **debt slavery**; those who refused to grow indigo faced imprisonment, torture, and destruction of property. The uprising, directed mainly at the planters, was supported by educated Bengalis and the Bengali press, eventually forcing British authorities to restrict some of the worst abuses.

### Q19: Trace the immediate spark and spread of the Great Rebellion of 1857.
In 1857, rumours spread across northern and central India that new **rifle cartridges were greased with cow and pig fat**, offending the religious sensibilities of Hindu and Muslim sepoys. At **Barrackpore**, sepoy **Mangal Pandey** attacked British officers; his subsequent execution spread further discontent. At **Meerut**, sepoys killed their British officers and marched to **Delhi**, where they proclaimed the elderly, politically powerless Mughal emperor **Bahadur Shah Zafar** as their leader. The revolt then spread across northern and central India, with sepoys capturing key cities including **Kanpur, Lucknow, and Jhansi**.

### Q20: Why do historians reject the term 'Sepoy Mutiny' in favour of 'Great Rebellion of 1857'?
The chapter notes that after India's Independence, historians rejected the term **'Sepoy Mutiny'** — which framed the uprising narrowly as **soldiers rebelling against their officers** (the literal meaning of 'mutiny') — because this label failed to reflect the event's true scale and character as a broader uprising involving **peasants, tribal groups, deposed rulers, and civilians**, and rooted in deep, longstanding grievances against colonial rule rather than a purely military disciplinary issue. Following many scholars, the chapter instead uses the term "**Great Rebellion of 1857**."

### Q21: Explain how the British response to the 1857 Rebellion compared in scale and brutality to the actions of the rebels, and what its immediate political consequence was.
The British response to the 1857 Rebellion was **systematic and extremely brutal**. On recapturing Delhi in September 1857, British forces conducted **house-to-house massacres**; at Kanpur, they carried out mass executions designed to strike terror in the population; and in a long punitive campaign, they burned villages and destroyed crops, causing **countless deaths — vastly more than those inflicted by the rebels** (such as the Kanpur massacre of over 200 British civilians under Nana Saheb). The rebellion ultimately failed, partly because the sepoys lacked unified command and a consistent strategy. Its immediate political consequence was that in **1858**, the **British Crown** took direct control of India from the East India Company, initiating the period of the **British Raj**, with the Indian Army reorganised to prevent future unified resistance.

### Q22: Compare the colonial approaches of the Portuguese, Dutch, and French in India, with reference to religious/cultural intervention.
The three powers differed notably in their level of intervention in Indian social and religious life:

| Power | Religious/Cultural Approach |
|---|---|
| **Portuguese** | Deep intervention — established the Goa Inquisition (1560), persecuted Hindus, Muslims, Jews, and suspect converts; forced conversions and destroyed many Hindu temples. |
| **Dutch** | Focused primarily on commercial dominance (spice trade); did not much intervene in social/religious life. |
| **French** | Also focused mainly on trade and political strategy (e.g., puppet rulers via Dupleix); rarely intervened religiously, with a rare exception being the 1748 destruction of Pondicherry's Vedapurishwaran temple at the request of Jesuit priests and Dupleix's wife. |

Thus, while all three pursued commercial and territorial goals, only the **Portuguese** systematically imposed religious transformation on the Indian population, while the Dutch and French were comparatively restrained in this regard.

### Q23: "Indians funded their own subjugation." Explain this statement with reference to British infrastructure and administration in India.
This statement reflects the fact that the British did not pay for the colonial project themselves — Indian taxpayers did. Most of the construction of India's **railway and telegraph networks**, though designed primarily to serve British strategic and commercial interests (moving raw materials to ports, distributing British goods, and moving troops to suppress rebellions), was **paid for by Indian tax revenue** rather than being a gift from the colonial rulers. Similarly, the **colonial administrative apparatus, military installations, and the lavish lifestyles of British officials in India** were all financed through Indian taxation. In essence, the very system and infrastructure used to control and exploit India was funded by the Indian population itself, making them, in effect, financiers of their own domination.

### Q24: Analyse how British economic and educational policies together created long-term structural damage to Indian society, going beyond simple military conquest.
British colonial control in India relied on much more than military force — it was cemented through interconnected economic and educational policies. **Economically**, heavy duties on Indian textile exports combined with minimal tariffs on British goods, along with British control of sea trade and exchange rates, destroyed India's world-renowned textile industry, driving artisans into poverty and overtaxed subsistence agriculture; India's share of world GDP collapsed from about a quarter to just 5 per cent by Independence. Devastating, poorly-managed famines (Bengal 1770–72, Great Famine 1876–78) killed tens of millions, while the 'drain of wealth' extracted an estimated $45 trillion from India between 1765 and 1938. **Educationally**, Macaulay's 1835 Minute deliberately sidelined India's traditional schooling systems (pathashalas, madrasas, viharas) in favour of English-medium education designed to create Indians "English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect" — producing a class of cheap clerks for the administration while alienating the wider population from their own cultural heritage. Together, these policies dismantled India's economic self-sufficiency and cultural institutions, entrenching poverty and social division that persisted well beyond the colonial period itself, showing that colonial domination operated as much through economic and cultural restructuring as through direct military conquest.

### Q25: "The European conquest and rule of India was not a 'civilising mission'." Evaluate this statement using evidence from the chapter, while also considering any unintended positive consequences of colonial rule.
The evidence in the chapter strongly supports this statement. India's own civilisation predated Europe's, and the chapter explicitly states that colonial rule "was a process of subjugation and exploitation executed systematically, with brutal repression if necessary." The claimed 'civilising mission' is contradicted by the devastating famines (with tens of millions of deaths), the deliberate deindustrialisation of India's once-thriving textile sector, the dismantling of effective traditional governance systems that Charles Metcalfe himself praised as "little republics," and an education policy (Macaulay's Minute) explicitly designed to create a subordinate, culturally anglicised class rather than genuinely improve Indian welfare. The estimated $45 trillion wealth drain further shows that colonial rule primarily benefited Britain rather than India. That said, the chapter does note some **largely unintended consequences**: British (and to a lesser extent French) documentation of India's geography and monuments helped found the discipline of archaeology, and the translation of Sanskrit texts (such as Charles Wilkins' 1785 translation of the Bhagavad Gita) introduced Indian classical culture to Europe, influencing philosophers, writers, and artists — described by Georg Hegel as like the "discovery of a new continent." However, these benefits were incidental side effects of a fundamentally exploitative system, and they came at the cost of massive theft of India's cultural artefacts. On balance, the evidence supports that colonial rule was not genuinely a civilising mission but a system of exploitation with occasional, unintended cultural cross-pollination.

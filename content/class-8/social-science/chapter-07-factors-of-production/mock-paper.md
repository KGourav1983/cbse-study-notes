## Answer Key

1. **Capital**, in economics, refers to any asset — physical or financial — used to produce goods and services. It comprises **monetary resources** and **durable assets** like machinery, tools, equipment, vehicles, computers, shops, and factories.

2. A **startup** is an entrepreneurial venture with **limited resources** that aims at rapid growth and expansion while leveraging technology.

3. **Kaizen** is a Japanese concept meaning **"continuous improvement."** It has been applied in Japan since the mid-1940s and has helped Japan achieve higher standards of living for its people.

4. **Interest** is the amount of money paid by the borrower of a loan to the lender for using their money for a specific period of time.

5. The **supply chain** is a network of individuals, organisations, resources, activities, and technology involved in the production and sale of goods.

6. **Labour** refers to the physical and mental effort used in production — the basic work carried out by carpenters, farmers, teachers, and others. **Human capital**, on the other hand, refers to the specialised **skills, knowledge, abilities, and expertise** required to perform that labour. In other words, human capital is not just the basic effort of labour but represents the **quality and efficiency** of that labour.

7. Any two of the following:
   - **Education and training** — e.g., a civil engineering student learns design and material principles through education, and hands-on training (observing sites, testing materials) prepares them to apply this knowledge practically.
   - **Healthcare** — good health supports cognitive development, helping children attend school regularly and learn better, and enabling workers to perform their best, physically and mentally.
   - **Social and cultural influences** — e.g., Japan's concept of *kaizen* ("continuous improvement") has helped it achieve higher living standards; the German work ethic's emphasis on punctuality and quality has contributed to its global leadership in technology and manufacturing.

8. The **demographic dividend** refers to the benefit a country gets when it has a large number of young and working people. When more people are working and earning, and fewer people depend on them, the country can grow businesses and improve living standards. This is relevant to India today because, as per the **Economic Survey of India 2024**, **65 per cent of India's population is below the age of 35 years**, giving the country a young, productive population that could help it reap this benefit — provided individuals have access to quality education, health, training, and skilling.

9. Production is called **labour-intensive** when it relies more on **labour** as an input — for example, output from the **agriculture, construction, and handicraft sectors**. Production is called **capital-intensive** when it relies more on **capital and specialised machinery** — for example, the production of **semiconductor chips or satellites**. The proportion of each factor used depends on the nature of the product being produced.

10. The process of mobile phone assembly follows these steps, as shown in the flow chart:

    **START**
    → **R&D team conceptualises new features**
    → **Acquire land and set up factory**
    → **Acquire raw materials**
    → **Assemble components & install software**
    → **Test phone** (functionality, quality & performance)
    → **Mass production & packaging**
    → **Distribution to retail stores**
    → **END**

    **Human effort at each stage:** Human effort is involved at every stage of this process to design, supervise, and improve products and processes. Teams of **software, electrical, and mechanical engineers**, along with **project managers**, use their expertise to develop the product's features and ensure it functions correctly. The **entrepreneur** provides guidance on how resources should be used throughout the process. Procuring resources like land, factory space, machinery, and skilled workers requires **financial capital**, showing that all the factors of production — land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship, and technology — work together like puzzle pieces at every stage to create the final product. (As a supporting fact, India is the world's second-largest mobile phone manufacturer after China in 2025, reflecting the scale at which this process operates.)

11. Businesses have responsibilities in two broad areas — towards **natural resources** and towards their **workers**.

    **Towards natural resources:** Producing goods uses natural resources like land, water, and minerals, which are limited and can be harmed if used carelessly. For example, leather factories in Tamil Nadu support the local economy but can pollute rivers and soil through their waste; improperly recycled smartphones can leak harmful substances like lead and mercury into the ground and water. Therefore, producers must adopt **sustainable practices** — reducing waste, avoiding pollution, and protecting the environment — to replenish natural resources for future use, ensuring today's needs don't compromise future generations' ability to meet theirs.

    **Towards workers:** Businesses have three key responsibilities:
    - **Fair compensation and working conditions** — ensuring workers are paid fairly and work in a safe environment.
    - **Skill development and training** — investing in training and education so workers remain competitive in the labour market.
    - **Workplace rights and protections** — adhering to laws and regulations on fair treatment, preventing discrimination, and providing benefits like healthcare or paid leave.

    **Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR):** Businesses are also motivated to address social and environmental concerns through CSR, which includes reducing polluting activities, addressing the well-being of local communities, and treating employees and customers with respect. Notably, **India was the first nation in the world to bring a CSR law, in 2014**, mandating companies to spend **2 per cent of their average profits of the last three years** on CSR activities — showing India's leadership in formalising business responsibility towards society.

### Q1: What are factors of production?
**Factors of production** are the resources or inputs used in producing goods and services. In economics, they are classified into four types: **land, labour, capital, and entrepreneurship**.

### Q2: What does the term "land" mean in economics?
In economics, **land** encompasses not only geographical land but also **natural resources** like soil, forests, water, air, sunlight, minerals, oil, and natural gas.

### Q3: Define "labour" as a factor of production.
**Labour** refers to the **physical and mental effort** used in production. It is essential in production, as seen in the work of carpenters, farmers, construction workers, teachers, and doctors.

### Q4: What is capital, as defined in the chapter?
**Capital**, in economics, refers to any asset — physical or financial — used to produce goods and services. It comprises **monetary resources** and **durable assets** like machinery, tools, equipment, vehicles, computers, shops, and factories.

### Q5: Who is an entrepreneur?
An **entrepreneur** is a person who comes up with an idea, takes risks, gathers other factors of production, and works hard to make their **startup** idea successful.

### Q6: What is meant by "technology" in the context of production?
**Technology** means the **application of scientific knowledge**. Any production-related activity uses some form of technology, and it acts as a facilitator that enables businesses to produce more goods with the same or fewer inputs.

### Q7: Define "human capital." How is it different from labour?
**Human capital** refers to the specialised **skills, knowledge, abilities, and expertise** required to perform labour. While **labour** is simply the physical and mental effort used in production, **human capital** represents the **quality and efficiency** of that labour — it is not just the basic effort but what makes that effort more valuable and productive.

### Q8: What is a "dividend," and what is the "stock market"?
The **stock market** is a special type of market where **shares** are bought and sold. A **dividend** is an amount of money paid regularly by a company to its shareholders out of its profits. Large companies raise money from the public through the stock market by offering them a share of the profits (dividends).

### Q9: What is meant by "interest" in relation to a business loan?
**Interest** is the amount of money paid by the borrower of a loan to the lender for using their money for a specific time. For example, Ratna took a loan from a bank to start her business and paid interest along with part of her loan amount over time.

### Q10: What is a "startup"?
A **startup** is an entrepreneurial venture with **limited resources** that aims at **rapid growth and expansion** while leveraging technology.

### Q11: What is the "demographic dividend"? Why is it relevant to India?
The **demographic dividend** refers to the benefit a country gets when it has a large number of young and working people. When more people are working and earning, and fewer people depend on them, the country can grow businesses and improve living standards. This is relevant to India because, according to the **Economic Survey of India 2024**, **65 per cent of people in India are below the age of 35 years**, giving India a young, productive population that could help it reap this benefit — provided individuals have access to quality education, health, training, and skilling.

### Q12: What is the "supply chain"? How can its disruption affect production?
The **supply chain** is a network of individuals, organisations, resources, activities, and technology involved in the production and sale of goods. When a disruption occurs in the supply chain — due to relying on sources from far-off places instead of local inputs — it can result in a **halt in the production process**, as was the case during the **COVID-19 pandemic**.

### Q13: State the Adult Literacy Rate figures for India as mentioned in the chapter, and explain why literacy matters for human capital.
As per **World Bank estimates for 2023**, the **Adult Literacy Rate** in India is **85 per cent for males and 70 per cent for females**. Literacy is an important characteristic of the population because it helps **enhance the skills and productivity** of human capital — it is a foundation on which further education, training, and skill development can build.

### Q14: List the three facilitators of human capital discussed in the chapter, with one example for each.
The three facilitators of human capital are:
- **Education and training** — e.g., a civil engineering student learns design and material principles, later applied to real infrastructure projects through hands-on training.
- **Healthcare** — good health supports cognitive development, helping children attend school regularly and workers perform better, both physically and mentally.
- **Social and cultural influences** — e.g., the Japanese concept of ***kaizen*** ("continuous improvement"), which has helped Japan achieve higher living standards, and the German work ethic's focus on punctuality and quality.

### Q15: Explain the ancient Indian concepts of "kalā," "vidyā," and "śhilpa śhāstras."
For ancient Indians, work was a form of expressing devotion and striving for perfection, an offering to the deity or receiver. Creating products involved a unique blend of ***kalā*** (art) and ***vidyā*** (knowledge), with knowledge passed on and built upon across generations. The ***śhilpa śhāstras*** are ancient texts containing detailed design guidelines on sculptures, paintings, buildings, wooden items, and jewellery — for example, texts on sculpture prescribe exact specifications regarding postures, colours, measurements, and proportions of figures.

### Q16: List the tasks performed by an entrepreneur, as illustrated in the chapter.
An entrepreneur:
- **Identifies a problem** and is resolute to solve it with an innovative solution
- **Takes risks** by investing money and time
- **Makes key decisions** regarding the operation and functioning of their business
- **Combines various factors of production**
- **Contributes to the welfare of society** with their innovation

### Q17: Describe the process of mobile phone assembly as depicted in the chapter's flow chart.
The process of mobile phone assembly follows these steps:
1. The **R&D team conceptualises new features**.
2. The business **acquires land** and sets up a factory.
3. It **acquires raw materials**.
4. Workers **assemble components** and install software.
5. The phone is **tested** for functionality, quality, and performance.
6. **Mass production and packaging** takes place.
7. Finally, the product undergoes **distribution to retail stores**.

### Q18: Explain, with examples, how businesses have responsibilities towards natural resources.
When producing goods, businesses use natural resources like **land, water, and minerals**, which are limited and can be harmed if not managed carefully. For example, leather factories in **Tamil Nadu** support the local economy, but their waste can pollute rivers and soil; similarly, improperly recycled old smartphones can leak harmful substances like **lead and mercury** into the ground and water, endangering people, animals, and plants. It is therefore important for producers to use natural resources responsibly, reduce waste, avoid pollution, and protect the environment, so that today's needs are met without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs.

### Q19: What responsibilities do businesses have towards their workers?
Businesses have the following responsibilities towards their workers:
- **Fair compensation and working conditions:** Ensuring workers are paid fairly and work in a safe environment.
- **Skill development and training:** Investing in training and education so workers develop skills necessary to remain competitive in the labour market.
- **Workplace rights and protections:** Adhering to laws and regulations relating to fair treatment, preventing discrimination, and providing benefits like healthcare or paid leave.

### Q20: What is Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)? Mention India's contribution in this regard.
**Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)** refers to businesses being motivated to address social and environmental concerns in their operations to benefit society and biodiversity — this includes reducing polluting activities, addressing the well-being of local communities, and treating employees and customers with respect. **India was the first nation in the world** to bring a CSR law, in **2014**, which mandated companies to spend **2 per cent of their average profits of the last three years** on CSR activities.

### Q21: Differentiate between labour-intensive and capital-intensive production, with examples from the chapter.
Production is called **labour-intensive** when it relies more on **labour** — for example, output from the **agriculture, construction, and handicraft sectors**. Production is called **capital-intensive** when it relies more on **capital and specialised machinery** — for example, the production of **semiconductor chips or satellites**. The proportion of each factor used in production depends on the specific product being made.

### Q22: "The factors of production complement each other and are interconnected." Explain this statement with examples.
Factors of production — land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship, and technology — work together like puzzle pieces to produce goods and services, and if any factor is **missing or misused**, production can become **inefficient or halted**. However, new techniques can also change the proportion of factors used: for instance, **increased machine use in agriculture** can lower dependence on labour, and **3-D printing** can help revive dying textile art forms by producing handloom products at scale to serve the market. Additionally, production inputs are available at different geographic locations, so businesses can procure and combine inputs from varied places — showing that geographic interconnectedness gives businesses access to varied resources, though this also creates supply chain vulnerabilities, as seen during the COVID-19 pandemic.

### Q23: Explain how technology acts as an "enabler" of production, and how it has expanded access to opportunities.
**Technology** is the application of scientific knowledge, and it enables businesses to produce more goods with the same or fewer inputs. Modern examples include **UPI** for instant payments, **GPS** for finding the shortest transport routes, **drones** spraying fertilisers to improve crop health, and **robots** assisting in surgical processes. Importantly, technological progress does not always mean replacing old technologies entirely — tools like **pulleys** and **wheelbarrows** remain in use even today. Technology has also expanded access to knowledge, skills, and jobs by eliminating geographical barriers — for example, the Government's **SWAYAM** platform offers free online courses from Grade 9 onwards, and the **National Career Service** portal helps people find job opportunities across sectors, from plumbing to accounting.

### Q24: Using J.R.D. Tata as an example, explain the qualities and contributions of a successful entrepreneur.
**J.R.D. Tata**, born in 1904, became the head of the **Tata Group** and played a major role in building modern India. He believed that businesses should not only make money but also help society — reflecting the entrepreneurial trait of contributing to societal welfare. He started India's first airline, **Tata Airlines**, in **1932** (which later became Air India), and under his leadership, the Tata Group expanded into steel, cars, power, and chemicals — demonstrating the entrepreneurial qualities of identifying opportunities, taking risks, and making key business decisions. He was also known for caring about his workers and providing good working conditions, reflecting a sense of responsibility towards employees. In **1992**, he received the **Bharat Ratna**, India's highest civilian award, for his service to the nation — showing that a successful entrepreneur's motivation and impact can extend well beyond profit alone.

### Q25: "For a country like India, the largest contribution to growth and productivity will probably come about from more efficiently using land, labour and capital" (Bibek Debroy). Explain this statement with reference to the interconnectedness of factors of production and the responsibilities of producers.
This statement highlights that India's economic growth depends heavily on how **efficiently** its core factors of production — **land, labour, and capital** — are utilised, rather than simply having more of them. As the chapter explains, these factors are **interconnected and complementary**: if one factor (such as skilled labour or adequate capital) is missing or misused, production can become inefficient or halt altogether. Efficient use also involves adopting **new techniques** — such as increased machine use in agriculture or 3-D printing in textiles — that can improve output without necessarily using more inputs. At the same time, efficient use must be balanced with **responsibility**: since land and natural resources are limited, producers must use them sustainably, avoiding pollution and reducing waste, so that today's productivity gains do not come at the cost of future generations' ability to meet their own needs. This combination of efficient resource use and responsible production is central to sustaining India's long-term growth and productivity.

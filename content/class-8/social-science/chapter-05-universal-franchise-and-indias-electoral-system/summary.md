# Universal Franchise and India's Electoral System

Think about this: every five years, nearly a billion people in India line up to do one simple thing — press a button. No test, no fee, no permission needed. Just one citizen, one vote, one equal voice. That's the idea this whole chapter is built around, so let's unpack it properly.

## Universal Adult Franchise

### What exactly is "franchise"?

You'll see this word a lot, so let's nail it down first. **Franchise** is a right granted by the constitution or government — here, it means the right to vote. Another word for it is **suffrage**.

### Why "universal" and why "adult"?

Here's the concept examiners love to test: **universal adult franchise** means every adult citizen gets **one vote**, and every vote carries **equal value**.

In India, this means:
- Every citizen **aged 18 and above** has the right to vote.
- It doesn't matter what your **caste, creed, race, religion, gender, education, or income** is.
- No one can vote on someone else's behalf.
- To actually vote, you must be registered in your **constituency** — an area whose voters elect a representative to a legislative body.
- A person can be barred from voting if found guilty of certain serious crimes.

This right is backed by **Article 326** of the Constitution, and it applies to elections for the Lok Sabha, every state legislative assembly, and all local elections in villages and cities.

> **Exam trap:** Don't say India "introduced" universal franchise later — the Constitution makers built it in **from the very start**, in 1950, even though literacy was only about 14% at the time (just 8% among women). Many countries gave this right gradually, but India didn't.

### Why does universal franchise matter?

A few reasons the textbook highlights:
- **Everyone is equal** — no discrimination based on caste, creed, sex, education, or wealth.
- **Everyone's voice matters** — all citizens can help decide the nation's future.
- **Power to determine one's future** — people choose who represents them.
- **Elected representatives are answerable to the electorate** — if voters are unhappy, they can vote leaders out.
- It **fosters civic engagement** — people participate in solving problems in their own area.

### A quick fact check on history

- Before Independence, only about **13%** of Indians could vote.
- India was actually **ahead of many countries** in giving women the right to vote — for comparison, Swiss women only got the vote in **1971**.

## Ensuring Franchise Reaches Everyone

It's one thing to give everyone the right to vote — it's another to make sure everyone can *actually* exercise it. Given how huge and diverse India is (its eligible-voter population is **fifteen times** that of the UK, and its area is **six times** that of France), this needs real effort.

The **Election Commission of India (ECI)** has introduced several accessibility measures:
- Electoral officials travelling to remote locations so no one is left out.
- **Home voting** for the elderly and persons with disabilities (introduced pan-India for the first time in 2024).
- **Postal voting** for specific categories of voters.
- **Braille-enabled voter cards** and app-based requests for assistance like wheelchairs and ramps.

> **Don't miss:** Even with all this, about **34%** of eligible voters did *not* vote in the 2024 elections — a good reminder that having a right and exercising it are two different things.

### A historical echo

Long before modern ballots, the **Uttaramerur inscriptions** (10th century) describe a transparent method of choosing representatives: candidates' names on palm-leaf fragments were sealed in a pot, and a young boy publicly drew names on the selection day — showing that the idea of fair, transparent selection is ancient in India.

## A Case Study: Electing a Class Representative

To understand how a "real" election works, the chapter uses the example of a Class Rep election at Suryodaya School — and it's genuinely useful for understanding *every* term below.

**The setup:** Three candidates — Ahmed (promised cleaner classrooms), Gurmat (proposed peer coaching), and Ravi (wanted more space for arts). Ms. Usha, the class teacher, was appointed the **Election Officer** to keep things fair.

**Campaigning:** Each candidate used a different method — posters, talking to classmates, even a music performance — while Ms. Usha explained the rules, including how the **secret ballot** would work (no one would know who voted for whom).

**Voting day:** Each student got a ballot paper, marked an 'X' for their choice, and a **braille ballot paper** was arranged for Neha. Ballots were collected and sealed until counting.

**The result — and a key concept:**
Out of 33 votes cast, one was unmarked (declared invalid). The count was:

| Candidate | Votes received |
|---|---|
| Ahmed | 8 |
| Gurmat | 12 |
| Ravi | 10 |

Gurmat won and became Class Rep — **despite getting only 12 out of 33 votes**, which is *not* even 50%. This is the real-world example of **First-Past-the-Post (FPTP)**: the candidate with the *most* votes wins, even without a majority.

> **Exam trap:** Students often assume winning an election means getting more than 50% of votes. Under FPTP (which India uses for Lok Sabha and State Assembly elections), that is **not** required — just the highest number of votes among all candidates.

**What if no one likes any candidate?** That's where **NOTA (None Of The Above)** comes in — it lets a voter formally reject all candidates. It doesn't change who wins, but it sends a signal that voters want better choices.

## The Election Commission of India (ECI)

### What is it?

The **ECI** is an **independent constitutional body** responsible for conducting free and fair elections. It was **established in 1950**, and India's first general elections were held in **1951–52**.

It conducts elections to the **Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies**, and the offices of the **President and Vice President**. (Elections to gram panchayats and urban local bodies are managed by **State Election Commissions** instead.)

### How is it structured?

| Level | Key Officer(s) | Role |
|---|---|---|
| National | Chief Election Commissioner + Two Election Commissioners | Overall national supervision |
| State/UT | Chief Electoral Officer | State-level coordination |
| District | District Election Officer → Returning Officer (RO) & Electoral Registration Officer (ERO) | RO conducts elections; ERO manages the voter list |

### What does the ECI actually do?

- **Setting election dates**
- **Registering political parties**
- **Enforcing the Model Code of Conduct**
- **Overseeing the entire electoral process**

## Managing India's Massive Elections

To see the scale involved, look at the 2024 Lok Sabha elections:

- About **980 million** eligible voters
- **543** parliamentary constituencies
- Over **1 million** polling stations

> **Don't miss:** Of the 543 Lok Sabha constituencies, **84 are reserved for Scheduled Castes** and **47 for Scheduled Tribes** (412 are general).

### How does actual voting happen?

1. **1st polling officer:** Checks your name on the voter list and your ID proof.
2. **2nd polling officer:** Inks your finger, gives you a chit, and takes your signature.
3. **3rd polling officer:** Checks your inked finger and takes the chit (NOTA is available here too).
4. **You:** Press the button on the **EVM (Electronic Voting Machine)** — you'll hear a beep — and check the printed slip in the **VVPAT** (Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail), which gives a paper record as backup for verification or recounting.

India's EVM and VVPAT systems have even been used (with ECI support) in countries like Namibia and Bhutan.

## The Model Code of Conduct (MCC)

The **MCC** lists the dos and don'ts for leaders, parties, and candidates to ensure elections stay free and fair. A few key elements:

- The **ruling party** cannot use government resources for election purposes (e.g., no new schemes announced to influence voters).
- Candidates must show **discretion and restraint** so elections stay peaceful.
- **Influencing voters through gifts** in exchange for votes is punishable.

> **Exam trap:** The MCC isn't a law passed by Parliament in one go — it was first **voluntarily adopted in Kerala in 1960**, circulated nationally by the ECI during the **1962** elections, and the ECI became genuinely proactive about enforcing it only from **1991**.

**A name worth remembering:** **T.N. Seshan**, Chief Election Commissioner from 1990, is remembered for reforms like strict campaign rules, voter IDs (to stop proxy voting), and strict watch on candidate spending — earning him credit for making Indian elections fairer and more transparent.

## Types of Elections in India

### Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies

These use **direct elections** with the **First-Past-the-Post** system.
- Winners to the Lok Sabha are called **Members of Parliament (MPs)**.
- Winners to state assemblies are called **Members of Legislative Assembly (MLAs)**.
- The party/coalition with a **majority of seats** in the Lok Sabha forms the national government; its leader usually becomes the **Prime Minister**. The same logic applies at the state level for the **Chief Minister**.
- A **coalition** is a group of two or more parties working together.

### Rajya Sabha

This is where students often get confused, so let's be precise:

| Feature | Lok Sabha | Rajya Sabha |
|---|---|---|
| Election type | Direct election by voters | **Indirect election** |
| Who elects | Citizens in constituencies | 233 seats elected by MLAs; 12 nominated by the President |
| Total strength (per chapter) | 543 constituencies | 245 members |
| Term | Full house re-elected together | 6-year term; **one-third retire every 2 years** |
| Nickname | — | "**Permanent House**" — never dissolved |
| Voting method | FPTP | **Single transferable vote system** |

> **Exam trap:** "Indirect election" doesn't mean unelected — it means elected *by other elected representatives* rather than directly by citizens. (Think of it like a school council chosen by class reps, rather than by the whole school.)

Rajya Sabha seats per state depend on population — a bigger state like Uttar Pradesh gets more seats than a smaller one like Arunachal Pradesh.

### Election of the President of India

The President is **not directly elected**. Instead, an **Electoral College** elects them, made up of:
- **MPs** from both Houses of Parliament (Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha)
- **MLAs** from every state and from the Union Territories of Delhi and Puducherry

This uses the **single transferable vote system**, and more populous states get a greater say.

**Not included** in this Electoral College:
- Nominated members of the Rajya Sabha (12)
- Nominated members of State Legislative Assemblies
- Members of Legislative Councils (elected or nominated) in **bicameral legislatures** — a system where the legislature has two separate chambers
- Nominated members of Delhi and Puducherry

> Why exclude nominated members? Because the President is meant to represent the *will of the people* — so only those who themselves were *directly elected* by the people get to vote in this election, keeping the process democratically legitimate.

### Election of the Vice President of India

Elected by an Electoral College made up of **elected and nominated members of both Houses of Parliament**, again using the **single transferable vote**. The Vice President also serves as the **chairperson of the Rajya Sabha**, and steps in for the President when needed.

## Challenges and the Road Ahead

Even though India's electoral system is celebrated as the world's largest democratic exercise, it faces real challenges:
- The **growing influence of money** in elections
- A significant number of **candidates with criminal records**
- **Voter apathy**, especially in urban areas

The way forward, according to the chapter, lies in **empowering voters with information** — media, education, and awareness campaigns helping people (especially youth) vote thoughtfully. As the chapter puts it: democracy starts with asking the right questions and ends with casting a responsible vote.

## Quick Recap

- **Franchise** = the right to vote; **universal adult franchise** = every citizen 18+ gets one equal vote, regardless of caste, creed, gender, education, or income (Article 326).
- India adopted universal franchise **from Independence itself**, despite low literacy — unlike many countries that restricted voting rights initially.
- The **ECI** (est. 1950) is an independent constitutional body that conducts elections to the Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, State Assemblies, and the offices of President and Vice President; structured at national (CEC + 2 ECs), state (CEO), and district (DEO → RO & ERO) levels.
- Key ECI functions: setting election dates, registering parties, enforcing the MCC, overseeing the whole process.
- The class-rep case study illustrates **secret ballot**, **campaigning**, and **First-Past-the-Post** (winning without a majority) and introduces **NOTA**.
- Voting steps: ID check → inking & chit → chit & finger check (NOTA available) → EVM vote + VVPAT slip check.
- 2024 Lok Sabha elections: ~980 million voters, 543 constituencies (412 general, 84 SC, 47 ST), 1 million+ polling stations.
- **Model Code of Conduct**: voluntary dos/don'ts for fair elections; adopted in Kerala (1960), circulated nationally (1962), enforced proactively from 1991; T.N. Seshan (CEC from 1990) strengthened it.
- **Lok Sabha/State Assembly** elections are **direct** (FPTP); **Rajya Sabha** elections are **indirect** (by MLAs, single transferable vote), and it is the "Permanent House."
- **President** and **Vice President** are elected by Electoral Colleges of *elected* MPs/MLAs (President) or all MPs (Vice President), using the single transferable vote; nominated members are excluded from the President's Electoral College.
- Challenges facing Indian democracy: money power, candidates with criminal records, and voter apathy — addressed through voter awareness and education.

### Q1: What is meant by "franchise"?
**Franchise** is a right granted by the constitution or government; in the context of elections, it refers to the **right to vote**. It is also known as **suffrage**.

### Q2: What is universal adult franchise?
**Universal adult franchise** means that every adult citizen of a country gets one vote, and all votes have equal value. In India, every citizen **aged 18 and above** has the right to vote, irrespective of caste, creed, race, religion, gender, education, or income.

### Q3: What is a constituency?
A **constituency** is an area whose voters elect a representative to a legislative body.

### Q4: What is the Model Code of Conduct (MCC)?
The **Model Code of Conduct** is a set of dos and don'ts followed by leaders, political parties, and candidates to ensure that elections are conducted **freely and fairly**.

### Q5: What is the Election Commission of India (ECI)?
The **Election Commission of India** is an **independent constitutional body** responsible for conducting free and fair elections. It was **established in 1950** and conducts elections to the Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies, and the offices of the President and Vice President.

### Q6: What is NOTA?
**NOTA (None Of The Above)** is an option available to voters who do not wish to vote for any of the contesting candidates. It allows voters to formally reject all candidates. NOTA does not change the election result, but it sends a message that voters want better choices.

### Q7: What is meant by a secret ballot?
A **secret ballot** is a voting method in which no one else can know who a particular voter voted for, ensuring that the voting process remains **private and fair**.

### Q8: What is a coalition?
A **coalition** is a group of two or more political parties working together, typically to form a government when no single party wins a majority.

### Q9: Give an example each of a direct election and an indirect election, as seen in the chapter.
- **Direct election:** Elections to the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies, where citizens directly vote for their representatives (as illustrated by the Class Representative election, where students directly voted for Ahmed, Gurmat, or Ravi).
- **Indirect election:** Election to the **Rajya Sabha**, where members are elected by the elected members (MLAs) of state legislative assemblies rather than directly by citizens.

### Q10: What is meant by a bicameral legislature?
A **bicameral legislature** is a system where the legislative body is divided into **two separate chambers or houses**.

### Q11: What is the First-Past-the-Post (FPTP) electoral system, and how does the Class Representative election illustrate it?
Under **First-Past-the-Post**, the candidate who receives the **most votes** in a constituency wins the election — even without securing 50% or more of the total votes. In the Class Representative election, out of 33 votes cast, Ahmed got 8, Gurmat got 12, and Ravi got 10. **Gurmat won and was declared Class Representative despite getting only 12 out of 33 votes**, which is less than half — showing exactly how FPTP works in practice.

### Q12: Describe the structure of the Election Commission of India.
The ECI's structure operates at three levels:
- **National level:** Chief Election Commissioner and two Election Commissioners.
- **State/UT level:** Chief Electoral Officer.
- **District level:** District Election Officer, who oversees the **Returning Officer (RO)**, who conducts elections, and the **Electoral Registration Officer (ERO)**, who manages the voter list.

### Q13: List the main functions performed by the Election Commission of India.
The ECI performs several crucial tasks, including:
- **Setting election dates**
- **Registering political parties**
- **Enforcing the Model Code of Conduct**
- **Overseeing the entire electoral process**

### Q14: How is the election of members to the Lok Sabha different from the election of members to the Rajya Sabha?
- **Lok Sabha:** Members are elected through **direct election** by voters in 543 constituencies, using the **First-Past-the-Post** system.
- **Rajya Sabha:** Members are elected through **indirect election** — 233 members are elected by the elected members (MLAs) of state legislative assemblies, while the President nominates 12 members. Voting uses the **single transferable vote system**, and the Rajya Sabha is a "Permanent House" where one-third of members retire every two years.

### Q15: Explain the process of voting at a polling station for Lok Sabha and State Assembly elections.
Voting proceeds through a sequence of checks:
1. The **1st polling officer** checks the voter's name on the voter list and verifies ID proof.
2. The **2nd polling officer** inks the voter's finger, gives a chit, and takes their signature.
3. The **3rd polling officer** takes the chit and checks the inked finger (NOTA is also available at this stage).
4. The voter **presses the button on the EVM** to cast their vote (a beep confirms it) and can check the printed slip generated in the **VVPAT** machine.

### Q16: What is an Electoral College, and how is the President of India elected?
An **Electoral College** is a group of elected representatives formed specifically to carry out an election on behalf of the wider electorate. The President of India is elected by an Electoral College consisting of:
- **Members of Parliament (MPs)** from both the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha
- **Members of the Legislative Assemblies (MLAs)** from each state and from the Union Territories of Delhi and Puducherry

Voting uses the **single transferable vote system**, and more populous states have a greater say in the outcome.

### Q17: Which groups of people are NOT involved in electing the President of India, and why?
The following are **not involved**:
- Nominated members of the Rajya Sabha (12)
- Nominated members of State Legislative Assemblies
- Members of Legislative Councils (elected and nominated) in bicameral legislatures
- Nominated members of the union territories of Delhi and Puducherry

This is because only **directly elected representatives** are allowed to vote for the President, which maintains **democratic legitimacy** — the President is meant to represent the will of the people, even if indirectly.

### Q18: Why are certain seats in the Lok Sabha reserved for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes?
Of the 543 Lok Sabha constituencies, **84 are reserved for Scheduled Castes** and **47 for Scheduled Tribes**, with 412 being general seats. Such reservation ensures that these communities get **guaranteed representation** in Parliament, in keeping with the principle that **everyone's voice matters** in India's democracy and that no group is excluded from decision-making that affects the nation.

### Q19: What are the key elements of the Model Code of Conduct? Explain with examples from the chapter.
Some elements of the MCC include:
- The **party in government** must not use government resources for election purposes — for example, **no new schemes or projects** may be announced that could influence voters.
- **Candidates** are expected to show discretion and restraint to keep elections peaceful.
- **Influencing voters through gifts** in exchange for votes is punishable — the chapter illustrates this with examples like a candidate distributing sarees and household appliances to women voters, or bundles of ₹500 notes found in a candidate's car during inspection.

### Q20: What role did T.N. Seshan play in strengthening India's electoral process?
**T.N. Seshan**, who became **Chief Election Commissioner in 1990**, introduced reforms such as requiring campaigning to follow precise rules, introducing **voter IDs** to eliminate proxy voting, and enforcing strict vigilance on candidate spending. He is remembered as the officer who made Indian elections **fairer, more transparent, and fearless**.

### Q21: Why do you think the Constitution makers decided to adopt universal franchise from the time of Independence itself, despite low literacy levels?
Even though India's literacy rate in 1947 was only about 14% (8% among women), the Constitution makers chose universal adult franchise rather than restricting voting to literate citizens. This reflects a **progressive constitutional vision** and a deep democratic belief that:
- Every citizen, regardless of education, wealth, or background, is **equally capable** of participating in choosing their representatives.
- **Everyone's voice matters** in shaping the nation's future, not just an educated minority.
- Excluding people based on literacy would have gone against the very idea that **everyone is equal** in a democracy.

### Q22: Why is a secret ballot important in an election? Explain using the Class Representative election as an example.
A **secret ballot** ensures that no one else knows who a particular voter chose, so that:
- Voters can vote according to their **own honest judgement**, without fear of pressure, embarrassment, or retaliation.
- The election remains **private and fair**, as Ms. Usha ensured in the Class Representative election, where no student would know who voted for whom.

Without secrecy, voters might feel pressured to vote a certain way to please others, undermining the fairness of the entire process.

### Q23: What is the single transferable vote system, and where is it used in India's electoral process?
The **single transferable vote system** is a special voting method used to ensure fair representation, particularly for smaller states or constituencies. According to the chapter, it is used in:
- Elections to the **Rajya Sabha** (by MLAs)
- Election of the **President of India** (by the Electoral College of MPs and MLAs)
- Election of the **Vice President of India** (by MPs of both Houses)

The exact mechanism of how the system works is left for study in higher classes, but its purpose is to ensure that even smaller states or groups get fair representation, rather than results being decided purely by simple majority.

### Q24: "India's electoral system is celebrated as the world's largest democratic exercise, but it faces real challenges." Discuss.
India's elections are remarkable in scale — with about 980 million voters, 543 Lok Sabha constituencies, and over 1 million polling stations in 2024 alone. However, the chapter highlights several ongoing challenges:
- The **growing influence of money** in elections.
- A **significant proportion of candidates with criminal records** contesting elections.
- **Voter apathy**, particularly in urban areas — in the 2024 elections, about 34% of eligible voters did not vote.

The chapter suggests that the way forward lies in **empowering voters with information** through media, education, and awareness campaigns, so that people — especially the youth — understand the importance of **voting wisely and responsibly**.

### Q25: Explain how the ECI's accessibility measures help ensure universal franchise is meaningfully exercised, not just legally guaranteed.
Simply granting the right to vote is not enough if citizens face practical barriers to exercising it. The ECI has introduced measures such as:
- Electoral officials **travelling to remote locations** to reach every voter.
- **Home voting** for the elderly and persons with disabilities (extended pan-India for the first time in 2024).
- **Postal voting** for specific categories of voters.
- **Braille-enabled voter cards** and app-based requests for assistance, including wheelchairs and ramps.

These measures show that ensuring **universal franchise** is not just about the legal right to vote, but about actively **removing barriers** — geographic, physical, or otherwise — so that "no voter is left behind," turning the constitutional guarantee into a lived reality for all citizens.

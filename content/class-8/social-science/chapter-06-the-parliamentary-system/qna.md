### Q1: What is a bicameral legislature? Give an example from the chapter.
A **bicameral** legislature is one that has **two houses** ('bi' means two, 'cameral' means chamber/house). The **Indian Parliament** is an example, consisting of the **Lok Sabha** and the **Rajya Sabha**.

### Q2: What is a bill?
A **bill** is a draft of a proposed law that needs to be approved by the Parliament before it can be enacted into law.

### Q3: What is a Money Bill, and where must it be introduced?
A **Money Bill** is a bill concerned with financial matters, including taxation and government borrowing. It can be introduced **only in the Lok Sabha**, and its introduction requires the **prior recommendation of the President**.

### Q4: Who presides over the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha respectively?
The **Lok Sabha** is presided over by the **Speaker**, who is elected by its own members. The **Rajya Sabha** is presided over by the **Vice President of India**, who acts as its **chairperson**.

### Q5: What is a Standing Committee?
A **Standing Committee** is a **permanent committee** consisting of MPs (the members may change, but the committee structure remains) that scrutinises government activities, makes recommendations, and asks questions about the actions taken by the government.

### Q6: Who constitutes the Union Executive?
The **Union Executive** consists of the **President**, the **Vice President**, and the **Council of Ministers** headed by the **Prime Minister**.

### Q7: What is the Question Hour?
The **Question Hour** is a select period in the Lok Sabha (typically the first hour of a Parliamentary session) during which **MPs can ask ministers questions** about government policies and activities. It is a key mechanism through which Parliament holds the Executive **accountable**.

### Q8: What is meant by "federalism"?
**Federalism** is a form of government in which power is distributed between the **centre, states, and local governments**. This system helps balance national unity with local interests.

### Q9: Differentiate between the Union List, State List, and Concurrent List.
- The **Union List** contains subjects on which only the **Union government** can legislate.
- The **State List** contains subjects on which only **State governments** can legislate.
- The **Concurrent List** contains subjects on which **both** the Union and State governments can legislate; however, if the Union government legislates on a Concurrent List subject, the state government is bound to follow it.

### Q10: What are the four broad heads under which the legislative functions of Parliament are classified?
The Constitution classifies Parliament's key functions under: **(1) Constitutional functions, (2) Lawmaking, (3) Executive accountability, and (4) Financial accountability**.

### Q11: What is the difference between a unicameral and a bicameral state legislature? Name the states with a bicameral system.
A **unicameral** legislature has only **one house**, while a **bicameral** legislature has **two houses**. In states, the lower house is called the **Vidhan Sabha**, and the upper house (where it exists) is called the **Vidhan Parishad**. The states with a **bicameral** system are **Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh**; the remaining states have a unicameral system.

### Q12: Name the three sessions in which Parliament typically meets each year.
Parliament typically meets three times a year in the **Budget Session**, the **Monsoon Session**, and the **Winter Session**.

### Q13: What is meant by "productivity" in the Parliamentary context?
**Productivity**, in the Parliamentary context, is measured by the **number of hours** either the Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha actually functioned against the time it was **scheduled** to function.

### Q14: How is the President of India related to the Executive, and what discretionary power can the President exercise?
The **President** is the **Head of State** and the **nominal head of the Executive**. The President appoints the Prime Minister and other ministers, summons the Parliament, and gives assent to bills, while the Council of Ministers aids and advises the President. However, in specific circumstances — such as a political crisis where **no party holds a clear majority** in a Lok Sabha election — the President can exercise **discretionary powers**.

### Q15: Explain the process by which a bill becomes an act, using the example of the RTE Act, 2009.
The **RTE Act** followed this journey:
- Its roots lay in the **Directive Principles of State Policy**, though the Constitution makers wanted it implemented within a decade of Independence — this did not happen.
- In the early 1990s, it was argued in court that free education was inherently part of the **Fundamental Right to Life**.
- The **86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002** introduced **Article 21A**, mandating free and compulsory education for children aged 6 to 14.
- The bill was **tabled in the Rajya Sabha**, studied by a **committee**, and modified.
- The major debate concerned **funding** for schools, infrastructure, and teachers.
- After the 2009 elections, it was passed in the **Lok Sabha** and received the **President's assent** in August 2009, becoming an Act.

In general, a bill's journey includes: introduction in either House → reading → referral to a Standing Committee → consideration and passing → clause-by-clause discussion and voting on amendments → vote on the bill → repetition of the process in the other House → Presidential assent → Gazette notification.

### Q16: Who is the *de facto* executive authority in India's Parliamentary system, and how are they selected?
The **Prime Minister** is the ***de facto* executive authority** in India's Parliamentary system (as opposed to the President, who is the nominal head). The **President appoints the leader of the majority party or coalition** in the Lok Sabha as the Prime Minister, who then serves with the support of the majority of MPs in the Lok Sabha.

### Q17: List the key functions of the Prime Minister.
The Prime Minister's key functions include: **leading the Council of Ministers, advising the President, coordinating the work of different ministries**, and **shaping national policies**.

### Q18: What is meant by "executive accountability," and through what mechanisms is it exercised?
**Executive accountability** means that the Prime Minister and Council of Ministers, who implement and enforce laws and run the government, are **answerable to the Lok Sabha** for their actions and decisions. This is exercised through mechanisms like the **Question Hour**, where MPs question ministers about policies and activities, and through **special committees**, which may include MPs from different political parties, where ministries must explain and answer questions about their policies.

### Q19: Differentiate between the Legislature and the Executive with respect to their composition and main role.
The **Legislature** (Parliament) is composed of the President, the Lok Sabha, and the Rajya Sabha, and its main role is **making laws and overseeing the work of the Executive**. The **Executive** is composed of the President, the Vice-President, and the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister, and its main role is **enforcing the laws made by the Legislature**.

### Q20: How does the Constitution ensure separation of powers, and what is the role of the Judiciary in this regard?
The Constitution assigns distinct powers and responsibilities to the **Legislature** (making laws), the **Executive** (implementing them), and the **Judiciary** (interpreting and applying the laws). The **Judiciary** acts as the **custodian of the Constitution**, checking that laws passed by Parliament and their implementation by the Executive do not violate the constitutional framework. This system, where the Legislature functions through its law-making role, the Executive through implementation, and the Judiciary through the courts to check both, forms the basis of the **separation of powers** and the **system of checks and balances**, ensuring no single organ becomes too powerful.

### Q21: Explain how the structure of the State Government parallels that of the Union Government.
The state-level structure mirrors the Union structure: the **Governor** (appointed by the President) is the constitutional/nominal head of the state, similar to the President at the Union level, while the ***de facto* executive authority** is the **Chief Minister**, similar to the Prime Minister. Both the President and Governor have a 5-year term. The Council of Ministers at the state level is selected by the Chief Minister and is **collectively responsible to the Vidhan Sabha**, just as the Union Council of Ministers is responsible to the Lok Sabha. Legislative power is also divided: the Union legislates on the **Union List and Concurrent List**, while states legislate on the **State List and Concurrent List**. Additionally, Money Bills originate only in the Lok Sabha at the Union level and only in the Vidhan Sabha at the state level.

### Q22: "If the Executive is part of the Legislature, how can we say there is separation of power?" Explain.
Although members of the Executive (the Prime Minister and Council of Ministers) are drawn from among the **MPs of Parliament**, separation of power is maintained through the **distinction of function**, not membership. The **Legislature's** core role is to make laws and hold the Executive accountable (through mechanisms like the Question Hour and committees), while the **Executive's** core role is to implement and enforce those laws and run the government's day-to-day functioning. The Executive is required to answer to and seek approval from the Legislature (e.g., through the annual budget and Question Hour), showing that despite overlapping membership, the two organs perform distinct, checked functions.

### Q23: Why do you think a substantial proportion of India's electoral college / Parliament being asked to work through Standing Committees is important for effective governance? Explain with reference to the chapter's discussion of accountability.
Standing Committees are important because they provide **detailed, ongoing scrutiny** of government activities that a full House, given limited sitting time, cannot always provide. As a **permanent structure** of MPs (even though individual members may change), they:
- Study bills thoroughly and **suggest modifications** before they are passed (as happened with the RTE bill).
- **Scrutinise government activities**, make recommendations, and ask questions about actions taken by ministries — as illustrated by the Standing Committee on Health and Family Welfare's review of the Ayush Ministry's actions.

This ensures **continuous accountability** of the Executive to the Legislature, beyond what occasional Question Hour sessions or full House debates alone could achieve, and helps uphold the constitutional principle of executive accountability even when Parliament itself is not in session.

### Q24: What are some of the key challenges affecting the effective functioning of India's legislatures, and how might these be addressed?
Some key challenges include: **regular absenteeism** of members, **non-cooperative conduct or communication**, **reduced quality of debates**, and **disruptions of the Question Hour**. Data shows that the number of Parliamentary sittings has declined over time (for example, from 677 sittings in the 1st Lok Sabha to 356 sittings in the 13th Lok Sabha). Concerns have also been raised about a substantial proportion of Lok Sabha representatives having **criminal cases** against them, and sessions being marked by **angry or biased debates** that fail to seriously address issues affecting people.

The chapter suggests that these challenges can be addressed by ensuring **more citizens stay informed** about matters of importance, **participate in public discussions** (including digital spaces offered by government departments during policymaking), and **constructively engage** with their political representatives on policy matters.

### Q25: "Governments will come and go, parties will rise and fall. But the nation must endure, and its democracy must live on" (Atal Bihari Vajpayee). Explain the significance of this statement in the context of India's Parliamentary system.
This statement highlights a core idea running through the chapter: that while **political power changes hands** — governments are formed and dissolved, and parties gain or lose support — the underlying **democratic institutions and values** of the nation must remain stable and enduring. India's Parliamentary system is designed with this in mind:
- **Checks and balances** among the Legislature, Executive, and Judiciary ensure that no single organ or party can become too powerful, regardless of which party is in office.
- The **Constitution's core values** — universal adult suffrage, separation of powers, federalism, and Fundamental Rights — remain the same irrespective of which government is in power.
- Citizens' continued participation, awareness, and engagement, as emphasised at the end of the chapter, are what ultimately **strengthen and sustain democracy** beyond any one government's term.

This reflects the idea that democratic institutions are built to outlast individual leaders and political parties, protecting citizens' rights and the nation's stability over time.

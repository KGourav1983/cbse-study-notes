# The Parliamentary System: Legislature and Executive

Have you ever wondered who actually "runs" the country day-to-day, and who makes sure they don't misuse that power? That's exactly what this chapter is about — how India's Parliament is built, what it does, and how power is deliberately split up so no single person or group gets to hold all of it.

## Composition of the Parliament of India

### Why does India have two houses instead of one?

Here's a question worth sitting with: if the Lok Sabha is directly elected by the people, why does India also need a Rajya Sabha? The Constitution makers actually debated this at length. They felt a single directly elected House would be inadequate to meet the challenges of independent India, and in the spirit of **federalism**, a "Council of States" was needed.

**Federalism** is a form of government where power is distributed between the centre, states, and local governments — helping balance national unity with local interests.

### The structure itself

The Indian **Parliament** is composed of:
- The **President**
- The **Lok Sabha** (House of the People / Lower House)
- The **Rajya Sabha** (Council of States / Upper House)

This two-house structure is called a **bicameral** system ('bi' = two, 'cameral' = chamber/house).

| Feature | Lok Sabha | Rajya Sabha |
|---|---|---|
| Election type | **Direct election** (universal adult suffrage) | **Indirect election** by the electoral college |
| Maximum members (Constitutional) | 550 | Based on state population |
| Basis of seat allocation | Population of each state | Population of each state |

> **Exam trap:** Don't assume both Houses are directly elected. Only the Lok Sabha is directly chosen by citizens — Rajya Sabha members reach Parliament indirectly, through the electoral college you studied in the previous chapter.

### Where did India's Parliamentary ideas come from?

It's tempting to think India simply copied Britain's system — but it's more layered than that. Yes, **Britain's system inspired India's Parliamentary democracy**, but Indian freedom fighters and Constitution makers also drew on:
- Practical experience from limited participation in British colonial governance structures.
- Memory of ancient republics (**mahājanapadas**).
- Village panchayats, where elders made collective decisions.

## Function: Presiding Officers

Every House needs someone to keep order. Think of it like a referee for debates:

- The **Lok Sabha** elects a **Speaker** from among its own members. The Speaker conducts sessions, allows members to speak, maintains discipline, and ensures rules are followed.
- The **Rajya Sabha** is presided over by the **Vice President of India**, who acts as its **chairperson**.

> **Don't miss:** The Sengol — a gold-plated silver sceptre dating back to the Chola period — was symbolically handed to Pt. Jawaharlal Nehru on 14 August 1947 to mark the transfer of power, and now sits near the Speaker's chair in the new Lok Sabha as a symbol of righteous rule.

Parliamentary discussions are also made accessible through translation services — simultaneous interpretation was earlier available in 12 languages, with six more (Bodo, Dogri, Maithili, Manipuri, Urdu, Sanskrit) recently added.

## The Union Executive

Here's a term students often use loosely: "executive." In this chapter it has a precise meaning — the part of government responsible for **implementing and enforcing laws**. This is just as important as the Parliament's law-*making* role.

The **Union Executive** consists of:
- The **President**
- The **Vice President**
- The **Council of Ministers**, headed by the **Prime Minister**

The Council of Ministers is chosen from among the MPs of both Houses, and is **collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha**.

## Legislative Functions of the Parliament

The Constitution classifies Parliament's key functions under four heads. Let's take them one at a time.

### 1) Constitutional function

Parliament is directly entrusted with upholding the Constitution's core values:
- Enabling parliamentary democracy through **universal adult suffrage**
- Following the **separation of powers** between legislature, executive, and judiciary
- Ensuring **federalism**
- Upholding **Fundamental Rights** and **Directive Principles of State Policy** through laws and policies

Two other well-known constitutional functions: **electing the President and Vice President**, and passing **Constitutional amendments**.

### 2) Lawmaking — how does a bill actually become a law?

This is the process students most often need to explain step-by-step, so let's use the chapter's own worked example: the **Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (RTE)**.

**Key term first:** A **bill** is a draft of a proposed law that needs Parliament's approval before it can be enacted into law. Once approved, it's brought into force through an instrument called an **act**.

**The RTE story, step by step:**
1. Its roots lay in the **Directive Principles of State Policy**, though the Constitution makers wanted it implemented within a decade of Independence — this didn't happen.
2. In the early 1990s, someone argued in court that it was inherently part of the **Fundamental Rights** (specifically the Right to Life).
3. The **86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002** introduced **Article 21A**, mandating free and compulsory education for children aged 6–14.
4. Six years later, it was tabled as a **bill in the Rajya Sabha**; a committee studied it and suggested modifications.
5. The major debate was about **funding** — schools, infrastructure, and teachers would be costly.
6. After the 2009 elections, the new government pursued it, and in **August 2009** it was passed in the **Lok Sabha** and received the **President's assent**, becoming an Act.

**The general process (simplified):**

Introduction in either House → Reading of the bill → Referred to Standing Committee → Taken up for consideration and passing → Clause-by-clause discussion and amendments voted → Vote on the bill → Process repeated in the other House → Presidential assent → Gazette notification

*(Note: not all bills are referred to committees.)*

Some useful terms along the way:
- **Reading:** Historically, a clerk read out the bill's contents because many MPs were illiterate; today MPs read the bill themselves before discussion.
- **Clause:** A specific part of a bill explaining its details (e.g., RTE's clause defining the 6–14 age group).
- **Gazette:** An official government publication announcing legal documents and official information.
- **Standing Committee:** A **permanent committee** of MPs (members may change, but the committee structure remains) that scrutinises government activities, makes recommendations, and asks questions about government actions.

> **Exam trap:** A **Money Bill** (concerned with taxation, government borrowing, etc.) can be introduced **only in the Lok Sabha**, and its introduction requires the **prior recommendation of the President**. Students sometimes forget the "prior recommendation" part.

### 3) Executive accountability

The Prime Minister and Council of Ministers are:
- Primarily responsible for **implementing and enforcing laws** and overseeing government functioning.
- Responsible for **day-to-day decisions** based on the Constitution and laws.
- **Answerable to the Lok Sabha**, especially through the **Question Hour** — a select period (typically the first hour of a session) where MPs question ministers about policies and activities. This is how Parliament holds the Executive "accountable."

Special committees, which can include MPs from different parties, also question ministries about their policies.

> **Fun fact worth remembering:** Parliament isn't always tense and serious — verses and poetic exchanges have featured in real debates, such as Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman quoting the *Tirukkural* while presenting the 2025–26 Budget, and the poetic sparring between Sushma Swaraj and PM Manmohan Singh in a 2011 Lok Sabha debate.

### 4) Financial accountability

Parliament **approves and monitors government expenditure** through the annual budget and by examining how funds are distributed to different ministries. The government must provide this information to Parliament in a timely and accurate manner.

## Executive Functions of Parliament

### The President

The **President** is the **Head of State** and the **nominal head of the Executive**. Key functions:
- Appoints the Prime Minister and other ministers
- Summons the Parliament
- Gives assent to bills

The Council of Ministers aids and advises the President — but in specific circumstances (like a political crisis where no party holds a clear Lok Sabha majority), the **President can exercise discretionary powers**.

### The Prime Minister and the Council of Ministers

Here's a distinction worth getting exactly right: the President is the **nominal** head, but the **Prime Minister** is the ***de facto* executive authority** in India's Parliamentary system.

- The President appoints the **leader of the majority party or coalition** in the Lok Sabha as Prime Minister.
- The PM serves with the support of the majority of Lok Sabha MPs.

**Key functions of the Prime Minister:**
- Leading the Council of Ministers
- Advising the President
- Coordinating the work of different ministries
- Shaping national policies

The PM and Council of Ministers are **collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha**. Most bills discussed in Parliament are actually initiated by the government. A permanent group of officials called **civil servants** (also called administrators or bureaucrats) help implement laws and policies under the direction of ministers.

> **Don't miss:** In 1956, Railway Minister **Lal Bahadur Shastri** resigned after a train accident, even though he wasn't personally blamed — he believed a minister must take **moral responsibility** for anything happening in their ministry.

### Differences Between the Legislature and the Executive

| Aspect | Legislature | Executive |
|---|---|---|
| **Composition** | Parliament: President, Lok Sabha, and Rajya Sabha | President, Vice-President, and Council of Ministers headed by the PM |
| **Main Role** | Making laws and overseeing the Executive's work | Enforcing laws made by the Legislature |
| **Bill introduction** | Can introduce some bills | Most bills are introduced by the Executive |
| **Checks** | Keeps a check on the Executive by asking questions and seeking explanations | Provides information/explanations to the Legislature; aids and advises the President |
| **Finances** | Sanctions all government-related expenses | Prepares and implements the approved budget |
| **Committees** | Consults different Parliamentary committees | Can function independently day-to-day; consults committees when required |

> **Exam trap:** A very common confusion — "If the Executive is part of the Legislature (since PM and ministers are MPs), how is there separation of power?" The answer lies in *function*, not membership: the Legislature's core role is making and overseeing laws, while the Executive's core role is implementing them — even though the same people are often involved in both.

## The Judiciary — Role of Checks and Balances

The **Judiciary** interprets and applies the laws of the land, including in disputes, and functions through a system of courts. It acts as the **custodian of the Constitution**, ensuring all branches of government operate within constitutional limits.

- If Parliament's laws violate the Constitutional framework, the Judiciary can step in.
- If the Executive's implementation of laws violates the Constitution, the Judiciary can step in there too.

Just as Parliament functions through its Legislature and Executive arms, the **Judiciary functions through its Courts**. Together, this three-way relationship — Legislature makes laws, Executive enforces them, Judiciary checks both — is what's meant by **separation of powers** and the **system of checks and balances**, ensuring no single organ becomes too powerful.

## Legislative and Executive Functions at the State Level

Just as the Union has a Parliament, each state has its own legislature and executive — commonly referred to through its **State Assembly**. Members here are called **MLAs (Members of Legislative Assembly)**.

### Union List, State List, and Concurrent List

The Constitution divides legislative subjects into three lists:
- **Union List:** Subjects only the Union government can legislate on.
- **State List:** Subjects only State governments can legislate on.
- **Concurrent List:** Subjects **both** can legislate on — but if the Union legislates on a Concurrent List subject, states must follow it.

> **Worked example:** Education is on the **Concurrent List**, yet the **RTE Act applies all over India** — because it was passed by the Union government, and states are bound to follow Union legislation on Concurrent List subjects.

### Parallel structure: Union vs. State Government

| Feature | Union Government | State Government |
|---|---|---|
| Constitutional Head | President (elected by electoral college) | Governor (appointed by the President) |
| Term of Office (Head) | 5 years | 5 years |
| Executive Head (nominal / de facto) | President (nominal) / **Prime Minister** (de facto) | Governor (nominal) / **Chief Minister** (de facto) |
| Selection of Executive | Leader of majority party/coalition in Lok Sabha | Leader of majority party/coalition in Vidhan Sabha |
| Council of Ministers selected by | Prime Minister | Chief Minister |
| Responsibility | Collectively responsible to Lok Sabha | Collectively responsible to Vidhan Sabha |
| Legislature structure | Bicameral (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha) | Unicameral or bicameral (Vidhan Sabha + Vidhan Parishad) |
| Lower House | Lok Sabha | Vidhan Sabha (Legislative Assembly) |
| Upper House | Rajya Sabha | Vidhan Parishad (Legislative Council) — only in some states |
| Term of Lower House | 5 years | 5 years |
| Presiding Officer (Lower House) | Speaker | Speaker |
| Legislative powers | Union List + Concurrent List | State List + Concurrent List |
| Financial powers | Money bills originate only in Lok Sabha | Money bills originate only in Vidhan Sabha |

### Structure of State Legislatures

Unlike the Centre (always bicameral), **State legislatures can be unicameral or bicameral**:
- The **Vidhan Sabha** (legislative assembly) exists in every state.
- The **Vidhan Parishad** (legislative council, the upper house) exists only in some states.

**States with a bicameral system:** Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh. All other states are **unicameral**.

## Challenges to the Effective Functioning of Legislatures

Even though the legislature has a critical role in enacting laws, overseeing administration, approving budgets, and discussing national issues, it faces real challenges:
- **Regular absenteeism** of members
- **Non-cooperative conduct or communication**
- **Reduced quality of debates**
- **Disruptions of Question Hour**

### How often does Parliament actually meet?

Parliament typically meets **three times a year** in **sessions**: **Budget Sessions, Monsoon Sessions, and Winter Sessions**. Each session has **sittings**, and Parliament usually sits for **6 hours a day** during sessions (extendable for urgent business). State Assemblies follow similar schedules.

> **Don't miss:** Data shows sittings have declined sharply over time — the 1st Lok Sabha (1952–57) had 677 sittings across 14 sessions, while the 13th Lok Sabha (1999–2004) had only 356 sittings across 14 sessions — a useful fact if asked to interpret Parliamentary "productivity" data.

**Productivity** in this context means the number of hours a House actually functioned against the time it was scheduled to. Concerns raised include a substantial proportion of Lok Sabha representatives having **criminal cases** against them, and sessions marked by **angry or biased debates** that don't seriously address people's concerns.

### The way forward

The chapter emphasises that democracy grows stronger when **citizens stay informed and engaged** — asking questions, participating in public discussions (including digital spaces offered during policymaking), and constructively engaging with representatives.

## Quick Recap

- India's **Parliament** = President + **Lok Sabha** (directly elected, max 550 seats) + **Rajya Sabha** (indirectly elected via electoral college) — a **bicameral** system rooted partly in British influence, partly in India's own traditions (mahājanapadas, village panchayats).
- The **Speaker** presides over the Lok Sabha; the **Vice President** presides over the Rajya Sabha as its chairperson.
- The **Union Executive** = President + Vice President + Council of Ministers (headed by PM); it implements and enforces laws.
- Parliament's four key functions: **Constitutional function, Lawmaking, Executive accountability, Financial accountability**.
- A **bill** becomes an **act** through: introduction → reading → Standing Committee → consideration/passing → clause-by-clause discussion → vote → repeat in other House → Presidential assent → Gazette notification. RTE (2009) is the chapter's worked example.
- **Money Bills** can only be introduced in the **Lok Sabha**, with the President's **prior recommendation**.
- **Question Hour** and Parliamentary committees are key tools of executive accountability.
- The **President** is the nominal head of the Executive (Head of State); the **Prime Minister** is the *de facto* executive authority, appointed as leader of the Lok Sabha's majority party/coalition.
- The **Legislature** makes laws and checks the Executive; the **Executive** implements laws and answers to the Legislature — separation of powers exists through *function*, even though the same people (MPs/ministers) are involved in both.
- The **Judiciary** acts as custodian of the Constitution, checking both the Legislature and Executive for constitutional violations — completing the system of **checks and balances**.
- At the **state level**: State Assembly, with MLAs, legislates on the **State List** and **Concurrent List**; if the Union legislates on a Concurrent List subject, states must follow it (e.g., RTE).
- **Governor** (nominal head) and **Chief Minister** (de facto executive) mirror the President–PM relationship at the state level.
- State legislatures can be **unicameral** (most states) or **bicameral** (Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, Uttar Pradesh — with a **Vidhan Sabha** and **Vidhan Parishad**).
- Parliament meets in **three sessions a year** (Budget, Monsoon, Winter), typically sitting **6 hours/day**; sittings have declined over decades, raising concerns about "productivity."
- Key challenges: absenteeism, disrupted debates, criminal cases against representatives; the remedy suggested is greater citizen awareness and engagement.

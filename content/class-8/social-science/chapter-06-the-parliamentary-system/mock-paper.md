## Answer Key

1. A **bill** is a draft of a proposed law that needs to be approved by the Parliament before it can be enacted into law.

2. The **Vice President of India** presides over the Rajya Sabha, acting as its **chairperson**.

3. "Education" falls under the **Concurrent List**.

4. The **Prime Minister** is the *de facto* executive authority in India's Parliamentary system.

5. Any one of: **Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, or Uttar Pradesh**.

6. A **Money Bill** is a bill concerned with financial matters, including **taxation and government borrowing**. It can be introduced **only in the Lok Sabha**. The special condition is that its introduction requires the **prior recommendation of the President**.

7. Any two of the following:
   - **Leading** the Council of Ministers.
   - **Advising** the President.
   - **Coordinating** the work of different ministries.
   - **Shaping** national policies.

8. A **Standing Committee** is a permanent committee consisting of MPs (the individual members may change, but the committee structure remains) that **scrutinises government activities**. One function it performs is that it **studies bills thoroughly and suggests modifications** (as it did with the RTE bill) — it also makes recommendations and asks questions about actions taken by the government.

9. At the Union level, the **nominal head** is the **President**, who is the Head of State and the nominal head of the Executive, but does not directly run the government day-to-day. The ***de facto* executive authority** is the **Prime Minister**, who actually leads the Council of Ministers, takes decisions on the day-to-day functioning of the government, and is supported by the majority of MPs in the Lok Sabha.

10. A **bill** must pass through a defined process before becoming an **act**:
    - It is first **introduced** in either House of Parliament.
    - This is followed by a **reading** of the bill, where MPs go through its contents (historically read out by a clerk for illiterate MPs; today, MPs read it themselves).
    - The bill may then be **referred to a Standing Committee**, which studies it thoroughly and suggests modifications.
    - It is then **taken up for consideration and passing**, followed by **clause-by-clause discussion**, where specific parts (clauses) of the bill are debated and amendments are voted upon.
    - A **vote is taken on the bill** in that House.
    - The **entire process is then repeated in the other House** of Parliament.
    - Once passed by both Houses, the bill receives **Presidential assent**.
    - Finally, it is published through a **Gazette notification**, an official government publication announcing legal documents.

    **Applying this to RTE, 2009:** The idea's roots lay in the Directive Principles of State Policy. In the early 1990s, it was argued in court that free education was part of the Fundamental Right to Life. The **86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002** introduced **Article 21A**, mandating free and compulsory education for children aged 6–14. The bill was **tabled in the Rajya Sabha**, studied by a **committee** which suggested modifications, with major debate around **funding** for schools, infrastructure, and teachers. After the 2009 elections, the bill was passed in the **Lok Sabha**, and in **August 2009** it received the **President's assent**, becoming an Act.

11. | Aspect | Legislature | Executive |
    |---|---|---|
    | **Composition** | The Parliament of India — composed of the President, the Lok Sabha, and the Rajya Sabha. | Composed of the President, the Vice-President, and the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister. |
    | **Main Role** | Making laws and overseeing the work of the Executive. | Enforcing the laws made by the Legislature. |
    | **Bill introduction** | Can introduce **some** bills in the Parliament. | **Most** of the bills are introduced by the Executive in the Parliament. |
    | **Accountability/Checks** | Keeps a check on the activities of the Executive by asking questions (e.g., through the Question Hour) and seeking explanations; sanctions all government-related expenses. | Provides information and explanations to the Legislature on its decisions and actions; aids and advises the President on important matters, including calling sessions of Parliament; prepares and implements the budget approved by Parliament. |

    In summary, the Legislature's central purpose is to **make laws and hold the government accountable**, while the Executive's central purpose is to **carry out and enforce** those laws — with the Legislature retaining oversight through financial sanction, questioning, and committee scrutiny.

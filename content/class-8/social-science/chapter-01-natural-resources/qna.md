### Q1: What is a "natural resource"?
A. Materials and substances that occur in Nature and are valuable to humans.



### Q2: When does an element of Nature become a "resource"?
A. When it is (i) technologically accessible, (ii) economically feasible to extract/use, and (iii) culturally acceptable to use.



### Q3: Give an example of something that exists in Nature but is NOT yet a "resource."
A. Petroleum deep under the ocean that we don't have the technology to access — it remains part of Nature, not a resource, until it becomes usable.



### Q4: In this chapter, what does the word "exploitation" mean?
A. It simply means the extraction, utilisation, and consumption of natural resources — not something negative.



### Q5: Name the three categories of natural resources based on their use.
A. (i) Resources essential for life (air, water, soil), (ii) Resources for materials (wood, marble, gold), (iii) Resources for energy (coal, sunlight, wind, water).



### Q6: What are renewable resources? Give two examples.
A. Resources that Nature can restore and regenerate over time if used sustainably. Examples: sunlight, wind energy, flowing water, forest timber.



### Q7: What are non-renewable resources? Give two examples.
A. Resources formed over very long periods that cannot be replenished at the rate we use them. Examples: coal, petroleum, iron ore, gold.



### Q8: What condition must be met for a resource to remain renewable?
A. The natural rhythm of restoration and regeneration must not be disturbed — we shouldn't use it faster than Nature can replace it.



### Q9: How long are India's coal reserves estimated to last?
A. About another 50 years, given current usage rates.



### Q10: Differentiate between "restoration" and "regeneration."
A. Restoration = returning something to its original healthy state after damage (e.g., a forest recovering after a wildfire). Regeneration = Nature's ability to create new life and conditions for thriving, going beyond simple restoration.



### Q11: What is the difference between an "ecosystem function" and an "ecosystem service"?
A. Ecosystem function = Nature's inherent working (a forest filters water naturally). Ecosystem service = the benefit humans receive from that function (we get clean water to use).



### Q12: How much oxygen does a mature tree produce daily, and how much does a human need?
A. A mature tree produces about 275 litres of oxygen/day; a human needs about 350 litres/day.



### Q13: Why are natural resources not evenly distributed, and what effects does this have?
A. Geography and geology determine where resources are found, leading to differences in industries, trade, jobs, and sometimes conflicts/displacement of local people.



### Q14: Give an example of a resource-sharing conflict within India.
A. The sharing of Kaveri River water among Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Puducherry required negotiation and management to maintain peace.



### Q15: What is the "Natural Resource Curse" or "Paradox of Plenty"?
A. A phenomenon where regions rich in natural resources experience slower economic growth because they fail to develop industries that convert raw resources into higher-value products.



### Q16: How has India avoided the "Natural Resource Curse"?
A. By investing in the development of industries and technology to convert resources into valuable products to meet growing needs.



### Q17: What is "stewardship" in the context of natural resources?
A. Using resources responsibly — enabling restoration and regeneration of renewables, and judicious, careful use of non-renewables.



### Q18: What caused the groundwater crisis in Punjab?
A. Shift to high-yielding water-intensive crop varieties in the 1960s (Green Revolution), free electricity leading to over-pumping, and use of chemical fertilisers/pesticides that also polluted groundwater.



### Q19: What percentage of Punjab's area is classified as "over-exploited" for groundwater, and what does that mean?
A. Almost 80%. It means groundwater is being drawn at a rate much greater than it can naturally be restored/replenished.



### Q20: How did Sikkim transform its farming practices, and with what results?
A. Sikkim's government promoted organic farming; farmers switched to compost and natural pest repellents. In 2016, Sikkim became India's first 100% organic state — biodiversity flourished, tourism increased, and farmer incomes rose by ~20% on average.



### Q21: Why is cement production considered problematic for the environment?
A. It is one of the most polluting industries — it releases fine dust harmful to lungs (humans and animals), reduces plant yields, and causes soil and water pollution.



### Q22: Name some traditional or sustainable alternatives being used to replace cement in construction.
A. Traditional materials like stone and mud, new plant-based materials, and recycled materials from waste plastic (e.g., the mud building at Auroville).



### Q23: What is the International Solar Alliance and why was it formed?
A. Launched by India and France in 2015, it's a coalition of sunshine-rich countries committed to harnessing solar power, sharing technical expertise, and enabling affordable financing for solar projects, especially in developing nations.



### Q24: What is Vṛikṣhāyurveda?
A. An ancient Indian botanical science (dating back centuries, formalised by Surapala around the 10th century CE) focused on plant/tree care — covering soil suitability, seed treatment, irrigation, natural pest management, and crop rotation for sustainable agriculture.



### Q25: What does "lokasangraha" (mentioned from the Bhagavad Gītā) mean, and how does it relate to this chapter?
A. It means the idea that everyone must go beyond personal desires and act for the wellbeing of all. It connects to the chapter's message that we must act as responsible stewards of natural resources for the good of all — present and future generations.

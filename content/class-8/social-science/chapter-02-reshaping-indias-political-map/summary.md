# Reshaping India's Political Map

Picture a map of India redrawn again and again over 600 years — new capitals rising, empires collapsing within a single lifetime, and ordinary farmers and traders somehow keeping the economy running through all of it. That's this chapter: the story of how India's political map kept getting reshaped between the 11th and 17th centuries, and how people adapted every time it did.

## What Do We Mean by "Medieval"?

Quick clarification before we start: the word "medieval" was originally a European term for the period between the fall of the Roman Empire and the Renaissance. India's history doesn't map onto that neatly at all — but for convenience, this chapter uses "medieval" to mean roughly the **11th to 17th centuries** in India. Don't confuse the two timelines if a question ever compares them.

## The Rise and Fall of the Delhi Sultanate

Here's a fact that sounds almost unbelievable: the average Delhi Sultan ruled for less than **nine years** — because roughly two out of every three seized power by eliminating their predecessor. That instability is the defining feature of this whole period.

The **Delhi Sultanate** began in 1192 after the defeat of King Prithviraj Chauhan, and was ruled by **five successive dynasties**, all of Turkic-Afghan origin:

| Dynasty | Also known as |
|---|---|
| Mamluks | "Slave dynasty" |
| Khiljis | Khaljis |
| Tughlaqs | — |
| Sayyids | — |
| Lodis | Lodhis |

A **Sultanate**, by the way, simply means a territory ruled by a "Sultan."

**Two rulers worth knowing well:**

- **Ala-ud-din Khilji** (turn of the 14th century) conducted sweeping military campaigns across north and central India while also successfully repelling Mongol invasions. His general **Malik Kafur** pushed the Sultanate's reach deep into the south.
- **Muhammad bin Tughlaq** briefly brought most of the subcontinent under one ruler for the first time since the Mauryan Empire — but he's remembered just as much for two spectacular missteps: forcibly shifting his capital from Delhi to Daulatabad and back (a 1,000+ km journey each way, with major loss of life), and introducing **token currency** (cheap copper coins declared equal in value to silver/gold) — a genuinely forward-thinking idea (most currency today is "token" currency too) that badly backfired at the time because it triggered widespread counterfeiting and confused trade.

> **Exam trap:** Don't describe token currency as simply "a bad idea." The chapter is clear that the *concept* was progressive — the problem was how confusing and easy to counterfeit it was in that specific context.

**How the Sultanate funded itself:** through plunder from military campaigns, taxes on conquered peoples, and the slave trade. Some sultans also imposed the ***jizya*** — a tax on non-Muslim subjects that exempted them from military service, but which in practice was often a real economic and social burden, and an incentive to convert. This period also saw destruction of temples, both for plunder and through **iconoclasm** (the deliberate destruction of religious images considered idolatrous).

**The end of the Sultanate:** At the close of the 14th century, **Timur**, a brutal Turkic-Mongol conqueror, devastated Delhi and withdrew with massive plunder, leaving chaos behind. The **Lodis** picked up the pieces as the last Sultanate dynasty — but by then, its territory had shrunk considerably.

## Resistance to the Delhi Sultanate

Not everyone fell under Delhi's control. A few standout examples:

- The **Eastern Ganga kingdom** of Kalinga (roughly today's Odisha, plus parts of Bengal and Andhra Pradesh) successfully resisted the Sultanate. Its ruler **Narasimhadeva I** defeated the Sultanate's governor of Bengal and built the famous **Sūrya temple at Konark** partly to commemorate his victories.
- The **Hoysalas** held out in the south (mostly present-day Karnataka) until internal conflicts weakened them enough to be absorbed into the rising Vijayanagara Empire.
- In Rajasthan, **Rana Kumbha** of Mewar successfully repelled invasions in the 15th century and built the massive **Kumbhalgarh Fort**, famous for its 36-km wall — one of the longest continuous walls in the world.
- The **Bahmani Sultanate** rose as an independent, rebellious power controlling much of the Deccan.

## The Vijayanagara Empire

While Delhi weakened, a new power rose in the south. In the 14th century, two brothers — **Harihara and Bukka** — who had served as governors under Muhammad bin Tughlaq, broke away from Delhi's authority and founded the **Vijayanagara Empire**.

Its golden age came under **Krishnadevaraya** in the 16th century, who combined military strength with a genuine cultural flowering:
- He patronised poets and scholars in Sanskrit, Telugu, and Kannada, and composed a Telugu epic himself (*Āmuktamālyada*), which even included his own thoughts on good governance.
- He funded major temples, including at Tirupati and in his own capital.

**The fall:** in 1565, a coalition of Deccan Sultanates defeated Vijayanagara's forces at the **Battle of Talikota**. The city was sacked and left in ruins, and the empire fragmented into smaller regions before ending completely in the mid-17th century.

## The Mughals

**Babur**, a Turkic-Mongol ruler pushed out of Central Asia, turned to India and defeated **Ibrahim Lodi** at the **First Battle of Panipat (1526)** — a battle that relied heavily on gunpowder, field artillery, and matchlock guns, recently introduced to Indian warfare. This defeat ended the Delhi Sultanate for good and founded the **Mughal Empire**.

After Babur's death, his son **Humayun** briefly lost control to the Afghan leader **Sher Shah Suri**, and then to a commander named **Himu**, before reclaiming Delhi. Humayun's son, **Akbar**, became emperor at just 13.

**Akbar** is best understood as a ruler of two phases:
- **Early on:** ruthless conquest, including a brutal siege of Chittorgarh where thousands of Rajput women reportedly committed ***jauhar*** (a mass self-immolation to avoid capture) rather than face defeat.
- **Later:** a deliberate shift toward political strategy over pure force — marriage alliances with neighbouring kingdoms, welcoming Rajput and regional leaders into his court, **abolishing the jizya**, and promoting ***sulh-i-kul*** ("peace with all"), a doctrine of tolerance across faiths. He also set up a "house of translation" that rendered major Sanskrit texts — the Mahābhārata, Rāmāyaṇa, Bhagavad Gītā — into Persian.

> **Common mistake:** Students sometimes describe Akbar as simply "the tolerant Mughal emperor," missing that his reign contained real brutality too. The textbook is explicit that his rule was "a blend of brutality and tolerance" — both halves matter for a complete answer.

His successors: **Jahangir** shared his father's love of art; **Shah Jahan** is remembered above all as the builder of the **Taj Mahal**, alongside Humayun's tomb and the Red Forts of Delhi and Agra.

**Aurangzeb** took the throne after defeating and executing his brother Dara Shikoh in the succession struggle. Under him, the Mughal Empire reached its **greatest territorial extent** — but at a cost: the last 25 years of his reign were consumed by constant warfare in the Deccan, draining the treasury. Unlike Akbar, Aurangzeb reimposed the *jizya* and a pilgrimage tax on Hindus, banned practices he considered un-Islamic, and ordered the demolition of temples (Banaras, Mathura, Somnath, among others) as well as Jain temples and Sikh gurudwaras. His death in 1707 is widely seen as the start of the Mughal Empire's rapid decline.

## Resistance to the Mughals

Resistance came from many directions at once:

- **Peasant uprisings:** the **Jat peasantry** rebelled against Mughal oppression in the 17th century, with 20,000 men fighting the Mughal army before their leader was killed.
- **Tribal resistance:** groups like the **Bhils, Gonds, Santhals, and Kochs** fought attempts to annex their land. **Rani Durgavati** of the Garha (Gond) kingdom personally led her army against a Mughal attack in 1564 and took her own life on the battlefield rather than be captured.
- **The Rajputs:** built on earlier traditions of resistance. **Rana Sanga** unified several Rajput clans before losing to Babur. **Maharana Pratap** refused to submit to Mughal authority; after the **Battle of Haldighati (1576)**, he waged years of **guerrilla warfare** from the Aravalli hills, notably supported by **Bhil archers**. Later, **Durga Das Rathore** of Marwar fought to protect Jodhpur's independence under Aurangzeb.
- **The Ahoms:** migrated from present-day Myanmar into the Brahmaputra Valley in the 13th century and resisted both the Sultanate and the Mughals. Their unique ***paik* system** required able-bodied men to provide labour or military service in exchange for land rights — letting them raise a large force without a permanent standing army. At the **Battle of Saraighat (1671)**, commander **Lachit Borphukan** and 10,000 men defeated a much larger Mughal force, preserving Ahom independence.
- **The Sikhs:** began as a purely spiritual movement under **Guru Nanak** in 15th-century Punjab, teaching equality and the oneness of God. Persecution under some Mughal rulers changed its trajectory: **Guru Arjan** was tortured to death under Jahangir; his son **Guru Hargobind** formed a Sikh army in response. Later, **Guru Tegh Bahadur** was executed by Aurangzeb for refusing to convert to Islam after standing up for persecuted Kashmiri Pandits. His son, **Guru Gobind Singh** (the 10th and final Guru), founded the **Khalsa**, a martial brotherhood devoted to justice and defence of the faith. Sikh confederacies were eventually unified by **Maharaja Ranjit Singh**, whose Sikh Empire resisted both Mughal remnants and, later, British expansion.

## Administering India

**Under the Delhi Sultanate:** the Sultan held absolute authority, assisted by a council of ministers. The key administrative tool was the ***iqta* system** — territories assigned to nobles (*iqtadars*) who collected taxes to fund the army; these posts were not hereditary. Tax pressure fell heaviest on the peasantry.

**Under the Mughals**, Akbar built a more structured system:

| Official | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| *Diwan* | Finances |
| *Mir Bakhshi* | Military matters |
| *Khan-i-Saman* | Public works, trade, industry, royal household |
| *Sadr* | Justice, religious and educational matters |

The empire was divided into **twelve provinces (*subahs*)**, each with its own set of these officials. Akbar also introduced the ***mansabdari* system**: officers (*mansabdars*) were ranked by *mansab* and required to maintain a set number of troops, elephants, horses, and camels for the state — letting the empire raise an army quickly without a large permanent one. Mansabdars were typically paid through land grants (*jagirs*), so were also called *jagirdars*. Despite Akbar's tolerance, non-Muslims rarely made up more than a third of top administrative officials.

**Todar Mal**, Akbar's finance minister, introduced a systematic land survey and crop-based revenue system that significantly boosted state income.

## People's Lives

Despite constant political upheaval, India remained economically vibrant and among the wealthiest regions in the world, built on:
- **Agriculture** as the backbone of the economy — land revenue typically took about one-fifth of produce (though some rulers pushed this as high as one-half).
- **Thriving trade networks**, exporting far more than it imported (textiles, spices, and craft goods went out; silk, horses, and metals came in) through ports like Calicut, Surat, and Hooghly.
- The ***hundi* system** — a written payment instruction that let merchants transfer money across political borders without physically carrying currency, reducing the risk of loss to plunder.
- **Temples as economic hubs** — not just places of worship, but centres that held land grants, funded merchant credit, and built irrigation and pilgrim infrastructure.

Life wasn't evenly prosperous, though: peasants often kept only a small share of their produce after taxes, sometimes falling into bonded labour, and frequent warfare caused displacement. Still, the chapter's overall picture is one of **resilience** — communities and traditions adapting and rebuilding again and again through centuries of political change.

## Quick Recap

- The **Delhi Sultanate** (1192–~1526) was ruled by five dynasties (Mamluks, Khiljis, Tughlaqs, Sayyids, Lodis) and was marked by political instability, military expansion, and taxes like the *jizya*.
- Kingdoms like the **Eastern Gangas**, **Hoysalas**, and **Mewar** successfully resisted the Sultanate.
- The **Vijayanagara Empire** (founded by Harihara and Bukka) reached its peak under **Krishnadevaraya** before falling at the **Battle of Talikota (1565)**.
- **Babur** founded the **Mughal Empire** after the **First Battle of Panipat (1526)**.
- **Akbar** combined early military brutality with later religious tolerance (*sulh-i-kul*, abolishing the *jizya*); **Aurangzeb** reversed this tolerance and reimposed the *jizya*, while expanding the empire to its largest extent — at great administrative and financial cost.
- Resistance to Mughal rule came from **peasants, tribal kingdoms** (like Rani Durgavati's), the **Rajputs** (Maharana Pratap's guerrilla warfare), the **Ahoms** (the *paik* system and the Battle of Saraighat), and the **Sikhs** (from Guru Nanak's teachings to the founding of the Khalsa).
- Administration relied on the ***iqta* system** under the Sultanate and the ***mansabdari* system** plus *subahs* under the Mughals.
- Despite political instability, India's economy remained strong through agriculture, trade, the *hundi* credit system, and temple-based economic activity — though common people often bore the heaviest burdens.

## Answer Key
1. The Delhi Sultanate was established in **1192**.
2. Any two of: **Mamluks (Slave dynasty), Khiljis, Tughlaqs, Sayyids, Lodis**.
3. **Babur** founded the Mughal Empire after the **First Battle of Panipat (1526)**, where he defeated Ibrahim Lodi.
4. **Jauhar** refers to the historical practice, among some Rajput communities, of women taking their own lives (often through mass self-immolation) to avoid capture and enslavement when a fort or kingdom faced certain defeat; it was regarded as a heroic act of final resistance and a means of preserving honour.
5. **Guru Gobind Singh**, the tenth and last Sikh Guru, founded the Khalsa.
6. The jizya was a tax imposed on non-Muslim subjects, which in principle granted them protection and exemption from military service. In practice, it often functioned as an economic burden and a source of public humiliation, creating a financial and social incentive for people to convert to Islam.
7. The Vijayanagara Empire was founded by the brothers **Harihara and Bukka**, who had earlier served as governors under **Muhammad bin Tughlaq** before rejecting Delhi's authority and establishing an independent kingdom.
8. The paik system required every able-bodied man in the Ahom kingdom to provide labour or military service to the state in exchange for land rights, allowing the rulers to build public infrastructure and maintain a large fighting force without a permanent standing army.
9. Guerrilla warfare is a style of fighting in which small groups, using their knowledge of the terrain, carry out surprise attacks and ambushes to defeat larger, better-equipped armies. **Maharana Pratap** used this strategy against the Mughals from the Aravalli hills after the Battle of Haldighati.
10. Muhammad bin Tughlaq forcibly shifted his capital from Delhi to Daulatabad, forcing people to travel over 1,000 km, only to reverse the decision a few years later when the plan misfired, causing great loss of life both times. He also introduced token currency — declaring cheap copper coins to have the value of silver or gold — which, despite being a progressive idea in principle, caused confusion in trade and encouraged widespread counterfeiting, damaging the economy.
11. Under the iqta system, territories were assigned to nobles called iqtadars, who were responsible for collecting taxes from that territory. After deducting their expenses (particularly for maintaining troops for the army), the remaining revenue was meant to go to the Sultan's treasury. This system created a network of local administrators loyal to the central authority, though their positions were not hereditary, and the tax burden generally fell most heavily on the peasantry.
12. Under the mansabdari system, officers called mansabdars were assigned a rank (mansab) that determined the number of elephants, horses, camels, and troops they were required to maintain for the state. This allowed the Mughal empire to assemble a large army at short notice without the cost of maintaining a permanent centralised force, while regular inspections ensured compliance. Mansabdars were typically paid through land grants (jagirs), which is why they were also known as jagirdars.
13. A hundi was a written instruction enabling a merchant to make a payment to someone in a different location, without physically transporting currency across political boundaries. This reduced the risk of merchants losing their wealth to plunder during long journeys, and allowed trading communities such as the Marwaris to build parallel systems of credit and trust that functioned independently of official government structures — effectively an early precursor to modern banking.
14. In his early years, Akbar's reign showed considerable brutality, most notably during the siege of Chittorgarh fort, which he besieged for over five months; when the fort was finally breached, he ordered the massacre of around 30,000 civilians, and hundreds of Rajput women committed jauhar rather than be captured. In his later reign, however, Akbar shifted toward political strategy over pure military force: he formed marriage alliances with princesses of neighbouring kingdoms, welcomed Rajput and regional leaders into his court, abolished the discriminatory jizya tax, and promoted the doctrine of sulh-i-kul ("peace with all"), encouraging tolerance of different faiths. He also established a house of translation at Fatehpur Sikri to render major Sanskrit texts into Persian. This blend of early brutality and later tolerance reflects how his approach evolved as his empire grew and stabilised.
15. Unlike Akbar, who abolished the jizya and promoted religious tolerance through sulh-i-kul, Aurangzeb reimposed the jizya tax on non-Muslims as well as a pilgrimage tax on Hindus, banned practices he considered un-Islamic such as music and dance at court, and ordered the demolition of temples at several important sites, along with Jain temples and Sikh gurudwaras. Militarily, Aurangzeb achieved the Mughal Empire's greatest territorial expansion, particularly in the south, but this came at a severe cost: he spent the last 25 years of his life fighting continuous wars in the Deccan against determined regional resistance. Maintaining these large armies drained the imperial treasury and placed enormous strain on the administration, which historians widely regard as one of the key factors behind the rapid decline of Mughal power following his death in 1707.
16. (Sample answer covering Rajputs and Ahoms — any two valid choices should be credited.) The Rajputs offered sustained resistance to Mughal authority, most notably through Maharana Pratap of Mewar, who refused to accept Mughal suzerainty. After the Mughal army gained the upper hand at the Battle of Haldighati (1576), Maharana Pratap escaped and waged years of guerrilla warfare from the Aravalli hills, receiving crucial support from Bhil archers who knew the terrain well. Later, Durga Das Rathore of Marwar continued Rajput resistance during Aurangzeb's reign, fighting to protect Jodhpur's independence. The Ahoms, meanwhile, resisted Mughal expansion into the Northeast using their unique paik system, which allowed them to raise a large fighting force from the general population without a permanent standing army. At the Battle of Saraighat (1671), Ahom commander Lachit Borphukan led around 10,000 men to defeat a much larger Mughal force of 30,000 soldiers on the Brahmaputra River, using superior knowledge of the local terrain and persistent guerrilla tactics, ultimately preserving Ahom independence.

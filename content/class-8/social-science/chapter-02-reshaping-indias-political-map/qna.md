# Top 25 Q&A — Reshaping India's Political Map

### Q1: What time period does the term "medieval" refer to in the context of Indian history, as used in this chapter?
In this chapter, "medieval" refers to the period from the **11th to the 17th centuries** in India — a different span from the term's original European usage.

### Q2: When was the Delhi Sultanate established, and after defeating which ruler?
The Delhi Sultanate was established in **1192**, after the defeat of **King Prithviraj Chauhan**, who had ruled parts of northwestern India.

### Q3: Name the five dynasties that successively ruled the Delhi Sultanate.
The **Mamluks** (Slave dynasty), the **Khiljis**, the **Tughlaqs**, the **Sayyids**, and the **Lodis**.

### Q4: What was the average length of a Delhi Sultan's reign, and why was it so short?
A Sultan's average reign was hardly more than **nine years**, largely because almost **two out of every three sultans** seized power by eliminating their predecessor, making successions frequently violent.

### Q5: What was the jizya, and what effect did it have on non-Muslim subjects?
The **jizya** was a tax imposed on non-Muslim subjects in exchange for protection and exemption from military service. In practice, it often served as an economic burden and a source of public humiliation, creating an incentive for people to convert to Islam.

### Q6: Describe two major but poorly executed decisions made by Muhammad bin Tughlaq.
He forcibly shifted his capital from **Delhi to Daulatabad** and later back again, a journey of over 1,000 km each way that caused great loss of life; he also introduced **token currency** (cheap copper coins valued as silver or gold), which caused confusion in trade and widespread counterfeiting.

### Q7: How did Timur's invasion affect Delhi at the end of the 14th century?
Timur, a Turkic-Mongol conqueror, launched a devastating attack on Delhi, killing or enslaving large numbers of people and leaving the city in ruins before withdrawing with huge plunder, leaving chaos behind.

### Q8: Name one kingdom that successfully resisted the Delhi Sultanate, and its notable ruler.
The **Eastern Ganga kingdom** of Kalinga successfully resisted the Sultanate; its ruler **Narasimhadeva I** defeated the Sultanate's governor of Bengal and built the Sūrya temple at Konark.

### Q9: Who founded the Vijayanagara Empire, and under whose earlier authority had they served?
The Vijayanagara Empire was founded by two brothers, **Harihara and Bukka**, who had earlier served as governors under **Muhammad bin Tughlaq** before rejecting Delhi's authority.

### Q10: Describe two achievements of Krishnadevaraya's reign.
Krishnadevaraya expanded and secured Vijayanagara's dominance over the Deccan, and also fostered a cultural renaissance — patronising poets and scholars in Sanskrit, Telugu, and Kannada, and composing the Telugu epic *Āmuktamālyada* himself.

### Q11: What happened at the Battle of Talikota (1565), and what were its consequences?
A coalition of Deccan Sultanates defeated Vijayanagara's forces at the **Battle of Talikota**. The city was sacked over several months, its buildings and temples destroyed, and much of its population massacred; the empire subsequently fragmented and ended completely in the mid-17th century.

### Q12: What was significant about the First Battle of Panipat (1526)?
Babur defeated **Ibrahim Lodi** at the First Battle of Panipat, relying heavily on gunpowder, field artillery, and matchlock guns recently introduced to Indian warfare. This victory ended the Delhi Sultanate and founded the **Mughal Empire**.

### Q13: Describe the two contrasting phases often noted in Akbar's reign.
In his early years, Akbar showed brutal military force, notably at the siege of Chittorgarh, where thousands were massacred or enslaved. In his later reign, he shifted toward political strategy — marriage alliances, welcoming Rajput and regional leaders into his court, abolishing the *jizya*, and promoting *sulh-i-kul* (tolerance of all faiths).

### Q14: What is meant by the doctrine of sulh-i-kul, and who promoted it?
*Sulh-i-kul*, meaning "peace with all," was a doctrine of religious tolerance promoted by **Akbar**, reflecting his policy of welcoming and respecting people of different faiths within his empire.

### Q15: For what architectural achievement is Shah Jahan best remembered?
Shah Jahan is best remembered as the builder of the **Taj Mahal** at Agra, along with Humayun's tomb and the Red Forts of Delhi and Agra.

### Q16: How did Aurangzeb's religious policies differ from Akbar's?
Unlike Akbar, Aurangzeb **reimposed the jizya** and a pilgrimage tax on Hindus, banned practices he considered un-Islamic (such as music and dance in his court), and ordered the demolition of temples in several cities, along with Jain temples and Sikh gurudwaras.

### Q17: What factors contributed to the decline of the Mughal Empire after Aurangzeb's death?
Aurangzeb spent the last 25 years of his reign fighting continuous wars in the Deccan, which depleted the empire's treasury and put a great strain on its administration — widely considered a key factor in the empire's rapid decline after his death in 1707.

### Q18: Describe the paik system used by the Ahom kingdom.
The **paik system** required every able-bodied man to provide labour or military service to the state in exchange for land rights, allowing Ahom rulers to build public infrastructure and raise a large force without maintaining a permanent standing army.

### Q19: What happened at the Battle of Saraighat (1671)?
Ahom commander **Lachit Borphukan**, leading around 10,000 men, defeated a much larger Mughal force of 30,000 soldiers on the Brahmaputra River, helping the Ahoms preserve their independence.

### Q20: Describe Maharana Pratap's response after the Battle of Haldighati (1576).
Although the Mughal army had the upper hand at Haldighati, Maharana Pratap escaped and, refusing to accept Mughal authority, waged years of **guerrilla warfare** from the Aravalli hills, notably supported by Bhil archers who also contributed their knowledge of the terrain.

### Q21: How did Sikhism's relationship with Mughal authority change over time, from Guru Nanak to Guru Gobind Singh?
Sikhism began as a purely spiritual movement under **Guru Nanak**, teaching equality and the oneness of God. As persecution grew under some Mughal rulers — including the torture and execution of **Guru Arjan** and later **Guru Tegh Bahadur** — the Sikh Gurus responded by building military capability, culminating in **Guru Gobind Singh's** founding of the **Khalsa**, a martial brotherhood committed to justice and defending the faith.

### Q22: What was the iqta system used under the Delhi Sultanate?
The **iqta system** assigned territories to nobles (*iqtadars*), who collected taxes to fund their administration and the army, with the remainder meant to go to the Sultan's treasury; these positions were not hereditary.

### Q23: Explain the mansabdari system introduced by Akbar, and why it was useful.
Under the **mansabdari system**, officers (*mansabdars*) were ranked by *mansab* and required to maintain a set number of troops, elephants, horses, and camels for the state. This allowed the empire to quickly assemble a large army when needed without maintaining a costly permanent standing force. Mansabdars were usually paid through land grants (*jagirs*), so were also called *jagirdars*.

### Q24: What was the hundi system, and why was it useful to merchants?
A **hundi** was a written instruction to make payment to an individual, which could be carried across political borders. It allowed merchants to transfer funds without physically carrying currency, reducing their vulnerability to plunder during transport — an early precursor to modern banking.

### Q25: Despite frequent political instability, how did temples contribute to India's economy during this period?
Temples functioned as economic hubs beyond their religious role — ruling classes donated land and wealth to them, temple managers used this to build irrigation systems and pilgrim accommodations, and temples provided merchants with credit that helped fund both internal and maritime trade.

# Top 25 Q&A — Rational Numbers

### Q1: What is a rational number?
A rational number is any number that can be written as p/q, where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0.

### Q2: Is every integer a rational number?
Yes. Any integer `n` can be written as `n/1`, which fits the p/q form with q ≠ 0.

### Q3: Is 0 a rational number?
Yes. 0 can be written as `0/1` (or as 0 divided by any non-zero integer), so it satisfies the definition of a rational number.

### Q4: State the closure property of rational numbers for addition and multiplication.
Rational numbers are **closed** under addition and multiplication — adding or multiplying any two rational numbers always produces another rational number.

### Q5: Are rational numbers closed under subtraction and division?
They are closed under subtraction, but **not** closed under division, because division by 0 is undefined and 0 is a rational number.

### Q6: Is addition of rational numbers commutative?
Yes. For any two rational numbers a and b, **a + b = b + a**.

### Q7: Is subtraction of rational numbers commutative?
No. In general, **a − b ≠ b − a** for rational numbers a and b (unless a = b).

### Q8: Is multiplication of rational numbers commutative?
Yes. For any two rational numbers a and b, **a × b = b × a**.

### Q9: State the associative property of addition for rational numbers.
For any three rational numbers a, b, and c: **(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)**. The way the numbers are grouped does not change the sum.

### Q10: Is multiplication of rational numbers associative?
Yes. For any three rational numbers a, b, and c: **(a × b) × c = a × (b × c)**.

### Q11: What is the additive identity for rational numbers?
**0** is the additive identity, because a + 0 = 0 + a = a for every rational number a.

### Q12: What is the multiplicative identity for rational numbers?
**1** is the multiplicative identity, because a × 1 = 1 × a = a for every rational number a.

### Q13: What is the additive inverse of a rational number a?
The additive inverse of a is **−a**, since a + (−a) = 0.

### Q14: What is the multiplicative inverse (reciprocal) of a non-zero rational number p/q?
The multiplicative inverse of p/q is **q/p**, since (p/q) × (q/p) = 1.

### Q15: Does 0 have a multiplicative inverse? Explain.
No. There is no rational number that, multiplied by 0, gives 1 — so 0 has **no** multiplicative inverse. This is one of the most common one-mark questions on this chapter.

### Q16: State the distributive property of multiplication over addition for rational numbers.
For any three rational numbers a, b, and c: **a × (b + c) = a × b + a × c**.

### Q17: How many rational numbers lie between two given rational numbers?
**Infinitely many.** Unlike whole numbers, there is no "next" rational number after a given one — you can always find another one closer.

### Q18: Describe the averaging method for finding a rational number between two rational numbers a and b.
Calculate the mean, **(a + b) ÷ 2**. The result always lies between a and b, and the process can be repeated to find as many numbers as needed.

### Q19: Find one rational number between 1/3 and 1/2.
Using the averaging method: (1/3 + 1/2) ÷ 2 = (5/6) ÷ 2 = **5/12**, which lies between 1/3 and 1/2.

### Q20: Simplify: (2/3) + (−5/6) using the properties of rational numbers.
Convert to a common denominator: 2/3 = 4/6. So (4/6) + (−5/6) = **−1/6**.

### Q21: Verify the distributive property for a = 1/2, b = 1/3, c = 1/4.
- Left side: a × (b + c) = 1/2 × (1/3 + 1/4) = 1/2 × 7/12 = 7/24.
- Right side: (a × b) + (a × c) = (1/2 × 1/3) + (1/2 × 1/4) = 1/6 + 1/8 = 4/24 + 3/24 = 7/24.

Both sides are equal (**7/24 = 7/24**), verifying the property.

### Q22: Why are integers, whole numbers, and natural numbers all considered rational numbers?
Because every one of them can be written in the p/q form required for a rational number — for example, 5 = 5/1, 0 = 0/1, and −3 = −3/1.

### Q23: How is a positive rational number represented on a number line?
It is marked to the **right** of 0, at a distance found by dividing the unit segment into as many equal parts as the denominator and counting off the number of parts equal to the numerator.

### Q24: How is a negative rational number represented on a number line?
It is marked to the **left** of 0, using the same method as for positive rational numbers but moving in the opposite direction.

### Q25: Find three rational numbers between 1/4 and 1/2.
First rewrite with a common denominator: 1/4 = 4/16 and 1/2 = 8/16. Any three fractions strictly between them work, for example **5/16, 6/16, and 7/16**.

# Rational Numbers

A **rational number** is any number that can be written in the form `p/q`, where `p` and `q` are integers and `q ≠ 0`. Rational numbers include natural numbers, whole numbers, integers, and fractions — every number you've worked with so far in school, in other words, except numbers like √2 that can't be written as a simple fraction.

This chapter builds on the number properties you met with whole numbers and integers, and extends them to rational numbers.

## 1. Properties of Rational Numbers

Rational numbers follow the same closure, commutative, and associative properties you've already seen — but not always for every operation. The table below summarises which property holds for which operation.

| Property | Addition | Subtraction | Multiplication | Division |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Closure | Yes | Yes | Yes | No (fails when dividing by 0) |
| Commutative | Yes | No | Yes | No |
| Associative | Yes | No | Yes | No |

**Closure property:** For any two rational numbers `a` and `b`, `a + b`, `a − b`, and `a × b` are always rational numbers.

**Commutative property:** Order doesn't matter for addition and multiplication.
- `a + b = b + a`
- `a × b = b × a`

**Associative property:** Grouping doesn't matter for addition and multiplication.
- `(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)`
- `(a × b) × c = a × (b × c)`

> Subtraction and division are **not** commutative or associative for rational numbers — the order and grouping you choose changes the answer. Always double-check subtraction/division steps in an exam rather than assuming you can reorder them.

## 2. The Role of 0 and 1

- **0** is the *additive identity*: `a + 0 = a` for every rational number `a`.
- **1** is the *multiplicative identity*: `a × 1 = a` for every rational number `a`.
- Every rational number `a` has an *additive inverse* `−a`, such that `a + (−a) = 0`.
- Every non-zero rational number `a = p/q` has a *multiplicative inverse* (reciprocal) `q/p`, such that `a × (q/p) = 1`.

Zero has **no** multiplicative inverse — there's no number you can multiply 0 by to get 1. This is a very common one-mark exam trap, so it's worth memorising directly.

## 3. Distributivity of Multiplication over Addition

For any three rational numbers `a`, `b`, and `c`:

```
a × (b + c) = a × b + a × c
```

This is the same distributive law you used with integers — it's what lets you split (or combine) terms when simplifying an expression quickly.

## 4. Rational Numbers Between Two Rational Numbers

Unlike integers, there is **no "next" rational number** after a given one — between any two rational numbers, however close together, there are infinitely many more rational numbers.

**Method — the averaging trick:** to find a rational number between `a` and `b`, calculate the mean:

```
(a + b) ÷ 2
```

Repeating this process lets you insert as many rational numbers as you like between any two given numbers.

**Worked example:** Find 3 rational numbers between 1/4 and 1/2.

1. First, write both fractions with a common denominator large enough to leave room: 1/4 = 4/16 and 1/2 = 8/16.
2. Any fraction strictly between 4/16 and 8/16 works, e.g. **5/16, 6/16, 7/16**.

## 5. Representing Rational Numbers on a Number Line

Just like integers, rational numbers can be marked on a number line:

- Positive rational numbers lie to the right of 0.
- Negative rational numbers lie to the left of 0.
- To place `p/q`, divide the distance between two consecutive integers into `q` equal parts and count `p` of them from 0.

## Quick Recap

- A rational number is any number expressible as `p/q`, with `q ≠ 0`.
- Addition and multiplication are closed, commutative, and associative for rational numbers; subtraction and division are not.
- 0 is the additive identity; 1 is the multiplicative identity; 0 has no reciprocal.
- Multiplication distributes over addition: `a × (b + c) = a × b + a × c`.
- There are infinitely many rational numbers between any two rational numbers — use the averaging method to find them.

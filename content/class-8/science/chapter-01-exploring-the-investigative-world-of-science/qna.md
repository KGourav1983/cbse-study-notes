### Q1: What is meant by "investigation" in science, according to this chapter?
**Investigation** in science means more than just looking at something and asking simple questions. It involves asking more **focused questions**, designing ways (such as simple experiments) to answer those questions, and then using **observations** to improve one's understanding.

### Q2: What three-step process does the chapter describe for investigating a scientific puzzle?
The chapter describes using questions as starting points to: **observe carefully, experiment thoughtfully, and explain clearly** what is seen.

### Q3: What did students learn about science in Grade 6 and Grade 7, as described in this chapter?
In **Grade 6**, students discovered that science begins with **wonder** — with simple "Why?" and "How?" questions about the world. In **Grade 7**, students learnt that science is always **evolving** — that each answer opens new questions, and ideas can slowly change as one explores deeper.

### Q4: What do the root and kite symbols on the textbook pages represent?
The **root** symbol, found at the bottom of left-hand pages, symbolises the deep, solid foundation of knowledge that keeps us connected to our environment, traditions, and cultural and natural heritage. The **kite** symbol, found at the top corner of right-hand pages, reminds us that curiosity must take flight if we are to explore the unknown.

### Q5: According to the chapter, when does investigation in science work best?
Investigation in science works best only when we **balance** the solid ground of careful observation with the **freedom of creative thinking**.

### Q6: What everyday kitchen phenomenon is used as the chapter's main example of scientific investigation?
The chapter uses the example of why a ***puri* or *batura* puffs up** like a balloon when placed in hot oil, and why a ***phulka* swells** when placed directly on a flame — and specifically, why **one side of a puri is thinner than the other**.

### Q7: What is the first step in investigating a scientific question, as illustrated by the puri example?
The first step is to **ask a scientific question**. In the case of the puri, the question framed was: *What are the different things that may change the way a puri puffs up when fried?*

### Q8: What two main things must be identified when designing a simple experiment?
Two main things must be identified: **what can be changed or controlled** during the experiment, and **what can be observed** to see whether those changes made a difference.

### Q9: List any three things that can be controlled or changed when investigating why a puri puffs up.
Any three of the following: the **thickness and size** of the rolled dough, the **type of flour** used (atta, maida, etc.), the **temperature of the hot oil**, and the **way the dough is dropped** into the oil (vertically, at an angle, or slowly).

### Q10: List two ways of observing or measuring the outcome of the puri-frying experiment.
Two ways include: checking whether the puri **puffs up** (a yes/no observation), and measuring the **time it takes to puff up** (measured in seconds). One can also check whether a very thick layer of dough still gives a thin side to the puri.

### Q11: Why is it important to change only one variable at a time during an experiment? Explain using the puri example.
It is important to change only **one variable at a time** while keeping other conditions the same, so that any difference observed in the outcome can be clearly linked to that one changed variable. For example, to test the effect of oil temperature (boiling hot, hot, or not very hot), one must use **circles of dough of the same thickness** and **drop them in the same way** each time — otherwise, it would be impossible to tell whether the oil temperature or some other factor caused the difference in puffing.

### Q12: Why is it recommended to keep notes while conducting an experiment?
It is a good idea to keep notes of everything observed and sensed during an experiment, such as whether the oil **splattered, smelled, or smoked**. This helps in recording all relevant details that might later help explain the results or lead to new questions.

### Q13: State two new questions that might arise after conducting the puri-frying experiments.
Two new questions mentioned in the chapter are: **Do puris puff better when made fresh or from stored dough?** and **What happens if a hole is pricked in the puri before frying?**

### Q14: What is meant by "systematic investigation"?
**Systematic investigation** refers to the step-by-step process of asking a scientific question, identifying variables to control and observe, changing one variable at a time, recording observations, and using the results to ask further questions. This is exactly how all scientific experiments, from the simplest to the most complicated, are carried out.

### Q15: Is the phenomenon of a puri puffing up completely understood by scientists today? What does this tell us about science?
No, even this simple, everyday observation of a puri puffing is **not completely understood by scientists today**. This shows that scientific investigation is an ongoing process, and that even familiar, everyday phenomena can still hold unanswered questions.

### Q17: How does the arrangement of particles differ between solids and gases, as mentioned in the chapter?
In materials that are **solid**, the particles **cannot move much**, while in **gases**, the particles can **move around freely**.

### Q18: Define elements, compounds, and mixtures as introduced in this chapter.
- An **element** is a pure substance.
- A **compound** is formed when **two or more elements are bonded together**.
- A **mixture** is a combination of substances that **can be separated physically**.

### Q19: What two effects of electric current are mentioned in the chapter, and give one use of each.
The two effects mentioned are the **heating effect**, which helps keep us warm, and the **magnetic effect**, which helps motors run and machines function.

### Q20: How are forces connected to weather events like storms and cyclones, according to the chapter?
Forces are connected to weather events because the concepts of **force and pressure** decide how air moves. A **small difference in pressure** can result in a gentle breeze, while a **stronger pressure difference** can lead to strong winds and, sometimes, even cyclones — powerful weather events that affect daily life, agriculture, and safety.

### Q21: Explain how the phases of the Moon arise, and how they are connected to human calendars.
The phases of the Moon arise because a slightly different part of the Moon is **illuminated** each night, depending on the **relative positions of the Earth, Moon, and Sun**; rough surfaces (like the Moon's surface) reflect light just as polished mirrors do. Watching the periodic cycles of these Moon phases, combined with observations of sunrises and sunsets, allowed early humans to develop the **first calendars** — showing how the routines that govern life on Earth are linked to the motions of objects far beyond our planet.

### Q22: What makes Earth "just right" for life, as described in the chapter's preview of the final chapter?
Earth lies at the **perfect distance from the Sun**, allowing water to remain liquid, and it has an **atmosphere** that provides the oxygen we breathe while shielding us from harmful ultraviolet rays. These conditions together make Earth suitable for supporting life.

### Q23: According to the chapter, what role do humans play in Earth's climate challenges, and what is needed to address them?
Humans are **at the heart of both the problem and any possible solution** regarding Earth's climate — human activities can cause small changes in Earth's temperature, disrupting climate patterns with dangerous consequences. However, humans are also the ones who can, and must, use **science** — through the same principles of observing, measuring, and experimenting used throughout the book — to understand these changes and guide their actions to protect the delicate balance on which life depends.

### Q24: "Investigation in science works best only when we balance the solid ground of careful observation with the freedom of creative thinking." Explain this statement with reference to the root and kite symbolism used in the chapter.
This statement captures the central idea of the chapter through the imagery of the **root** and the **kite**. The root, appearing at the bottom of left-hand pages, represents the **solid, careful observation** and the deep foundation of knowledge connecting us to our environment and heritage — without this grounding, ideas would have no basis in reality. The kite, appearing at the top of right-hand pages, represents **curiosity taking flight** — the creative thinking needed to ask new questions and imagine possibilities beyond what is immediately observed. Neither alone is sufficient for good investigation: careful observation without imagination limits discovery, while creative thinking without grounded observation is not truly scientific. True scientific investigation, as demonstrated in the puri-frying example, requires **both** — carefully controlling and observing variables (the root) while remaining curious enough to ask new questions after each round of experiments (the kite).

### Q25: Using the puri-frying investigation as a model, describe the complete process a young scientist would follow to systematically investigate an everyday phenomenon.
A young scientist investigating an everyday phenomenon, such as why a puri puffs up unevenly, would follow these steps:
- **Start with curiosity and careful observation** of the phenomenon — noticing, for instance, that a puri puffs up like a balloon and that one side is thinner than the other.
- **Ask a focused scientific question**, such as: What are the different things that may change the way a puri puffs up when fried?
- **Identify variables to control**, such as the thickness and size of the rolled dough, the type of flour used, the temperature of the oil, and the way the dough is dropped into the oil.
- **Identify variables to observe or measure**, such as whether the puri puffs up (yes/no) or the time it takes to puff up (in seconds).
- **Change only one variable at a time** while keeping all other conditions the same, so that the effect of that single variable can be clearly identified — for example, using dough circles of the same thickness and dropping them the same way when testing different oil temperatures.
- **Keep detailed notes** of everything observed and sensed during the experiment, such as whether the oil splattered, smelled, or smoked.
- **Use the results to generate new questions**, such as whether fresh or stored dough puffs better, or what happens if a hole is pricked in the puri before frying.

This entire process is called **systematic investigation**, and it is exactly how all scientific experiments — from the simplest to the most complex — are conducted.

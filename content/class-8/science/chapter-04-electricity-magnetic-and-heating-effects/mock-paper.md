## Answer Key

1. When electric current flows through a conductor (like a wire), it produces a **magnetic field** around it. This phenomenon is known as the **magnetic effect of electric current**.

2. An **electromagnet** is a current-carrying coil that behaves as a magnet. For practical applications, most electromagnets have an iron core to make them stronger.

3. **Hans Christian Oersted** discovered the link between electricity and magnetism in the year **1820**.

4. When an electric current passes through a conductor, it faces resistance to its flow, causing some of the electrical energy to be converted into heat energy, warming the conductor. This is known as the **heating effect of electric current**.

5. A dry cell is called "dry" because the electrolyte inside it is **not a liquid but a thick moist paste**, unlike a Voltaic cell which uses a liquid electrolyte.

6. When a Voltaic cell becomes 'dead', the **chemicals in it have been used up** through the chemical reaction, and the cell can **no longer supply any electricity**.

7. The strength of an electromagnet can be increased by:
   - **Increasing the electric current** flowing through the coil (e.g., by using more cells in the battery)
   - **Increasing the number of turns** of the coil

8. In a **Voltaic cell**, the electrodes are two metal rods of **different materials** dipped in a **liquid electrolyte** (usually a weak acid or salt solution). In a **dry cell**, the electrodes are a **zinc container** (negative terminal) and a **carbon rod covered with a metal cap** (positive terminal), and the electrolyte is a **thick moist paste** surrounding the carbon rod, rather than a liquid.

9. A **nichrome wire offers higher resistance** to the flow of current compared to a copper wire of the same size and length. Since resistance causes electrical energy to be converted into heat, nichrome wire heats up more effectively than copper wire for the same current, making it well-suited as a **heating element** in appliances like electric irons, where generating heat is the purpose of the device.

10. Any two of the following:
    - Using **wires, plugs, and sockets that are rated** for the specified electric current of the connections
    - Including **safety devices** in household circuits to minimise incidents of overheating
    - Avoiding prolonged or unnecessary use of high-current appliances that could overheat wiring

11. Any four of: **electric room heater, electric stove, electric kettle, electric iron, water heating immersion rod, hair dryer**. The common part present in all of them is a rod or coil of wire called a **heating element**.

12. **Activity:** A magnetic compass is placed beneath a wire that is connected to an electric cell through a switch (the wire is fixed between two nails on a cardboard, positioned slightly above the compass).

    **Diagram description:** The circuit consists of a cell connected via connecting wires and a switch to a straight wire stretched between two nails; a magnetic compass is placed directly beneath this wire, with its needle initially aligned in the North-South direction.

    **Procedure and Observation:** While watching the compass needle, the switch is moved to the 'ON' position, allowing current to flow through the wire. It is observed that the **compass needle deflects** from its original direction. When the switch is moved to the 'OFF' position, stopping the current, the **needle returns to its original direction**. This is repeated a few times, with the same result each time.

    **Conclusion:** Since the compass needle (a tiny magnet) deflects only in the presence of a magnetic field, and it deflects specifically when current flows through the wire, this demonstrates that a **current-carrying wire produces a magnetic field** around it — known as the magnetic effect of electric current. The magnetic field disappears once the current stops flowing, which is why the needle returns to its original position when the switch is turned OFF.

13. (a) When current flows through the coil alone (without the iron nail), it produces a magnetic field, but the effect is relatively weak, causing only a small deflection in a nearby compass needle. When an **iron nail is inserted into the core of the coil**, the coil becomes a **much stronger magnet** — the deflection of the compass needle increases significantly, and the electromagnet can now attract iron paper clips, which it may not have been able to do with just the coil. This is because the iron core itself gets magnetised and adds to the overall magnetic effect, making iron-core electromagnets far more effective for practical use, such as in lifting electromagnets.

    (b) Increasing the number of cells from 1 to 2 increases the **current** flowing through the coil, since a battery with more cells provides a larger current than a single cell. Since the strength of the magnetic field produced by the electromagnet depends on the magnitude of the current flowing through the coil, this results in a **stronger magnetic field**. This would be observed as a **greater deflection** of a nearby compass needle and the ability of the electromagnet to attract a **larger number of iron paper clips** compared to when it was connected to a single cell.

    (c) The poles of the electromagnet can be reversed by **reversing the direction of the current** flowing through the coil — for example, by reversing the connections of the coil to the battery terminals. This would cause the end of the coil that was previously the north pole to become the south pole, and vice versa.

# Electricity: Magnetic and Heating Effects

Have you ever seen a crane in a scrapyard pick up an entire pile of iron scraps without any hooks or ropes — just a flat magnetic disc? That's not a permanent magnet. It's electricity doing something you might not expect: acting like a magnet. Let's find out how.

## 4.1 Does an Electric Current Have a Magnetic Effect?

Here's the experiment that changes everything: place a magnetic compass beneath a wire connected to a cell (with a switch in between). When the switch is **OFF**, the compass needle points normally. The moment you flip the switch **ON**, the needle suddenly **deflects** away from its original direction. Turn it OFF again, and the needle swings back.

What's going on? The compass needle is itself a tiny magnet, and it only deflects when another magnetic influence is nearby. Since nothing physically changed except the flow of current, the conclusion is unavoidable:

**When electric current flows through a conductor (like a wire), it produces a magnetic field around it.** This is called the **magnetic effect of electric current**. The magnetic field disappears the moment the current stops.

The region around a magnet or a current-carrying wire where its magnetic effect can be felt (for example, by deflecting a compass needle) is called a **magnetic field**.

> **Exam trap:** Don't say "the wire becomes a magnet forever." The magnetic field exists **only while current flows** — switch off the current, and the magnetic effect vanishes immediately.

### Be a Scientist: Hans Christian Oersted

This discovery — that electricity and magnetism are linked — was first made by **Hans Christian Oersted** in **1820**. While giving a demonstration, he noticed that a nearby compass needle deflected whenever an electric circuit was opened or closed. He investigated further, confirmed that electric current produces a magnetic field, and published his findings — a discovery that other scientists then verified and built upon.

### 4.1.1 Electromagnets

Here's where it gets genuinely useful. Wrap an insulated wire tightly around an iron nail to form a coil, then connect the ends to a cell. Bring the nail near iron paper clips — they **cling to it**. Disconnect the cell, and the clips **fall off**.

What's happening: the current-carrying coil is behaving exactly like a magnet, but only while current flows. **A current-carrying coil that behaves as a magnet is called an electromagnet.**

Digging deeper with a cylindrical coil (wire wound around a paper tube):
- When current passes through the coil alone, it deflects a magnetic compass — a **weak** effect.
- Insert an **iron nail** into the core of the coil, and the deflection becomes **much stronger** — the coil now attracts iron clips too.
- **For practical applications, most electromagnets have an iron core to make them stronger.**

**Does an electromagnet have poles like a bar magnet?**

Yes! Just like a magnet, an electromagnet has two poles — **North and South**. You can find out which end is which using the same rule you already know: **unlike poles attract**. If the north pole of a compass is attracted toward one end of the electromagnet, that end must be the **south pole**.

### Worked Example — Factors Affecting Electromagnet Strength

The chapter describes an investigation comparing different setups. Here's how to reason through it, step by step:

**Setup 1: Same coil, different number of cells (1 cell vs 2 cells vs 4 cells)**
- A single cell gives a small current → weak magnetic field → less compass deflection → few clips attracted.
- More cells (e.g., 2 or 4) give a **larger current** → **stronger magnetic field** → more compass deflection → more clips attracted.

**Setup 2: Same number of cells, different number of turns of coil**
- More turns of wire in the coil → stronger electromagnet (even with the same current).

**Setup 3: Reversing the direction of current**
- Reversing the direction of current flow **reverses the poles** of the electromagnet (what was North becomes South, and vice versa).

**Conclusion:** The strength of an electromagnet can be increased by (a) increasing the current flowing through the coil, or (b) increasing the number of turns of the coil, or both. Its poles can be reversed by reversing the direction of current.

> **Exam trap:** Students often forget that BOTH current AND number of turns affect strength — an exam question might test only one of these, so read carefully whether the question is changing the cells or changing the turns.

### 4.1.2 Lifting Electromagnets

**Lifting electromagnets** are strong electromagnets hung from cranes. Here's the clever part — the crane operator has full control:
- Current **ON** → electromagnet lifts iron/steel objects
- Current **OFF** → magnetic field disappears → objects are released

These are widely used in **factories and scrap yards** to move, lift, and sort heavy metal items efficiently — no ropes or hooks needed.

### A Step Further: Earth as a Giant Magnet

Remember from Grade 6 that a freely suspended magnet always rests along the north-south direction because Earth itself behaves like a giant magnet? Here's why: deep inside the Earth, the movement of liquid iron in the core creates electric currents, which generate a magnetic field. This field helps migratory birds, fish, and animals navigate, and also acts as a **shield blocking harmful particles from space**, helping protect life on Earth.

## 4.2 Does a Current-Carrying Wire Get Hot?

Have you ever noticed a phone charger cable feels slightly warm after use? That's the same principle at work here.

Using a special **nichrome wire** connected to a cell through a switch: touch the wire when the switch is OFF — it feels normal. Turn the switch ON for about 30 seconds, then OFF, and touch the wire again (briefly, and only under supervision) — it feels noticeably **warm**.

**Why does this happen?** When electric current flows through any conductor, it faces some **opposition or resistance** to its flow. Different conductors offer different levels of resistance — for example, **nichrome wire offers higher resistance than a copper wire of the same size and length**. This resistance converts some electrical energy into **heat energy**.

**This warming is known as the heating effect of electric current.**

> **Exam trap:** Don't assume all wires heat up equally. The amount of heat generated depends on: (1) the **material** of the wire, (2) its **thickness**, (3) its **length**, (4) the **magnitude of current**, and (5) the **duration** for which current flows. A common exam scenario compares 1 cell vs 2 cells — more cells means more current means **more heat generated** for the same duration.

### Household Appliances Using the Heating Effect

Many everyday devices work on this exact principle. Each contains a rod or coil of wire called a **heating element**, which can sometimes be seen glowing red hot:

| Appliance | Uses heating effect for |
|---|---|
| Electric room heater | Space heating |
| Electric stove | Cooking |
| Electric kettle | Boiling water |
| Electric iron | Pressing clothes |
| Water heating immersion rod | Heating water |
| Hair dryer | Drying hair |

Even the **incandescent lamp** you studied in Grade 7 glows because its filament is heated by electric current — same principle.

### When the Heating Effect Causes Problems

The heating effect is useful, but it can also cause trouble:
- **Energy loss** in wires during transmission
- **Overheating** in appliances may melt plastic parts of plugs and sockets, or even cause **fires**

This is why household circuits include **safety devices** to minimise such incidents, and why it's important to use wires, plugs, and sockets rated for the specified current.

### Industrial Use: Steel Furnaces

Beyond the home, the heating effect has serious industrial applications — for example, specially designed **high-temperature furnaces** in steel manufacturing use electric current to produce heat, melting and recycling scrap steel into usable steel.

## 4.3 How Does a Battery Generate Electricity?

### 4.3.1 Voltaic Cell

The **Voltaic cell** (also called a **Galvanic cell**) is one of the earliest electric cells ever made. Its structure:

- Two metal rods of **different materials**, called **electrodes**, partly dipped into a liquid called an **electrolyte** (usually a weak acid or salt solution), inside a glass or plastic container.
- A **chemical reaction** between the electrodes and the electrolyte produces electricity.
- When connected in a circuit, current flows from the **positive terminal**, through the circuit, to the **negative terminal**.
- Over time, the chemicals get used up, and the cell becomes **'dead'** — it can no longer supply electricity.

> **Exam trap:** "Dead" doesn't mean the cell is empty or harmless — it just means the chemical reaction can no longer produce useful current. (You'll see later that dead batteries can still be hazardous.)

### Ever Heard Of: Volta and Galvani

The names "Voltaic" and "Galvanic" cell come from two Italian scientists. **Luigi Galvani** noticed in the late 1700s that a dead frog's leg kicked when touched with two different metals (copper and iron), and believed the electricity came from the frog itself. **Alessandro Volta** disagreed — he believed the electricity came from the **combination of metals and liquid**, not the frog. To test this, he replaced the frog's leg with saltwater-soaked paper and still got an electric current, proving his idea and leading to the **invention of the first battery**.

### Worked Example — The Lemon Cell

This is the chapter's practical demonstration of a Voltaic cell using easily available materials:

**Setup:**
- Insert a **copper wire** and an **iron nail** into a lemon, keeping them apart by a small distance.
- Repeat for 5-6 lemons, then join the copper wires and nails of adjacent lemons together in a chain.
- Connect an **LED** between the copper wire of the first lemon and the iron nail of the last lemon.

**Observation:** If connected correctly, the LED glows — confirming the cell is generating electricity. (If it doesn't glow, reverse the LED's connections — recall that current only passes through an LED when its **positive terminal, the longer wire,** connects to the battery's positive terminal.)

**What's what in this cell:**
- **Electrodes:** the copper wires and iron nails
- **Electrolyte:** the lemon juice (which conducts electricity) — salt solutions can also be used instead

> A step further: common metal pairs for Voltaic cells include zinc/copper, zinc/silver, aluminium/copper, iron/copper, magnesium/copper, and lead/copper. Some metals (like copper) act as **positive electrodes**, while others (like zinc) act as **negative electrodes**, due to their chemical properties.

### 4.3.2 Dry Cells

Voltaic cells are an important discovery but aren't convenient for everyday use. **Dry cells** are the most widely used electric cells today. They're called "dry" because the **electrolyte is not a liquid but a thick moist paste**.

Structure of a dry cell:
- **Zinc container** — acts as the **negative terminal**
- **Carbon rod** at the centre, covered with a metal cap — acts as the **positive terminal**
- The carbon rod is surrounded by the paste-like electrolyte

**A dry cell is a single-use cell** — once used up, it must be disposed of.

### 4.3.3 Rechargeable Batteries

**Rechargeable batteries** can be recharged and reused multiple times, preventing wastage and saving money over time. They're used across a huge range of devices — watches, phones, laptops, tablets, inverters, and electric vehicles.

However, rechargeable batteries **do not last forever** — after being charged and used many times, they slowly wear out (this is why an older phone's battery needs charging more often than a new one).

> **A step further:** The **lithium-ion (Li-ion) battery** is today's most common type of rechargeable battery, relying on metals like lithium and cobalt that are mined and processed in limited parts of the world. Scientists are now developing **solid-state batteries**, which replace liquid/paste electrolytes with solid materials — these would be safer, charge faster, and last longer.

> **A step further — battery disposal:** Even a "dead" battery isn't harmless — it may still contain acids and metals like lead, cadmium, nickel, or lithium, which can cause fires or environmental harm if thrown in regular garbage. Many of these materials can be recycled through special **e-waste facilities**.

## Quick Recap

- **Magnetic effect of electric current:** current flowing through a conductor produces a **magnetic field** around it; this was discovered by **Hans Christian Oersted** in 1820. The field disappears when current stops.
- A **magnetic field** is the region around a magnet or current-carrying wire where its magnetic effect can be felt.
- An **electromagnet** is a current-carrying coil that behaves as a magnet; adding an **iron core** makes it stronger.
- Electromagnets have **two poles (North and South)**, just like bar magnets; unlike poles attract.
- Electromagnet strength increases with **more current** (more cells) or **more turns** of the coil; reversing current direction **reverses the poles**.
- **Lifting electromagnets** are used in cranes/scrapyards — current ON lifts objects, current OFF releases them.
- **Heating effect of electric current:** current flowing through a conductor faces **resistance**, converting some electrical energy into heat; **nichrome** has higher resistance than copper of the same size.
- Heat generated depends on the wire's **material, thickness, length**, the **current**, and the **duration** of flow.
- Household appliances using this effect: room heaters, stoves, kettles, irons, immersion rods, hair dryers — all contain a **heating element**.
- Overheating can cause **energy loss, melted plugs/sockets, or fires** — hence the need for safety devices and correctly rated wires/plugs.
- A **Voltaic (Galvanic) cell** generates electricity via a chemical reaction between two different metal **electrodes** dipped in an **electrolyte**; discovered through the work of **Galvani and Volta**.
- A **lemon cell** demonstrates this principle practically, using copper wire and iron nail electrodes with lemon juice as electrolyte.
- **Dry cells** use a paste-like electrolyte (zinc container = negative terminal, carbon rod = positive terminal) and are single-use.
- **Rechargeable batteries** can be reused multiple times but eventually wear out; **Li-ion batteries** are the most common type today, with **solid-state batteries** as a promising future technology.
- Dead batteries should be disposed of via **e-waste recycling facilities**, not regular garbage.

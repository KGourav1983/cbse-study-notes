### Q1: What is the magnetic effect of electric current?
When electric current flows through a conductor (like a wire), it produces a **magnetic field** around it. This phenomenon is known as the **magnetic effect of electric current**. The magnetic field disappears when the current stops flowing.

### Q2: Define a magnetic field.
A **magnetic field** is the region around a magnet or a current-carrying wire where its magnetic effect can be felt, such as by the deflection of a compass needle.

### Q3: What is an electromagnet?
A **current-carrying coil that behaves as a magnet** is called an **electromagnet**. For practical applications, most electromagnets have an iron core to make them stronger.

### Q4: Define the heating effect of electric current.
When an electric current passes through a conductor, the conductor faces resistance to the flow of current, and some of the electrical energy is converted into heat energy, causing the conductor to get heated. This is known as the **heating effect of electric current**.

### Q5: What is an electrolyte?
An **electrolyte** is a liquid (usually a weak acid or salt solution) or paste that helps conduct electricity between the electrodes of a cell, enabling the chemical reaction that generates electric current.

### Q6: Who discovered the link between electricity and magnetism, and in which year?
**Hans Christian Oersted**, a professor in Denmark, discovered the link between electricity and magnetism in **1820**, after noticing that a nearby compass needle deflected whenever an electric circuit was closed or opened.

### Q7: What is a Voltaic cell (Galvanic cell)?
A **Voltaic cell**, also known as a **Galvanic cell**, consists of two metal rods (**electrodes**) made of different materials, partly dipped in a liquid called an **electrolyte**, placed in a glass or plastic container. A chemical reaction between the electrodes and the electrolyte produces electricity.

### Q8: Why are dry cells called "dry"?
Dry cells are called "dry" because the electrolyte inside them is **not a liquid but a thick moist paste**, unlike the liquid electrolyte used in a Voltaic cell.

### Q9: State two poles present in an electromagnet, and how their polarity can be identified.
Just like a bar magnet, an electromagnet has two poles: **North and South**. The polarity of an end can be identified using a magnetic compass — since **unlike poles attract**, if the north pole of the compass is attracted towards a particular end, that end is the **south pole** of the electromagnet.

### Q10: Name two rechargeable batteries used in everyday devices, and state their key advantage over dry cells.
Examples of rechargeable batteries include those used in **laptops and mobile phones**. Their key advantage is that they **can be recharged and reused multiple times**, which prevents wastage and saves money over time, unlike dry cells which are single-use and must be disposed of after use.

### Q11: Differentiate between a dry cell and a Voltaic cell based on their electrolyte and structure.
A **Voltaic cell** uses a **liquid** electrolyte (usually a weak acid or salt solution) with two metal electrodes dipped in it inside a container. A **dry cell** uses a **thick moist paste** as the electrolyte, with a **zinc container** acting as the negative terminal and a **carbon rod** covered with a metal cap acting as the positive terminal.

### Q12: Why does a nichrome wire get warmer than a copper wire of the same size and length when current flows through both?
A nichrome wire offers **higher resistance** to the flow of current compared to a copper wire of the same size and length. This higher resistance causes **more electrical energy to be converted into heat energy**, making the nichrome wire heat up more than the copper wire for the same current.

### Q13: List four factors on which the heat generated in a current-carrying wire depends.
The heat generated in a wire depends on:
- The **material** of the wire
- The **thickness** of the wire
- The **length** of the wire
- The **duration** for which the current flows (and the magnitude of the current)

### Q14: Explain, with reference to the activity using a compass and electric circuit, how the discovery of the magnetic effect of current was made.
When a wire carrying current is placed near a magnetic compass, and the switch is turned ON allowing current to flow, the **compass needle deflects** from its original direction. When the switch is turned OFF and current stops, the needle **returns to its original direction**. This deflection shows that the current-carrying wire has a magnetic effect on the compass needle, demonstrating that **electric current produces a magnetic field**.

### Q15: Explain why inserting an iron nail into a current-carrying coil increases its magnetic strength.
When current passes through a plain cylindrical coil, it behaves as a weak magnet, causing only a small deflection in a nearby compass needle. When an **iron nail is inserted into the core of the coil**, the coil becomes a **much stronger magnet** — the compass needle deflection increases significantly, and the coil can now attract iron paper clips. This is why most practical electromagnets are built with an iron core.

### Q16: State two ways in which the strength of an electromagnet can be increased.
The strength of an electromagnet can be increased by:
- **Increasing the electric current** flowing through the coil (e.g., using more cells)
- **Increasing the number of turns** of the coil

### Q17: How can the polarity (poles) of an electromagnet be reversed?
The polarity of an electromagnet can be reversed by **reversing the direction of the current** flowing through the coil — what was the north pole becomes the south pole, and vice versa.

### Q18: Describe how a lifting electromagnet is used in factories and scrap yards.
A **lifting electromagnet** is a strong electromagnet hung from a crane. The crane operator controls it by switching the current **ON and OFF**. When the current is ON, the electromagnet lifts iron/steel objects; when the current is switched OFF, the magnetic field disappears and the objects are released. This allows heavy metal items to be moved, lifted, and sorted efficiently in factories and scrap yards.

### Q19: Explain, with the help of the lemon cell activity, how a simple electric cell can be constructed using easily available materials.
A copper wire and an iron nail are inserted into a lemon, kept apart by a small distance, acting as the two **electrodes**; the lemon juice acts as the **electrolyte**. This is repeated for several lemons, and the copper wire and nail of adjacent lemons are joined together to form a chain. When an LED is connected between the copper wire of the first lemon and the iron nail of the last lemon (with correct polarity), the **LED glows**, confirming that the chemical reaction between the electrodes and the lemon juice generates electric current — just like in a Voltaic cell.

### Q20: A student sets up two identical electromagnet coils, one connected to a single cell and the other to a battery of two cells. Explain, with reason, which electromagnet will be stronger.
The electromagnet connected to the **battery of two cells** will be stronger. This is because a single cell provides only a small amount of current, resulting in a weaker magnetic field and less deflection of a compass needle, while a battery with more cells provides a **larger current**, creating a **stronger magnetic field** — resulting in greater compass deflection and the ability to attract more iron paper clips.

### Q21: Explain why household appliances like electric kettles and irons often have a visible glowing coil, while the heating effect is also responsible for problems like melted plugs. How can such problems be minimised?
Appliances like electric kettles and irons contain a **heating element** — a rod or coil of wire — that gets heated due to the **heating effect of electric current** when current passes through it; in some appliances, this element can be seen glowing red hot. However, if wires, plugs, or sockets are not rated for the current flowing through them, excessive heating can cause **plastic parts to melt or even lead to fires**. Such problems can be minimised by using **wires, plugs, and sockets rated for the specified electric current** of the connections, and by including **safety devices** in household circuits.

### Q22: Compare the working principle of an electric bell/electromagnet-based device and an electric iron, explaining which effect of electric current each is based on.
An electromagnet-based device, such as a lifting electromagnet or electric bell, works on the **magnetic effect of electric current** — the current-carrying coil produces a magnetic field that can attract iron/steel objects. An **electric iron**, on the other hand, works on the **heating effect of electric current** — current passing through a heating element inside the iron faces resistance, generating heat that is used to press clothes. Both are applications of electric current but rely on two different effects.

### Q23: Describe how a Voltaic cell generates electricity, and explain why it eventually stops working.
A Voltaic cell consists of two metal electrodes made of different materials, partly dipped in an electrolyte (a weak acid or salt solution). A **chemical reaction** takes place between the electrodes and the electrolyte, which produces electricity; when the circuit is connected, current flows from the positive terminal through the circuit to the negative terminal. Over time, the **chemicals in the electrolyte get used up** through this reaction, and the cell can no longer sustain the chemical reaction needed to produce current. At this point, the cell is said to be **'dead'** and cannot supply any more electricity.

### Q24: Explain why it is important to recycle rechargeable batteries and dry cells rather than throwing them in regular garbage.
Even when a battery stops working, it is not completely inactive — it may still contain **acids and metals like lead, cadmium, nickel, or lithium**, which can cause fires or be harmful to the environment if thrown in regular garbage. Additionally, many of the materials used in these batteries are **valuable and can be recycled and reused**. Special **e-waste recycling facilities** exist for the safe disposal of used batteries, and recycling them is beneficial for both the environment and people.

### Q25: A student made an electromagnet using a coil with 50 turns and a single cell, and observed a certain deflection in a nearby compass needle. She then repeated the experiment using the same coil (50 turns) but with a battery of 4 cells, and separately, with the same single cell but a coil of 100 turns. Explain what she would observe in each case, and state the general principle these observations demonstrate.
With the **battery of 4 cells** (same 50-turn coil), the current flowing through the coil would be **larger** than with a single cell, producing a **stronger magnetic field**, resulting in a **greater deflection** of the compass needle compared to the single-cell setup. With the **100-turn coil** (same single cell), even though the current remains the same as before, the **increased number of turns** would also make the coil a **stronger magnet**, again producing a greater compass deflection than the original 50-turn, single-cell setup. This demonstrates the general principle that **the strength of an electromagnet can be increased either by increasing the current flowing through the coil or by increasing the number of turns of the coil, or both**.

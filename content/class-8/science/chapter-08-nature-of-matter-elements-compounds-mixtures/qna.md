### Q1: What is a mixture? What are its components?
A **mixture** is formed when two or more substances are mixed together such that each substance retains its own properties. The individual substances that make up a mixture are called its **components**, and they do not react chemically with each other.

### Q2: Define a pure substance.
A **pure substance** is a kind of matter that cannot be separated into other kinds of matter by any physical process; it consists of the same type of particles throughout.

### Q3: What is an element?
An **element** is a pure substance made up of identical particles called **atoms**, which cannot be further broken down into simpler substances. Elements are the building blocks of all matter (e.g., hydrogen, oxygen, gold, sulfur).

### Q4: What is a compound?
A **compound** is formed when different elements combine chemically in a **fixed ratio** to form a new substance with properties completely different from its constituent elements. The elements of a compound cannot be separated by any physical method.

### Q5: What are alloys? Give two examples.
**Alloys** are uniform mixtures of two or more metals (or a metal and a small amount of another element) mixed so uniformly that individual substances cannot be seen. Examples: **brass** (copper + zinc) and **bronze** (copper + tin).

### Q6: Differentiate between uniform and non-uniform mixtures.
- **Uniform mixtures**: components are evenly distributed and cannot be distinguished, even with a microscope (e.g., sugar dissolved in water).
- **Non-uniform mixtures**: components are generally visible with the naked eye or a magnifying device (e.g., a sprout salad with visible green gram, chickpeas, onion, and tomato).

### Q7: What are minerals? What is a native mineral?
**Minerals** are the substances that make up most rocks and can be viewed with the eyes, a magnifying glass, or a microscope. A **native mineral** is a mineral that exists as a pure element (not a compound), such as gold, silver, copper (metals) or sulfur, carbon (non-metals).

### Q8: State whether the following are true or false, and correct the false ones: "Assertion: Air is a mixture. Reason: A mixture is formed when two or more substances are mixed, without undergoing any chemical change."
Both the **Assertion and the Reason are true**, and the Reason correctly explains the Assertion. Air is indeed a **uniform mixture** mainly of nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapour, and a mixture (as defined) is formed without any chemical change occurring between its components.

### Q9: Classify the following as elements, compounds, or mixtures: carbon dioxide, sand, seawater, magnesium oxide, aluminium, gold, oxygen, iron sulfide, air, sodium chloride, sulfur, hydrogen.
- **Elements**: aluminium, gold, oxygen, sulfur, hydrogen
- **Compounds**: carbon dioxide, magnesium oxide, iron sulfide, sodium chloride
- **Mixtures**: sand, seawater, air

### Q10: Is it possible for a substance to be classified as both an element and a compound? Explain why or why not.
No, a substance cannot be classified as both. An **element** is made of only one type of atom and cannot be broken down further by chemical means, whereas a **compound** is made of two or more different elements combined chemically in a fixed ratio. Since these definitions are mutually exclusive — a substance either consists of a single type of atom (element) or of multiple elements chemically combined (compound) — no single substance can fit both categories at once.

### Q11: Water, a compound, has different properties compared to those of the elements oxygen and hydrogen from which it is formed. Justify this statement.
This statement is correct because compounds always have properties completely different from their constituent elements. **Hydrogen is a flammable fuel**, and **oxygen supports combustion** — yet water, formed by their chemical combination in a fixed 2:1 ratio, **extinguishes fire** rather than burning or supporting burning. This shows that the compound water has entirely new properties, distinct from both hydrogen and oxygen.

### Q12: Explain, with reference to Activity 8.3, how it can be shown that water is made up of hydrogen and oxygen.
When electricity is passed through water (containing a few drops of dilute sulfuric acid) using a 9 V battery, gas bubbles collect at both terminals inside two water-filled test tubes. On testing these gases with a burning candle:
- One test tube produces a **pop sound**, confirming the presence of **hydrogen gas**.
- In the other test tube, the candle's flame **glows brighter**, confirming the presence of **oxygen gas**.
Since these gases are not water vapour (they don't condense back into water), this proves that water is composed of two different elements — hydrogen and oxygen — in a fixed ratio, represented as: **Water → Hydrogen + Oxygen**.

### Q13: Sodium (a soft metal) and chlorine (a hazardous gas) combine to form sodium chloride, a substance essential and safe for daily use. Explain what this tells us about compounds.
This example illustrates that a **compound's properties are completely different** from those of its constituent elements. Sodium is a reactive, soft metal, and chlorine is a hazardous, poisonous gas — yet when they combine chemically in a fixed **1:1 ratio**, they form **sodium chloride** (common salt), a harmless, taste-enhancing substance essential for life. This shows that compounds are entirely new substances, not simply a "sum" of the properties of their elements.

### Q14: Describe Activity 8.4 (heating sugar) and explain how it demonstrates that sugar is a compound, not an element.
When sugar is heated gently in a boiling tube, it first turns brown, then chars to black. Water droplets are observed near the mouth of the tube — since the tube is being heated (not cooled), this water must have come from the dry sugar itself, not from condensation of air moisture. What is left behind in the tube is **charcoal (carbon)**. Since sugar decomposes on heating to give **carbon and water**, and water itself is made of hydrogen and oxygen, sugar cannot be a single element — it must be a **compound** made of the elements **carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen**.

### Q15: Describe Activity 8.5 (iron filings and sulfur powder). How do Sample A and Sample B differ, and what does this experiment demonstrate?
Iron filings and sulfur powder are mixed to form **Sample A**, then half of Sample A is heated and ground to form **Sample B**.
- **Sample A** (mixture): shows visible black and yellow particles; iron is attracted to a magnet (components separable); reacting with dilute HCl produces iron chloride and colourless, odourless **hydrogen gas** that burns with a 'pop' sound, while sulfur remains unreacted.
- **Sample B** (compound, iron sulfide): shows a uniform black colour and texture throughout; is **not** attracted by a magnet (components inseparable); reacting with dilute HCl produces iron chloride and colourless **hydrogen sulfide gas** with a rotten-egg odour.
This demonstrates the fundamental difference between a mixture (components retain their individual properties and can be physically separated) and a compound (a completely new substance forms with different properties, and its elements cannot be separated by physical means).

### Q16: Why does sulfur not react with dilute hydrochloric acid in Sample A, but iron sulfide (Sample B) does react to give a different gas?
In **Sample A**, sulfur exists as a separate, unreacted component of the mixture — its individual properties are retained, and since sulfur itself does not react with dilute hydrochloric acid, it is left behind as a yellow solid while only the iron reacts to release hydrogen gas. In **Sample B**, however, iron and sulfur have chemically combined to form the compound **iron sulfide**, a completely new substance with its own distinct chemical properties. Iron sulfide reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid differently than iron alone, producing iron chloride and **hydrogen sulfide gas** (rotten-egg smell) instead of plain hydrogen gas.

### Q17: In which of the following cases are all the examples correctly matched? Give reasons. (i) Elements — water, nitrogen, iron, air. (ii) Uniform mixtures — minerals, seawater, bronze, air. (iii) Pure substances — carbon dioxide, iron, oxygen, sugar. (iv) Non-uniform mixtures — air, sand, brass, muddy water.
The correct match is **(iii) Pure substances — carbon dioxide, iron, oxygen, sugar**, because all four are pure substances: iron and oxygen are **elements**, while carbon dioxide and sugar are **compounds**.
- (i) is incorrect because water and air are not elements — water is a compound, and air is a mixture.
- (ii) is incorrect because bronze is a uniform mixture (alloy) and seawater and air are uniform mixtures too, but minerals are mostly compounds, not mixtures.
- (iv) is incorrect because brass is a uniform mixture (alloy), not a non-uniform one, though air, sand, and muddy water are non-uniform mixtures.

### Q18: Consider the reaction A + B → C, where A and B cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical reactions. Which statement correctly describes A, B, and C? (i) A, B, and C are all compounds and only C has a fixed composition. (ii) C is a compound, and A and B have a fixed composition. (iii) A and B are compounds, and C has a fixed composition. (iv) A and B are elements, C is a compound, and has a fixed composition.
The correct answer is **(iv) A and B are elements, C is a compound, and has a fixed composition**. Since A and B cannot be broken down into simpler substances, they must be **elements** by definition. When two elements combine to form a new substance C, that new substance is a **compound**, and compounds are always formed in a **fixed ratio/composition** by their constituent elements.

### Q19: Iron reacts with moist air to form iron oxide, and magnesium burns in oxygen to form magnesium oxide. Classify all the substances involved as elements, compounds, or mixtures, with justification.
- **Iron**: an **element**, as it is made of identical iron atoms and cannot be broken down into simpler substances.
- **Moist air**: a **mixture**, since it consists of multiple gases (nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide) and water vapour, each retaining its own properties.
- **Iron oxide**: a **compound**, formed when the element iron chemically combines with oxygen (from moist air) in a fixed ratio, producing a new substance with different properties.
- **Magnesium**: an **element**, as it is made of identical magnesium atoms.
- **Oxygen**: an **element**, made of identical oxygen atoms/molecules.
- **Magnesium oxide**: a **compound**, formed by the chemical combination of the elements magnesium and oxygen in a fixed ratio.

### Q20: How would our daily lives be changed if water were not a compound but a mixture of hydrogen and oxygen?
If water were a mixture rather than a compound, hydrogen and oxygen would retain their own individual properties instead of forming a new substance with different properties. Since **hydrogen is a highly flammable fuel** and **oxygen supports combustion**, such a "mixture" would likely be highly flammable and dangerous instead of being able to extinguish fire, as real water does. It would also mean hydrogen and oxygen could be separated from this "water" easily through physical processes (like those used for ordinary mixtures), rather than needing a chemical process like electrolysis — fundamentally changing water's usefulness for drinking, firefighting, and countless other daily purposes.

### Q21: Read the labels on items like detergents or snacks, and try to list the mixtures and compounds they contain. Based on the chapter, explain how you would go about classifying an ingredient as a mixture or a compound.
To classify an ingredient, first check whether it is described as containing more than one distinct substance that retains individual properties (like a blend of oils, minerals, and flavourings in a snack) — such a combination would be a **mixture**. If the label lists a single named substance with a specific formula-like composition and consistent properties throughout (such as sodium chloride or a specific chemical name), it is more likely a **compound**, since compounds are pure substances formed from elements combined in a fixed ratio, and cannot be separated by physical means. (Since exact packaged snack/detergent labels are not part of this chapter's fixed examples, students should apply this reasoning framework — components separable/retaining properties → mixture; fixed composition, new properties, physically inseparable → compound.)

### Q22: Analyse a setup where iron filings react with dilute hydrochloric acid in a test tube, producing a gas that is collected. Identify the gas produced and write the word equation of the chemical reaction.
The gas produced is **hydrogen gas**, which is colourless, has no smell, and burns with a characteristic 'pop' sound when tested with a burning splinter or candle. The word equation for the reaction is:
**Iron + Dilute Hydrochloric acid → Iron chloride + Hydrogen gas**

Two examples of compounds made only from non-metals, based on this chapter, are:

### Q24: How can gold be classified as both a mineral and a metal? Explain with reference to native minerals.
Gold can be classified as both because it is a **native mineral** — a mineral that exists in its pure elemental form in rocks, without being combined with other elements to form a compound. At the same time, gold is also classified as a **metal**, one of the two broad categories (metals and non-metals) into which elements are divided. Since gold is a pure element found naturally in rock as a mineral, and it also possesses the characteristic properties of a metal, it correctly belongs to both classifications simultaneously.

### Q25: Design an experiment-based argument (referring to Activities 8.1 and 8.2) to prove that air contains both carbon dioxide and dust particles, and explain why these findings matter for understanding air quality.
To prove air contains **carbon dioxide**: expose freshly prepared, colourless lime water (calcium hydroxide solution) to air in a petri dish for a few hours, stirring at intervals. The lime water turns milky because carbon dioxide from the air reacts with calcium hydroxide to form insoluble calcium carbonate: **Calcium hydroxide + Carbon dioxide → Calcium carbonate + Water**. This milkiness confirms CO₂ is present in the air.

To prove air contains **dust particles**: place a clean black sheet of paper, free of visible dust, undisturbed near an open window or in a garden for a few hours. Tiny particles settle visibly on its surface (viewable more closely with a magnifying glass), proving dust particles are suspended in the air.

These findings matter because dust particles and gases like carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, ozone, nitrogen dioxide, and sulfur dioxide are the major air **pollutants** — they are not an integral part of clean air, and their presence (measured using tools like the **air quality index, AQI**) directly affects how safe air is to breathe, which has real consequences for health and the environment.

# Nature of Matter: Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures

Look around you right now — your water bottle, the air you're breathing, the pen in your hand. Have you ever stopped to wonder what these things are actually *made of* at their core? Some are single pure substances, some are combinations of substances — and figuring out which is which is exactly what this chapter teaches you to do.

## 8.1 What Are Mixtures?

Think about your favourite *poha* or a sprout salad. They taste amazing because of the many ingredients mixed together — and that's exactly the everyday idea behind a scientific **mixture**.

> **Mixture**: formed when two or more substances are mixed together such that each substance retains its own properties. The individual substances that make up a mixture are called its **components**. The components of a mixture do not react chemically with each other.

Mixtures come in two flavours based on how visible their components are:

- **Non-uniform mixtures**: components are generally visible with the naked eye or a magnifying device (e.g., green gram, chickpeas, onion, and tomato in a sprout salad).
- **Uniform mixtures**: components are evenly distributed and cannot be distinguished, even with a microscope (e.g., sugar dissolved in water).

> **Exam trap:** Don't assume a "mixture" always looks like a jumble of visibly different things — uniform mixtures like sugar-water look completely homogeneous, yet they're still mixtures because the sugar hasn't chemically changed into something new.

### Alloys — a special kind of uniform mixture

**Stainless steel** is a mixture of iron, nickel, chromium, and a small amount of carbon, mixed so uniformly that you can't see the individual substances. Such uniform mixtures of metals are called **alloys**. Other examples:
- **Brass** = copper + zinc
- **Bronze** = copper + tin

> **Our scientific heritage:** Ancient Indian texts like the *Charaka Samhita*, *Susruta Samhita*, *Rasaratna Samucchaya*, and *Rasa Jala Nidhi* describe alloys (called ***Mishraloha***) used for medicinal purposes. For example, **Bronze** (*Kamsya*) — made of Copper (*Tamra*, 4 parts) and Tin (*Vanga*, 1 part) — was used to improve digestion and boost immunity.

### Is air a mixture?

Air is a **uniform mixture** of mainly nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapour.
- **Oxygen** is needed by living beings to stay alive and helps in combustion.
- **Nitrogen** makes up about 78% of air and does not take part in combustion.
- Water vapour in air condenses into droplets when warm air touches a cool surface.

**Confirming CO₂ in air (Activity 8.1):** Calcium oxide (quicklime) added to water reacts vigorously to form calcium hydroxide (**lime water**) and releases heat. When lime water is left exposed to air, it slowly turns milky. This happens because carbon dioxide reacts with calcium hydroxide to form calcium carbonate (insoluble tiny white particles) and water:

> **Calcium hydroxide + Carbon dioxide → Calcium carbonate + Water**

Since lime water turns milky on exposure to air, this confirms the presence of carbon dioxide in the air.

**Dust in air (Activity 8.2):** A clean black sheet of paper left near an open window for a few hours collects visible tiny particles. This shows **dust particles are suspended in air** — they are not an integral part of air and are considered pollutants. Their amount varies with time and place.

> Major air pollutants include particulate matter (dust, soot) and gases like carbon monoxide, ozone, nitrogen dioxide, and sulfur dioxide. The **air quality index (AQI)** describes air quality.

### Types of mixtures

In everyday usage, the components of a mixture can themselves be mixtures (like *poha*). But in **science**, all components of a mixture must be pure substances only. Mixtures are classified by the physical states of their components:

| Mixture Type | Examples | Uniform / Non-uniform |
|---|---|---|
| Gas and gas | Air | Uniform |
| Gas and liquid | Aerated water (soda water); Oxygen dissolved in water | Uniform |
| Solid and gas | Carbon particles in air | Non-uniform |
| Liquid and liquid | Acetic acid in water (vinegar); Oil and water | Vinegar: Uniform; Oil-water: Non-uniform |
| Solid and liquid | Sand and water; Seawater | Sand-water: Non-uniform; Seawater: Uniform |
| Solid and solid | Baking powder (baking soda + tartaric acid); Alloys | Uniform |

## 8.2 What Are Pure Substances?

You've seen "pure" written on milk, ghee, or spice packets — but science uses this word very differently from everyday language.

- **Common usage**: "pure" means **unadulterated**. **Adulteration** is the illegal process of adding cheaper or poor-quality substances to a product to increase quantity or cut costs — this can even make the product hazardous to health.
- **Scientific usage**: a **pure substance** has no other substance present in it — even something labelled "pure" can be scientifically **impure** if it's made of more than one substance.

> **Pure substance**: a kind of matter that cannot be separated into other kinds of matter by any physical process. All its constituent particles are of the same type.

> **Exam trap:** Students often carry over the everyday meaning of "pure" (unadulterated) into science exams. Remember: scientifically, even 100% unadulterated milk is *impure* because it's a mixture of water, fat, proteins, and more.

## 8.3 What Are the Types of Pure Substances?

You already know water can be cooled to ice or heated to vapour, and you get water back either way — its particles remain unchanged throughout. But what happens if we do something more drastic, like passing electricity through it?

**Electrolysis of water (Activity 8.3):** Water with a few drops of dilute sulfuric acid is electrolysed using a 9 V battery. Gas bubbles collect at both terminals.
- Bringing a burning candle near one test tube produces a **pop sound** → **hydrogen gas**.
- Bringing a burning candle near the other test tube makes the flame **glow brighter** → **oxygen gas**.

This shows that water is composed of two constituents:

> **Water → Hydrogen + Oxygen**

### 8.3.1 Elements

Hydrogen and oxygen, obtained above, are pure substances called **elements**.

> **Element**: a substance made up of identical particles called **atoms**, which cannot be further broken down into simpler substances. Elements are the building blocks of all matter. Examples: gold, silver, sulfur, carbon, hydrogen, oxygen.

- Atoms of most elements can't exist independently — two or more atoms combine to form a stable particle called a **molecule** (e.g., two hydrogen atoms form one molecule of hydrogen; two oxygen atoms form one molecule of oxygen).
- Elements are classified into **metals** (gold, silver, magnesium, iron, aluminium) and **non-metals** (carbon, sulfur, hydrogen, oxygen).
- Some elements, like **silicon and boron**, have intermediate properties between metals and non-metals — these are called **metalloids**.

> **Quick facts:** 118 elements are known at present, and most exist as solids. Eleven elements are gases at room temperature (all non-metals, e.g., oxygen, helium, nitrogen). Only two elements are liquid at room temperature — **mercury** (metal) and **bromine** (non-metal). Gallium and caesium are solids that turn liquid around 30 °C (303 K).

### 8.3.2 Compounds

Why can't we separate the hydrogen and oxygen in water using physical means? Their particles are so tightly attached that physical separation is generally impossible — that's what makes water a **compound**.

> **Compound**: formed when different elements combine in **fixed ratios** to form something entirely new. The properties of a compound are completely different from the properties of the elements that form it. The constituent elements of a compound cannot be separated by any physical method.

- In water, hydrogen and oxygen atoms are combined chemically in a fixed ratio of **2:1**.
- **Sodium chloride** (common salt) = sodium (a soft metal) + chlorine (a hazardous gas), combined in a **1:1 ratio**. Though dissolved salt can be separated from water by evaporation (a physical process), the sodium and chlorine within salt itself cannot be separated by physical means.

**Decomposing sugar (Activity 8.4):** Heating sugar in a boiling tube turns it brown, then black (charring). Water droplets appear near the mouth of the tube (from the dry sugar, not the air), and charcoal (carbon) is left behind.

- Sugar decomposes on heating to give **carbon and water**.
- Since water itself consists of hydrogen and oxygen, sugar cannot be an element.
- **Sugar is a compound** made of the elements **carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen**.

**Comparing a mixture and a compound (Activity 8.5):** Iron filings (5.6 g) and sulfur powder (3.2 g) are studied in two forms:

| Test | Sample A (Iron + Sulfur mixed, unheated) | Sample B (Iron + Sulfur heated → black mass) |
|---|---|---|
| Appearance | Black and yellow particles visible separately | Uniform black colour and texture throughout |
| Magnet test | Iron filings get attracted to magnet — components separable | No effect of magnet — components not separable |
| Reaction with dilute HCl | Iron reacts to form iron chloride + **hydrogen gas** (colourless, no smell, burns with 'pop' sound); sulfur left unreacted as yellow solid | Iron sulfide reacts to form iron chloride + **hydrogen sulfide gas** (colourless, rotten egg-like odour) |

> **Iron + Dilute Hydrochloric acid → Iron chloride + Hydrogen gas**
> **Iron sulfide + Dilute Hydrochloric acid → Iron chloride + Hydrogen sulfide**

- **Sample A** is a **mixture** of iron and sulfur — components retain their properties and can be separated (e.g., by a magnet).
- **Sample B** (iron sulfide) is a **compound** — a completely new substance with different properties, and iron and sulfur can no longer be separated.

> **Exam trap:** Students often confuse a mixture of iron and sulfur with the compound iron sulfide, since both look like a mix of "iron and sulfur" on paper. Remember: in the mixture, properties of iron and sulfur are retained (magnetic, separable); in the compound, a completely new substance forms (non-magnetic, inseparable).

## 8.4 How Do We Use Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures?

Elements, compounds, and mixtures are everywhere in daily life and industry:

- **Air** = mixture of oxygen, nitrogen, carbon dioxide.
- **Water** = compound of hydrogen and oxygen.
- **Iron and aluminium** (elements) are used to construct bridges, buildings, and vehicles.
- Chemists use knowledge of how elements combine into compounds to invent **life-saving medicines, vaccines, and fertilisers** that boost crop production.
- Engineers design materials like **stainless steel** (an alloy, stronger and more durable than pure iron) using their understanding of compounds and mixtures.
- **Wood, steel, and concrete** — common building materials — are all mixtures.

> **A step further:** **Graphene aerogel**, made from carbon, is said to be the lightest material on Earth — so light that even grass can hold it. Being highly porous, it has a high absorbing capacity, making it potentially useful for cleaning up oil spills (on both seas and land), as well as for energy-saving devices and special building coatings.

## 8.5 What Are Minerals?

Most rocks are a mixture of **minerals**, visible with the eyes, a magnifying glass, or a microscope.

- **Native minerals** are pure elements (not compounds) — these can be metals (gold, silver, copper) or non-metals (sulfur, carbon).
- Most minerals, however, are **compounds** made up of more than one element — e.g., quartz, calcite, mica, pyroxene, and olivine.
- Many everyday items are made from minerals or elements extracted from them:
  - **Cement** is made from calcite, quartz, alumina, and iron oxide (minerals or obtained from minerals).
  - **Talcum powder** is made from the mineral talc.

> **Our scientific heritage:** **Dhokra art**, an old craft from Bihar and Odisha, uses molten brass or bronze poured into wax-based clay moulds to create strong, shiny golden figures of animals, people, and nature — reflecting tribal creativity using the properties of metal alloys.

Not everything around us is matter, though — light, heat, electricity, and even thoughts and emotions are important parts of our world, but they are **not made of matter**. Understanding what matter is, and isn't, helps us better understand the world around us.

## Quick Recap

- A **mixture** is formed when two or more substances combine without a chemical reaction; each retains its own properties. The individual substances are called **components**.
- Mixtures can be **non-uniform** (components visible, like a sprout salad) or **uniform** (components evenly distributed and indistinguishable, like sugar-water).
- **Alloys** (like stainless steel, brass, bronze) are uniform mixtures of metals.
- Air is a **uniform mixture** of nitrogen, oxygen, argon, CO₂, and water vapour; dust in air is a suspended pollutant, not an integral part of air.
- Lime water turning milky (Calcium hydroxide + Carbon dioxide → Calcium carbonate + Water) confirms CO₂ in air.
- In science, all components of a mixture must be **pure substances**.
- A **pure substance** cannot be separated into other kinds of matter by any physical process; it's scientifically different from "unadulterated" in common usage.
- Pure substances are of two types: **elements** and **compounds**.
- **Elements** are made of identical atoms and cannot be broken into simpler substances (e.g., hydrogen, oxygen, gold, sulfur); classified as **metals**, **non-metals**, and **metalloids** (silicon, boron).
- Electrolysis of water proves **Water → Hydrogen + Oxygen** (H:O ratio = 2:1); tested with a burning candle — hydrogen gives a "pop," oxygen makes the flame glow brighter.
- **Compounds** form when elements combine chemically in a **fixed ratio**, creating a substance with entirely different properties from its constituent elements, which cannot be separated by physical methods (e.g., water, sodium chloride, sugar, iron sulfide).
- The iron + sulfur experiment shows the key difference: the **mixture** (Sample A) is magnetic and separable; the **compound** iron sulfide (Sample B) is non-magnetic and inseparable, reacting with dilute HCl to give differently-smelling gases.
- Elements, compounds, and mixtures all have important daily-life and industrial applications — from building materials to medicines to fertilisers.
- **Minerals** are natural substances in rocks; most are compounds, but a few (native minerals like gold, silver, sulfur, carbon) are pure elements.
- Not everything around us is matter — light, heat, electricity, thoughts, and emotions are not made of matter.

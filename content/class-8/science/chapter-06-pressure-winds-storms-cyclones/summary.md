# Pressure, Winds, Storms, and Cyclones

Have you ever noticed how a school bag with broad straps feels so much more comfortable than one with thin straps — even when both bags weigh exactly the same? That's not your imagination. It's physics, and it's exactly where this chapter begins.

## 6.1 Pressure

Here's the puzzle: two siblings carry identically heavy bags, but one bag "hurts" and the other doesn't. Same weight — so why does it feel different?

The answer lies in **area**. The weight of a bag with **narrow straps** acts on a **smaller area** of your shoulder, while the same weight spread over **broad straps** acts on a **larger area**. Since the area changes even though the force (weight) stays the same, we need a quantity that captures this relationship — that quantity is **pressure**.

**Pressure is defined as the force per unit area:**

$$\text{Pressure} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}$$

> At this stage, we only consider forces that act **perpendicular** to the surface on which pressure is being calculated.

This single idea explains a surprising number of everyday observations:
- Broad bag straps feel more comfortable than narrow ones (**less pressure** on shoulders)
- A bucket with a **broad handle** is easier to lift than one with a narrow handle
- People carrying pots or baskets on their heads place a **round cloth pad** underneath — increasing the area, reducing the pressure

### SI Unit of Pressure

Since **force** is measured in **newton (N)** and **area** in **metre² (m²)**, the SI unit of pressure is **newton/metre² (N/m²)**. This unit also has a special name: the **pascal**, denoted **Pa**.

### Worked Example — Calculating Pressure

**Problem:** A force of 100 N is applied on a cardboard of area 2 m². Find the pressure applied.

**Step 1:** Write the formula.
$$\text{Pressure} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}$$

**Step 2:** Substitute the values.
$$\text{Pressure} = \frac{100\text{ N}}{2\text{ m}^2}$$

**Step 3:** Calculate.
$$\text{Pressure} = 50\text{ N/m}^2$$

### Smaller Area → Higher Pressure

Try this thought experiment: is it easier to drive a nail using its **flat head** or its **pointed end**? Is it easier to cut an apple with the **sharp edge** of a knife, or the **blunt edge**?

In both cases, the answer is obvious once you think about area: **when the area over which a force is applied is smaller, the resulting pressure is higher**, making the task easier. This is exactly why nails have pointed tips and knives have sharp edges — concentrating the same force onto a tiny area creates enormous pressure.

> **Exam trap:** Don't confuse "more force" with "more pressure." A sharp knife doesn't necessarily apply *more force* than a blunt one — it applies the *same* force over a much *smaller area*, which is what makes the pressure (and cutting ability) much higher.

### Do Liquids Exert Pressure?

**Worked activity — Equal water columns, equal bulge:** Two transparent pipes of the **same length but different diameters** are each fitted with a balloon at one end and filled with water to the **same height**. Despite the pipes holding *different amounts (weights) of water* due to their different diameters, **both balloons bulge to the same extent**.

**What this tells us:** since the weight of water differs but the bulge doesn't, the bulge cannot be caused by weight alone — it must be caused by **pressure**, and specifically, **the pressure exerted by a liquid depends on the height of its column**, not on the total weight or the shape of the container.

**Follow-up:** Adding **more water** (increasing the height of the column) in one pipe causes the balloon to **bulge more** — confirming that **pressure increases as the height of the liquid column increases**.

**Real-world application:** This is exactly why **overhead water tanks are placed at a height** — a greater height increases the pressure at the tap, producing a stronger stream of water.

### Do Liquids Exert Pressure on the Sides Too?

**Worked activity:** A plastic bottle with **four holes at the same height** near its base is filled with water. When the tape sealing the holes is removed, water **spurts out from all the holes**, showing that liquids exert pressure **not just downward at the bottom, but also sideways on the walls**. In fact, **liquids exert pressure in all directions**.

> **Ever heard of:** This is also why a **dam's base is broader than its top** — the horizontal water pressure near the bottom of a dam is much larger (since pressure increases with depth), so a broader base is needed to withstand it.

## 6.2 Pressure Exerted by Air

You already know that air surrounds us — this envelope of air around the Earth is called the **atmosphere**, containing nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and other gases, extending many kilometres above the Earth's surface. But does air itself exert pressure?

**Worked activity — Lifting a covered paper plate:** An inverted paper plate with a stick attached is covered first with a **folded** sheet of chart paper, then with the **same sheet unfolded** (larger area, same weight). Lifting the plate requires **more effort when the sheet is unfolded** — even though the weight of the covering sheet hasn't changed at all.

**Conclusion:** Air exerts a **force** on the covering sheet, and this force **increases as the area increases**. Since force per unit area is pressure, this confirms that **air exerts pressure** — and in fact, **air exerts pressure on all objects**. The pressure exerted by the air around us is called **atmospheric pressure**.

- Blowing air into a balloon inflates it because the air inside exerts pressure on the balloon's walls **in all directions** — this is why the balloon expands evenly.

### How Large Is Atmospheric Pressure?

**Worked activity — The rubber sucker:** Pressing a rubber sucker onto a smooth surface pushes out most of the air trapped between the sucker and the surface, **reducing the air pressure inside the sucker**. Since the pressure of the air surrounding the sucker is now **higher** than the pressure inside, the sucker sticks firmly. To remove it, the applied force must be strong enough to overcome this pressure difference.

**A striking figure:** The force exerted by the atmospheric air column over an area of just **15 cm × 15 cm** is nearly equal to the force of gravity on an object of mass **225 kg (2250 N)**! We aren't crushed by this because the **pressure inside our bodies is also equal to atmospheric pressure**, balancing it out — this internal pressure comes from the movement of fluids and gases in our tissues and organs.

> A step further — while the SI unit of pressure is N/m² (pascal), the **practical unit of air pressure is millibar (mb)**, equal to 100 Pa. Air pressure is also expressed in **hectopascal (hPa)**, also equal to 100 Pa.

## 6.3 Formation of Wind

Recall: air escapes from an inflated balloon left open, and air escapes from a punctured bicycle tube. In both cases, air moves from a **high pressure region to a low pressure region**.

**Worked activity — Two connected balloons:** One inflated balloon and one uninflated balloon are connected by a straw. Air flows from the **inflated balloon (higher pressure)** into the **uninflated balloon (lower pressure)**, until both balloons reach roughly the **same size** — at which point the pressures are equal and the airflow **stops**.

**Conclusion: air moves from a region of high air pressure to a region of low air pressure.**

### Connecting This to Sea and Land Breeze

You studied this in Grade 7 — now here's the pressure explanation:
- **During the day:** land heats up faster than water. The warm, lighter air above the land **rises**, creating a **low pressure area** over land. Air from the **high-pressure sea** rushes in to fill this — giving rise to a **sea breeze**.
- **At night:** water stays warmer than land, so a **low pressure area** forms above the sea instead. Wind now blows **from land to sea**, giving rise to a **land breeze**.

> **Exam trap:** Students often forget that sea/land breeze is entirely explained by **pressure differences** between land and sea, driven by their different heating/cooling rates — it's the same principle as the balloon activity, just at a much larger scale.

## 6.4 High-Speed Winds Result in Lowering of Air Pressure

**Worked activity — Blowing between two balloons:** Two inflated balloons hung a few centimetres apart, when air is blown into the **narrow gap between them**, actually **move towards each other** rather than apart!

**Why?** Blowing air between the balloons **creates a low-pressure area** in that gap. The **higher air pressure surrounding** the balloons then **pushes them towards each other**. Blowing harder increases the speed of airflow — and increases how quickly the balloons come together.

**Conclusion: high-speed winds are accompanied by a reduced air pressure.**

### Why This Matters — Roofs Blowing Off in Storms

When high-speed winds blow **over** a house's roof, a **low-pressure area forms above the roof**, while the pressure **inside/below the roof stays normal (higher)**. If this pressure difference is large and the roof is weak, the roof can be **blown away**.

> **Exam trap:** This is exactly why it's recommended to **keep doors and windows open** during high-speed storms — allowing wind to pass **through** the house reduces the pressure difference between inside and above the roof, helping **prevent the roof from being blown off**.

## 6.5 Storms, Thunderstorms, and Lightning

When land gets heated, warm and moist air (being **lighter**) rises, creating a **low pressure area**. Cooler air from surrounding high-pressure regions flows in to take its place — and this air, too, gets heated and rises, creating a **continuous cycle of wind circulation**.

As this rising air **expands, it cools**, and the moisture in it **condenses** to form water droplets — creating **clouds**. These droplets merge into heavier drops, which eventually fall as **rain, hail, or snow**.

**The strong winds accompanied by rain is called a storm.** Storms are more frequent in hot, humid, tropical regions like India. Under certain conditions, warm air rises to great heights where low temperatures convert water droplets into **ice particles**.

### How Lightning Forms

Strong winds blow **upwards and downwards** within storm clouds, causing **water droplets and ice particles to rub against each other**. Just as you learned in "Exploring Forces" — rubbing two objects together causes them to become **charged**.

**The charge separation process:**
- **Positively charged, lighter ice particles** move **upwards**, occupying the **upper part** of the cloud.
- **Negatively charged, heavier water droplets** occupy the **lower part** of the cloud.
- When this negatively charged lower part of the cloud moves close to the ground, it induces a **positive charge** on the ground and nearby objects (trees, buildings).

Normally, **air acts as an electrical insulator**, keeping opposite charges apart. But when the charge build-up becomes very large, **the insulating property of air breaks down**, and a **sudden flow of charges** occurs, producing a bright flash of light called **lightning**.

- Lightning rapidly **heats the air around it**, causing it to **expand** and produce a loud sound called **thunder**.
- **A storm accompanied by lightning and thunder is called a thunderstorm.**
- Lightning can occur **within a cloud**, **between clouds**, or **between a cloud and the ground**.

> A step further — India has various regional names for local thunderstorms: ***Kalboishakhi*** (West Bengal, Bihar, Jharkhand) and ***Bordoisila*** (Assam) occur before the monsoon and help kharif crops grow; in Kerala, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu they're called **mango showers**, supporting mango ripening; thunderstorms in Karnataka also help coffee plant growth.

### Staying Safe from Lightning

Lightning can ignite fires, damage buildings, and cause severe burns or death. Precautions to take:
- **Stay away from tall objects**
- Find a **low-lying open area and crouch down**; **do not lie down flat**
- **Minimise contact with the ground**
- **Avoid using an umbrella with a metallic rod**
- If in water, **get out of it**
- Being inside a **bus or car** is comparatively safer

> **Ever heard of:** A **lightning conductor** is a metallic rod installed along a building's walls during construction. One pointed end is kept **higher than the building's highest point**, while the other end is **buried deep in the ground**, providing an easy path for electric charges to safely transfer into the ground.

## 6.6 Cyclone

**Cyclones are large storms that form over warm ocean waters.**

**How a cyclone forms, step by step:**
1. As ocean water heats up, the warm, moist air above it **rises**.
2. As this moist air rises, water vapour **condenses** to form raindrops. Since evaporation requires heat, **condensation releases that heat back into the atmosphere**.
3. This released heat **further warms the rising air**, causing it to rise even higher, creating an **even lower pressure** area.
4. Air from surrounding regions **rushes in** to fill this low-pressure zone, and it too starts rising.
5. **Earth's rotation** causes this moving air to **spin**.
6. This cycle repeats, creating a **very low-pressure area with high-speed winds revolving around it** — this spinning system of clouds, winds, and rain is called a **cyclone**.

### The Eye of the Cyclone

The region of **lowest pressure** sits at the **centre** of the cyclone, called the **eye of the cyclone**. At the eye, the wind is surprisingly **calm**, but the **surrounding region** experiences **strong winds and heavy rainfall**.

- As a cyclone moves from ocean to land, it generates **higher wind speeds** than regular thunderstorms.
- Once it reaches land, the **source of moist air is cut off**, and the cyclone **gradually loses strength**.

### The Destructive Power of Cyclones

Even as a cyclone weakens over land, it leaves behind destruction that can take **months or years** to repair. For example, **the Amphan cyclone (2020) had peak wind speeds of 270 km/h.**

Ways cyclones cause damage:
- Strong winds push ocean water toward shore, creating a **wall of water 3-12 metres high**, flooding coastal areas and even areas far from the sea
- **Heavy rainfall** can cause rivers to overflow and trigger **landslides**
- Seawater rushing inland can **contaminate drinking water** and damage farmland (the **salt makes soil less fertile**, affecting crops)
- **Roads get blocked** by fallen trees and debris, delaying rescue and relief
- **Power outages** can last for days, disrupting emergency services and daily life

### Staying Protected

- Stay updated on weather reports and alerts from the **India Meteorological Department (IMD)**
- **Weather monitoring satellites** allow tracking of cyclones and prediction of their path, reducing impact on life and property
- National and international organisations work together to monitor cyclone-related disasters
- In cyclone-prone areas, keep an **emergency kit** ready
- During a cyclone, move quickly to a nearby **designated cyclone shelter**

## Quick Recap

- **Pressure = Force / Area**; SI unit is **newton/metre² (N/m²)**, also called **pascal (Pa)**.
- **Smaller area → higher pressure** for the same force — this is why nails have pointed tips and knives have sharp edges.
- **Broad straps/handles reduce pressure** by spreading force over a larger area.
- **Liquids exert pressure** that depends on the **height of the liquid column** (not weight or container shape); liquids exert pressure **in all directions**, including sideways on container walls — this is why dam bases are broader than their tops, and overhead tanks are placed at a height.
- **Air exerts pressure** too — called **atmospheric pressure**; it acts on all objects and in all directions. The atmospheric force over 15 cm × 15 cm is nearly equal to the weight of a 225 kg object (2250 N), balanced by the pressure inside our bodies.
- **Air moves from high pressure to low pressure regions** — this explains wind formation, including **sea breeze (day)** and **land breeze (night)**.
- **High-speed winds are accompanied by reduced (lower) air pressure** — this is why roofs can blow off in storms, and why keeping doors/windows open reduces this risk.
- A **storm** is strong winds accompanied by rain; a **thunderstorm** is a storm accompanied by **lightning and thunder**.
- **Lightning** occurs due to charge separation in clouds (positive ice particles up top, negative water droplets below) followed by a sudden discharge when air's insulating property breaks down; **thunder** is the sound caused by rapidly expanding heated air.
- Safety during lightning: avoid tall objects, crouch in low-lying open areas, avoid metallic umbrellas, get out of water; cars/buses are comparatively safe.
- **Lightning conductors** protect buildings by providing a safe path for charges into the ground.
- A **cyclone** is a large storm forming over warm ocean waters, driven by rising warm moist air, condensation releasing heat, and Earth's rotation causing the system to spin; the **eye of the cyclone** is the calm, lowest-pressure centre, surrounded by strong winds and heavy rain.
- Cyclones cause destruction through storm surges, flooding, landslides, contaminated water, blocked roads, and power outages; the **IMD** and satellite monitoring help track and predict cyclones for safety.

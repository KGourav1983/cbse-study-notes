# Health: The Ultimate Treasure

Quick question before we start: is a person who never catches a cold automatically "healthy"? Most people would say yes — but that's actually not the full picture, and this chapter is all about why.

## 3.1 Health: Is It More Than Not Falling Sick?

Here's a scenario worth thinking about: a Grade 8 student moves to a new school, has no friends, and busy parents. He isn't physically sick — no fever, no infection — but he starts spending all his time on his phone, gets headaches, loses weight, and can't sleep. Is he "healthy"?

Clearly not, even though nothing is "wrong with his body" in the traditional sense. That's exactly the point.

As per the **World Health Organization (WHO)**, health is defined as a **'state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease.'**

A healthy person:
- Can perform various tasks efficiently and cope with difficult situations
- Adjusts well with peer groups and other members of society
- Takes care of their body, maintains a positive mindset, and enjoys social life

> **Exam trap:** If asked to "define health," don't just write "not being sick." Examiners specifically look for the **WHO definition** with all three components: physical, mental, and social well-being.

### Our Scientific Heritage: Ayurveda's View of Health

Ayurveda teaches that true health is a **balance of body, mind, and surroundings**. This balance is maintained through:
- ***Dinacharya*** (daily routine) and ***ritucharya*** (seasonal routine)
- Eating fresh, wholesome food suited to one's ***prakriti*** (body constitution)
- Regular exercise, cleanliness, restful sleep, and a calm mind
- Practices like yoga, meditation, and mindfulness

## 3.2 How Can We Stay Healthy?

Staying healthy isn't about one big thing — it's about several small factors working together: your **lifestyle** (how you live) and your **environment** (your surroundings).

### 3.2.1 Maintain a Healthy Lifestyle

**Good habits to build:**
- Eat a balanced diet with plenty of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
- Avoid processed, fatty, or sugary food and drinks
- Stay physically active — outdoor play, walking, running, cycling, exercising
- Limit screen time and spend more time in nature
- Get enough sleep to help the body and mind rest and recover
- Practice yoga or breathing exercises like *pranayama*
- Say **'NO'** to harmful substances like tobacco, alcohol, and addictive drugs

**Habits that harm health:**
- Spending too much time on mobile phones or digital screens
- Eating fast food and junk food every day
- Sleeping very late or not getting enough sleep
- Skipping meals, especially breakfast

### 3.2.2 Keep the Environment Clean

Have you ever found it hard to breathe around smoke or dust? That's not a coincidence — clean air and water are directly tied to your health.

- In cities, **air pollution** from vehicles and factories can cause coughing or asthma.
- The **Air Quality Index (AQI)** tells us how clean the air is.
- A dirty, unhygienic surrounding (like a polluted playground full of flies and mosquitoes) makes people fall sick more often, compared to a clean, well-maintained one.

But don't forget — health isn't only physical. Even in a clean environment with good food, a person can feel unwell if they're **lonely or upset**. Spending time with friends and family, talking, laughing, and having fun keeps the mind healthy too.

## 3.3 How Do We Know That We Are Unwell?

When your body isn't working the way it should, it gives you clues. These clues come in two forms, and students often mix them up:

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Symptom** | What you *feel* | Pain, tiredness, dizziness |
| **Sign** | What can be *seen or measured* | Fever, rash, high blood pressure, swelling |

> **Exam trap:** "Symptom" and "sign" are NOT interchangeable. A symptom is subjective (only you can feel it); a sign is objective (a doctor can observe or measure it). "High body temperature during fever" is a **sign**, not a symptom.

## 3.4 Diseases: What Are the Causes and Types?

A **disease** is a condition that affects the normal working of the body or mind — this happens when one or more organs or organ systems stop functioning properly.

Diseases are caused either by:
- **Pathogens** — disease-causing organisms like bacteria, viruses, fungi, worms, or protozoa
- **Poor nutrition or an unhealthy lifestyle**

Based on cause and spread, diseases fall into two major types:

| Type | Caused by pathogens? | Spreads person to person? | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Communicable diseases** | Yes | Yes | Typhoid, dengue, flu, chickenpox, COVID-19 |
| **Non-communicable diseases (NCDs)** | No — linked to lifestyle, diet, environment | No | Cancer, diabetes, asthma |

> **Exam trap:** A very common mistake is assuming all diseases spread from person to person. Remember: NCDs like diabetes and asthma **do not spread** — they're not caused by pathogens at all.

In India today, NCDs like diabetes, heart disease, and cancer have become more common, largely due to more processed food, less exercise, and longer lifespans. In fact, **most deaths in India today are caused by NCDs**, not infections.

### 3.4.1 How Are Communicable Diseases Caused and Spread?

All communicable diseases are caused by pathogens, which can enter the body through the air, contaminated food or water, and more. They spread through:

- **By air** — coughing or sneezing
- **Direct contact** — shaking hands
- **Indirect contact** — sharing personal items
- **Contaminated food or water**
- **Vectors** — insects (like mosquitoes and houseflies) that spread pathogens from one host to another

### Common Communicable Diseases

**Diseases spread through the air:**

| Disease | Causal agent | Site of infection | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|---|
| Common cold and influenza | Virus | Respiratory tract | Nasal congestion, sore throat, fever, cough, body ache |
| Chickenpox | Virus | Respiratory tract, skin | Mild fever, itchy skin, rashes, blisters |
| Measles | Virus | Skin, respiratory tract | Fever, sore throat, reddish rashes |
| Tuberculosis (TB) | Bacteria | Lungs | Cough, fever, fatigue, loss of appetite, night sweats |

**Diseases spread through contaminated water and food:**

| Disease | Causal agent | Site of infection | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hepatitis A | Virus | Liver | Fatigue, fever, nausea, vomiting, jaundice |
| Cholera | Bacteria | Intestine | Diarrhoea and dehydration |
| Typhoid | Bacteria | Intestine | Headache, abdominal discomfort, fever, diarrhoea |
| *Ascariasis* (roundworms) | Worms | Intestine | Worms in stool, poor growth, anaemia |

**Diseases transmitted by insects (vectors):**

| Disease | Causal agent | Site of infection | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|---|
| Malaria | Protozoa | Skin, blood | High fever, profuse sweating, periodic chills |
| Dengue fever | Virus | Skin, blood | Fever, headache, muscle and joint pain, nausea |

**Simple but important precautions against communicable diseases:**
- Keeping ourselves and our surroundings clean
- Washing hands with soap and water
- Covering the mouth and nose while coughing or sneezing
- Wearing a mask in crowded places
- Avoiding sharing personal items like towels and handkerchiefs
- Keeping home, food, and water clean
- Staying at home and resting when unwell

Some infectious diseases (like *Ascariasis*) are caused by worms that live inside the body as **parasites** — organisms that live in or on another living being.

### 3.4.2 How Are Non-Communicable Diseases Caused?

Non-communicable diseases are linked to **lifestyle, diet, and/or environment**, not pathogens. A special subtype worth remembering:

- **Deficiency diseases** — caused by a lack of specific nutrients in the diet (e.g., scurvy, anaemia, goitre — studied in Grade 6). These are non-communicable.
- **Chronic diseases** — diseases that persist for a long time (**more than 3 months**), such as cancer, diabetes, and asthma.

**Diabetes** is a good case study: it's becoming more common in adults and children, and India now has one of the highest numbers of diabetes cases in the world. It develops due to a combination of:
- Hormonal imbalances
- Unhealthy eating habits
- Lack of physical activity
- Being overweight or obese

> **Exam trap:** Don't confuse "chronic" with "communicable." Chronic just means *long-lasting* — it says nothing about whether the disease spreads. Cancer is chronic AND non-communicable.

## 3.5 How to Prevent and Control Diseases?

"Prevention is better than cure" applies to both communicable and non-communicable diseases.

### Ability of the Body to Fight Diseases

Ever noticed how some people fall sick more often than others, even living in similar conditions? That comes down to **immunity** — the natural ability of the body to fight diseases, carried out by the **immune system**.

**Vaccines** train the immune system to recognise and attack harmful germs, providing what is called **acquired immunity** — protection developed after exposure to a pathogen or a vaccine.

- Vaccines can be made from **weakened or dead pathogens**, from **inactive/harmless parts** of the pathogen, or newer types that instruct body cells to make a harmless part of the germ for the immune system to learn from.
- Example: a **tetanus shot** contains an inactivated bacterial toxin that helps develop protection without causing the disease.

> **Exam trap:** Vaccines are **preventive, not curative** — they stop disease before it happens but do NOT treat someone who is already sick.

### Edward Jenner and the Smallpox Vaccine (Worked Example — Scientific Method)

This is the classic example of the scientific method in action:

1. **Observation:** Jenner noticed that milkmaids who had **cowpox** (a milder disease) did not catch **smallpox** (a deadly disease), likely because the two viruses are related.
2. **Hypothesis:** Content in the pus of cowpox blisters protected people from smallpox.
3. **Experimentation:** He injected cowpox sap into a boy, who later showed no illness when exposed to smallpox.
4. **Results:** People infected with cowpox sap became resistant to smallpox.
5. **Application:** Mass vaccination based on this discovery eventually helped **eradicate smallpox worldwide** (smallpox was declared eradicated in 1979, following global vaccine campaigns starting around 1965).

This was the world's **first vaccine**.

> **Our scientific heritage:** Long before Jenner, India had a traditional method called **variolation** — using material from a smallpox sore to scratch the skin and create a mild infection that built immunity. People who performed this were called ***teekedaars***.

India today is one of the **world's largest vaccine producers**, supplying vaccines to many countries — Indian vaccine companies played a key role during the COVID-19 pandemic.

### 3.5.1 Treatment of Diseases

If the immune system fails to protect us, doctors may prescribe **antibiotics** — medicines that kill bacteria causing the disease.

> **Exam trap:** Antibiotics work **only against bacterial infections**. They target parts of bacterial cells that are different from human/animal cells, so they do **NOT** work against viruses or diseases caused by protozoa (like malaria) or worms.

**Penicillin** — the first antibiotic — was discovered by **Alexander Fleming** in 1928, after he noticed a mould on a discarded petri dish had stopped bacteria from growing nearby.

### Antibiotic Resistance

Because of the indiscriminate (careless/unnecessary) use of antibiotics, a serious problem called **antibiotic resistance** has developed — bacteria that were once killed by an antibiotic survive and multiply despite treatment with it.

**How resistance develops (step-by-step):**
1. A few bacteria naturally develop resistance to an antibiotic.
2. When the antibiotic kills disease-causing bacteria, it also kills "good" bacteria protecting the body — but the resistant bacteria survive.
3. These resistant bacteria grow and take over.
4. Some resistant bacteria transfer their resistance to other bacteria, spreading the problem further.

**How resistant bacteria spread through the community:**
- Taking antibiotics when not required, or taking an incomplete dose, causes resistance to develop in bacteria in humans and animals.
- Resistant bacteria in animals spread through food products, manure, and crops, eventually reaching the human digestive system.
- Resistant bacteria in humans can spread directly to other patients or indirectly through healthcare workers, especially in hospitals with dirty surfaces, and patients carry it back into the community.

**Precautions to reduce antibiotic resistance:**
- Use antibiotics only when prescribed by a doctor
- Take the correct dose for the right duration
- Avoid unnecessary use of antibiotics (including in cattle/animals)

### Traditional Medicine Systems

Systems like **Ayurveda, Siddha, and Unani** have been used in India for years to manage common health problems, using natural substances (herbs, oils, minerals) and focusing on healthy lifestyle and balanced diet. They can help with some conditions and everyday well-being, but **may not be effective for all diseases and at all stages**.

For non-communicable diseases specifically, therapies focus on **managing symptoms and improving quality of life** through medication, lifestyle changes, and rehabilitation. **Early diagnosis and continuous care** are key to controlling disease progression and preventing complications.

## Quick Recap

- **Health (WHO definition):** a state of complete **physical, mental, and social well-being**, not merely the absence of disease.
- Ayurveda views health as a balance of **body, mind, and surroundings**, maintained through *dinacharya*, *ritucharya*, and practices like yoga and meditation.
- Staying healthy depends on **lifestyle** (diet, exercise, sleep, avoiding harmful substances) and **environment** (clean air, water, surroundings) — plus emotional well-being (relationships, avoiding loneliness).
- **Symptom** = what you feel (pain); **sign** = what can be seen/measured (fever, rash).
- A **disease** disrupts normal body/mind function; caused either by **pathogens** or by **poor nutrition/unhealthy lifestyle**.
- **Communicable diseases** are caused by pathogens and spread person to person (e.g., typhoid, dengue, flu, chickenpox, COVID-19); spread via air, direct/indirect contact, contaminated food/water, or **vectors** (insects).
- **Non-communicable diseases (NCDs)** are not caused by pathogens and don't spread; linked to lifestyle/diet/environment (e.g., cancer, diabetes, asthma) — now the leading cause of death in India.
- **Deficiency diseases** (scurvy, anaemia, goitre) result from lack of specific nutrients; **chronic diseases** last more than 3 months.
- Worms causing disease live as **parasites** inside the body.
- **Immunity** is the body's natural ability to fight disease, powered by the **immune system**.
- **Vaccines** provide **acquired immunity** by training the immune system using weakened/dead pathogens or harmless parts of them; they are **preventive, not curative**.
- **Edward Jenner** developed the first vaccine (smallpox) after observing that milkmaids with cowpox didn't catch smallpox; India's traditional method was called **variolation**, performed by ***teekedaars***.
- **Antibiotics** (first discovered: **penicillin**, by **Alexander Fleming**, 1928) kill bacteria but do **not** work against viruses or protozoa.
- **Antibiotic resistance** develops from indiscriminate/incomplete antibiotic use and spreads through humans, animals, food, and hospitals.
- Traditional systems (**Ayurveda, Siddha, Unani**) support everyday well-being but aren't effective for all diseases/stages; NCD treatment relies on medication, lifestyle change, and early diagnosis.

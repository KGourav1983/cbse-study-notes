### Q1: According to the WHO, what is the definition of health?
As per the **World Health Organization (WHO)**, health is defined as a **'state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease.'**

### Q2: Define a disease.
A **disease** is a condition that affects the normal working of the body or mind, occurring when one or more organs or organ systems stop functioning properly.

### Q3: What are pathogens?
**Pathogens** are disease-causing organisms, which may include bacteria, viruses, fungi, worms, or protozoa.

### Q4: Differentiate between a symptom and a sign, with one example each.
A **symptom** is what a person feels, such as pain, tiredness, or dizziness. A **sign** is something that can be seen or measured, such as fever, rash, high blood pressure, or swelling (e.g., high body temperature during fever).

### Q5: What is immunity?
**Immunity** is the natural ability of the body to fight diseases, carried out by a special system in the body called the **immune system**.

### Q6: What are vectors? Give one example.
**Vectors** are insects that spread pathogens from an infected person or animal to a healthy one. **Mosquitoes** and houseflies are examples of vectors.

### Q7: Define communicable and non-communicable diseases.
**Communicable diseases** are diseases caused by pathogens that can spread from one person to another, e.g., typhoid, dengue, flu. **Non-communicable diseases** are not caused by pathogens and do not spread from person to person; they are usually linked to lifestyle, diet, and/or environment, e.g., cancer, diabetes, asthma.

### Q8: What are deficiency diseases? Give examples.
**Deficiency diseases** are non-communicable diseases caused by a lack of specific nutrients in the diet. Examples include **scurvy, anaemia, and goitre**.

### Q9: What are chronic diseases?
**Chronic diseases** are diseases that persist for a long time — more than **3 months** — such as cancer, diabetes, and asthma.

### Q10: What are antibiotics, and against what type of infections do they work?
**Antibiotics** are medicines that kill bacteria causing a disease. They work **only against bacterial infections**, because they target parts of bacterial cells that are different from human or animal cells; they do not work against viruses or diseases caused by protozoa.

### Q11: What is antibiotic resistance?
**Antibiotic resistance** is a phenomenon where bacteria that were earlier killed by a given antibiotic are found to survive and multiply despite treatment with that antibiotic, making common infections harder to treat.

### Q12: What are parasites?
**Parasites** are organisms that live in or on another living being, feeding on nutrients from the host. Some worms causing infectious diseases live as parasites inside the human body, especially in the digestive system.

### Q13: State the various modes by which communicable diseases spread from an infected person to a healthy person.
Communicable diseases spread through:
- **By air** — when an infected person coughs or sneezes
- **Direct contact** — such as shaking hands
- **Indirect contact** — by sharing personal items
- **Contaminated food or water**
- **Vectors** — insects like mosquitoes and houseflies that carry pathogens

### Q14: List any four precautions that help prevent the spread of communicable diseases.
Any four of the following:
- Washing hands with soap and water to remove pathogens
- Covering the mouth and nose while coughing or sneezing
- Wearing a mask in crowded places
- Avoiding sharing of personal items like towels and handkerchiefs
- Keeping home, food, and water clean
- Staying at home and resting when unwell

### Q15: What is a vaccine, and how does it provide protection?
A **vaccine** helps the body fight certain diseases by training the immune system to recognise and attack harmful germs, providing what is known as **acquired immunity** — protection developed after exposure to a pathogen or a vaccine. Vaccines can be made from weakened or dead pathogens, or from inactive/harmless parts of the pathogen.

### Q16: Why are vaccines described as "preventive, not curative"?
Vaccines help **minimise serious diseases before they happen** by training the immune system in advance, but they do **not treat** a disease once a person is already sick. This is why they must be given before exposure to a pathogen, not after someone has fallen ill.

### Q17: Explain, with reference to Edward Jenner's work, how the first vaccine was discovered.
Edward Jenner **observed** that milkmaids who had **cowpox** (a milder disease) did not catch **smallpox** (a deadly disease), likely because the two viruses are related. He formed the **hypothesis** that content in the pus of cowpox blisters protected people from smallpox. He tested this through **experimentation** by injecting cowpox sap into a boy, who later showed no illness when exposed to smallpox. The **results** showed that people infected with cowpox sap became resistant to smallpox, and this **application** through mass vaccination eventually helped eradicate smallpox worldwide.

### Q18: Differentiate between communicable and non-communicable diseases based on cause and spread, with two examples of each.
**Communicable diseases** are caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, worms, or protozoa) and spread from one infected person to a healthy person, e.g., **typhoid and dengue**. **Non-communicable diseases** are not caused by pathogens and do not spread between people; they are linked to lifestyle, diet, or environment, e.g., **diabetes and asthma**.

### Q19: How do communicable diseases spread through contaminated water and food? Name two such diseases.
Pathogens present in contaminated drinking water or food can enter the body when consumed, causing infection, especially of the intestine or liver. Two such diseases are **cholera** and **typhoid**, both caused by bacteria and affecting the intestine, causing symptoms like diarrhoea, fever, and abdominal discomfort. Preventive measures include maintaining personal hygiene, consuming properly cooked food, and drinking boiled water.

### Q20: Explain how antibiotic resistance develops in bacteria, step by step.
Antibiotic resistance develops as follows:
- A **few bacteria** naturally develop resistance to a given antibiotic.
- When the antibiotic kills the disease-causing bacteria, it also kills the "good" bacteria that protect the body, but the **resistant bacteria survive**.
- These resistant bacteria then **grow and take over**, multiplying despite the antibiotic being present.
- Some resistant bacteria can **transfer their resistance** to other bacteria, spreading the problem further and causing more infections that are difficult to treat.

### Q21: Explain how resistant bacteria can spread from animals and the environment into the human community.
Antibiotics given to animals (e.g., cattle) when not required can cause **resistance to develop in bacteria within those animals**. These resistant bacteria can spread through **soil in crop fields via manure or animal faeces**, reaching the human digestive system through crop produce, or through **exposure to animal food products** like milk, butter, and eggs — eventually spreading resistant bacteria into the human community.

### Q22: A doctor refuses to prescribe antibiotics to a patient suffering from a common cold. Explain why this is the correct medical decision.
The common cold is caused by a **virus**, not bacteria. Antibiotics work only against bacterial infections because they target specific parts of bacterial cells that differ from human cells; they have **no effect on viruses**. Prescribing antibiotics unnecessarily for a viral infection would not help the patient and could instead contribute to the development of **antibiotic resistance** in bacteria present in the body, making future bacterial infections harder to treat.

### Q23: "Prevention is better than cure." Explain this statement with reference to both communicable and non-communicable diseases.
For **communicable diseases**, prevention involves practices like maintaining hygiene, washing hands, covering the mouth while coughing/sneezing, avoiding sharing personal items, drinking clean water, and getting vaccinated — these stop pathogens from spreading in the first place. For **non-communicable diseases**, prevention involves maintaining a healthy lifestyle: eating a balanced diet, exercising regularly, avoiding tobacco and alcohol, managing stress, and getting enough sleep. In both cases, preventing a disease from occurring is far more effective and less costly than treating it after it has developed, since many chronic and communicable diseases can cause long-term complications once established.

### Q24: A community sanitation campaign in Bhadrak district, Odisha, helped more people build and use toilets, significantly reducing open defecation. What effect did this have on child health, and what does this case study demonstrate?
This campaign led to **fewer cases of diarrhoea and infections** among children, improving overall child health. This case study demonstrates that **simple steps like good sanitation can greatly reduce the spread of communicable diseases**, showing how environmental and community-level interventions play a major role in disease prevention, alongside individual healthy habits.

### Q25: Diabetes cases in India have risen sharply in both adults and children in recent years. Explain the factors responsible for this rise, and suggest lifestyle changes that could help address it.
Diabetes often develops due to a combination of factors: **hormonal imbalances, unhealthy eating habits, lack of physical activity, and being overweight or obese**. In India, changes in lifestyle — such as eating more processed food and getting less exercise, along with people living longer lives — have contributed to diabetes and other non-communicable diseases becoming more common, making NCDs a leading cause of death in India today. Addressing this requires lifestyle changes such as eating a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains, avoiding processed and sugary foods, staying physically active, and maintaining a healthy body weight, alongside early diagnosis and continuous medical care to manage the condition.

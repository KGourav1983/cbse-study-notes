## Answer Key

1. As per the **World Health Organization (WHO)**, health is defined as a 'state of complete **physical, mental, and social well-being**, and not merely the absence of disease.'

2. **Pathogens** are disease-causing organisms, such as bacteria, viruses, fungi, worms, or protozoa.

3. A **symptom** is what a person feels (e.g., pain), while a **sign** is something that can be seen or measured (e.g., fever).

4. **Edward Jenner** developed the first vaccine, which protected against **smallpox**.

5. **Alexander Fleming** discovered penicillin in the year **1928**.

6. **Acquired immunity** is the protection developed by the body after exposure to a pathogen or a vaccine, achieved when a vaccine trains the immune system to recognise and attack harmful germs.

7. **Communicable diseases** are caused by pathogens and spread from one person to another, e.g., **dengue**. **Non-communicable diseases** are not caused by pathogens, do not spread between people, and are usually linked to lifestyle, diet, or environment, e.g., **diabetes**.

8. Any four of the following:
   - Eating a balanced diet with plenty of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
   - Avoiding processed, fatty, or sugary food and drinks
   - Staying physically active by playing outdoors, walking, running, cycling, or exercising
   - Limiting screen time and spending more time in nature
   - Getting enough sleep
   - Practising yoga or breathing exercises like *pranayama*
   - Saying 'NO' to harmful substances like tobacco, alcohol, and addictive drugs

9. **Vectors** are insects that spread pathogens from an infected person or animal to a healthy person. Two diseases spread by vectors are **malaria** and **dengue fever**.

10. Antibiotics work only against **bacterial infections** because they target parts of bacterial cells that differ from human or animal cells. The common cold is caused by a **virus**, and antibiotics have no effect on viruses, so prescribing them would not cure the infection. Additionally, unnecessary antibiotic use can contribute to the development of **antibiotic resistance**, making future bacterial infections harder to treat.

11. **Deficiency diseases** are non-communicable diseases caused by a lack of specific nutrients in the diet. Examples include **scurvy** and **anaemia** (goitre is also an accepted example).

12. **Antibiotic resistance** is a phenomenon where bacteria that were earlier killed by a given antibiotic are found to survive and multiply despite treatment with that antibiotic. This happens due to indiscriminate or incomplete use of antibiotics, and it makes common bacterial infections harder to treat, increasing the risk of complications, prolonged illness, and even death.

13. Edward Jenner's discovery of the smallpox vaccine followed the scientific method in these stages:
    - **Observation:** Jenner observed that milkmaids who had **cowpox** (a milder disease affecting cows and occasionally humans) did not catch **smallpox** (a deadly disease that caused blisters and killed millions), likely because the two viruses were related.
    - **Hypothesis:** He proposed that the content in the pus of cowpox blisters protected people from smallpox.
    - **Experimentation:** To test this, he injected cowpox sap into a boy and later exposed the boy to smallpox to observe the outcome.
    - **Results:** The boy showed no illness when exposed to smallpox — confirming that people infected with cowpox sap became resistant to smallpox.
    - **Application:** This discovery led to the invention of the first vaccine. Mass vaccination campaigns based on this principle were carried out worldwide (with an intensified vaccine campaign around 1965), and smallpox was eventually **eradicated worldwide by 1979** — a landmark success of vaccination.

14. Antibiotic resistance develops and spreads through the following steps:
    **Development of resistance:**
    - Initially, only a **few bacteria** naturally carry resistance to a given antibiotic.
    - When antibiotics are taken — especially when not required, or in an incomplete dose — they kill the disease-causing bacteria as well as the "good" bacteria that protect the body, but the **resistant bacteria survive** this attack.
    - These surviving resistant bacteria then **grow and multiply**, taking over the bacterial population.
    - Some resistant bacteria can even **transfer their resistance** to other bacteria, spreading the problem to new bacterial strains.

    **Spread through the community:**
    - **Through animals:** Antibiotics given to livestock (cattle, pigs, poultry) when not needed cause resistance to develop in bacteria within those animals. This spreads through animal food products (milk, butter, eggs) that humans consume, and through **manure or animal faeces** entering the soil of crop fields, from where it reaches the human digestive system via crop produce.
    - **Through humans:** Resistant bacteria developing in a human body due to unnecessary or incomplete antibiotic use can cause serious illness requiring hospital care. In hospitals, these resistant bacteria can spread directly to other patients or indirectly through healthcare workers and dirty surfaces, and patients then carry these resistant bacteria back into the wider community when they go home.

    Together, these pathways allow antibiotic-resistant bacteria to spread widely through the community, making infections increasingly difficult to treat.

### Q1: What is a constituent particle?
A **constituent particle** is the basic unit that makes up a larger piece of a substance or material.

### Q2: What are interparticle spaces?
**Interparticle spaces** are the tiny gaps or spaces that exist between the constituent particles of matter.

### Q3: What are interparticle attractions?
**Interparticle attractions** are the forces of attraction, holding the constituent particles of matter together. Their strength depends on the nature of the substance and the distance between the particles.

### Q4: Define melting point and boiling point.
- **Melting point**: the minimum temperature at which a solid changes into a liquid at atmospheric pressure.
- **Boiling point**: the temperature at which a liquid boils and turns into vapour at atmospheric pressure.

### Q5: Why are liquids and gases together called fluids?
Both liquids and gases **flow** and do not retain a fixed shape, so they are together classified as **fluids**.

### Q6: Who first proposed the idea of the atom in ancient India, and what did he call it?
**Acharya Kanad**, an ancient Indian philosopher, first spoke about the idea of an atom, which he called ***Parmanu***. He believed matter is made up of tiny, indivisible, eternal *Parmanu*, an idea written in his work called the ***Vaisheshika Sutras***.

### Q7: State whether the following are true or false: (i) Solids have a fixed shape and a fixed volume. (ii) The interparticle interactions in solids are very strong, and the interparticle spaces are very small.
(i) **True.** Solids have both a definite shape and a definite volume.
(ii) **True.** In solids, the particles are tightly packed together and held by strong **interparticle attractions**, leaving very little interparticle space.

### Q8: What is evaporation, and how is it different from boiling?
**Evaporation** is the slow formation of vapour that occurs only at the surface of a liquid, at any temperature. It differs from boiling because:
- **Boiling** occurs only at the boiling point, is fast, and vapour forms throughout the liquid (seen as bubbles).
- **Evaporation** occurs at all temperatures, is slow, and happens only at the surface.

### Q9: Correct the false statement: "Melting process involves a decrease in interparticle attractions during the transformation."
This statement is actually **true** as it stands — during melting, particles gain thermal energy, move apart slightly, and the **interparticle attractions decrease/weaken**, allowing the solid to change into a liquid.

### Q10: Fill in the blank type: The primary difference between solids and liquids is that the constituent particles are ______ in solids and ______ in liquids, in terms of packing and movement.
In **solids**, particles are **closely packed** and held in fixed positions (they can only vibrate). In **liquids**, particles are **less tightly packed**, with weaker interparticle attraction, allowing them to **move within a limited space**, though they cannot move as freely as in gases.

### Q11: Why do gases mix easily, while solids do not?
Gases mix easily because their particles have **negligible interparticle attraction** and are free to move rapidly in all directions, so gas particles from different substances can spread and intermingle throughout the available space. In solids, particles are held tightly in fixed positions by **strong interparticle attraction**, so they cannot move to mix with particles of another solid.

### Q12: Why is it possible to pile up sand, but not water?
Sand is made of solid grains, whose particles are held together by **strong interparticle attraction**, keeping each grain in a fixed shape that can be stacked. Water is a liquid whose particles have weaker attraction and can move freely within a limited space, so water always flows and spreads out to take the shape of its container — it cannot hold a pile-able shape.

### Q13: Sugar and sand are both solids. Why does sugar dissolve in water but sand does not?
When sugar is placed in water, the **water particles pull the sugar's constituent particles out of the grain and spread them into the interparticle spaces between water particles**, so sugar dissolves. In the case of sand, the constituent particles are held together by attraction too strong for water particles to pull apart, so sand does not dissolve and remains **insoluble** in water.

### Q14: Why does the water in the ocean taste salty, even though the salt is not visible?
When salt dissolves in ocean water, it breaks up into its **constituent particles**, which are far too small to be seen. These particles occupy the **interparticle spaces** between water particles and remain evenly distributed throughout the water. Although invisible, their presence can be sensed through taste, which is why ocean water tastes salty.

### Q15: Grains of rice and rice flour take the shape of the container when placed in different jars. Are they solids or liquids? Explain.
Both rice grains and rice flour are **solids**, not liquids. Each individual grain (or powder particle) keeps its own fixed shape and volume — a single rice grain does not deform to match the container. What appears to "take the shape" of the jar is simply the loose collection of separate solid grains settling and filling the available space between them, unlike a true liquid, whose *individual particles* flow and rearrange to take the container's shape.

### Q16: A student says, "When you dissolve sugar in water, the sugar disappears completely and stops existing." Is this correct? Explain with reference to Activity 7.2 (tasting the water).
This is **not correct**. When sugar dissolves in water, it breaks up into its **constituent particles**, which are too small to be seen — but they do not stop existing. Their continued presence in the solution can be confirmed because the water tastes sweet after dissolving, showing that sugar particles are distributed throughout the water, occupying the **interparticle spaces**.

### Q17: If we could remove all the constituent particles from a chair, what would happen? Choose the correct answer with justification: (i) Nothing will change. (ii) The chair will weigh less due to lost particles. (iii) Nothing of the chair will remain.
The correct answer is **(iii) Nothing of the chair will remain.** This is because the chair, like all matter, is entirely made up of its **constituent particles** — there is no additional material holding it together besides these particles and the forces of attraction between them. If all constituent particles were removed, there would be nothing left to form the chair.

### Q18: Why does spilled water on a table eventually disappear even without heating, while boiling water needs continuous heat?
Spilled water disappears due to **evaporation**, a slow process that occurs at the surface at any temperature, without requiring continuous heating — particles at the surface gradually gain enough energy to escape into the air over time. Boiling, on the other hand, only happens at the **boiling point** and requires continuous heat because particles throughout the liquid (not just the surface) need enough thermal energy to rapidly overcome interparticle attraction and escape as vapour.

### Q19: Explain, with reference to Activity 7.6 (the syringe experiment), why gases are compressible but liquids are practically incompressible.
In the syringe experiment, pushing the plunger with air trapped inside easily reduces the air's volume, because **gas particles have a lot of space between them** in their natural state, and this space can be reduced by applying external pressure. When the same activity is repeated with water, the volume barely changes, because **liquid particles are already closely packed** with very little interparticle space left to compress.

### Q20: With the help of a diagram/description, explain what happens to the arrangement of particles as ice melts and then changes into water vapour.
- In **ice (solid)**, particles are tightly packed in fixed positions, held by strong interparticle attraction; they can only vibrate about their positions.
- On heating to the **melting point (0 °C)**, particles vibrate more vigorously, interparticle attraction weakens, and particles move out of their fixed positions — ice becomes **liquid water**, with particles a little more loosely packed and free to move within a limited space.
- On further heating to the **boiling point**, particles gain enough energy to almost completely overcome the interparticle attraction and move freely apart in all directions — water changes into **water vapour (gas)**, with particles widely spaced and negligible attraction between them.

### Q21: Why does the fragrance of an incense stick, burnt in one corner of a room, eventually reach every corner of the room?
The particles of the fragrance released from the incense stick get spread by **constantly moving air particles**, which hit and mix with the fragrance particles, carrying them throughout the room. Since gas particles move freely and rapidly in all available space, the fragrance particles gradually reach every corner, even though they started in just one location.

### Q22: Describe the potassium permanganate experiment (Activity 7.8) and explain how it demonstrates the movement of particles in a liquid. How does the outcome change if hot water, room-temperature water, and ice-cold water are compared?
When a few grains of potassium permanganate are dropped into water, streaks of pink colour initially spread from the grain, and with time, the entire bulk of water becomes a uniform pink. This happens because **water particles are in constant motion** — they pull potassium permanganate particles out of the grain and then, through their movement, distribute them throughout the liquid. When the experiment is repeated in three tumblers of hot, room-temperature, and ice-cold water, the pink colour spreads **fastest in hot water**, less quickly at room temperature, and **slowest in ice-cold water**, showing that the movement of particles increases as heat (thermal energy) is provided.

### Q23: Explain, with a labelled description, how soap helps remove oil stains from clothes, using the particulate nature of matter.
When clothes stained with oil are washed with soap, numerous **soap particles** surround the oil particles present on the fabric. Each soap particle has two ends: one end attaches to the oil, while the other end mixes with water. This dual attachment helps **lift the oil off** the fabric and allows it to be washed away with the water, demonstrating how the particulate nature of matter explains an everyday cleaning process.

### Q24: A candle that has just been extinguished after burning for some time shows wax in different physical states around the wick. Identify and explain the different states of the wax likely to be present, and how the arrangement of particles would differ in each state.
After a candle is extinguished, the wax is likely to be present in **all three states**:
- **Solid wax** (the unmelted, hardened body of the candle) — particles tightly packed in fixed positions with strong interparticle attraction, only vibrating.
- **Liquid wax** (the melted pool near the wick) — particles a little more loosely packed than the solid, with slightly weaker attraction, free to move within a limited space.
- **Gaseous wax vapour** (rising as smoke/vapour just above the extinguished wick) — particles widely spaced with negligible interparticle attraction, moving freely in all directions.
This shows how the same substance can exist in different states depending on the thermal energy its particles have received from the candle's heat.

### Q25: 'Gases can spread and fill all the available space.' Do you consider this property of gases to be beneficial or harmful? Justify your answer with examples from the chapter.
This property of gases can be seen as **both beneficial and harmful**, depending on the context:
- **Beneficial:** It allows fragrances (like from an incense stick or perfume) to spread and be smelled throughout a room, and it is what allows air we breathe to fill every space we occupy.
- **Harmful:** The same property means harmful gases or **Suspended Particulate Matter (SPM)** released into the air (as in air pollution) can spread rapidly and fill large volumes of space, making the air we breathe unsafe over a wide area rather than staying contained near the source.
Thus, whether this property is beneficial or harmful depends on **what kind of gas or particles** are spreading, showing that the same fundamental behaviour of matter can have very different real-world consequences.

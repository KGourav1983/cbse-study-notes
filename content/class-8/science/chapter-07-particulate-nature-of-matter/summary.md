# Particulate Nature of Matter

Have you ever wondered why you can pile up sand into a little hill, but you can never do the same with water? Or why a perfume sprayed in one corner of a room reaches your nose across the hall within minutes? The answers to both lie in one big idea this chapter is all about: **matter is made of particles**, and the way those particles behave decides everything — shape, flow, smell, even how a chair stays solid.

## 7.1 What Is Matter Composed Of?

Take a stick of chalk. Break it. Break it again. Keep going until your fingers can't break it anymore, then grind it with a mortar and pestle into a fine powder. Look at that powder under a magnifying glass — every tiny speck is still chalk.

This tells us something important: breaking or grinding chalk is only a **physical change** — the substance doesn't turn into something new, it just gets smaller. If we imagine continuing this breaking process endlessly, we'd eventually reach tiny units that cannot be broken down any further. These are called **constituent particles**.

> **Constituent particle**: the basic unit that makes up a larger piece of a substance or material.

The same logic applies to sand and clay — they aren't the smallest units of rock either; they're made of huge numbers of their own constituent particles.

### Where does dissolved sugar go?

Here's a classic "have you ever noticed" moment: stir sugar into water, and it vanishes — yet the water tastes sweet. So the sugar hasn't disappeared, it's just become invisible.

- Sugar breaks up into its constituent particles when it dissolves.
- These particles are far too small to see, but their presence can still be sensed (by taste).
- Each tiny grain of sugar is made of millions upon millions of these constituent particles.

So where do all these sugar particles actually go? They slot into the tiny **interparticle spaces** — the gaps that exist between water particles.

> **Exam trap:** Students often think dissolving is a chemical change because the sugar "disappears." It isn't — no new substance is formed, so it remains a physical change.

## 7.2 What Decides Different States of Matter?

If particles make up everything, why is iron hard and unbending while water flows and air spreads everywhere? The answer is the *strength* of the forces holding those particles together.

**Interparticle attractions** are the forces of attraction between constituent particles. Two things affect their strength:
- The nature of the substance itself
- The distance between the particles (even a small increase in distance sharply weakens the attraction)

It's ultimately this strength of interparticle attraction that decides whether a substance is a solid, liquid, or gas.

> **Our scientific heritage:** Long before modern chemistry, the ancient Indian philosopher **Acharya Kanad** proposed the idea of the *Parmanu* (atom) — tiny, indivisible, eternal particles that make up matter. This idea appears in his work, the *Vaisheshika Sutras*.

### 7.2.1 Solid State

Pick up an iron nail, a stone, or a piece of wood and try hammering it — it keeps its shape. That's because in **solids**, particles are packed tightly together and held by very strong interparticle attractions.

- Solids have a **definite shape** and a **definite volume**.
- Particles cannot move past one another — they can only **vibrate or oscillate** about a fixed position.
- When you heat a solid, particles vibrate more and more vigorously until they break free of their fixed positions — the solid melts into a liquid.

> **Melting point**: the minimum temperature at which a solid changes into a liquid at atmospheric pressure.

Not all solids melt at the same temperature — it depends on how strong their interparticle attractions are:

| S. No. | Material | Melting Point |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ice | 0 °C |
| 2 | Urea | 133 °C |
| 3 | Iron | 1538 °C |

Weaker attractions → lower melting point (like ice). Stronger attractions → much higher melting point (like iron).

### 7.2.2 Liquid State

Pour the same 200 mL of water into a bottle, then a jug, then a glass — the water takes on a completely new shape each time, but the **volume stays the same** (200 mL, unless some sticks to the container walls).

- Liquids have **no fixed shape** — they take the shape of their container.
- Liquids do have a **definite volume**.
- Particles are free to move, but only within a limited space, because interparticle attractions, while slightly weaker than in solids, are still strong enough to keep particles close together.

You can push your finger through water and it doesn't cut it permanently — the water simply flows around your finger and closes back up once you remove it. That's a nice everyday proof that liquid particles can move past each other.

Heat a liquid enough and it reaches its **boiling point** — the temperature at which it turns into vapour at atmospheric pressure, with bubbles forming throughout the liquid, not just at the surface.

> **Exam trap:** Don't confuse boiling with evaporation. Evaporation happens slowly, only at the surface, and at *any* temperature (that's why spilled water on a table eventually disappears even without heating). Boiling is fast, happens throughout the liquid, and only at the boiling point.

### 7.2.3 Gaseous State

Trap incense-stick smoke in one gas jar, then place another empty jar on top and remove the barrier between them — the smoke spreads to fill both jars completely.

- Gases have **no fixed shape and no fixed volume** — they expand to fill all available space.
- Interparticle attractions in gases are **negligible**.
- Particles move freely and rapidly in all directions.

Because both liquids and gases flow and don't hold a fixed shape, they are together classified as **fluids**.

## 7.3 How Does Interparticle Spacing Differ in the Three States?

### Compressibility test

Trap air in a syringe (thumb over the opening) and push the plunger — the air compresses easily, showing gas particles have lots of space between them that can be squeezed. Try the same with water in the syringe, though, and you'll find it's **practically incompressible** — liquid particles are already packed close together.

### The "missing volume" clue

Here's a neat detective-style experiment: mark the water level (A), add sugar and mark the new level (B), then dissolve the sugar and mark the level again (C). You'll notice the final level (C) is actually a bit *lower* than B — the volume of the solution is less than the sum of the volumes of water and sugar separately.

- This proves there is **empty space** between water particles.
- Dissolved sugar particles simply occupy these interparticle spaces rather than adding on top of the water's volume.
- Try the same with sand (insoluble) — since sand doesn't dissolve, it settles at the bottom and actually **increases** the total volume, unlike sugar.

> **Common misconception:** The space between particles in a solid is *not* filled with air — it contains nothing at all.

Putting it all together:

| Property | Solid | Liquid | Gas |
|---|---|---|---|
| Interparticle spacing | Minimum | A little more than solids | Maximum |
| Packing of particles | Closely packed | A little loosely packed | Free |
| Interparticle attraction | Maximum | Slightly weaker than solids | Minimum (negligible) |

> **A step further:** The word "particle" can mean different things in different contexts. In air pollution, **Suspended Particulate Matter (SPM)** refers to tiny dust particles floating in air — these are much, much bigger than the constituent particles (atoms and molecules) that make up matter. Even a single dust particle is built from a huge number of constituent particles.

## 7.4 How Particles Move in Different States of Matter?

### In liquids

Drop a few grains of potassium permanganate into water without stirring. At first you'll see pink streaks spreading from the grain; eventually, the whole glass of water turns a uniform pink.

- This happens because water particles are in **constant motion** — they pull particles out of the potassium permanganate grain and carry them throughout the liquid.
- **Temperature effect:** the same experiment in hot water shows the colour spreading fastest; in ice-cold water, slowest. **Heat increases particle movement.**
- Substances like sand don't spread this way because their constituent particles are held together too strongly for water particles to pull them apart — that's why sand is insoluble in water.

### In gases

Light an incense stick in one corner of a room. Within minutes, its fragrance reaches every corner — even though nothing visibly "carries" it there.

- This happens because air particles are constantly moving and colliding with fragrance particles, pushing them throughout the room.

> **Ever heard of…** Soap cleans oil stains through the particulate nature of matter — soap particles surround oil particles on fabric. One end of each soap particle attaches to the oil while the other end mixes with water, lifting the oil away and washing it off.

## Putting It All Together

Everything in this chapter comes down to **thermal (heat) energy**:

- In **solids**, thermal energy is low, so particles stay close and are held by strong attractive forces — only small vibrations are possible.
- At the **melting point**, thermal energy is used to partly overcome these attractions, letting particles move out of fixed positions into the liquid state.
- In the **gaseous state**, particles have enough thermal energy to almost completely overcome the attractive forces and move freely in all directions.

> **A step further:** The tiny particles that build up all matter are ultimately **atoms and molecules**. Some elements, like iron and gold, exist as individual atoms. Others, like hydrogen, oxygen, and sulfur, can't exist independently — their atoms combine to form **molecules** (e.g., two hydrogen atoms form a hydrogen molecule; a water molecule has two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom). You'll study atoms and molecules in more detail in higher grades.

## Quick Recap

- Matter is made up of extremely small **constituent particles** that cannot be seen even with an ordinary microscope.
- Breaking or grinding a substance (like chalk) is a **physical change** — the particles get smaller, but the substance stays the same.
- Particles are held together by **interparticle attractions**; their strength depends on the substance and the distance between particles.
- **Solids**: definite shape and volume, particles tightly packed, strongest attraction, particles only vibrate.
- **Liquids**: no fixed shape but definite volume, particles a little more loosely packed, moderate attraction, particles move within a limited space.
- **Gases**: no fixed shape or volume, negligible attraction, particles move freely in all available space.
- **Melting point** = solid → liquid transition temperature; **boiling point** = liquid → gas transition temperature (fast, throughout the liquid); **evaporation** happens slowly, only at the surface, at any temperature.
- Interparticle spaces exist even in solids (and contain nothing, not air); this is proven by the sugar-in-water volume experiment and by gases being compressible while liquids are practically incompressible.
- Diffusion (spreading of particles) happens because particles are always moving — faster movement with more heat (seen with potassium permanganate in hot vs. cold water), and it explains how fragrances spread through a room.
- Liquids and gases are together called **fluids** because both flow and lack a fixed shape.
- **Suspended Particulate Matter (SPM)** refers to dust particles in air pollution — much larger than constituent particles like atoms and molecules.
- **Acharya Kanad** proposed the idea of *Parmanu* (atom) in the *Vaisheshika Sutras*, long before modern atomic theory.
- The state of matter is ultimately decided by the **thermal energy** of its particles, which controls both interparticle distance and the strength of interparticle attraction.

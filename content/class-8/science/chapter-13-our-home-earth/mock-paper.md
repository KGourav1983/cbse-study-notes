## Answer Key

1. The **habitable zone** (or 'Goldilocks zone') is the range of distances from the Sun (or another star) over which the temperature allows water to remain in liquid form.

2. The **greenhouse effect** is the process by which gases such as carbon dioxide in a planet's atmosphere trap heat by absorbing the radiation given off by the planet's surface after it is warmed by the Sun, preventing this heat from escaping into space.

3. The **ozone layer** is formed from **oxygen** — specifically, ozone is a three-atom oxygen molecule.

4. The **biosphere** consists of all living beings on Earth, along with the places where they live, spanning land, water, and air.

5. **Asexual reproduction** is a type of reproduction in which a single parent produces new individuals that are exact copies of itself.

6. **Gametes** are specialised reproductive cells produced by each parent that carry only half of the parent's genetic material; when male and female gametes join, they form a new cell with a complete set of instructions.

7. The three components of the **triple planetary crisis** are **climate change**, **biodiversity loss**, and **pollution**.

8. **Mars** has an atmosphere 100 times thinner than that of Earth.

9. **Asexual reproduction** involves only a single parent, and the offspring produced are exact copies of that parent's genetic instructions. **Sexual reproduction** involves two parents (male and female), whose gametes combine at fertilisation to produce offspring that share traits with both parents but are not exact copies of either — leading to variation.

10. Mars and Mercury do not have a thick atmosphere because they are relatively **small planets**, and their **gravity is too weak** to hold on to atmospheric gases, which then escape into space. This is why Mars's atmosphere is 100 times thinner than Earth's, and why Mercury has no atmosphere at all — Earth, being the right size, has strong enough gravity to retain a thick, life-supporting atmosphere.

11. Two examples of vegetative propagation mentioned in the chapter are: a **stem cutting of a money plant** growing into a new plant, and the **'eyes' of a sprouted potato** (or a piece of ginger) growing into a new plant.

12. In **fish and frogs**, fertilisation is **external** — sperm and eggs are released into water, where they combine to form a zygote, and the embryo also develops in water. In **birds and mammals**, fertilisation is **internal** — sperm are deposited inside the female's body; in birds, the fertilised egg is then laid and the embryo develops after laying, while in mammals, the embryo develops inside the mother's body until birth.

Any two of the following: Water is essential because it forms the **hydrosphere**, which is home to millions of aquatic life forms; it acts as a good **solvent** and transports nutrients from soil to leaves in plants; in animals, it regulates **body temperature**, aids digestion, and ensures hydration; and freshwater is needed to grow crops and support people.

Any two of the following: the **Montreal Protocol (1987)**, which reduced harmful CFCs and helped the ozone layer recover; the **Earth Summit (1992)**, which led to international efforts on climate change and biodiversity; the **Kyoto Protocol (2005)**, which committed countries to reduce greenhouse gas emissions; and the **Paris Agreement (2015)**, which set a goal to limit global warming to below 1.5°C.

15. Earth is suitable for sustaining life because of three key factors working together:

    **Position:** Earth's distance from the Sun places it in the **habitable zone** (or 'Goldilocks zone') — the range of distances over which water can remain in liquid form. If Earth were closer to the Sun, it would be too hot and water would evaporate; if farther away, it would be too cold and water would freeze. Since liquid water is essential for life, Earth's position is the most important reason it can support life. About 70% of Earth's surface is covered in water, giving it the name the **Blue Planet**.

    **Size:** Earth's nearly circular orbit keeps sunlight and heat nearly steady throughout the year, avoiding extreme seasons. More importantly, Earth is the right size to hold on to its atmosphere through gravity — if Earth were much smaller (with the same density), its gravity would be too weak to hold onto atmospheric gases, which would escape into space, as seen with Mars (atmosphere 100 times thinner than Earth's) and Mercury (no atmosphere at all). If Earth were too large, its stronger gravity could crush living things. The oxygen in this atmosphere also allows the formation of the **ozone layer**, which blocks harmful ultraviolet rays from the Sun.

    **Magnetic field:** Earth behaves like a giant magnet, with its magnetic field believed to originate from the movement of molten iron in Earth's core. Earth is constantly hit by harmful high-energy particles from space — **cosmic rays** from across the universe and the **solar wind** from the Sun — which can damage the atmosphere and reduce the ozone layer. Earth's magnetic field acts like a protective shield, pushing most of these harmful particles away and keeping the atmosphere, and therefore life, safe.

    Together, Earth's position, size, and magnetic field create the unique combination of conditions that allow life to emerge and thrive.

16. **Climate change** refers to long-term changes in temperature, rainfall, and weather patterns on Earth. It is largely caused by the burning of fossil fuels like coal and oil, which releases greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide and methane into the atmosphere. Normally, Earth maintains a balance because trees, plants, and even tiny ocean plankton absorb carbon dioxide as they grow. However, burning fossil fuels releases *extra* carbon that had been locked underground for millions of years, and the Earth cannot absorb this fast enough. As a result, more heat gets trapped in the atmosphere, causing **global warming**. 

    The effects of this include: melting of ice caps, rising sea levels which could flood many coastal cities, extreme weather conditions, and the disappearance of many plants and animals as their habitats are disturbed. Climate change affects crop growth, water supply, wildlife habitats, and human health.

    Alongside climate change, the world faces two other major environmental challenges. **Biodiversity loss** happens when natural habitats are destroyed, causing plants and animals to disappear and disturbing entire ecosystems — for example, if grasses vanish, herbivores like deer that feed on them struggle to survive, and predators that eat those herbivores lose their food too. **Pollution** — of air (from factories, vehicles, and burning fuels, causing breathing problems, smog, and acid rain) and of water and soil (from factory, farm, and plastic waste, harming aquatic life and reducing crop yield) — further threatens life.

    Together, these three interconnected challenges — **climate change, biodiversity loss, and pollution** — are known as the **triple planetary crisis**, and addressing them requires action such as using renewable energy, reducing pollution, protecting biodiversity, and adopting sustainable practices at both the individual and global level.

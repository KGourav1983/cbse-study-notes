### Q1: What is the habitable zone (or 'Goldilocks zone')?
The **habitable zone** (or **'Goldilocks zone'**) is the range of distances from the Sun (or another star) over which the temperature allows **water to remain in liquid form** — neither too hot (causing water to evaporate) nor too cold (causing water to freeze).

### Q2: Why is Earth called the Blue Planet?
Earth is called the **Blue Planet** because about 70 per cent of its surface is covered with water, which makes the Earth appear blue when seen from space.

### Q3: Define the greenhouse effect.
The **greenhouse effect** is the process by which gases such as carbon dioxide in a planet's atmosphere trap heat by absorbing the radiation given off by the planet's surface after it has been warmed by the Sun, preventing this heat from escaping into space.

### Q4: What are the atmosphere, hydrosphere, geosphere, and biosphere?
- The **atmosphere** is the layer of gases (air) surrounding the Earth.
- The **hydrosphere** consists of all the water on Earth — ponds, lakes, rivers, seas, oceans, and groundwater.
- The **geosphere** consists of the solid parts of the Earth — rocks, soil, and minerals.
- The **biosphere** consists of all living beings, along with the places where they live (spanning land, water, and air).

### Q5: Define asexual reproduction.
**Asexual reproduction** is a type of reproduction in which a single parent produces new individuals that are exact copies of itself, in terms of the genetic instructions inside the cell.

### Q6: Define sexual reproduction. What are gametes?
**Sexual reproduction** is a type of reproduction in which two parents (male and female) are involved, and their genetic instructions combine to produce offspring. Each parent produces specialised reproductive cells called **gametes**, which carry only half of the parent's genetic material; male and female gametes join to form a new cell with a complete set of instructions.

### Q7: Explain why Earth's crust is often described as being "as thin as an apple's skin."
Almost all of life on Earth — mountains, rivers, forests, animals, and people — exists only on the Earth's **crust**, its thin outer layer. Compared to the entire size of the Earth (which includes the much larger mantle and core), the crust is extremely thin. If the Earth were shrunk to the size of an apple, the crust would be as thin as the apple's skin — showing just how delicate and small this life-supporting layer is relative to the whole planet.

### Q8: Venus is farther from the Sun than Mercury, yet it is the hottest planet in the solar system. Explain why.
Venus is the hottest planet not because of its distance from the Sun, but because of its **atmosphere**. The air on Venus is almost entirely made up of **carbon dioxide gas**, which traps heat and does not let it escape — this is called the **greenhouse effect**. Because of this thick, heat-trapping atmosphere, Venus (average temperature 450°C) becomes hotter than Mercury (average temperature 170°C), even though Mercury is closer to the Sun.

### Q9: Explain how Earth's distance from the Sun makes it suitable for supporting life.
Earth's distance from the Sun places it in the **habitable zone**, where the temperature is just right for **water to exist in liquid form**. If Earth were closer to the Sun, it would be too hot and water would evaporate; if it were farther away, it would be too cold and water would freeze. Since liquid water is essential for life to evolve and be sustained, Earth's position in the habitable zone is the most important reason it can support life.

### Q10: Explain how the size of the Earth helps make it habitable.
The size of the Earth contributes to its habitability in two ways:
- Earth's **nearly circular orbit** keeps the amount of sunlight and heat it receives nearly steady throughout the year, preventing extreme summers and winters.
- Earth is the **right size to hold on to its atmosphere** through gravity. If Earth were much smaller (with the same density), its gravity would be too weak to hold onto atmospheric gases, and they would escape into space — as seen with Mars (atmosphere 100 times thinner than Earth's) and Mercury (no atmosphere at all). If Earth were too large, gravity would be so strong that it could crush living things.

### Q11: What is the ozone layer, and why is it important for life on Earth?
The **ozone layer** is a part of the Earth's atmosphere formed when some of the oxygen in the atmosphere converts into **ozone**, a three-atom oxygen molecule. It is important because it acts like a shield, **blocking harmful ultraviolet (UV) rays** from the Sun that can damage living cells, thereby protecting life on Earth.

### Q12: Explain the role of Earth's magnetic field in sustaining life. What are cosmic rays and solar wind?
Earth behaves like a giant magnet, and this **magnetic field** is believed to originate from the movement of molten iron in Earth's core. Earth is constantly hit by high-energy particles from space — **cosmic rays** (which come from far across the universe) and the **solar wind** (particles that come from the Sun). These particles can damage the atmosphere, reduce the ozone layer, and let in more harmful UV rays. Earth's magnetic field acts like a **protective shield**, pushing many of these harmful particles away from the Earth, thereby keeping the atmosphere — and life — safe.

### Q14: What is geodiversity? Why is it important?
**Geodiversity** refers to the variety of landforms, rocks, and soils on Earth, along with the processes that shape and alter them. It is important because it helps create unique habitats where different types of life can thrive, showing that the non-living parts of nature — soil, rocks, and water — are not just a background, but actively help shape the story of life itself.

### Q15: What is vegetative propagation? Give two examples mentioned in the chapter.
**Vegetative propagation** is a form of asexual reproduction in plants in which a new plant grows from a part of the parent plant — such as a leaf, stem, or root — when it is planted in soil. Examples mentioned in the chapter include a **stem cutting of a money plant** growing into a new plant, and the **'eyes' of a sprouted potato** or a **piece of ginger** growing into a new plant.

### Q16: Explain reproduction in Hydra and Planaria. What do scientists study using Planaria?
**Hydra**, a simple animal, reproduces asexually by **budding** — it grows tiny buds on its body that break off and develop into new individuals. **Planaria**, a type of flatworm, can **regrow an entire new individual from just a fragment of its body**. Scientists study Planaria to understand the process of **regeneration** in animals.

### Q17: Explain sexual reproduction in plants, describing pollination and fertilisation.
Plants use different parts of their flowers to produce gametes. **Pollen grains**, found inside the **anther** of a flower, are the male gametes, while **ovules**, found deep inside the flower, are the female gametes. **Pollination** is the process by which pollen is carried to another flower by wind, insects, or animals. When the male and female gametes combine, it is called **fertilisation**, which forms a **zygote** that develops into the seed. The fleshy part of the flower around the ovule develops into a **fruit**.

### Q18: Differentiate between external and internal fertilisation in animals, with examples.
In **external fertilisation**, gametes (sperm and eggs) are released outside the body, into water, where they combine to form a zygote — this occurs in animals like **fish and frogs**, and the resulting embryo also develops in water. In **internal fertilisation**, sperm are deposited inside the body of the female, and fertilisation takes place inside — this occurs in **birds and mammals, including humans**.

### Q19: Explain the difference in embryo development between birds and mammals.
In **birds**, after internal fertilisation, the fertilised egg (zygote) is *laid* by the female, and the embryo develops *after* the egg is laid, during the process of hatching; the mother packs sufficient nutrition into the egg to last the embryo until it hatches. In most **mammals**, the zygote develops into an embryo *inside the body of the female* — the mother's body directly provides all the food and oxygen the baby needs to grow until it is born, which is a different strategy of supplying nutrition compared to egg-laying animals.

### Q20: Why do offspring produced through sexual reproduction show both similarities and variations compared to their parents?
Offspring show **similarities** to their parents because they inherit genetic instructions (**genes**) from both parents, which guide how their bodies develop — for example, ensuring a calf grows into a cow. However, offspring also show **variation** because each parent's gamete carries only half of that parent's genetic material, and the specific combination of genes from the mother and father that come together is different each time. This is why even siblings from the same parents can look different from one another — it depends on which pieces of each parent's 'instruction set' combine.

### Q21: What is the "triple planetary crisis"? Name and briefly explain its three components.
The **triple planetary crisis** refers to the three biggest environmental challenges facing life on Earth today: **climate change**, **biodiversity loss**, and **pollution**.
- **Climate change** refers to long-term changes in temperature, rainfall, and weather patterns, largely caused by the extra greenhouse gases released from burning fossil fuels.
- **Biodiversity loss** occurs when natural habitats are destroyed, causing plants and animals to disappear and disturbing ecosystems.
- **Pollution** includes air, water, and soil pollution from factories, vehicles, farms, and plastic waste, which harms both people and nature.

### Q22: Explain how the greenhouse effect contributes to climate change, and how this differs from the natural greenhouse effect that keeps Earth warm.
Normally, the mild, natural greenhouse effect on Earth is beneficial — gases like carbon dioxide trap just enough heat to keep the temperature suitable for liquid water, and this balance is maintained because trees, plants, and ocean plankton absorb carbon dioxide as they grow. However, when humans burn fossil fuels like coal and oil, they release large amounts of **extra** carbon dioxide and methane that had been locked underground for millions of years. The Earth cannot absorb this extra carbon fast enough, so more heat gets trapped in the atmosphere than usual, causing **global warming** — melting ice caps, rising sea levels, extreme weather, and species loss. So while the natural greenhouse effect sustains life, this **excess, human-caused** greenhouse effect is what drives harmful climate change.

### Q23: With an example, explain how the destruction of one part of an ecosystem can disturb the whole balance of nature.
Earth's living systems function together as a balance, so even a small change in one part can have wide-ranging effects. For example, if a forest is cut down and the grasses in that ecosystem vanish, herbivores like **deer or grasshoppers** that depend on grass for food will struggle to survive. Since predators such as **tigers or foxes** in turn depend on these herbivores for food, they lose their food source too. This shows that every organism has a role in an ecosystem, and losing even a few species weakens nature's overall ability to support life — while also potentially impacting rainfall, soil, and air quality in that area.

### Q24: Name any two international agreements aimed at protecting Earth's environment, and explain what each aims to achieve.
Two such agreements are:
- The **Montreal Protocol (1987)**, which helped reduce harmful chemicals like **chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)**, allowing the ozone layer to slowly recover.
- The **Paris Agreement (2015)**, which committed countries to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, with a goal of limiting global warming to below **1.5°C** (though the world is not yet on track to meet this goal, as of 2025).

(Other acceptable answers: the **Earth Summit, 1992**, which led to international efforts on climate change and biodiversity; or the **Kyoto Protocol, 2005**, which committed countries to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.)

### Q25: "Mars lies at the edge of the habitable zone but currently cannot support life as we know it." Explain this statement using concepts from the chapter.
Mars lies at the **edge of the Sun's habitable zone**, which means conditions there are only borderline suitable for liquid water to exist. While scientists believe Mars may have had liquid water — even lakes — in the past, and hence conditions that could have supported simple life forms, no proof of life has been found by the spacecraft and rovers sent to study it so far. Additionally, Mars's **atmosphere is 100 times thinner than Earth's**, likely because Mars is smaller and its gravity is too weak to hold onto a thick atmosphere — this would make it very difficult for liquid water and life to be sustained on its surface today. This shows that being in the habitable zone alone is not enough; a planet also needs the right size (for gravity and atmosphere) to actually support and sustain life, which is why Mars continues to interest scientists studying the possibility of past or future life there.

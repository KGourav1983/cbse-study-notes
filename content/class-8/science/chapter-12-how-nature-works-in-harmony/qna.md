### Q1: Define habitat. Give one example.
A **habitat** is a place that provides the right living conditions for an organism to live and grow. For example, a pond is a habitat for fish, providing food, oxygen, shelter, and space.

### Q2: What are biotic and abiotic components of a habitat? Give two examples of each.
**Biotic components** are the living beings present in a habitat, such as plants, animals, and microorganisms — for example, **fish and trees**. **Abiotic components** are the non-living things in a habitat, such as air, water, soil, sunlight, and temperature — for example, **water and soil**.

### Q3: Define population. Give an example.
A **population** is a group of organisms of the *same kind (species)* living together in a habitat at a given time. For example, all the fish of one particular species living in a pond form a population.

### Q4: What is a community?
A **community** comprises different **populations** sharing the same habitat. The biotic components of a habitat — plants, animals, and microorganisms — together form the community, and these organisms interact with and depend on one another for survival.

### Q5: Define ecosystem. Name its two main types with examples.
An **ecosystem** is formed when the biotic components (plants, animals, microorganisms) and abiotic components (air, water, soil, sunlight, temperature) of a habitat interact with each other. The two main types are:
- **Aquatic ecosystems** — ponds, rivers, lakes
- **Terrestrial ecosystems** — forests, farms, grasslands, or even a single large tree

### Q6: Differentiate between aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems.

| Basis | Aquatic Ecosystem | Terrestrial Ecosystem |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Ecosystem based in water | Ecosystem based on land |
| Examples | Ponds, rivers, lakes | Forests, farms, grasslands |
| Key abiotic factor | Water is the dominant medium | Soil is the dominant medium |

### Q7: Who are producers? Why are they also called autotrophs?
**Producers** are organisms, mainly green plants, that make their own food through the process of **photosynthesis**. They are called **autotrophs** because the word comes from *auto* (self) + *troph* (food) — meaning they are self-feeding and do not depend on other organisms for food.

### Q8: Differentiate between herbivores, carnivores, and omnivores with one example each.
- **Herbivores** eat only plants and plant products — for example, **deer and hare**.
- **Carnivores** eat only animals — for example, the **leopard**.
- **Omnivores** eat both plants and animals — for example, **crows, foxes, and mice**.

### Q9: Define food chain with a suitable example.
A **food chain** is a simple sequence that shows "who eats whom" in an ecosystem, where each organism feeds on the one before it. For example:

**Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle**

### Q10: What is a trophic level? Explain the different trophic levels using a food chain example.
A **trophic level** is the specific position an organism occupies in a food chain, based on how it obtains its food. Using the example Grass → Hare → Fox:
- **Producers** (green plants like grass) occupy the **first trophic level**.
- **Herbivores** (like hares and deer) occupy the **second trophic level**.
- **Small carnivores** (like frogs) occupy the **third trophic level**.
- **Large carnivores** (like foxes, tigers, or vultures) occupy the next trophic level.

### Q11: What is a food web? How is it different from a food chain?
A **food web** is a network formed when many food chains in an ecosystem interlink with one another, since a single organism may be eaten by two or more different organisms. It differs from a **food chain**, which is only a single, straight-line sequence of "who eats whom." A food web gives a much more realistic picture of feeding relationships in an ecosystem than one isolated food chain.

### Q12: Who are decomposers? Explain the process of decomposition and why it is important.
**Decomposers**, also called **saprotrophs** (*sapro* = rotten + *troph* = food), are organisms like fungi and bacteria that break down complex substances in dead plants and animals into simpler ones. This process is called **decomposition**. It is important because:
- It returns essential **nutrients to the soil**, which plants then use for growth.
- It ensures dead matter and waste do not simply accumulate — **nothing is wasted in nature; everything is reused**.

### Q13: Using the elephant corridor example, explain how loss of forest habitat can bring elephants into conflict with humans.
Cutting down trees for roads and buildings, along with changes in rainfall and temperature, causes forests to dry up and shrink. Since elephants are adapted to forest life, this **loss of habitat** forces them to wander into nearby farms and villages in search of food and water, sometimes damaging crops and harming people. To reduce this conflict, wildlife ecologists have identified and marked **elephant corridors**, which connect large forest areas and allow elephants to move safely between them without entering human settlements.

### Q14: Describe the pond study involving fish and dragonflies. What does it show about ecosystem interactions?
Researchers compared **Pond A** (with fish, many flowering plants) and **Pond B** (without fish, fewer flowering plants). They found that:
- Fish eat dragonfly larvae, so **Pond A had fewer dragonflies**.
- With fewer dragonflies (which eat bees, flies, and butterflies), **Pond A had more pollinating insects**.
- More pollinators led to **greater seed production** in flowering plants around Pond A.

This study shows that **biotic components** (fish, dragonflies, pollinators, plants) and **abiotic components** (temperature, water, nutrients) are closely interconnected, and a change in one organism's population can indirectly affect an entirely different part of the ecosystem.

### Q15: With the help of a food chain, explain how trophic levels can be represented as a pyramid.
Consider the food chain: **Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle**. If we count the number of organisms at each level, producers (grass) are usually the most numerous, followed by fewer herbivores (grasshoppers), even fewer small carnivores (frogs), and the fewest large carnivores (eagles) at the top. When arranged from the highest number at the base to the lowest number at the top, this forms a **pyramid shape**, known as a food/trophic pyramid, with **producers** always forming the broad base and top carnivores forming the narrow tip.

### Q16: Differentiate between mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism, giving one example of each.

| Type of interaction | Effect on organisms | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Mutualism** | Both organisms benefit | Honeybees and flowers |
| **Commensalism** | One organism benefits, the other is unaffected | Orchids growing on tree branches |
| **Parasitism** | One organism benefits, the other is harmed | Ticks feeding on a dog |

### Q17: Explain how competition among organisms helps maintain balance in an ecosystem.
Organisms living in the same habitat often **compete** for common resources such as food, water, physical space, or sunlight. This competition **controls population size** — if it did not exist, one species could multiply excessively and use up shared resources, causing an **imbalance** in the ecosystem. Competition thus keeps populations of different species in check and helps maintain overall ecological balance.

### Q18: Explain, with reference to the frog leg export example, how disturbing one part of an ecosystem can cause wider ecological damage.
In the 1980s, India exported large numbers of **Indian bullfrog** legs, leading to large-scale harvesting and a sharp decline in frog populations. Since frogs are natural predators of insects, their reduced numbers caused a **rise in agricultural pests**. This forced farmers to use more **synthetic pesticides**, which in turn harmed the soil, water quality, and overall environmental and human health. As a result, the **Government of India banned the export of frog legs** to prevent further ecological damage. This example shows how removing even one species from a food chain can trigger a chain of harmful, cascading effects.

### Q19: Trace the chain of events that occurs when plants in a pond start dying due to pollution.
The sequence of cascading effects is as follows:

**Plants in the pond die (due to pollution) → less oxygen is produced in the water → fish population drops → fewer consumers to control insects → insect population increases → insects spread to nearby farmland → farmers are compelled to use pesticides → further environmental issues arise**

This shows that ecosystems function as interconnected chains, where a single small disturbance can trigger far-reaching consequences.

### Q20: What benefits do ecosystems provide to humans?
Ecosystems support human well-being in several ways:
- **Forests** provide fresh air, fertile soil, food, fibres, timber, and medicines.
- **Aquatic ecosystems** provide water and food.
- Ecosystems also offer **aesthetic and recreational value**.

These benefits show how closely human life is connected to nature, and why overusing or misusing natural resources disturbs this balance.

### Q21: Describe the importance of the Sundarbans ecosystem and the threats it faces today.
The **Sundarbans**, located where the Ganges and Brahmaputra rivers meet between India and Bangladesh, contain the **largest mangrove forest in the world** and were declared a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987**. They are important because they:
- Slow down strong winds and waves during storms and floods
- Absorb carbon dioxide and release oxygen
- Provide habitat to many endangered species of flora and fauna

However, the Sundarbans face serious threats: mangrove trees are being cut for fuelwood and farming, illegal hunting and overuse of forest resources threaten wildlife, and pollution from industrial waste and untreated sewage is damaging the water and habitat.

### Q22: What was the Green Revolution? Explain its benefits and drawbacks.
The **Green Revolution** refers to the mid-20th century period when the use of tractors, machines, synthetic fertilisers, and pesticides greatly **increased food production** in India, helping to overcome the food crisis faced between 1950 and 1965 and making the country food secure.

However, these farming methods are now considered **unsustainable** because of:
- Overuse of synthetic chemicals, which reduces soil fertility and humus content
- Excessive groundwater extraction
- Growing only one type of crop for commercial gain (**monoculture**), which harms biodiversity

### Q23: Why is monoculture considered harmful to an ecosystem? Suggest a more sustainable alternative.
**Monoculture** means growing the same crop repeatedly on the same land. It is harmful because it:
- **Reduces biodiversity** in the area
- **Affects pollinators**, which are crucial for food production
- Leads to **soil degradation** over time, as the same nutrients are repeatedly drawn from the soil

A more sustainable alternative is to adopt **organic and natural farming methods**, which reduce dependence on synthetic fertilisers and pesticides and minimise interference with natural ecosystems, helping protect soil health, biodiversity, and long-term food security.

### Q24: In the food chain Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake, if frogs disappear from the ecosystem, what will happen to the population of grasshoppers and snakes? Explain why.
If **frogs disappear**, two effects will occur:
- The **grasshopper population will increase**, because their natural predator (the frog) is no longer present to control their numbers.
- The **snake population will decrease**, because snakes depend on frogs as a food source, and losing this food source reduces the resources available for snakes to survive and reproduce.

This shows how removing a single trophic level from a food chain disturbs the balance of the entire chain, not just the levels directly next to it.

### Q25: Why is it not possible for an ecosystem to exist with only producers and no consumers or decomposers?
An ecosystem cannot function with only producers because:
- **Consumers** are needed to keep producer populations in check through feeding relationships; without them, producer populations could grow unchecked and compete excessively among themselves for light, water, and space.
- **Decomposers** are essential to break down dead plant matter and recycle nutrients back into the soil. Without decomposers, dead organic matter would simply accumulate, and the soil would gradually lose the nutrients needed to support plant growth.
- Ecosystems function through **interactions** among producers, consumers, and decomposers — removing any one group breaks the cycle of energy flow and nutrient recycling that keeps the ecosystem balanced and self-sustaining.

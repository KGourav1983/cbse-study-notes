## Answer Key

1. A **habitat** is a place that provides the right living conditions for an organism to live and grow.

2. **Decomposers** (or saprotrophs) are organisms, such as fungi and bacteria, that break down complex substances in dead plants and animals into simpler ones, returning nutrients to the soil.

3. Two examples of aquatic ecosystems are **ponds and rivers** (lakes is also an acceptable second example).

4. Producers are also called **autotrophs**.

5. A **population** is a group of organisms of the same kind (species) living together in a habitat at a given time.

6. **Monoculture** is the practice of growing the same crop repeatedly on the same piece of land.

7. **Biotic components** are the living beings in a habitat, such as plants, animals, and microorganisms — for example, **fish** in a pond. **Abiotic components** are the non-living things in a habitat, such as air, water, soil, sunlight, and temperature — for example, **water** in a pond.

8. A **food chain** is a simple, straight-line sequence showing "who eats whom" in an ecosystem (e.g., Grass → Grasshopper → Frog). A **food web** is a network formed when several food chains interlink with one another, since an organism may be eaten by more than one type of predator. A food web therefore gives a more complete and realistic picture of feeding relationships than a single food chain.

9. Any two of the following: Ecosystems provide **fresh air, fertile soil, food, fibres, timber, and medicines** (from forests); they provide **water and food** (from aquatic ecosystems); and they offer **aesthetic and recreational value**.

10. In **commensalism**, one organism benefits while the other is unaffected — for example, an **orchid growing on a tree branch** gets physical support while the tree branch is unaffected. In **mutualism**, both organisms benefit — for example, **honeybees and flowers**, where the bee gets nectar and the flower gets pollinated.

11. Any two of the following: Overuse of synthetic fertilisers and pesticides can **reduce soil fertility** by decreasing friendly microorganisms and lowering humus content; it can **reduce natural predator populations**, which increases pest numbers; it can cause **pests to develop resistance**, making them harder to control; and it can lead to **soil erosion** due to the loss of humus that binds soil particles.

12. In the food chain **Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle**, each organism occupies a specific **trophic level** based on how it obtains food:
    - **Grass** (a producer) occupies the **first trophic level**, as it makes its own food through photosynthesis.
    - The **grasshopper**, a herbivore that eats grass, occupies the **second trophic level**.
    - The **frog**, a small carnivore that eats the grasshopper, occupies the **third trophic level**.
    - The **snake**, which eats the frog, occupies the **fourth trophic level**.
    - The **eagle**, a large carnivore at the top of this chain, occupies the **fifth (highest) trophic level**.
    
    If the number of organisms at each level were counted and arranged from the highest number at the base to the lowest at the top, they would form a **pyramid shape**, with producers forming the widest base and top carnivores forming the narrowest tip. This pyramid shows that producers are the most numerous, while the top carnivores are the fewest, since energy and biomass decrease as we move up each trophic level.

13. Loss of forest habitat leads to elephants entering human settlements through the following chain of events: Changes in rainfall and temperature affect vegetation, and cutting down trees for roads and buildings makes this worse. This causes forests to **dry up and shrink**, destroying the natural home of animals like elephants. Since elephants are specifically adapted to forest life, this sudden loss of habitat makes it difficult for them to survive, and they are forced to **wander into nearby farms and villages** in search of food (such as bananas and sugarcane) and water, which can lead to crop damage and even harm to people and domestic animals. To address this, wildlife ecologists have identified and marked **elephant corridors** that connect large forest areas, allowing elephants to move safely between them without coming into conflict with human settlements. This example shows how closely connected the different elements of nature are.

14. Human-made ecosystems like agricultural fields are indeed necessary, since farming is a major livelihood in India and directly supports the country's food security — this was clearly demonstrated during the **Green Revolution** (mid-20th century), when the use of tractors, machines, synthetic fertilisers, and pesticides helped India overcome a serious food crisis and greatly increased food production.
    
    However, these methods are now recognised as **unsustainable**. The overuse of synthetic chemicals has reduced soil fertility by decreasing friendly microorganisms and lowering humus content, making soil more prone to erosion. Excessive groundwater extraction has placed further strain on natural resources, and growing only one type of crop for commercial gain (**monoculture**) has reduced biodiversity, harmed pollinators, and led to soil degradation. Overuse of pesticides has also reduced natural predator populations, allowing pest numbers to rise, with some pests even developing resistance to the chemicals used against them.
    
    This is why agricultural fields, while necessary, **must be made sustainable** — for example, through organic and natural farming practices that reduce dependence on synthetic inputs and minimise interference with natural ecosystems, so that farming can continue to support both human needs and long-term environmental and soil health.

# How Nature Works in Harmony

Have you ever wondered why elephants sometimes wander into farms and villages in states like Odisha, Jharkhand, or Assam? It's not random — it's usually because their forest habitat has shrunk, waterholes have dried up, and food has become scarce. Wildlife ecologists even mark **corridors** so elephants can move safely between forest patches without clashing with humans.

This one example tells you everything about this chapter: **nature is a web of connections**, and a small change in one part of the web (like cutting trees for a road) ripples outward and affects things you wouldn't expect (like elephants raiding a farm). This chapter is about learning to see those connections.

## 12.1 What Makes Up a Habitat?

Think about the last time you saw fish in a pond, or ants on a tree trunk. Each of these organisms is living in a **habitat** — simply the place where an organism lives, which could be as small as the bark of a tree or as large as an entire forest.

Every habitat is made of two kinds of things:

- **Biotic components** — the living beings: plants, animals, and microorganisms.
- **Abiotic components** — the non-living things: air, water, soil, sunlight, and temperature.

A pond habitat, for example, has fish, frogs, algae, and lotus (biotic) along with water, sunlight, and soil (abiotic). A forest habitat has trees, birds, and deer (biotic) along with soil, air, and sunlight (abiotic).

> **Exam trap:** Students often just list organisms as "biotic" and stop there — but examiners want you to also identify the *abiotic* components in the same habitat, and ideally explain how the two depend on each other (e.g., "fish get oxygen from water, an abiotic component").

Different habitats offer different living conditions — that's exactly why some organisms live on land and others live in water. A forest may be warm in the day and cool at night; a snake active at night and a rodent active in the day both share the same habitat but experience it differently. This is how organisms **coexist in harmony** in the same space.

## 12.2 Population — How Many of the Same Kind?

If you counted all the fish of one particular species in a pond, you'd get a number — say, 40. This entire group of the *same kind* of organism living together in one habitat is called a **population**.

Populations are measured at a given place and time — for instance, you might record 20 individuals of one plant species and only 5 of another in the same 1 m × 1 m patch of your school garden.

## 12.3 Community — Many Populations Together

A habitat is rarely home to just one population. A **community** is formed when *different populations* share the same habitat — for example, the fish population, the frog population, and the lotus population in a pond together make up the pond's community. These organisms don't live in isolation; they interact with and depend on one another.

### Does Every Organism in a Community Matter? A Real Study

Here's a case that shows just how tightly community members are linked. Researchers compared two ponds:

- **Pond A** — had fish, and a large number of flowering plants around it.
- **Pond B** — had no fish, and fewer flowering plants around it.

Working through the logic step by step:

1. Fish eat dragonfly larvae, so Pond A (with fish) had **fewer adult dragonflies**.
2. Dragonflies eat flies, bees, and butterflies. With fewer dragonflies in Pond A, there were **more bees, flies, and butterflies**.
3. These insects are **pollinators** — they carry pollen between flowers.
4. More pollinators near Pond A meant **more seed production** in the surrounding plants.

So a single biotic component (fish) indirectly boosted seed production in plants — showing how biotic components (fish, dragonflies, pollinators, plants) and abiotic components (water, nutrients, temperature) all interact and affect each other.

**Side note — pollination:** A flower has a stalk, **sepals**, **petals**, and two reproductive parts — **carpels** (female) and **stamens** (male). Stamens release **pollen grains**, and wind, water, insects, bats, and birds carry this pollen to carpels — a process called **pollination**, essential for forming fruits and seeds.

## 12.4 Putting It All Together: The Ecosystem

When the biotic components (plants, animals, microorganisms) of a habitat interact with its abiotic components (air, water, soil, sunlight, temperature), together they form an **ecosystem**.

| Type of Ecosystem | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Aquatic ecosystems** | Ponds, rivers, lakes |
| **Terrestrial ecosystems** | Forests, farms, even a single large tree (banyan, mango, *pilkhan*) |

Ecosystems can be large or small, and they often overlap — a river running through a forest links an aquatic ecosystem with a terrestrial one. Farmland is a special case: a **human-made ecosystem**.

Just as biotic components depend on abiotic ones (sunlight and water for photosynthesis, soil for nutrients), abiotic components are also shaped by biotic ones — plants release oxygen, plant roots hold soil in place and prevent erosion, and they help retain soil moisture and cool the atmosphere.

Organisation in nature builds up in a hierarchy:

**Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem**

## 12.5 Who Eats Whom? Food Chains and Trophic Levels

Every organism needs food, and how organisms get their food connects them to one another.

- **Producers (autotrophs)** — organisms that make their own food, like green plants, through photosynthesis. (*auto* = self + *troph* = food)
- **Consumers (heterotrophs)** — organisms that cannot make their own food and depend on others. (*hetero* = other + *troph* = food)

Consumers are further classified by what they eat:

| Type | What they eat | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Herbivores** | Only plants | Deer, hare |
| **Carnivores** | Only animals | Leopard |
| **Omnivores** | Both plants and animals | Crows, foxes, mice |

### Worked Example: Building a Food Chain

A **food chain** is a simple sequence showing "who eats whom" in an ecosystem. Let's build one step by step for a grassland with grass, grasshopper, frog, snake, and eagle:

1. Grass (producer) is eaten by the grasshopper.
2. The grasshopper is eaten by the frog.
3. The frog is eaten by the snake.
4. The snake is eaten by the eagle.

**Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle**

Each position in this chain is called a **trophic level** (*troph* = food):

1. **Producers** (green plants) — first trophic level
2. **Herbivores** (like hares, deer, grasshoppers) — second trophic level
3. **Small carnivores** (like frogs) — third trophic level
4. **Large carnivores** (like snakes, eagles, tigers) — occupy the next level(s)

> **Exam trap:** Students often confuse "food chain" with "food web" — a food chain is one straight-line sequence, while a food web is the *network* formed when many food chains interlink (see below). Also, remember producers always occupy the **first** trophic level, never any other.

If you counted organisms at each level (say, many grass plants, fewer grasshoppers, still fewer frogs, fewest eagles) and stacked them from most to least, you'd get a **pyramid shape** — this is why trophic levels are often drawn as an ecological pyramid, widest at the producer base.

### Food Web

In a real ecosystem, one organism is rarely eaten by just one type of predator — a mouse might be eaten by an owl, a fox, *and* a hawk. When multiple food chains interlink like this, they form a network called a **food web**.

## 12.6 What Happens to Waste in Nature?

Every organism eventually dies, and living things constantly produce waste. So why doesn't the world fill up with dead matter?

**Decomposers** (or **saprotrophs**, *sapro* = rotten + *troph* = food) — mainly fungi and bacteria — break down complex substances in dead plants and animals into simpler ones. Mushrooms growing on dead wood, or beetles and flies breaking down animal dung, are everyday examples.

This process is called **decomposition**, and it returns important nutrients to the soil, which plants then use to grow. In nature, nothing is wasted — everything is reused.

## 12.7 How Does One Small Change Lead to Another?

Ecosystems are chains of cause and effect. Consider this real sequence, all triggered by pollution:

**Plants in a pond die (due to pollution) → less oxygen in water → fish die → insects increase (fewer fish to eat them) → insects spread to nearby farmland → farmers use pesticides → further environmental harm**

A real historical example: in the 1980s, India exported large numbers of **Indian bullfrog** legs. This large-scale harvesting caused frog populations to crash. Since frogs eat insects, fewer frogs meant a *rise in agricultural pests*, forcing farmers to use more synthetic pesticides — which harmed soil, water, and human health. The Government of India eventually **banned the export of frog legs** to prevent further ecological damage.

> **Exam trap:** When asked to explain a "chain of events," don't just state the final outcome — examiners want you to show each *link* in the chain, in order, the way it's shown above.

An ecosystem stays in balance when interactions among organisms and their environment keep populations and resources stable. This balance is **dynamic, not fixed** — it can shift due to natural or human-made changes.

## 12.8 How Do Interactions Maintain Balance?

Organisms don't only eat each other — they also **compete** for shared resources like food, water, space, or sunlight. This competition is actually useful: it keeps any one species from multiplying uncontrollably, which keeps the ecosystem balanced.

Beyond competition and feeding, organisms form other kinds of relationships:

| Interaction | What happens | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Mutualism** | Both organisms benefit | Honeybees (get nectar) and flowers (get pollinated) |
| **Commensalism** | One benefits, the other is unaffected | Orchids (get physical support) growing on tree branches (unaffected) |
| **Parasitism** | One benefits, the other is harmed | Ticks (feed on blood) on a dog (gets skin irritation) |

> **Exam trap:** The easiest way to lose marks here is mixing up commensalism and mutualism. Ask yourself: does the *second* organism actually gain something? If yes → mutualism. If it's simply unaffected → commensalism. If it's harmed → parasitism.

## 12.9 What Do Ecosystems Give Us — and What Threatens Them?

Ecosystems aren't just background scenery — they actively support human life. Forests give us fresh air, fertile soil, food, fibres, timber, and medicines. Aquatic ecosystems give us water and food. Ecosystems also have aesthetic and recreational value.

### Case Study: The Sundarbans

The **Sundarbans**, located where the Ganges and Brahmaputra rivers meet India and Bangladesh, contain the **largest mangrove forest in the world** — a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987. These mangroves:

- Slow down strong winds and waves during storms and floods
- Absorb carbon dioxide and release oxygen
- Provide habitat to many endangered species

Yet the Sundarbans face serious threats: mangrove trees are cut for fuelwood and farming, illegal hunting continues, and industrial waste and untreated sewage pollute the water. Similar threats — deforestation, overuse of resources, invasive species, unsustainable land use, and pollution — affect ecosystems across India (forests, rivers, wetlands, grasslands, coasts). **Protected areas** like national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and biosphere reserves (e.g., Jim Corbett National Park, Manas National Park, Chilika Lake) help conserve these habitats for the future.

### Human-made Ecosystems

Fish ponds, farms, and parks are **human-made ecosystems**. When well designed, they can reduce pollution, support biodiversity, and give people recreational space — but unlike natural ecosystems, they need continuous human care and management.

### How Healthy Ecosystems Serve Our Farms

Between 1950 and 1965, India faced a food crisis due to low crop production. The **Green Revolution** — the use of tractors, machines, synthetic fertilisers, and pesticides in the mid-20th century — boosted food production and helped India become food secure.

However, these methods are now considered **unsustainable** because of:

- Overuse of synthetic chemicals, which reduces soil fertility by killing friendly microorganisms and lowering humus (organic matter that binds soil)
- Excessive groundwater extraction
- **Monoculture** — growing the same crop repeatedly, which reduces biodiversity and harms pollinators
- Reduced natural predator populations, which allows pests to increase
- Some pests developing resistance to pesticides

This is pushing many farmers toward **organic and natural farming methods** that minimise interference with natural ecosystems while still protecting food security.

## Quick Recap

- A **habitat** is a place providing the right living conditions for an organism; it has **biotic** (living) and **abiotic** (non-living) components.
- A **population** = same species, same habitat, same time. A **community** = different populations sharing a habitat.
- An **ecosystem** = biotic + abiotic components interacting together. Types: **aquatic** (ponds, rivers, lakes) and **terrestrial** (forests, farms, grasslands); farmland is a **human-made ecosystem**.
- Organisation hierarchy: **Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem**
- **Producers/autotrophs** make their own food (plants); **consumers/heterotrophs** depend on others — classified as **herbivores**, **carnivores**, **omnivores**.
- A **food chain** shows a straight-line "who eats whom" sequence; interlinked food chains form a **food web**. Each position in a chain is a **trophic level** (producers first, then herbivores, then carnivores).
- **Decomposers/saprotrophs** (fungi, bacteria) break down dead matter through **decomposition**, recycling nutrients back to soil.
- Small changes cascade through ecosystems — e.g., pond pollution → plant death → less oxygen → fish die → more insects → pest problems on farms.
- **Competition** for resources keeps populations balanced. Other relationships: **mutualism** (both benefit), **commensalism** (one benefits, other unaffected), **parasitism** (one benefits, one harmed).
- Ecosystems give humans air, water, food, medicine, and recreation — the **Sundarbans** mangrove forest is a key example under threat from deforestation, hunting, and pollution; **protected areas** help conserve such ecosystems.
- The **Green Revolution** boosted food production but caused unsustainable practices like **monoculture** and overuse of chemicals — pushing a shift toward sustainable, organic farming.

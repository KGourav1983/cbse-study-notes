## Answer Key

1. A **cell** is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms.

2. **Robert Hooke** first used the word "cell," in his 1665 book ***Micrographia***, after observing cork slices under a microscope and seeing small, honeycomb-like empty spaces.

3. The **cell membrane** encloses the cytoplasm and nucleus, is porous, and allows the entry of materials essential for life processes and the exit of waste material.

4. Bacteria have a **nucleoid** instead of a true nucleus.

5. ***Lactobacillus*** is responsible for turning milk into curd.

6. Yeast releases **carbon dioxide** during fermentation, which makes dough rise.

7. **Unicellular organisms** are made of a single cell that carries out all life functions by itself, e.g., **bacteria**. **Multicellular organisms** are made of many cells working together, each often performing specialised functions, e.g., **humans** or **plants**.

8. Two differences between plant and animal cells:
   - Plant cells have a **cell wall**, which is absent in animal cells.
   - Plant cells have **plastids/chloroplasts** for photosynthesis and a large **vacuole**; animal cells lack chloroplasts and usually have no vacuole (or only a small one).

9. Cell shape relates to function. **Muscle cells** are spindle-shaped, thin, and flexible, allowing them to contract and relax to produce movement. **Nerve cells** are long and branched, allowing them to reach different parts of the body and transmit messages quickly. Their shapes are adapted to the specific jobs they perform.

10. **Decomposition** is the process by which microorganisms break down complex substances in dead plants and animals into simpler, nutrient-rich substances that return to the soil. It is mainly carried out by **fungi and bacteria**.

11. Legume roots have nodules containing ***Rhizobium*** bacteria, which trap nitrogen from the air and add it to the soil. Growing legumes in rotation naturally restores soil **nitrogen fertility**, reducing the need for chemical fertilisers and keeping the soil healthy for the next crop.

12. A plant cell diagram should show, from outside to inside: the **cell wall** (outermost covering), **cell membrane** (just inside the cell wall), **cytoplasm** (filling the cell), **nucleus** (round structure, usually pushed to one side), a large central **vacuole**, and small oval **chloroplasts**/plastids scattered in the cytoplasm. All parts must be clearly labelled with straight label lines.
    *(Since this is a text-based answer key, the student's diagram should match the structure shown in Fig. 2.5(b) of the chapter — cell wall, cell membrane, nucleus, chloroplast, cytoplasm, and vacuole, all clearly labelled.)*

13. Curd contains bacteria such as ***Lactobacillus***, which ferments the sugar (lactose) present in milk into lactic acid, forming curd. These bacteria **grow well in warm conditions**, so their activity — and hence curd formation — is faster in warm milk. In cold milk, low temperature slows down bacterial growth and activity, so curdling is much slower or does not happen properly.

14. **Yeast** is a fungus that respires and breaks down sugar to release energy, producing **carbon dioxide** (which makes dough rise and become fluffy) and a small amount of **alcohol** (giving fermented dough its characteristic smell) — this is used in making bread, cakes, and similar foods. ***Lactobacillus*** is a bacterium that feeds on lactose in milk and ferments it, producing **lactic acid** instead of alcohol, which curdles the milk and gives curd its sour taste. Both are examples of fermentation by microorganisms, but they act on different substrates and yield different products.

15. (a) In Flask A, the **yeast respires and ferments the sugar solution**, breaking it down to release energy for its own life processes. This process releases **carbon dioxide gas** as a by-product, along with a small amount of alcohol.
    (b) The **lime water in Test tube B turns milky**. This happens because the carbon dioxide gas produced by the yeast in Flask A travels through the connecting tube into the lime water, and carbon dioxide reacts with lime water to turn it milky — confirming that CO₂ gas was released during fermentation.
    (c) If yeast had **not** been added to Flask A, no fermentation would take place, so **no carbon dioxide would be produced**, and the **lime water in Test tube B would remain clear and unchanged**.

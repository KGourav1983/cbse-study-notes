### Q1: What is a cell?
A **cell** is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. Every living being — unicellular or multicellular — is made up of one or more cells.

### Q2: Define microorganisms.
**Microorganisms** (or **microbes**) are tiny living organisms that cannot be seen with the naked eye and can only be observed using a microscope. They include bacteria, protozoa, algae, fungi, and viruses.

### Q3: What is a lens, and how did it lead to the invention of the microscope?
A **lens** is a curved piece of glass, thick in the middle and thin at the edges (like a lentil seed), which makes small objects look bigger. Over time, lenses were combined and improved to build **microscopes**, which allowed humans to finally see the tiny living world that was invisible to the naked eye.

### Q4: State the three basic parts of a cell.
The three basic parts of a cell are:
- **Cell membrane** – the outer boundary that encloses the cell
- **Nucleus** – the structure that regulates all cell activities and growth
- **Cytoplasm** – the material between the membrane and nucleus, where most life processes occur

### Q5: What is a cell wall? Which cells possess it?
The **cell wall** is a rigid outer covering present outside the cell membrane. It provides **rigidity and strength**. It is found in **plant cells, fungal cells, and bacterial cells**, but is absent in animal cells.

### Q6: What is the function of cytoplasm in a cell?
**Cytoplasm** fills the space between the cell membrane and the nucleus. It contains carbohydrates, proteins, fats, and mineral salts, and most of the **life processes** of the cell take place within it.

### Q7: Differentiate between unicellular and multicellular organisms, with one example each.
**Unicellular** organisms are made up of a single cell that performs all life functions on its own, e.g., **bacteria**. **Multicellular** organisms are made up of many cells that work together, e.g., **humans/plants**.

### Q8: Name four categories of microorganisms.
The four main categories of microorganisms are **bacteria, protozoa, algae, and fungi**. (Viruses are also microscopic but are acellular, so they are often treated as a separate category.)

### Q9: Differentiate between a plant cell and an animal cell.
| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---|---|---|
| **Cell wall** | Present | Absent |
| **Plastids/chloroplast** | Present | Absent |
| **Vacuole** | Large and prominent | Usually absent, or small if present |

The **cell wall** gives plant cells rigidity, while **chloroplasts** help them make food through photosynthesis — features animal cells lack.

### Q10: What is a nucleoid, and how does it differ from a nucleus?
A **nucleoid** is the region in a bacterial cell containing genetic material, but it is **not enclosed by a membrane**. A true **nucleus**, found in cells like plant, animal, yeast, and protozoan cells, is a well-defined structure enclosed by a nuclear membrane. This lack of a membrane-bound nucleus is a key feature distinguishing **bacteria** from other cell types.

### Q11: What are plastids? Name one type and state its function.
**Plastids** are tiny rod-shaped structures found in plant cells. One important type is the **chloroplast**, which contains **chlorophyll** and helps the plant carry out **photosynthesis**. In non-green plant parts, plastids help store substances.

### Q12: What is the function of the vacuole in plant and animal cells?
In plant cells, the **vacuole** is large and helps store important substances, remove waste, and maintain the shape of the cell, giving strength and support. In animal cells, vacuoles are usually absent or small, and when present, store substances dissolved in water.

### Q13: Why do muscle cells and nerve cells have different shapes?
Cell shape is related to the function a cell performs. **Muscle cells** are spindle-shaped, thin, and flexible so they can contract and relax to produce movement (e.g., pushing food through the food pipe). **Nerve cells (neurons)** are long and branched so they can reach different parts of the body and transmit messages quickly.

### Q14: Differentiate between the roles of yeast and *Lactobacillus* in fermentation.
**Yeast** is a unicellular fungus that respires and breaks down sugar, releasing **carbon dioxide** (which makes dough rise) and a small amount of **alcohol**. ***Lactobacillus*** is a bacterium that feeds on lactose (milk sugar) and produces **lactic acid**, which turns milk into sour **curd**. The key difference: yeast produces alcohol + CO₂, while *Lactobacillus* produces lactic acid.

### Q15: What is decomposition, and how do microorganisms help clean the environment?
**Decomposition** is the process by which microorganisms (mainly fungi and bacteria) break down the complex substances of dead plants, fallen leaves, and animal bodies into simpler, nutrient-rich substances. These nutrients return to the soil, helping plants grow, while also clearing dead matter from the environment — making microbes essential recyclers of nature.

### Q16: Explain how manure is formed from fruit and vegetable peels (with reference to soil microorganisms).
Soil contains various microorganisms like **fungi and bacteria**. When fruit and vegetable peels are buried in soil, these microorganisms act on the plant waste and slowly break it down into a simpler, dark-coloured, nutrient-rich substance called **manure**. This process needs the right **temperature and moisture** to occur efficiently, and manure formed this way increases soil fertility.

### Q17: What is biogas? Explain how it is formed and list its uses.
**Biogas** is a mixture of gases, mainly carbon dioxide and a large proportion of **methane**, produced when certain bacteria (living in oxygen-free environments) decompose plant and animal waste or household wastewater. Biogas is used as a fuel for **cooking, heating, generating electricity, and running vehicles**.

### Q18: What are root nodules? Explain the role of *Rhizobium* bacteria in them.
**Root nodules** are swollen regions found on the roots of certain legumes (like beans, peas, and lentils) that house ***Rhizobium*** bacteria. These bacteria **trap nitrogen from the air** and convert it into a form the plant can use, helping the plant grow better without needing chemical fertiliser.

### Q19: Why do farmers grow legumes in rotation with other crops?
Legume roots host **Rhizobium** bacteria, which fix atmospheric nitrogen into the soil. Growing legumes in rotation naturally **increases the nitrogen content of the soil**, keeping it fertile and healthy for the next, non-legume crop — reducing the need for chemical fertilisers.

### Q20: Why don't pickles and murabbas get spoiled by microorganisms even though microbes are everywhere?
Pickles and murabbas are prepared with high concentrations of **salt or sugar**, which act as **preservatives**. These high concentrations prevent microorganisms from growing on the food, protecting it from spoilage.

### Q21: In an experiment, dough made with yeast (bowl A) rises and becomes fluffy with a distinct smell, while dough without yeast (bowl B) does not. Explain why.
Yeast is a microorganism (a **fungus**) that respires and breaks down sugar in the dough to release energy. During this process, it releases **carbon dioxide**, which forms bubbles and makes the dough soft and fluffy. It also produces a small amount of **alcohol**, giving the dough its distinct smell. Since bowl B has no yeast, none of this fermentation occurs, so the dough remains unchanged.

### Q22: In an experiment, milk kept warm with added curd turns into curd, but milk kept cold does not. Explain why.
Curd contains bacteria such as ***Lactobacillus***, which feeds on the sugar (lactose) in milk, multiplies, and ferments the milk into curd, producing lactic acid. These bacteria **grow well in warm conditions**, so fermentation happens efficiently in the warm bowl. In the cold bowl, low temperature slows bacterial growth, so the milk does not curdle properly.

### Q23: What are microalgae? State two ways in which they are important.
**Microalgae** are microscopic, plant-like organisms found in water, soil, air, and on trees that make their own food using sunlight. They are important because:
- They release oxygen during food-making, producing **more than half of Earth's oxygen supply**.
- They serve as food for aquatic animals and are used by humans as **health supplements** (e.g., *Spirulina*) and in making **biofuel**.

### Q24: Describe the levels of organisation in a multicellular organism, giving an example at each level.
Multicellular organisms are organised as: **Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ system → Organism**. For example, muscle **cells** group to form muscle **tissue**; different tissues combine to form the **stomach (organ)**; the stomach along with other organs forms the **digestive system (organ system)**; and all organ systems together make up the complete **organism**, such as a human being.

### Q25: In an experiment, a flask with warm sugar solution and yeast is connected to a test tube containing lime water. After some hours, the lime water turns milky. Explain this observation, and state what would happen if yeast was not added.
The yeast in the flask respires and ferments the sugar, releasing **carbon dioxide gas**. This gas travels through the tube into the lime water. Carbon dioxide reacting with lime water turns it **milky**, confirming CO₂ was produced. If **yeast was not added**, no fermentation would occur, no carbon dioxide would be released, and the **lime water would remain unchanged (clear)**.

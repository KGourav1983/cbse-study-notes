## Answer Key

1. A **force** is a push or pull on an object resulting from the object's interaction with another object. Its SI unit is **newton (N)**.

2. **Friction** is the force that comes into play when an object moves or tries to move over another surface. It always acts in a direction opposite to the direction of motion.

3. **Weight** is the force with which the Earth pulls an object towards itself. Its SI unit is **newton (N)**.

4. **Buoyant force (upthrust)** is the force applied by a liquid on an object in the **upward direction** when the object is placed in the liquid.

5. The two kinds of static charges are **'positive'** and **'negative'**.

6. **Gravitational force is always an attractive force** — unlike magnetic or electrostatic force, which can be either attractive or repulsive.

7. **Contact forces** act only when there is physical contact between two objects, e.g., **muscular force** (caused by the action of muscles). **Non-contact forces** act even when the objects involved are not in contact, e.g., **magnetic force** (exerted by a magnet on another magnet or magnetic material).

8. **Mass** is the amount of matter in an object, measured in grams or kilograms, and its value **remains the same everywhere**. **Weight** is the gravitational force with which the Earth (or another planet) pulls an object, measured in newton, and it **can vary from place to place** since gravitational force is not the same everywhere.

9. A rough surface has **more irregularities** than a smooth surface. Since friction arises due to the irregularities in two surfaces locking into each other and opposing motion, a **rougher surface produces greater friction**. This greater frictional force decelerates the object more quickly, causing it to stop sooner than it would on a smoother surface with less friction.

10. **Archimedes' Principle** states that when an object is fully or partially immersed in a liquid, it experiences an upward force equal to the **weight of the liquid displaced** by the object.

11. We slip on smooth surfaces like ice or polished floors because such surfaces have **very few irregularities**, resulting in **very little friction** between our feet (or footwear) and the surface. Since friction is what normally helps us grip a surface and maintain balance while walking, the reduced friction on smooth surfaces makes it easier for our feet to slide, causing us to slip.

12. **Activity:** Two balloons are inflated and hung close together (without touching) from a table. Both balloons are rubbed with a woollen cloth and then released.

    **Observation 1:** The two rubbed balloons **move away from each other**, as if repelling one another.

    **Observation 2:** When the same woollen cloth (used for rubbing) is brought close to one of the rubbed balloons, the balloon and the cloth **move towards each other**, as if attracting one another.

    **Conclusions:**
    - Since both balloons were charged in the **same way** (by rubbing with the same cloth), they acquired **similar (like) charges**. Since they repelled each other, we conclude that **like charges repel each other**.
    - The balloon and the woollen cloth, on the other hand, acquired **opposite kinds of charge** during the rubbing process — the balloon gets one type of charge while the cloth gets the other type. Since they attracted each other, we conclude that **unlike charges attract each other**.
    - This establishes that there are **two kinds of static charges** (positive and negative), and that the **force exerted by a charged body on another charged or uncharged body is called electrostatic force**, which is a non-contact force.

13. (a) The weight difference between the marks 0 N and 1 N is **1 N**, and there are **5 divisions** between these marks. Therefore, the value of one small division is calculated as:
    $$\text{One small division} = \frac{1\text{ N}}{5} = 0.2\text{ N}$$
    The smallest value of weight that this spring balance can measure is **0.2 N**.

    (b) When the ball is thrown **vertically upward**, its **speed goes on decreasing** as it moves up, due to the pull of the **gravitational force** acting downward on it, opposing its upward motion. The ball eventually comes to a **momentary stop at its topmost point**, at which instant its **direction of motion changes** from upward to downward. As the ball then falls **downward** under the continued action of the same gravitational force, its **speed goes on increasing** until it reaches the ground. Throughout this entire motion — both while going up and coming down — the force responsible for these changes in speed and direction is the **gravitational force** exerted by the Earth, which is always directed **downward, towards the Earth**. This type of motion, where an object moves in a vertical direction under the influence of gravitational force, is called **vertical motion**.

### Q1: Define force.
A **force** is a push or pull on an object resulting from the object's interaction with another object.

### Q2: State the SI unit of force and its symbol.
The SI unit of force is **newton**, and its symbol is **N**.

### Q3: What is muscular force?
The force resulting due to the action of muscles in our body is called **muscular force**. It occurs when muscles **contract and elongate** while performing an activity such as walking, running, lifting, or jumping.

### Q4: Define friction.
The force that comes into play when an object moves or tries to move over another surface is called the **force of friction**, or simply **friction**.

### Q5: What is magnetic force?
The force exerted by a magnet on another magnet or a magnetic material is called **magnetic force**. Since it acts from a distance without contact, it is a non-contact force.

### Q6: Define electrostatic force.
The force exerted by a charged body on another charged body or an uncharged body is called **electrostatic force**. It is a non-contact force.

### Q7: Define gravitational force.
The force with which the Earth attracts objects towards itself is called the **gravitational force**, also known as the force of gravity or simply gravity.

### Q8: What is the weight of an object? State its SI unit.
The **weight** of an object is the force with which the Earth pulls the object towards itself. Since weight is a force, its SI unit is also **newton (N)**.

### Q9: What is buoyant force (upthrust)?
The force applied by a liquid on an object in the **upward direction** is known as **upthrust** or **buoyant force**.

### Q10: List four effects that a force applied on an object may have.
A force applied on an object may:
- Make the object **move from rest**
- **Change the speed** of the object if it is moving
- **Change the direction** of motion of the object
- Bring about a **change in the shape** of the object

### Q11: Differentiate between contact forces and non-contact forces, giving one example of each.
**Contact forces** act only when there is physical contact between objects, e.g., **muscular force**. **Non-contact forces** act even when objects are not in contact, e.g., **magnetic force**.

### Q12: Differentiate between mass and weight.
**Mass** is the amount of matter in an object, measured in grams or kilograms, and remains the **same everywhere**. **Weight** is the gravitational force with which the Earth (or another planet) pulls an object, measured in newton, and **varies from place to place** since gravitational force can differ across locations.

### Q13: Why does an object at rest not necessarily mean that no force is acting on it?
If an object is at rest, it does not mean no force is acting on it — it means that the **forces acting on the object are balancing one another**, resulting in no net change in the object's state.

### Q14: Explain why friction is called a contact force, and state the direction in which it acts.
Friction is called a **contact force** because it arises due to **two surfaces being in contact** with each other. Friction **always acts in a direction opposite to the direction in which the object is moving or trying to move**.

### Q15: What causes friction between two surfaces, even if they appear smooth?
Friction arises due to **irregularities in the two surfaces** that are in contact. Even surfaces that appear smooth actually have a large number of minute irregularities. When placed in contact, the irregularities of the two surfaces **lock into each other**, opposing any effort to move one surface over the other.

### Q16: State two kinds of static charges, and explain how like and unlike charges behave with reference to the balloon experiment.
The two kinds of static charges are called **'positive'** and **'negative'**. In the balloon experiment, two balloons rubbed with the same woollen cloth acquired similar charges and **repelled** each other when brought close, showing that **like charges repel each other**. When the woollen cloth (with an opposite kind of charge) was brought near a rubbed balloon, they **attracted** each other, showing that **unlike charges attract each other**.

### Q17: Explain why an object dropped in water experiences an upward force, and state the condition under which the object will float or sink.
When an object is placed in a liquid, it experiences an upward force called **buoyant force** (or upthrust) exerted by the liquid, in addition to the downward **gravitational force** acting on it. If the gravitational force is **more than** the buoyant force, the object **sinks**; if the two forces are **equal**, the object **floats**.

### Q18: Explain, with reference to an object thrown vertically upward, how its speed and direction change during its motion (before it stops at the top and while it falls back).
While the ball moves **upward**, its speed goes on **decreasing** due to the downward pull of gravitational force, until it comes to a **momentary stop** at the topmost point; at this instant, its direction of motion changes. While it moves **downward**, under the same gravitational force, its speed goes on **increasing** as it falls back towards the ground. This motion in a vertical direction under the influence of gravitational force is called **vertical motion**.

### Q19: State Archimedes' Principle, and explain its significance in determining whether an object floats or sinks.
**Archimedes' Principle** states that when an object is fully or partially immersed in a liquid, it experiences an upward force equal to the **weight of the liquid it displaces**. If the weight of the liquid displaced by the object is **smaller** than the weight of the object, the object will **sink** in the liquid; if the weight of the liquid displaced is **equal** to the weight of the object, the object will **float** in the liquid.

### Q20: Explain, with the help of the activity involving ring magnets, how magnetic force is demonstrated to be a non-contact force.
When two ring magnets are placed on a vertical stick with their **like poles facing each other**, the second (upper) magnet **stays floating above the first magnet**, without touching it, because the repulsive force between the like poles balances the gravitational pull on it. When gently pushed down, a force is felt resisting the push. This demonstrates that **a magnet can exert force on another magnet without being in physical contact with it**, confirming that magnetic force is a **non-contact force**.

### Q21: A spring balance has a range of 0 to 10 N, with 5 divisions between each pair of consecutive 1 N marks. Calculate the smallest value of weight that this spring balance can measure.
The weight difference between two consecutive bigger marks (e.g., between 0 N and 1 N) is **1 N**. Since there are **5 divisions** between these marks, the value of one small division is calculated as:
$$\text{One small division} = \frac{1\text{ N}}{5} = 0.2\text{ N}$$
Therefore, the smallest value of weight that this spring balance can measure is **0.2 N**.

### Q22: The mass of an object is 1 kg on the Earth, the Moon, and Mars, but its weight is different on each — 10 N on Earth, 1.6 N on the Moon, and 3.8 N on Mars. Explain why the mass remains the same while the weight changes.
**Mass** is the amount of matter contained in an object, and this amount does not change regardless of location — hence the mass remains **1 kg** on the Earth, Moon, and Mars. **Weight**, however, is the **gravitational force** with which a planet or moon pulls the object towards itself, and this gravitational force is **different for the Earth, Moon, and Mars** due to their differing masses and sizes. Since weight depends on the strength of gravitational pull, and this pull varies from place to place, the **weight of the same object differs** at each location even though its mass stays constant.

### Q23: A ball rolling on a rough floor stops sooner than the same ball rolling on a smooth floor. Explain this observation with reference to the concept of friction.
The ball rolling on a floor experiences a **force of friction** acting in a direction opposite to its motion, which gradually slows it down until it stops. Friction arises due to the **irregularities present in the two surfaces in contact** — the floor and the ball. A **rough floor has more irregularities** than a smooth floor, causing **greater friction** between the ball and the rough floor. This greater frictional force decelerates the ball more quickly, causing it to **stop sooner** on the rough floor compared to the smoother floor, where friction is comparatively less.

### Q24: Explain, using the concept of electrostatic force, why a plastic scale rubbed with polythene is able to attract small pieces of paper even without touching them.
When the plastic scale is rubbed vigorously with polythene, **electrical charges (static charges) build up on the surface of the scale**, making it a **charged object**. A charged object exerts a force — called **electrostatic force** — on uncharged objects made of certain materials, such as small pieces of paper. Since this force acts even when the scale and the paper pieces are **not in contact**, it is classified as a **non-contact force**. This is why the charged scale is able to attract and pull the small pieces of paper towards itself without physically touching them.

### Q25: A coin dropped into a glass of water sinks, but a larger wooden block placed in water floats. Explain this difference in terms of the forces acting on each object.
Both the coin and the wooden block experience two opposing forces when placed in water: the downward **gravitational force** (their weight) and the upward **buoyant force** exerted by the water. For the **coin**, the gravitational force acting on it is **greater than** the buoyant force provided by the water it displaces, so the coin **sinks**. For the **wooden block**, despite being larger, the gravitational force acting on it is **balanced by (equal to)** the buoyant force exerted by the water — meaning the weight of water displaced by the block equals the block's own weight — so the block **floats**. This difference arises because the buoyant force depends on factors such as the volume of liquid displaced and the density of the liquid, and different materials interact differently with these factors even when placed in the same liquid.

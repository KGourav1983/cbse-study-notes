# Exploring Forces

Have you ever wondered why it's suddenly so much easier to pedal your bicycle once you crest a hill and start heading down — even though you've stopped pushing the pedals entirely? Something invisible is at work. This chapter is about naming and understanding exactly what that "something" is: **force**.

## 5.1 What Is a Force?

Try this thought experiment: how many different ways can you move a large cardboard box? You could push it, pull it with a rope, lift it up, or carry it. Notice something? Every single one of these methods involves the same basic action in disguise.

**Generally, the push or pull applied on an object is called force in science.**

That's it — no matter how complicated something feels (a hurricane, a rocket launch, a gentle nudge), if you trace it back, it's a push or a pull.

## 5.2 What Can a Force Do to the Objects on Which It Is Applied?

Think about everyday moments: your friend grabs your moving bicycle from behind to stop it, you hit a moving ball with a bat, you press an inflated balloon. Each of these involves a force — and each one *does* something different to the object.

**The force applied on an object may:**
- Make an object move from rest
- Change the speed of an object if it is moving
- Change the direction of motion of an object
- Bring about a change in the shape of an object
- Cause **some or all** of these effects together

> **Exam trap:** A single force can cause more than one effect at once — for example, hitting a moving ball with a bat can change both its **speed** and its **direction**. Don't assume only one effect happens per force.

**Important nuance:** none of these changes — in speed, direction, or shape — happen *without* the action of a force. If you see any of these changes happening to an object, you can be sure a force is behind it.

> A step further: If an object is at **rest**, does that mean no force is acting on it? Not necessarily — it could mean the forces acting on it are **balancing one another**. (You'll study balanced forces in detail in higher grades.)

## 5.3 Are Forces an Interaction Between Two or More Objects?

Here's something easy to overlook: when you push a table, it's not just "your hand doing something" — it's your hand and the table **interacting**. Look back at all the examples above (friend and bicycle, bat and ball, hand and balloon) — every single one involves **two objects**.

This gives us the full, formal definition:

**A force is a push or pull on an object resulting from the object's interaction with another object.**

**The SI unit of force is newton** (written with a small 'n') **and its symbol is N.**

> A step further: When you push a table, do you feel a force on your hand too? Yes — and the moment you stop pushing, that force on your hand disappears as well. **Whenever two objects interact, each object experiences a force from the other.** Once the interaction stops, neither object experiences the force anymore.

## 5.4 What Are the Different Types of Forces?

### 5.4.1 Contact Forces

**Forces that act only when there is physical contact between objects are called contact forces.** This contact can be **direct** (using your hands) or **indirect** (using a stick or rope).

#### Muscular Force

Every time you walk, run, lift, push, jump, or stretch, the force involved comes from the action of your muscles. **The force resulting due to the action of muscles is known as muscular force.** It occurs when muscles **contract and elongate** while doing any activity.

- Animals, birds, fish, and insects all use muscular forces for movement and survival.
- Humans have long used the muscular force of animals (like bullocks and horses) to carry out tasks such as ploughing fields or pulling carts.
- Muscular force even works **inside your body** — it helps you chew food and push it through the alimentary canal during digestion, and the expansion/contraction of your **heart muscles** circulates blood throughout your body.

#### Friction

Have you ever noticed that a ball rolling on flat ground eventually stops on its own, with nobody touching it? Or that your bicycle slows down and stops if you stop pedalling, even on flat ground? Something is clearly acting on these objects, even though nothing seems to be touching them in an obvious "pushing" way.

The answer: **the force that comes into play when an object moves or tries to move over another surface is called the force of friction or simply friction.**

Key facts about friction:
- **Friction always acts in a direction opposite to the direction in which the object is moving or trying to move.**
- Friction is a **contact force**, since it arises due to two surfaces in contact.
- Friction arises due to **irregularities in the two surfaces in contact** — even surfaces that look smooth actually have tiny irregularities. When two surfaces touch, these irregularities lock into each other, opposing motion.
- **Friction depends on the nature of the surfaces in contact** — it's greater on rough surfaces and smaller on smoother ones. (This is why an object placed on glass, cloth, wood, or sand each stops after travelling a different distance.)

> **Exam trap:** Don't say friction only acts on solid surfaces. **Air, water, and other liquids also exert friction** on objects moving through them — this is why aeroplanes, ships, boats, and high-speed trains are specially shaped to reduce friction from air or water.

### 5.4.2 Non-Contact Forces

**Forces whose effect can be experienced even when objects are not in contact are called non-contact forces.**

#### Magnetic Force

You already know magnets attract objects made of magnetic materials, and that **like poles repel** (North-North, South-South) while **unlike poles attract** (North-South). Here's the key new idea: this attraction/repulsion happens **without the magnets touching each other**.

**The force exerted by a magnet on another magnet or a magnetic material is called magnetic force.** Since it acts from a distance without contact, it is a **non-contact force**.

(A classic demonstration: two ring magnets placed on a vertical stick with like poles facing each other — the upper magnet actually **floats** above the lower one due to this repulsive force!)

#### Electrostatic Force

Rub a plastic scale or straw vigorously with polythene, then bring it close to small pieces of paper (without touching them) — the paper pieces get **pulled towards** the scale.

**Why does this happen?**
- When two objects of certain materials are rubbed together, **electrical charges build up on their surfaces**. These are called **static charges** because they don't move by themselves.
- An object that acquires static charges is called a **charged object**.
- A charged object exerts a force on uncharged objects (like paper pieces), even without touching them.

**Worked example — Two rubbed balloons:**
- Two balloons are rubbed with a woollen cloth and released near each other → they **move away from each other (repel)**.
- The same woollen cloth is then brought near one of the rubbed balloons → the balloon and cloth **move towards each other (attract)**.

**What this tells us:**
- Both balloons acquired the **same (similar) kind of charge** — since they repelled each other, we conclude that **like charges repel**.
- The balloon and the woollen cloth acquired **opposite kinds of charge** (one got charged while rubbing the other) — since they attracted each other, we conclude that **unlike charges attract**.
- The two kinds of static charges are called **'positive'** and **'negative'**.

**The force exerted by a charged body on another charged body or an uncharged body is called electrostatic force. It is a non-contact force.**

> **Exam trap:** Students often confuse magnetic force and electrostatic force since both can attract objects from a distance. Remember: magnetic force involves **magnets and magnetic materials**; electrostatic force involves **charged objects created by rubbing**. Also, unlike magnetic force, electrostatic charge is generated through friction/rubbing between materials.

> A step further: When charges **move**, they constitute an **electric current** in a circuit — the very same current that makes a lamp glow, or produces heating and magnetic effects (as studied in the previous chapter).

#### Gravitational Force

Throw a ball straight up — hard or gently, it doesn't matter — it **always comes back down**. This happens with every object you can throw, drop, or launch upward.

**Since all objects fall towards the Earth, it means the Earth attracts (pulls) them. The force with which the Earth attracts objects towards itself is called the gravitational force.** It's also called the **force of gravity**, or simply **gravity**.

Key properties of gravitational force:
- It acts **without contact** — so it's a **non-contact force**.
- **Gravitational force is always an attractive force** — unlike magnetic or electrostatic force, which can be either attractive or repulsive.

**Vertical motion under gravity:**
- When an object is **dropped from a height**, it takes a **straight vertical path downward** before touching the ground.
- When an object is **thrown vertically upward**, it moves up straight, **slows down**, **stops momentarily** at the top, and then takes a straight vertical path back down.
- While going up, **speed decreases** until the object momentarily stops (its direction then changes); while coming down, **speed increases**.
- This motion — moving in a vertical direction under the influence of gravitational force — is called **vertical motion**.

## 5.5 Weight and Its Measurement

**The force with which the Earth pulls an object towards itself is called the weight of the object.** Weight measures **how strongly** an object is pulled by the Earth. Since weight is a force, **its SI unit is also newton (N)**.

**Does the Earth pull every object with equal force?** No! When different objects (of different masses) are hung one by one from a spring, the spring **stretches by different amounts** for each object — showing that the Earth pulls different objects with **different forces**, i.e., their weights are different.

### The Spring Balance

A **spring balance** is a device used to measure weight (force). It consists of a spring fixed at one end with a hook at the other end. When an object is hung from the hook, the spring stretches, and the amount of stretching indicates the object's weight. Spring balances usually have two scales — one marked in **newton (weight)** and another in **gram (corresponding mass, assuming Earth's gravity)**.

### Worked Example — Reading a Spring Balance Scale

Suppose a spring balance has a maximum reading of **10 N**, meaning its range is 0 to 10 N.

**Step 1:** Find the weight difference between two consecutive bigger marks (e.g., between 0 N and 1 N, or between 1 N and 2 N).
→ This difference is **1 N**.

**Step 2:** Count the number of smaller divisions between these two bigger marks.
→ There are **5 divisions**.

**Step 3:** Calculate the value of one small division:
$$\text{One small division} = \frac{1\text{ N}}{5} = 0.2\text{ N}$$

**Conclusion:** The smallest value that this spring balance can read is **0.2 N**.

> **Exam trap:** Always check the specific range and smallest division of the spring balance you're using — different balances can have different scales, so this calculation must be redone for each instrument.

### Mass vs. Weight

Students often use "mass" and "weight" interchangeably in daily life, but in science, they are **not the same**:

| Feature | Mass | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| What it measures | Amount of matter in an object | Gravitational force pulling the object |
| SI unit | gram (g) or kilogram (kg) | newton (N) |
| Changes with location? | **No** — remains the same everywhere | **Yes** — varies slightly across Earth, and significantly on other planets |

**Worked example — Weight on different planets (mass = 1 kg in each case):**

| Location | Earth | Moon | Mars | Venus | Jupiter |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Weight of 1 kg object | 10 N | 1.6 N | 3.8 N | 9 N | 25.4 N |

Notice the mass stays exactly **1 kg** everywhere, but the weight changes dramatically depending on the gravitational pull of that location.

> **Exam trap:** In casual speech we say "the weight of the wheat bag is 10 kg" — but scientifically this is **incorrect**. Kilogram is a unit of **mass**, not weight. The correct scientific term for a force measured in kg-equivalent should actually be expressed in **newtons**. Examiners specifically test this mass-weight mix-up.

Since the weight of an object is *almost* the same everywhere on Earth, it's practically acceptable to measure an object's weight (using a spring balance) to indirectly determine its mass — or you can compare weights using a beam balance.

## 5.6 Floating and Sinking

Ever pulled a mug of water out of a bucket and noticed it feels *lighter* while still underwater, but suddenly feels heavier the moment it clears the surface? That's the buoyant force at work.

**Activity insight:** Push an empty, tightly-closed bottle into a bucket of water. You'll feel an **upward push**, and if released, the bottle **bounces back up** to the surface.

**This indicates that water applies a force on the bottle in the upward direction. In fact, all liquids apply a similar force.**

**The force applied by a liquid on an object in the upward direction is known as upthrust or buoyant force.**

**How floating and sinking are decided:**
- When an object is placed in a liquid, **gravitational force** pulls it **downward**, while **buoyant force** pushes it **upward**.
- If the gravitational force is **more than** the buoyant force → the object **sinks**.
- If the two forces are **equal** → the object **floats**.
- One factor the buoyant force depends on is the **density of the liquid**.

> A step further — **Archimedes' Principle:** When an object is fully or partially immersed in a liquid, it experiences an upward force equal to the **weight of the liquid it displaces**. If the weight of liquid displaced is **smaller** than the weight of the object, the object sinks. If the weight of liquid displaced **equals** the weight of the object, it floats.

> **Ever heard of:** **Pumice** is a volcanic rock that can actually float on water! It forms when lava containing lots of gas and water vapour cools quickly, trapping tiny gas bubbles inside — creating a light, porous rock that is less dense than water.

## Quick Recap

- A **force** is a push or pull on an object resulting from its interaction with another object; the **SI unit of force is newton (N)**.
- A force can: make an object move from rest, change its speed, change its direction of motion, change its shape, or cause a combination of these effects.
- Forces require **at least two objects interacting**; when interaction stops, the force stops too.
- **Contact forces** act only with physical contact — examples: **muscular force** (from muscle action) and **friction** (opposes relative motion between two surfaces, arises from surface irregularities, greater on rough surfaces, and also acts in liquids/gases).
- **Non-contact forces** act without physical contact — examples: **magnetic force** (magnets/magnetic materials), **electrostatic force** (charged objects; like charges repel, unlike charges attract), and **gravitational force** (Earth's pull, always attractive).
- **Weight** is the force with which the Earth pulls an object; its SI unit is **newton (N)**, measured using a **spring balance**.
- **Mass** (in g/kg) stays constant everywhere; **weight** (in N) varies with location/gravity — e.g., 1 kg weighs 10 N on Earth but only 1.6 N on the Moon.
- **Buoyant force (upthrust)** is the upward force exerted by a liquid on an object placed in it; if gravitational force > buoyant force, the object sinks; if equal, it floats — this follows **Archimedes' Principle**.

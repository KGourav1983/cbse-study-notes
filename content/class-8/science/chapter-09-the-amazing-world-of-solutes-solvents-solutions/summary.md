# The Amazing World of Solutes, Solvents, and Solutions

Have you ever made ORS at home and wondered why every single sip tastes exactly the same — never saltier, never sweeter, no matter which part of the glass you drink from? That one everyday observation is actually the doorway into everything this chapter teaches: how substances dissolve, how much they can dissolve, and why some things float while others sink.

## Uniform vs Non-uniform Mixtures — A Quick Recall

When you dissolve sugar and salt in water, the components spread out evenly throughout — that's why every sip of ORS tastes the same. But mix chalk powder, sand, or sawdust with water, and the components don't distribute evenly at all.

- **Uniform mixtures**: components are evenly distributed throughout (e.g., sugar + salt + water).
- **Non-uniform mixtures**: components are not evenly distributed (e.g., sand + water, sawdust + water).

## 9.1 What Are Solute, Solvent, and Solution?

> **Solution**: a uniform mixture, such as salt or sugar dissolved in water.

When a solid is mixed with a liquid to form a solution:
- The solid component is called the **solute**.
- The liquid component is called the **solvent**.
- The solute dissolves in the solvent to form a solution.

> **Solute + Solvent → Solution**

When a solution is formed by mixing **two liquids**, it's not always obvious which one is "dissolving" the other. In such cases:
- The substance present in the **smaller amount** is the solute.
- The substance present in the **larger amount** is the solvent.

> **Exam trap:** Don't assume the solute is always the substance present in the smaller quantity in *every* case. In the *Chashni* (sugar syrup) used for Gulab jamun, a *large* amount of sugar (solid) is dissolved in a *small* amount of water (liquid) — yet water is still considered the solvent and sugar the solute. The solid-in-liquid rule (solid = solute, liquid = solvent) takes priority over the "smaller amount" rule, which only applies when mixing two liquids.

## 9.2 How Much Solute Can a Fixed Amount of Solvent Dissolve?

Ever tried stirring in "just one more spoon" of sugar into your tea, only to watch it sit stubbornly at the bottom no matter how hard you stir? That's your tea reaching its dissolving limit.

**Activity 9.1** shows this clearly: as you keep adding spoonfuls of salt to a fixed amount of water and stirring, the salt initially dissolves completely. After a few spoons, a stage comes where the added salt no longer dissolves completely and settles at the bottom — the water has reached its limit.

- **Unsaturated solution**: a solution in which more solute can still be dissolved at a given temperature.
- **Saturated solution**: a solution in which the solute stops dissolving and begins to settle at the bottom, at a particular temperature.

### Concentration, dilute, and concentrated

> **Concentration**: the amount of solute present in a fixed quantity of solution (or solvent).

Based on concentration, solutions can be called:
- **Dilute solution**: less amount of solute.
- **Concentrated solution**: more amount of solute.

> **Exam trap:** "Dilute" and "concentrated" are **relative terms**, not fixed categories — a solution is only dilute or concentrated *compared to another solution*. For example, 1 spoon of salt dissolved in water is dilute compared to 2 spoons dissolved in the same amount of water, but might still be more concentrated than a different solution with even less salt.

> **Solubility**: the maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a fixed quantity of the solvent (at a particular temperature).

### 9.2.1 How does temperature affect the solubility of a solute?

**Activity 9.2 (baking soda in water):**
- At **20 °C**, a beaker of water dissolves a certain amount of baking soda before it stops dissolving (saturation reached).
- Heating the same setup to **50 °C** dissolves the previously undissolved baking soda — more can now be added before it saturates again.
- Heating further to **70 °C** again dissolves the undissolved baking soda.

**Conclusion:** Water at 70 °C dissolves more baking soda than water at 50 °C, and water at 20 °C dissolves the least of all. In general, **for most substances, solubility increases with an increase in temperature**. This also means that a saturated solution at one temperature can behave like an **unsaturated solution** if heated further.

> **Our scientific heritage:** Water has traditionally been used as a solvent in **Ayurveda**, ***Siddha***, and other Indian medicine systems for preparing formulations. Hydro-alcoholic extracts of herbs, as well as oils, ghee, and milk, have also been used as solvents to achieve therapeutic benefits.

> **Be a scientist:** **Asima Chatterjee**, renowned for developing anti-epileptic and anti-malarial drugs, used solvents and solutions extensively to extract and isolate compounds from medicinal plants. She was the second Indian woman to earn a Doctorate of Science (after Janaki Ammal), the first woman to receive the Shanti Swarup Bhatnagar Award in chemical science, and was honoured with the Padma Bhushan.

## 9.3 Solubility of Gases

Many gases, including **oxygen**, dissolve in water — and even though oxygen dissolves only to a small extent, this small dissolved quantity is what sustains all aquatic life (plants, fishes, and other organisms).

- A mixture of gases dissolved in water is a **uniform mixture**, since gases dissolve evenly.
- **Solubility of gases generally decreases as temperature increases** — this is the *opposite* trend compared to solids dissolving in liquids.

> **Exam trap:** Students often assume all substances follow the same "solubility increases with heat" rule. Remember the contrast: **solids** in liquids → solubility usually **increases** with temperature; **gases** in liquids → solubility usually **decreases** with temperature. This is why cold water holds more dissolved oxygen, keeping aquatic life healthy, while warmer water holds less.

## 9.4 Why Do Objects Float or Sink in Water?

Husk floats when you wash rice, but the rice itself sinks. Oil floats on water. Generally, it's believed that objects that **float** in a liquid are **lighter**, and objects that **sink** are **heavier**, than the liquid.

But here's the catch: a wooden stick and an iron rod of the *same size* — the iron rod feels much heavier. This "heaviness for a given size" is a special property called **density**.

> **Note:** The density of a substance is *not* the only factor that decides whether it will float or sink in a particular liquid.

## 9.5 What Is Density?

Think of a crowded bus with people packed tightly together (high density) versus the same bus with just a few people (low density). Or a dense forest with trees close together versus a sparse one with trees far apart.

> **Density**: the mass present in a unit volume of a substance.

**Density = Mass / Volume**

- Density is **independent of shape or size**.
- Density **depends on temperature and pressure**.
- Pressure mainly affects the density of **gases**; its effect on solids and liquids is negligible.

### Units of density

The SI units of mass and volume are **kilogram (kg)** and **cubic metre (m³)**, so the SI unit of density is **kilogram per cubic metre (kg/m³)**. For liquids, other convenient units are also used: **gram per millilitre (g/mL)** and **gram per cubic centimetre (g/cm³)**.

**Conversion factor:**
> 1 kg/m³ = 1000 g/m³ = 1000 g/1000 L = 1 g/L = 1 g/1000 mL = 1 g/1000 cm³

Since the mass of 1 mL of water is close to 1 g at room temperature, 10 mL of water is approximately 10 g, and 100 mL of water is approximately 100 g.

### Worked example: Relative density

Suppose an aluminium block has a mass of 27 g and a volume of 10 cm³.

**Step 1:** Apply the density formula.
$$\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{27\ \text{g}}{10\ \text{cm}^3} = 2.7\ \text{g/cm}^3$$

**Step 2:** Interpret this using **relative density**.

> **Relative density of a substance with respect to water = Density of that substance / Density of water at that temperature**

Since aluminium's density (2.7 g/cm³) is 2.7 times that of water (1 g/cm³), we say the **relative density of aluminium with respect to water is 2.7**. Relative density is a **number without any units**.

### 9.5.1 Determination of Density

To calculate density, we need both **mass** and **volume**.

**Measuring mass (Activity 9.3):**
- **Mass**: the quantity of matter present in an object; measured using a **balance** (e.g., a digital weighing balance).
- Steps: switch on the balance, tare/reset it to zero, place the object, and record the reading.

> **A step further:** **Mass** and **weight** are often used interchangeably, but they are scientifically different. **Mass** is the quantity of matter in an object (units: g, kg). **Weight** is the force by which the Earth attracts an object (units: **newtons, N**). Most balances (except two-pan balances) actually measure weight but display values in mass units.

**Measuring volume:**
- **Volume**: the space occupied by an object; SI unit is **cubic metre (m³)**.
- Smaller volumes are conveniently measured in **cubic centimetre (cm³)** or **decimetre cube (dm³)**.
- Volume of liquids is expressed in **litres (L)**, equivalent to 1 dm³; a common submultiple is **millilitre (mL)**, equivalent to 1 cm³.
- A **measuring cylinder** is used to measure liquid volume — a narrow, transparent cylindrical container with markings.

> **Exam trap:** Students often think a wider, larger-capacity measuring cylinder is always "better" for accuracy. In fact, the **smaller the cylinder's capacity, generally the finer its measurement scale** (e.g., 0.1 mL smallest reading for a 10–25 mL cylinder, 1 mL for a 100 mL cylinder, 2 mL for a 250 mL cylinder, 5 mL for a 500 mL cylinder) — so choosing a cylinder that lets you measure the required volume in **one step, with the finest possible scale**, gives the best accuracy.

**Reading a measuring cylinder correctly (Activity 9.5):** Water in a measuring cylinder forms a curved surface called the **meniscus**.
- For **colourless liquids** (like water), read the mark that coincides with the **bottom** of the meniscus.
- For **coloured liquids**, read the mark that coincides with the **top** of the meniscus.
- Eyes should be level with the bottom of the meniscus while taking the reading.

**Volume of regular solids (Activity 9.6):** Use the formula **Volume = l × w × h** for cuboid-shaped objects.

**Worked example:** A notebook has length = 25 cm, width = 18 cm, height = 2 cm.
$$\text{Volume} = l \times w \times h = 25\ \text{cm} \times 18\ \text{cm} \times 2\ \text{cm} = 900\ \text{cm}^3$$

**Volume of irregular solids (Activity 9.7) — Displacement method:**
1. Fill a measuring cylinder with water to a known volume (initial volume, A).
2. Tie the object with a thread and lower it into the cylinder.
3. Record the new, higher volume (final volume, B).
4. **Volume of the object = B − A** (the volume of water displaced).

**Worked example:** A stone is lowered into a measuring cylinder. Initial volume = 50 mL, final volume = 55 mL.
$$\text{Volume of stone} = 55\ \text{mL} - 50\ \text{mL} = 5\ \text{mL} = 5\ \text{cm}^3$$

**Worked example: Calculating density of the stone.** If the stone's mass (measured by balance) is 16.400 g and its volume (from displacement) is 5 cm³:
$$\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{16.400\ \text{g}}{5\ \text{cm}^3} = 3.28\ \text{g/cm}^3$$

> **Let us dig deeper!** Earth is made of layers — **crust, upper mantle, lower mantle, outer core, and inner core** — each with a different density range. The **crust** (outermost) is the **lightest**, and density **increases toward the centre**, since pressure and temperature rise deeper inside the Earth, making materials heavier and more compact.

> **Ever heard of...** Before large ships existed, people used **bamboo rafts and hollowed wooden logs** to travel across rivers and seas — bamboo floats easily because it is light and hollow. Such traditional boats are still used today in some regions, both for transport and as tourist attractions.

### 9.5.2 Effect of Temperature on Density

> Generally, the **density of a substance decreases with heating and increases with cooling**.

This connects back to the *Particulate Nature of Matter* chapter: as temperature increases, particles of a substance (solid, liquid, or gas) move apart and spread out. This **increases volume** while **mass stays the same** — and since Density = Mass/Volume, an increase in volume with constant mass means **density decreases**.

- This explains why **hot air rises**: it is less dense than the cooler air around it.
- **Hot air balloons** work on exactly this principle.

### 9.5.3 Effect of Pressure on Density

Pressure affects density differently depending on the state of matter:

| State of matter | Effect of increasing pressure on density |
|---|---|
| Gases | Particles move closer together; volume decreases → **density increases** significantly |
| Liquids | Small effect, since liquids are nearly incompressible |
| Solids | Even less affected than liquids; changes in density are usually **negligible** |

> **Exam trap:** Don't assume pressure affects all states of matter equally. Only **gases** show a significant density change with pressure — solids and liquids barely change because their particles are already closely packed (as covered in the *Particulate Nature of Matter* chapter).

> **Ever heard of...** Why does ice float on water? Water's density is **highest at 4 °C**. As water cools further and freezes into ice at 0 °C, its particles rearrange to take up **more space** (a process called expansion), so the same mass of water now occupies a larger volume — making ice's density **lower** than liquid water's. This is why ice floats, forming an insulating layer that keeps the water underneath warm enough for aquatic life to survive even in extreme cold.

## Quick Recap

- A **solution** is a uniform mixture; components spread evenly and are not visible separately.
- **Solute** (usually solid) dissolves in the **solvent** (usually liquid): Solute + Solvent → Solution. For two liquids, the smaller-quantity substance is the solute.
- An **unsaturated solution** can still dissolve more solute at a given temperature; a **saturated solution** cannot dissolve any more solute at that temperature (excess settles at the bottom).
- **Concentration** = amount of solute in a fixed quantity of solution; solutions can be **dilute** (less solute) or **concentrated** (more solute) — these are relative terms.
- **Solubility** = maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a fixed quantity of solvent at a particular temperature.
- For **most solids**, solubility in a liquid **increases** with temperature; for **gases**, solubility **decreases** with an increase in temperature.
- Cold water dissolves more oxygen than warm water — important for aquatic life.
- **Density** = Mass/Volume; it is independent of shape/size but depends on temperature and pressure.
- SI unit of density: **kg/m³**; also commonly used: **g/mL**, **g/cm³**.
- **Relative density** (with respect to water) = Density of substance / Density of water; it has **no units**.
- **Mass** is measured using a **balance**; **volume** of liquids is measured using a **measuring cylinder** (reading taken at the **meniscus** — bottom for colourless liquids, top for coloured liquids).
- Volume of **regular (cuboid) solids** = l × w × h; volume of **irregular solids** is found by the **displacement method** (Volume = Final water level − Initial water level).
- **Mass** is the quantity of matter (units: g, kg); **weight** is the force of Earth's attraction on an object (units: newtons, N) — these are scientifically different, even though used interchangeably in daily life.
- **Density decreases with an increase in temperature** (particles spread out, volume increases, mass stays constant) — this is why hot air rises and hot air balloons work.
- **Pressure** significantly increases the density of **gases** (particles compressed closer); its effect on **liquids and solids is negligible**.
- **Ice floats on water** because water's density is highest at 4 °C, and ice (formed at 0 °C) expands and becomes less dense than liquid water.
- Earth's layers (crust, upper mantle, lower mantle, outer core, inner core) have increasing density from the crust toward the centre.

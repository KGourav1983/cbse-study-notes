### Q1: Define solute, solvent, and solution.
A **solute** is the substance that dissolves (usually the solid component in a solid-liquid solution), and a **solvent** is the substance in which the solute dissolves (usually the liquid component). Together they form a **solution**, a uniform mixture: Solute + Solvent → Solution.

### Q2: What is meant by an unsaturated solution and a saturated solution?
An **unsaturated solution** is one in which more solute can still be dissolved at a given temperature. A **saturated solution** is one in which the solute stops dissolving and begins to settle at the bottom — no more solute can be dissolved at that particular temperature.

### Q3: Define solubility.
**Solubility** is the maximum amount of solute that can be dissolved in a fixed quantity of solvent (or solution) at a particular temperature.

### Q4: Define density and give its formula.
**Density** is the mass present in a unit volume of a substance. It is given by the formula: **Density = Mass / Volume**.

### Q5: What is meant by the concentration of a solution? Differentiate between a dilute and a concentrated solution.
**Concentration** is the amount of solute present in a fixed quantity of solution (or solvent). A **dilute solution** has a relatively **less** amount of solute, while a **concentrated solution** has a relatively **more** amount of solute. Dilute and concentrated are **relative terms**.

### Q6: What is relative density? State whether it has any units.
**Relative density** of a substance with respect to water is the ratio: Density of that substance ÷ Density of water at that temperature. It is a **number without any units**, since it is a ratio of two densities.

### Q7: What is the meniscus? How should it be read for colourless and coloured liquids?
The **meniscus** is the curved surface formed by a liquid inside a measuring cylinder. For **colourless liquids**, the reading is taken at the mark that coincides with the **bottom** of the meniscus. For **coloured liquids**, the reading is taken at the mark that coincides with the **top** of the meniscus.

### Q8: Differentiate between mass and weight.
**Mass** is the quantity of matter present in an object or substance, measured in **grams (g)** or **kilograms (kg)**. **Weight** is the force by which the Earth attracts an object towards itself, measured in **newtons (N)**. Although used interchangeably in daily language, they are scientifically different; most balances actually measure weight but display readings in mass units.

### Q9: How does temperature generally affect the solubility of a solid solute and the solubility of a gas in a liquid?
For **most solid solutes**, solubility in a liquid **increases with an increase in temperature** — a saturated solution at one temperature can become unsaturated if heated further. For **gases**, however, solubility in a liquid generally **decreases with an increase in temperature** — this is the opposite trend compared to solids.

### Q10: Why is dissolved oxygen important for aquatic life, and how does temperature affect its availability in water?
Even though **oxygen dissolves in water only to a small extent**, this small dissolved amount sustains all aquatic life, including plants, fishes, and other organisms. Since the solubility of gases **decreases as temperature increases**, more oxygen can dissolve in **cold water**, ensuring sufficient oxygen for aquatic life; as water warms up, the solubility of oxygen decreases, reducing the oxygen available.

### Q11: How does temperature generally affect the density of a substance? Explain the reason based on particle behaviour.
Generally, the **density of a substance decreases with heating and increases with cooling**. As temperature increases, the particles of a substance (solid, liquid, or gas) move away from each other and spread out, resulting in an **increase in volume** while the **mass remains unchanged**. Since Density = Mass/Volume, an increased volume with the same mass causes the **density to decrease**.

### Q12: How does pressure affect the density of gases, liquids, and solids differently?
Pressure affects density differently depending on the state of matter:
- **Gases**: increasing pressure pushes particles closer together, decreasing volume and **increasing density** significantly.
- **Liquids**: pressure has only a **small effect**, since liquids are nearly incompressible.
- **Solids**: even less affected than liquids; changes in density due to pressure are usually **negligible**.

### Q13: Explain why hot air rises, using the concept of density.
As air is heated, its particles gain energy and move apart, causing the **volume of the air to increase** while its mass stays constant. Since Density = Mass/Volume, this increase in volume causes the **density of hot air to decrease**, making it **less dense than the cooler air** around it. Because less dense substances rise above denser ones, hot air moves upward — this is the same principle on which **hot air balloons** work.

### Q14: Why does ice float on water? Explain with reference to the density of water at different temperatures.
Water has a special property: its **density is highest at 4 °C**. As the temperature drops further and water freezes into ice at 0 °C, its particles rearrange into a structure that takes up **more space** — a process called **expansion**. Since the same mass of water now occupies a **larger volume**, its **density decreases**, making ice **less dense (lighter)** than liquid water. As a result, ice floats on the surface of water, which is important because the floating ice layer keeps the water underneath warm enough for fish and other aquatic creatures to survive in extremely cold weather.

### Q15: Explain, with reference to the *Chashni* (sugar syrup) example, why the substance present in a larger amount is not always the solvent.
Normally, when two liquids are mixed, the substance present in the smaller amount is called the solute, and the one in larger amount is the solvent. However, when a **solid is dissolved in a liquid**, this "larger amount" rule does not apply — the **solid component is always the solute**, and the **liquid component is always the solvent**, regardless of their relative amounts. This is illustrated by *Chashni*, the sugar syrup of Gulab jamun, where a **large amount of sugar (solid)** is dissolved in a **small amount of water (liquid)**, yet water is still considered the solvent and sugar the solute.

### Q16: Describe how the volume of an irregularly shaped solid object, such as a stone, can be determined using the displacement method.
- Fill a measuring cylinder with water up to a known volume and record this as the **initial volume (A)**.
- Tie the object (e.g., a stone) with a thread and slowly lower it into the measuring cylinder, making sure it is fully immersed.
- The water level rises; record this new, higher reading as the **final volume (B)**.
- Subtract the initial volume from the final volume: **Volume of the object = B − A**. This is the volume of water displaced by the object, which equals the object's own volume.

### Q17: Calculate the volume of a notebook with length 25 cm, width 18 cm, and height 2 cm.
For a regular cuboid-shaped object, **Volume = l × w × h**.

$$\text{Volume} = 25\ \text{cm} \times 18\ \text{cm} \times 2\ \text{cm} = 900\ \text{cm}^3$$

The volume of the notebook is **900 cm³**.

### Q18: A stone is lowered into a measuring cylinder containing 50 mL of water, and the water level rises to 55 mL. If the mass of the stone is 16.400 g, calculate its density.
**Step 1: Find the volume of the stone using the displacement method.**
$$\text{Volume of stone} = \text{Final volume} - \text{Initial volume} = 55\ \text{mL} - 50\ \text{mL} = 5\ \text{mL} = 5\ \text{cm}^3$$

**Step 2: Apply the density formula.**
$$\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{16.400\ \text{g}}{5\ \text{cm}^3} = 3.28\ \text{g/cm}^3$$

The **density** of the stone is **3.28 g/cm³**.

### Q19: The mass of an aluminium block is 27 g and its volume is 10 cm³. Calculate its density and its relative density with respect to water.
**Step 1: Calculate density.**
$$\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{27\ \text{g}}{10\ \text{cm}^3} = 2.7\ \text{g/cm}^3$$

**Step 2: Calculate relative density with respect to water** (density of water ≈ 1 g/cm³ at that temperature).
$$\text{Relative density} = \frac{\text{Density of aluminium}}{\text{Density of water}} = \frac{2.7\ \text{g/cm}^3}{1\ \text{g/cm}^3} = 2.7$$

The density of aluminium is **2.7 g/cm³**, and its **relative density** with respect to water is **2.7** (this value has **no units**), meaning aluminium is 2.7 times denser than water.

### Q20: A stone sculpture weighs 225 g and has a volume of 90 cm³. Calculate its density and predict whether it will float or sink in water.
**Step 1: Apply the density formula.**
$$\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{225\ \text{g}}{90\ \text{cm}^3} = 2.5\ \text{g/cm}^3$$

**Step 2: Compare with the density of water** (approximately 1 g/cm³).

Since the density of the stone sculpture (2.5 g/cm³) is **greater than** the density of water (1 g/cm³), the stone sculpture is **denser than water** and will therefore **sink** in water.

### Q21: Object A has a mass of 200 g and a volume of 40 cm³. Object B has a mass of 240 g and a volume of 60 cm³. Which object is denser?
**For Object A:**
$$\text{Density}_A = \frac{200\ \text{g}}{40\ \text{cm}^3} = 5\ \text{g/cm}^3$$

**For Object B:**
$$\text{Density}_B = \frac{240\ \text{g}}{60\ \text{cm}^3} = 4\ \text{g/cm}^3$$

Since **Density A (5 g/cm³) is greater than Density B (4 g/cm³)**, **Object A is denser** than Object B.

### Q22: A block of iron has a mass of 600 g and a density of 7.9 g/cm³. Calculate its volume.
Rearranging the density formula to solve for volume:
$$\text{Volume} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Density}} = \frac{600\ \text{g}}{7.9\ \text{g/cm}^3} \approx 75.9\ \text{cm}^3$$

The volume of the iron block is approximately **75.9 cm³**.

### Q23: Reema has a piece of modeling clay that weighs 120 g. She first moulds it into a compact cube of volume 60 cm³. Later, she flattens it into a thin sheet. Predict what will happen to its density, with reason.
Since density depends only on mass and volume (Density = Mass/Volume), and **the mass of the clay does not change** when its shape is altered (moulding or flattening does not add or remove clay), and **density is independent of shape or size**, the **density of the clay will remain the same** even after being flattened into a thin sheet. Only the shape changes; the mass and the actual volume of clay material stay constant, so density stays unchanged (still 120 g ÷ 60 cm³ = 2 g/cm³).

### Q24: You pour oil into a glass containing water, and the oil floats on top. What does this tell you about the densities of oil and water? Explain using the concept of floating and sinking.
Since the oil **floats** on top of the water rather than sinking or mixing, this indicates that **oil is less dense than water**. Generally, it is believed that objects (or liquids) that float in a liquid are lighter — meaning they have a lower density — than the liquid they are placed in, while those that sink are denser. Since oil consistently rests above the water layer, we can conclude that **water is denser than oil**.

### Q25: Analyse a scenario where an unpeeled orange floats in water while a peeled orange (same orange) sinks. Explain why this happens.
The **unpeeled orange floats** because its peel contains many tiny air pockets, which lower the overall average density of the whole orange (peel + fruit) to less than that of water, allowing it to float. Once the orange is **peeled**, these air-pocket-containing peel layers are removed, so only the denser fruit part remains. Without the air pockets from the peel, the average density of the peeled orange becomes **greater than that of water**, causing it to **sink**. This shows that although the fruit itself hasn't changed, removing the low-density peel structure changes the object's overall density relative to water, which is not the only factor — but a key one — in determining whether an object floats or sinks.

# Keeping Time with the Skies

Have you ever spotted the Moon shining in broad daylight and wondered how that's even possible? Or noticed that it's a perfect circle one week and a thin sliver the next? These aren't random coincidences — they're the result of predictable astronomical cycles, and understanding them is exactly what lets us build calendars, track festivals, and even launch satellites.

## 11.1 How Does the Moon's Appearance Change and Why?

**Activity 11.1** involves tracking the Moon starting from the sunrise after a full Moon day — recording the date, whether the Moon was seen at sunrise or sunset, the shape of its bright portion, whether that bright portion is increasing or decreasing compared to the previous day, and whether the Moon appears closer to or farther from the Sun in the sky.

### 11.1.1 Phases of the Moon

Here's the pattern this observation reveals: the bright portion of the Moon shrinks from a full circle to a half circle in about a week, and keeps shrinking for another week until it's not visible at all.

- This two-week shrinking period is called the **waning** period of the Moon.
- The day the Moon appears as a full bright circle is called the **full Moon day** (or ***Purnima***).
- The day it is not visible at all is called the **new Moon day** (or ***Amavasya***).
- After the new Moon, the bright side grows to a half circle in about a week, and to a full circle in another week — this growing period is called the **waxing** period.

> In India, the waning period is generally called ***Krishna Paksha***, and the waxing period is called ***Shukla Paksha***. The full cycle from one full Moon to the next takes about a month.

> **Phases of the Moon**: the changing shapes of the bright portion of the Moon from one day to another, as seen from the Earth.

### 11.1.2 Locating the Moon

Here's something you might not expect: on a full Moon day, the Moon is nearly **opposite** the Sun in the sky — when the Sun rises in the East, the Moon is almost setting in the West. As days pass and the bright part decreases, the Moon appears to move closer to the Sun in the sky. When the bright part is a half circle, the Moon is overhead at sunrise; a few days later, the crescent Moon appears even closer to the Sun.

- A **waxing Moon** is easiest to spot at **sunset**.
- A **waning Moon** is easiest to spot at **sunrise**.
- Because of these shifts, the Moon always rises and sets at **different times than the Sun**.

> **Exam trap:** Many people assume the Moon rises exactly when the Sun sets — this is **not always true**. The Moon actually rises about **50 minutes later each day**, so its rising time keeps shifting relative to sunset, and it can even rise during daylight hours (e.g., around 2:00–4:00 p.m.).

### 11.1.3 Making Sense of Our Observations

Here's the key insight: **the shape of the Moon itself never changes** — only what we *see* from Earth changes.

- The Moon does not emit its own light; it shines because it **reflects sunlight**.
- The half of the Moon facing the Sun is **illuminated**; the half facing away is **non-illuminated**.
- As the Moon revolves around the Earth, **only one half of the Moon always faces the Earth** — but this Earth-facing half is not always the illuminated half.
- We can only see the illuminated **portion** of the Moon that happens to be facing Earth. Sometimes this is the entire illuminated half; sometimes only part of it; on New Moon day, none of it (since only the non-illuminated portion faces Earth then).

**Activity 11.2 (ball-and-lamp model):** A small ball on a stick represents the Moon, a lamp/torch represents the Sun, and your head represents the Earth. As you hold the ball at arm's length and slowly turn around (anti-clockwise), you observe:
- At position **A** (ball opposite the lamp): the **entire illuminated portion faces you** — like the **full Moon**.
- At position **E** (ball towards the lamp): you face the **non-illuminated portion only** — like the **new Moon**.
- At other positions: the line separating illuminated and non-illuminated portions appears **curved**, matching the shapes we see of the real Moon.

Using positions A through H around the Earth (Fig. 11.5 in the textbook):
- At **B and H**: more than half the illuminated portion is visible — the **gibbous phase**.
- At **D and F**: less than half the illuminated portion is visible — the **crescent phase**.
- From A → C → E: the **waning** phase sequence.
- From E → G → back to A: the **waxing** phase sequence.
- On **New Moon day**, the Moon appears **closest to the Sun**; on **Full Moon day**, it appears **farthest** from the Sun — matching the Activity 11.1 observations.

> Since the Earth's rotation period (1 day) is much smaller than the Moon's revolution period (nearly a month), on a given day, **people on different parts of the Earth see nearly the same phase**.

**Why the Moon's position shifts each day:** The Moon moves ahead a little in its orbit during each 24-hour rotation of the Earth, so the Earth needs to rotate **a little extra** for the Moon to appear in nearly the same spot — this is why the **Moon takes about 50 minutes longer** each day to return to roughly the same position in the sky.

> **Exam trap:** A very common misconception is that the Moon's phases are caused by **Earth's shadow** falling on it. This is **incorrect**. Phases occur due to the changing relative orientation of the Sun, Moon, and Earth as the Moon revolves — Earth's shadow on the Moon instead causes a **lunar eclipse**, a completely different phenomenon. Lunar eclipses can only happen on a full Moon day, and solar eclipses only on a new Moon day, but they don't happen every month because of the slight tilt of the Moon's orbit relative to Earth's orbit around the Sun.

## 11.2 How Did Calendars Come into Existence?

The Sun's apparent daily rise-and-set motion (caused by Earth's rotation on its axis) is the natural cycle that forms the foundation of the **day**, our basic unit of time.

> **Mean solar day**: the average time the Sun takes to go from its highest position in the sky on one day to its highest position the next day — equal to **24 hours**.

The Sun's highest position can be found by measuring shadow length: the shadow is **shortest** when the Sun is highest in the sky.

**Worked example (Activity 11.3 — measuring a solar day):** A 1 m stick is fixed vertically in sunlight, and the shadow tip is marked with a dot every minute from 11:00 a.m. to around 1:10 p.m. The time of the shortest shadow is recorded and compared across consecutive days.

| Date | Time of shortest shadow | Duration of day |
|---|---|---|
| 22 March 2025 | 12:20 | — |
| 23 March 2025 | 12:20 | 24:00 |
| 24 March 2025 | 12:19 | 23:59 |

**Step:** Find the average of the recorded day durations — this should come out to be **nearly equal to 24 hours**, confirming the mean solar day.

### Larger natural cycles

- The **Moon** takes about **29.5 days** (nearly a month) to cycle through all its phases → this is the basis for the **month**.
- The **Earth** takes nearly **365 and a quarter days** to complete one revolution around the Sun, undergoing one full cycle of seasons in this time → this defines the **solar year**.

### 11.2.1 Lunar calendars

Ancient observers noticed that roughly **12 cycles of Moon phases (12 lunar months)** fit into one cycle of seasons. This gave rise to **lunar calendars**: day as the shortest unit, a month of about 29.5 days, and a lunar year of 12 lunar months.

> **Exam trap:** A lunar year is only **354 days** long (not 365), so **seasons do not stay synchronised** to the same lunar months in successive lunar years — this is a key limitation of purely lunar calendars.

### 11.2.2 Solar calendars

Since knowing the arrival of seasons mattered for agriculture, **solar calendars** were created to stay synchronised with the seasons. The **Gregorian calendar** (widely used today) is a solar calendar.

- Months are adjusted to add up to **365 days** — hence some months have 30 days, others 31, and February has 28.
- Since Earth actually takes an extra quarter-day beyond 365 days to orbit the Sun, these extra hours add up to about **one day every four years**.
- This is corrected using a **leap year**: an extra day is added every four years (a year divisible by four gets a leap day), giving **February 29 days** in that year.

> **A step further:** Adding a leap day every four years actually over-corrects slightly over time, since Earth takes *slightly less* than 365.25 days per spring equinox cycle. To fix this, leap years are **skipped every 100 years** (e.g., 1700, 1800, 1900), but to avoid lagging too much, a leap year is **added back every 400 years** (e.g., 1600, 2000).

> **A step further:** The time between successive spring equinoxes is called the **tropical year**, and the Gregorian calendar is based on it. The **sidereal year** (the time for the same stars to rise again at sunset) is used by astronomers to track Earth's orbital position — it's longer than the tropical year by only about 20 minutes.

> **Our scientific heritage:** Ancient Indian astronomers determined the year length (~365 days) through careful sky observation, without modern instruments or knowledge that Earth orbits the Sun. They noted the Sun doesn't always rise exactly East — it rises northward of East in summer and southward in winter, with these extremes occurring at the **solstices** (around June 21 and December 21). The Sun's apparent northward movement (December to June) is called ***Uttarayan***, and its southward movement (June to December) is ***Dakshinayan*** — recorded in the *Taittirīya Saṁhitā*. Ancient texts like the *Surya Siddhanta* also tracked equinoxes/solstices using the stars visible at sunset.

### 11.2.3 Luni-solar calendars

**Luni-solar calendars** primarily use the Moon's phases for counting days/months but also adjust to stay in sync with the seasons.

- 12 lunar months = 354 days, falling short of the solar year (~365 days) by about **11 days**.
- Every **2–3 years**, this accumulated shortfall becomes close to a full month, so an extra month called ***Adhika Maasa*** (intercalary month) is added.
- Such calendars combine elements of both solar and lunar calendars and are widely used across India.

> **Ever heard of...** Indian luni-solar month names include *Chaitra, Vaisakha, Jyeshtha, Ashadha, Shravana, Bhadrapada, Ashwin, Kartika, Margashirsha (Agrahayan), Pausha, Magha,* and *Phalguna*. Calendars where the new month starts the day after the **new Moon** and ends on the new Moon are called ***Amant***; those where the month starts the day after the **full Moon** and ends on the full Moon are called ***Purnimant***.

### 11.2.4 The Indian National Calendar

The **Indian National Calendar** is used alongside the Gregorian calendar for official purposes.

- It is a **solar calendar** with **365 days** in a regular year.
- The year begins on **22 March** (the day after the spring equinox).
- Unlike the Gregorian calendar, its months have either **30 or 31 days** — names taken from traditional Indian calendars.
- In a regular year, months 2 to 6 have 31 days, the rest have 30 days.
- Leap years match the Gregorian calendar by **adding a day to Chaitra** (the first month); in such years, the new year begins on **21 March**.

> **Ever heard of...** In 1952, the Government of India set up a **Calendar Reform Committee (CRC)** to recommend a uniform national calendar. The recommended '**Unified National Calendar**' was adopted from **21 March 1956 CE** (1 Chaitra 1878 *Saka*), following the general principles of the *Surya Siddhanta*.

> **Be a scientist:** **Meghnad Saha (1893–1956)** was a pioneering Indian astrophysicist who studied stars and developed the **Saha equation**. The Saha Institute of Nuclear Physics (Kolkata) is named after him. He also chaired the Calendar Reform Committee.

## 11.3 Are Festivals Related to Astronomical Phenomena?

Many Indian festivals are tied to Moon phases and are based on lunar or luni-solar calendars:

| Festival | Based on |
|---|---|
| *Diwali* | New Moon of the month of *Kartika* |
| *Holi* | Full Moon of *Phalguna* |
| *Buddha Purnima* | Full Moon of *Vaisakha* |
| *Eid-ul-Fitr* | Sighting of the crescent Moon at the end of *Ramazan* |
| *Dussehra* | Tenth day of the month of *Ashwina* |

Because of this, these festivals fall on **different Gregorian dates** in successive years.

- For **luni-solar calendar** festivals, the Gregorian date shift is **typically less than a month**, since the intercalary month corrects for the lunar-solar mismatch every few years.
- For **purely lunar calendar** festivals (like *Eid-ul-Fitr*), there's **no such correction**, so the festival can occur in **different months** of the Gregorian year, year after year (shifting by about 11 days each year).

> **A step further:** Some festivals — *Makar Sankranti, Pongal, Bihu, Vaisakhi, Poila Baisakh, Puthandu* — follow a **solar sidereal calendar** and occur on nearly the same Gregorian date every year, since they were historically tied to a solstice or equinox. Due to a slight difference between sidereal and tropical years (caused by the slow wobble of Earth's axis, like a wobbling top), these festival dates slowly drift — for example, ***Makar Sankranti* moves ahead by one day every 71 years**.

> **Ever heard of...** Since many festival dates depend on the exact lunar phase at **sunrise**, and sunrise occurs earlier in Eastern India than Western India, festival dates can shift by a day between regions in the same year. To maintain uniformity, the **Positional Astronomy Center** of the Government of India publishes the ***Rashtriya panchang*** annually, calculating celestial positions for a central Indian location to advise on festival/holiday dates.

> **Ever heard of...** The Moon and moonlight have inspired Indian classical music **ragas** (*Chandrakauns, Chandranandan, Shubhapantuvarali*) and dance **mudras** (*Chandrakala, Ardhachandran* in Bharatanatyam), as well as traditional art forms like *Warli* painting and *Dhokra* brass sculpture, which prominently depict the Moon and Sun.

## 11.4 Why Do We Launch Artificial Satellites in Space?

The **Moon** is Earth's natural satellite. In addition, many countries send up **man-made (artificial) satellites** that orbit the Earth, appearing as tiny moving specks in the night sky.

- Most orbit about **800 km** above Earth's surface.
- They take roughly **100 minutes** to complete one orbit.
- They help with: **communication, navigation, weather monitoring, disaster management, and scientific research**.

> **Our scientific heritage:** ISRO's **Cartosat** series captures high-quality Earth images for mapping, city planning, and disaster management (used in the *Bhuvan* mapping platform). **AstroSat** makes scientific observations of stars and celestial objects. Other ISRO missions include **Chandrayaan 1, 2, 3** (Moon), **Aditya L1** (Sun), and **Mangalyaan** (Mars). ISRO also enables Indian students to build and launch small satellites like *AzaadiSat, InspireSat-1,* and *Jugnu*.

**Activity 11.4 (spotting satellites):** Just before sunrise or after sunset, with a clear, unobstructed view of the sky, look for a moving point of light with steady or flickering brightness moving fast across the sky (visible to the naked eye or with binoculars). Mobile apps/websites can also show satellite pass timings for your location.

> **A step further:** Many artificial satellites and rocket parts become **space junk (space debris)** after their useful life, crowding space and risking collisions with working satellites. Small debris burns up in the atmosphere on falling; larger pieces can crash to the ground. Countries are now working together to remove this debris.

> **Be a scientist:** **Vikram Ambalal Sarabhai (1919–1971)**, known as the **Father of the Indian Space Programme**, pioneered India's first artificial satellite launches. The **Vikram Sarabhai Space Centre (VSSC)** in Thiruvananthapuram (which develops rockets and launch vehicle technology) is named after him.

> **A step further:** Near the sea, **tides** (the rise and fall of water levels) also follow a regular pattern closely related to the **Moon's position and phase** — similar tides recur about **50 minutes later** each day, matching the Moon's daily shift in rising time.

## Quick Recap

- The **Moon** doesn't emit its own light — it **reflects sunlight**; only the illuminated half faces the Sun.
- **Phases of the Moon**: the changing shapes of the Moon's bright portion as seen from Earth, caused by the changing relative orientation of Sun, Moon, and Earth (NOT Earth's shadow — that causes lunar eclipses).
- **Full Moon (*Purnima*)**: entire illuminated portion visible; **New Moon (*Amavasya*)**: no illuminated portion visible.
- **Waning period** (*Krishna Paksha*): bright portion shrinks over ~2 weeks, full Moon → new Moon. **Waxing period** (*Shukla Paksha*): bright portion grows over ~2 weeks, new Moon → full Moon. Full cycle ≈ 1 month.
- **Gibbous phase**: more than half illuminated portion visible. **Crescent phase**: less than half visible.
- On full Moon day, the Moon is nearly **opposite** the Sun (farthest); on new Moon day, it's **closest** to the Sun in the sky.
- **Waxing Moon** → easiest to see at **sunset**; **waning Moon** → easiest to see at **sunrise**. The Moon rises about **50 minutes later** each day, so it doesn't always rise exactly at sunset.
- **Mean solar day** = 24 hours = time for the Sun to return to its highest position; shadows are **shortest** at the Sun's highest point.
- **Month** ≈ 29.5 days (Moon's phase cycle); **Solar year** ≈ 365.25 days (Earth's revolution/season cycle).
- **Lunar calendars**: 12 lunar months = 354 days; don't stay synced with seasons.
- **Solar calendars** (e.g., **Gregorian**): 365 days/year, synced to seasons; **leap year** adds a day every 4 years (skipped every 100 years, added back every 400 years) to correct for the extra quarter-day.
- **Tropical year**: time between successive spring equinoxes (basis of Gregorian calendar). **Sidereal year**: time for the same stars to rise at sunset again; ~20 minutes longer than the tropical year.
- **Luni-solar calendars**: primarily lunar, but add an intercalary month (***Adhika Maasa***) every 2–3 years to stay synced with seasons; combine solar + lunar elements.
- **Indian National Calendar**: solar calendar, 365 days, starts 22 March (21 March in leap years); months have 30 or 31 days; leap day added to *Chaitra*.
- Many Indian festivals (*Diwali, Holi, Buddha Purnima, Eid-ul-Fitr, Dussehra*) are tied to Moon phases via lunar/luni-solar calendars, so they shift on the Gregorian calendar; luni-solar festival shifts are usually **less than a month**, purely lunar festival shifts (like *Eid-ul-Fitr*) can span **different months** year to year.
- A few festivals (*Makar Sankranti, Pongal, Bihu, Vaisakhi, Poila Baisakh, Puthandu*) follow a **solar sidereal calendar** and stay on nearly the same Gregorian date each year, slowly drifting (e.g., *Makar Sankranti* by 1 day per 71 years) due to Earth's axial wobble.
- **Artificial satellites** orbit ~800 km above Earth, complete an orbit in ~100 minutes, and support communication, navigation, weather monitoring, disaster management, and research; ISRO missions include Cartosat, AstroSat, Chandrayaan, Aditya L1, and Mangalyaan.
- **Vikram Sarabhai** is the Father of the Indian Space Programme; **Meghnad Saha** chaired the Calendar Reform Committee that gave India its national calendar.

### Q1: Define the phases of the Moon.
The **phases of the Moon** are the changing shapes of the bright (illuminated) portion of the Moon, as seen from the Earth, from one day to another.

### Q2: Define full Moon day and new Moon day.
The **full Moon day** (*Purnima*) is the day when the Moon appears as a full bright circle. The **new Moon day** (*Amavasya*) is the day when the Moon is not visible at all.

### Q3: What is the waxing period and the waning period of the Moon?
The **waning period** is the roughly two-week period during which the bright portion of the Moon shrinks from a full circle to invisibility (full Moon to new Moon). The **waxing period** is the roughly two-week period during which the bright portion grows from invisible to a full circle (new Moon to full Moon).

### Q4: What is meant by the gibbous phase and the crescent phase of the Moon?
The **gibbous phase** is when more than half of the illuminated portion of the Moon can be seen from Earth. The **crescent phase** is when less than half of the illuminated portion of the Moon can be seen from Earth.

### Q5: Define mean solar day.
The **mean solar day** is the average time the Sun takes to go from its highest position in the sky on one day to its highest position in the sky the next day, and is equal to **24 hours**.

### Q6: What is a leap year? How is it identified in the Gregorian calendar?
A **leap year** is a year in which an extra day is added to the calendar (to February, making it 29 days) to adjust for the extra quarter-day the Earth takes to complete one revolution around the Sun beyond 365 days. In the Gregorian calendar, a year is a leap year if it is **divisible by four**.

### Q7: What is a lunar calendar? State one limitation of lunar calendars.
A **lunar calendar** is a calendar based on the Moon's phases, with the day as the shortest unit, a month of about 29.5 days, and a lunar year of 12 lunar months (354 days). Its limitation is that the **seasons do not remain synchronised** to the same lunar months in successive lunar years, since the lunar year (354 days) is shorter than the solar year (~365 days).

### Q8: What is a luni-solar calendar?
A **luni-solar calendar** is a calendar that primarily uses the Moon's phases for counting days and months but also adds an extra intercalary month (*Adhika Maasa*) every few years to stay synchronised with the cycle of seasons, thus combining elements of both solar and lunar calendars.

### Q9: What is the Indian National Calendar? State two of its features.
The **Indian National Calendar** is a solar calendar used by the Government of India alongside the Gregorian calendar for official purposes. Two features: (i) it has **365 days** in a regular year, beginning on **22 March** (the day after the spring equinox); (ii) unlike the Gregorian calendar, its months have either **30 or 31 days**, with names taken from traditional Indian calendars.

### Q10: Why does the Moon appear different on different days, even though its own shape never changes?
The Moon's actual shape never changes — only what we **see from Earth** changes. The Moon does not emit its own light but shines by **reflecting sunlight**; only the half facing the Sun is illuminated. As the Moon revolves around the Earth, the Earth-facing half is not always the illuminated half — sometimes we see the entire illuminated portion (full Moon), sometimes only part of it (crescent/gibbous), and sometimes none of it (new Moon), which is why the Moon appears different from day to day.

### Q11: Explain why the Moon rises and sets at different times than the Sun.
The Moon moves ahead a little in its orbit around the Earth during each 24-hour period, while the Earth completes one rotation about its axis in that same 24 hours. Because the Moon has shifted position in its orbit, the Earth needs to **rotate a little further** for the Moon to reappear at nearly the same spot in the sky. As a result, the Moon takes about **50 minutes longer** each day to return to nearly the same position, causing it to rise and set at different (progressively later) times compared to the Sun each day.

### Q12: Explain, with reference to Activity 11.2 (the ball-and-lamp model), how the Moon's phases occur.
In this activity, a ball on a stick represents the Moon, a lamp represents the Sun, and the observer's head represents the Earth. As the ball is held at arm's length and moved around the head, the portion of the ball that appears illuminated to the observer changes depending on the ball's position relative to the lamp. When the ball is held **opposite** the lamp, the observer faces the entire illuminated portion (like a **full Moon**). When the ball is held **towards** the lamp, the observer faces only the non-illuminated portion (like a **new Moon**). At intermediate positions, only part of the illuminated portion is visible, and the boundary between illuminated and non-illuminated regions appears **curved** — this mirrors how, in reality, the changing relative positions of the Sun, Moon, and Earth cause us to see different fractions of the Moon's illuminated half, producing its phases.

### Q13: Why is it incorrect to say that the Moon's phases are caused by the Earth's shadow falling on the Moon?
This is a common misconception. The Moon's phases occur due to the **relative change in orientation** of the Sun, Moon, and Earth as the Moon revolves around the Earth — specifically, because we only see the fraction of the Moon's illuminated half that happens to be facing Earth at a given time. **Earth's shadow falling on the Moon** is a completely different phenomenon that causes a **lunar eclipse**, not the regular monthly phases. Lunar eclipses are rare and brief events that occur only on full Moon days (and even then, not every month, due to the slight tilt of the Moon's orbit relative to Earth's orbit around the Sun).

### Q14: Describe how Activity 11.3 can be used to measure the duration of a solar day, and what result is expected.
A 1 m stick is fixed vertically in a sunlit, flat area of ground. Starting at 11:00 a.m., a dot is marked on the ground at the tip of the stick's shadow every minute, continuing until around 1:10 p.m. By counting the dots, the time at which the shadow was **shortest** (indicating the Sun's highest position) is identified and recorded for several consecutive days. The **duration of the solar day** for a given date is found by calculating the difference in the time of shortest shadow between that date and the previous date. When averaged over several days, this duration comes out to be **nearly equal to 24 hours**, confirming the definition of the mean solar day.

### Q15: Explain how the natural cycles of the Sun, Moon, and Earth's seasons give rise to the three basic units of time: day, month, and year.
The apparent daily rise-and-set motion of the **Sun**, caused by Earth's rotation on its axis, forms the basis of the **day** (the Sun returns to its highest position roughly every 24 hours — the mean solar day). The **Moon's** revolution around the Earth causes it to cycle through all its phases in about 29.5 days, forming the basis of the **month**. The Earth's revolution around the **Sun**, taking nearly 365 and a quarter days and producing one complete cycle of seasons, forms the basis of the **solar year**. Together, these three independent, repeating astronomical cycles gave ancient people a natural and reliable way to measure the passage of time.

### Q16: Why do solar calendars, such as the Gregorian calendar, need leap years?
A solar calendar is designed so that its year length matches the time Earth takes to complete one revolution around the Sun, which is approximately **365 and a quarter days**. Since a regular calendar year only counts 365 days, this leaves an extra quarter-day uncounted each year, which adds up to approximately **one full day every four years**. If this were not corrected, the calendar would slowly drift out of sync with the actual seasons over time. To prevent this, an extra day is added to the calendar (as 29 February) once every four years — this is called a **leap year** — keeping the solar calendar well synchronised with the seasons.

### Q17: Why do luni-solar calendars require the addition of an intercalary month (Adhika Maasa)?
A luni-solar calendar is based primarily on the Moon's phases, with 12 lunar months adding up to only **354 days** in a lunar year. However, the cycle of seasons (the solar year) takes approximately **365 days**, meaning the lunar year falls short of the solar year by nearly **11 days** each year. If left uncorrected, this accumulating difference would cause the lunar months to drift steadily out of alignment with the seasons. Therefore, every 2–3 years, once this accumulated shortfall becomes close to a full month, an extra intercalary month called ***Adhika Maasa*** is added to the calendar to bring the lunar and solar cycles back into step.

### Q18: Explain why festivals based on a purely lunar calendar, such as Eid-ul-Fitr, occur on different Gregorian calendar dates (and even different months) each year, while festivals based on a solar sidereal calendar, such as Makar Sankranti, occur on nearly the same date each year.
Festivals based on a **purely lunar calendar**, like *Eid-ul-Fitr* (tied to the sighting of the crescent Moon at the end of *Ramazan*), follow the lunar year, which is only **354 days** long — about 11 days shorter than the solar year on which the Gregorian calendar is based. Since lunar calendars do not correct for this difference (no intercalary month is added), the festival's Gregorian date shifts earlier by about 11 days every year, eventually moving through **different months** of the Gregorian calendar over the years. In contrast, festivals based on a **solar sidereal calendar**, like *Makar Sankranti*, are tied to the position of the Earth in its orbit relative to the Sun (similar to a solar year), so they fall on **nearly the same Gregorian date** every year, with only a very slow drift over long periods (e.g., *Makar Sankranti* shifts by about one day every 71 years) due to the small difference between the sidereal and tropical years.

### Q19: A luni-solar calendar festival's Gregorian date typically shifts by less than a month across years, but a purely lunar calendar festival's date can shift across several months. Explain why, with reference to the role of the intercalary month.
Luni-solar calendars **add an intercalary month (Adhika Maasa)** every 2–3 years specifically to correct for the roughly 11-day-per-year shortfall between the 354-day lunar year and the ~365-day solar year. This periodic correction keeps the lunar months from drifting too far from their usual position in the solar year, so festivals based on luni-solar calendars shift by less than a month in the Gregorian calendar. Purely **lunar calendars, by contrast, make no such correction** — every year, the lunar calendar falls about 11 days further behind the solar year, with no intercalary month to reset this drift. Over successive years, this uncorrected 11-day annual shift accumulates, causing festivals based on purely lunar calendars (like *Eid-ul-Fitr*) to gradually move through different months of the Gregorian calendar.

### Q20: Amol was born on 6th May on a full Moon day. Will his birthday fall on a full Moon day every year? Explain your answer.
No, Amol's birthday will **not** fall on a full Moon day every year. This is because his birthday (6th May) is fixed according to the **solar (Gregorian) calendar**, which has a year length of about 365 days, while the **Moon's phase cycle** (from one full Moon to the next) takes only about **29.5 days**. Since 365 days is not an exact multiple of 29.5 days, the phase of the Moon on any fixed solar calendar date (like 6th May) will not repeat the same way each year — the full Moon and the fixed birthday date will drift out of alignment over successive years.

### Q21: A total of 37 full Moons happen during 3 years in a solar calendar which has 36 months. Show that at least two of the 37 full moons must happen during the same month of the solar calendar.
A solar calendar spanning 3 years has **36 months** in total (3 years × 12 months). However, there are **37 full Moons** occurring during this same 3-year period. Since there are more full Moons (37) than there are months (36) to distribute them into, by the basic counting principle, it is **not possible** for every month to contain at most one full Moon — if each of the 36 months contained only one full Moon, that would account for only 36 full Moons, one short of the actual 37. Therefore, **at least one month must contain two full Moons**, meaning at least two of the 37 full Moons must happen during the same month of the solar calendar.

### Q22: On a particular night, Vaishali saw the Moon in the sky continuously from sunset to sunrise. What phase of the Moon would she have noticed, and why?
Vaishali would have noticed the **full Moon**. This is because, on a full Moon day, the Moon is nearly **opposite the Sun** in the sky — as observed in the chapter, when the Sun sets in the West, the full Moon rises in the East around the same time, and it remains visible in the sky throughout the night, setting only around the time the Sun rises the next morning. Only during the full Moon phase would the Moon be visible continuously across the entire span from sunset to sunrise.

### Q23: Ravi said, "I saw a crescent Moon, and it was rising in the East, when the Sun was setting." Kaushalya said, "Once I saw the gibbous Moon during the afternoon in the East." Who out of the two is telling the truth, and why?
**Kaushalya is telling the truth**, and **Ravi is not**. A crescent Moon (less than half illuminated, appearing closer to the Sun in the sky) is only visible near the Sun in the sky — it would rise close to sunrise or set close to sunset, not rise in the East exactly when the Sun is setting in the West (which would place it opposite the Sun, a position associated with the full Moon, not a crescent). A gibbous Moon (more than half illuminated), however, appears somewhat closer to opposite the Sun; it is plausible for a gibbous Moon to be seen rising in the East during the afternoon, since a waxing gibbous Moon rises before sunset and would already be visible low in the eastern sky in the afternoon.

### Q24: If we stopped having leap years, would the seasons gradually shift relative to fixed Gregorian calendar dates? Explain the reasoning, and describe what would eventually happen to a fixed date like Indian Independence Day (15th August).
Yes, without leap years, the seasons would **gradually shift** relative to fixed calendar dates. This is because the Earth actually takes about 365 and a quarter days to complete one revolution around the Sun, but a calendar without leap years would count only 365 days per year, missing about a quarter of a day every year. This missing quarter-day would **accumulate** year after year — after about 4 years, nearly a full day would be lost; over many decades, this accumulated shortfall would become significant enough that a fixed calendar date (like 15th August) would slowly move earlier relative to the actual position of the Earth in its seasonal cycle. Over a long enough period (many centuries), this drift would be large enough that a date currently falling in one season (like the Indian monsoon/late-summer period in August) could eventually shift to occur during a completely different season, such as winter.

### Q25: On which periodic phenomenon are each of the following measures of time based: (i) day, (ii) month, (iii) year? Explain briefly for each.
(i) The **day** is based on the Earth's **rotation about its own axis**, which causes the Sun to appear to rise and reach its highest position in the sky roughly every 24 hours (the mean solar day).
(ii) The **month** is based on the **Moon's revolution around the Earth**, during which it cycles through all its phases (new Moon to full Moon and back) in about 29.5 days.
(iii) The **year** is based on the **Earth's revolution around the Sun**, which takes nearly 365 and a quarter days and during which the Earth undergoes one complete cycle of seasons, forming the basis of the solar year.

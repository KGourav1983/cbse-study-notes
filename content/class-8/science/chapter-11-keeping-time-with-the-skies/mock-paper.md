## Answer Key

1. The **phases of the Moon** are the changing shapes of the bright (illuminated) portion of the Moon, as seen from the Earth, from one day to another.

2. The **mean solar day** is the average time the Sun takes to go from its highest position in the sky on one day to its highest position in the sky the next day; it is equal to **24 hours**.

3. The waxing period of the Moon is called ***Shukla Paksha*** in India.

4. **False.** The Moon's phases are caused by the changing relative orientation of the Sun, Moon, and Earth as the Moon revolves around the Earth — not by Earth's shadow. Earth's shadow falling on the Moon causes a **lunar eclipse**, a separate phenomenon.

5. **Vikram Ambalal Sarabhai** is known as the Father of the Indian Space Programme.

6. During the **waxing period**, the bright portion of the Moon **increases** in size, growing from invisible (new Moon) to a half circle in about a week, and to a full circle (full Moon) in another week. During the **waning period**, the bright portion of the Moon **decreases** in size, shrinking from a full circle (full Moon) to a half circle in about a week, and continuing to shrink until it becomes invisible (new Moon) after another week.

7. A **leap year** is a year in which an extra day (29 February) is added to the calendar. It is needed because the Earth actually takes about 365 and a quarter days to complete one revolution around the Sun, but a regular calendar year counts only 365 days. The extra quarter-day per year adds up to approximately one full day every four years; without correction, the calendar would gradually drift out of sync with the seasons. Adding a leap day every four years (for years divisible by four) keeps the solar calendar synchronised with the seasons.

8. A **waxing Moon** has its bright portion increasing and, according to the chapter's observations, appears farther from the Sun in the sky as it waxes toward full Moon; since the full Moon rises around sunset (being nearly opposite the Sun), a waxing Moon is generally visible and rising around sunset, making it **easiest to spot at sunset**. A **waning Moon**, on the other hand, has its bright portion decreasing and appears to move closer to the Sun in the sky as it wanes toward new Moon; since it is positioned nearer the Sun, it is more prominently visible in the sky just before sunrise, making it **easiest to spot at sunrise**.

9. A **luni-solar calendar** is a calendar that primarily uses the Moon's phases for counting days and months, but also makes periodic adjustments to stay in sync with the cycle of seasons (the solar year). An intercalary month, called ***Adhika Maasa***, is added because 12 lunar months only add up to **354 days**, which is about **11 days shorter** than the solar year (~365 days). Every 2–3 years, this accumulated 11-day-per-year shortfall becomes close to a full month, so the extra month is inserted to bring the lunar calendar back into alignment with the solar year and the seasons.

10. In the ball-and-lamp model (Activity 11.2), a small ball mounted on a stick represents the **Moon**, a lamp or torch represents the **Sun**, and the observer's own head represents the **Earth**. The observer holds the ball at arm's length, slightly above head height, and slowly turns around (anti-clockwise) while keeping the ball at arm's length, observing how much of the ball appears illuminated at each position.
    - When the ball is held at a position **opposite** to the lamp (position A), the observer faces the **entire illuminated portion** of the ball — this corresponds to the **full Moon**.
    - When the ball is held **towards** the lamp (position E), the observer faces only the **non-illuminated portion**, and cannot see any illuminated part — this corresponds to the **new Moon**.
    - At intermediate positions (B, C, D, F, G, H), the observer sees varying fractions of the illuminated portion, and the boundary line between the illuminated and non-illuminated parts of the ball appears **curved**.
    This model demonstrates that the phases occur because the fraction of the Moon's illuminated half that faces the Earth changes continuously as the Moon (the ball) revolves around the Earth (the observer's head), relative to the fixed direction of the Sun (the lamp).

11. Festivals based on a **purely lunar calendar**, such as *Eid-ul-Fitr* (tied to the sighting of the crescent Moon at the end of the month of *Ramazan*), follow a lunar year that adds up to only **354 days** — about **11 days shorter** than the solar year of approximately 365 days on which the Gregorian calendar is based. Since purely lunar calendars **do not add any correcting intercalary month** to compensate for this yearly shortfall (unlike luni-solar calendars), the lunar calendar drifts earlier relative to the Gregorian calendar by about 11 days every single year. Over successive years, this uncorrected 11-day annual drift accumulates, causing the Gregorian calendar date of such festivals to shift steadily earlier each year, and over a period of a few years, this shift becomes large enough that the festival moves through **entirely different months** of the Gregorian calendar.

12. A solar calendar spanning 3 years contains **36 months** in total (3 years × 12 months per year). However, there are **37 full Moons** occurring during this same 3-year period, since a full Moon occurs once every lunar cycle (about 29.5 days), and this doesn't divide evenly into whole years. If it were possible for each of the 36 months to contain **at most one** full Moon, then the total number of full Moons across all 36 months could be at most 36. But we are told there are **37 full Moons** in total, which is **one more** than the maximum of 36 that could occur if every month had no more than one full Moon each. Therefore, it is impossible for all 37 full Moons to be spread out with only one per month — **at least one month must contain two full Moons**, proving that at least two of the 37 full Moons must happen during the same month of the solar calendar.

13. The Moon's phases occur due to the **relative change in orientation** of the Sun, Moon, and Earth as the Moon revolves around the Earth. Specifically, the half of the Moon facing the Sun is always illuminated, but as the Moon orbits the Earth, the fraction of this illuminated half that faces the Earth (and is therefore visible to us) keeps changing — sometimes we see all of it (full Moon), sometimes part of it (crescent/gibbous), and sometimes none of it (new Moon). This is a completely different mechanism from Earth's shadow. When the **Earth's shadow** actually falls on the Moon, it blocks sunlight from reaching the Moon at that specific time, and this event causes a **lunar eclipse**, not the Moon's regular monthly phases. Lunar eclipses are rare, brief events that can only occur on full Moon days, and even then, not every month, due to the slight tilt of the Moon's orbit with respect to the Earth's orbit around the Sun.

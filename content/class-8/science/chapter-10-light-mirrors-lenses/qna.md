### Q1: What is a spherical mirror? Name its two types.
A **spherical mirror** is a mirror whose reflecting surface is shaped like a part of a hollow sphere. Its two types are the **concave mirror** (reflecting surface curves inwards) and the **convex mirror** (reflecting surface curves outwards).

### Q2: Define incident ray, reflected ray, and normal.
The **incident ray** is the ray of light that falls on the mirror. The **reflected ray** is the ray of light that comes back from the mirror. The **normal** is the line drawn at the point of incidence, making an angle of 90° to the reflecting surface of the mirror.

### Q3: Define angle of incidence and angle of reflection.
The **angle of incidence (i)** is the angle between the normal and the incident ray. The **angle of reflection (r)** is the angle between the normal and the reflected ray.

### Q4: State the two laws of reflection.
The two laws of reflection are:
1. The **angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection** (i = r).
2. The **incident ray, the normal to the mirror at the point of incidence, and the reflected ray all lie in the same plane**.

### Q5: What is a lens? Name its two types based on shape.
A **lens** is a piece of transparent material, usually made of glass or plastic, which has curved surfaces. Its two types are the **convex lens** (thicker at the middle than at the edges) and the **concave lens** (thicker at the edges than at the middle).

### Q6: How are spherical mirrors actually manufactured?
Spherical mirrors are **not** made by slicing a hollow glass sphere. Instead, they are created by **grinding and polishing a flat piece of glass into a curved surface**. If a reflective coating (like a thin layer of aluminium) is applied on the **outer curved surface**, it forms a **concave mirror**; if applied on the **inner curved surface**, it forms a **convex mirror**.

### Q7: How does a convex lens differ from a concave lens in terms of light behaviour? What other names are they known by?
A **convex lens converges** the light beam falling on it — bringing parallel rays closer together — and is therefore also called a **converging lens**. A **concave lens diverges** the light beam — spreading parallel rays apart — and is therefore also called a **diverging lens**.

### Q8: How does a concave mirror affect a beam of multiple parallel light rays, compared to a convex mirror?
When multiple parallel beams of light fall on a **concave mirror**, the reflected beams get closer together, that is, they **converge**. When multiple parallel beams fall on a **convex mirror**, the reflected beams spread apart, that is, they **diverge**. (In contrast, a plane mirror reflects multiple parallel beams as parallel reflected beams.)

### Q9: Differentiate between the image formed by a concave mirror and a convex mirror as the object distance changes.
In a **concave mirror**, when the object is placed close to the mirror, the image is **erect and enlarged**; as the object is moved farther away, the image becomes **inverted**, initially enlarged and then getting smaller. In a **convex mirror**, the image is **always erect and diminished (smaller)** than the object, regardless of distance, though the size decreases slightly further as the object moves away.

### Q10: Differentiate between the image formed by a convex lens and a concave lens as the object distance changes.
When an object is placed close to a **convex lens** and viewed through it, the image appears **erect and enlarged**; as the distance increases, the image appears **inverted**, initially enlarged and then diminishing in size. An object placed behind a **concave lens** always appears **erect and diminished** in size through the lens, though its exact size changes as its distance from the lens increases.

### Q11: List two everyday uses each of concave mirrors and convex mirrors.
**Concave mirrors** are used as: (i) reflectors in torches and headlights of cars/scooters to concentrate light, and (ii) dental mirrors, which give an enlarged view of teeth when held close inside the mouth. **Convex mirrors** are used as: (i) side-view mirrors on vehicles, which give a wider view of traffic behind, and (ii) road safety mirrors at intersections/sharp bends, or surveillance mirrors in stores to monitor a large area.

### Q12: What is meant by "lateral inversion" in the context of mirror images, and in which types of mirrors is it seen?
**Lateral inversion** refers to the left-right flipping of an image as seen in a mirror (the left side of the object appears as the right side of the image, and vice versa). Lateral inversion of the image is seen in **all three types of mirrors — plane, concave, and convex**.

### Q13: Explain why a concave mirror can be used to burn paper using sunlight, based on the concept of convergence.
When the reflecting surface of a **concave mirror** is held facing the Sun, the parallel rays of sunlight falling on it get **reflected and converge** to a single point. This concentration of light at one small point produces sufficient **heat**, which can be enough to ignite (burn) a piece of paper placed at that point — this is how devices like **solar concentrators** use concave mirrors (or lenses) to generate heat for purposes such as steam generation, large-scale cooking, or solar furnaces.

### Q14: Explain, with reference to Activity 10.5, how it is shown that the incident ray, normal, and reflected ray all lie in the same plane.
In this activity, a beam of light is shone onto a mirror placed on a flat sheet of chart paper, part of which extends beyond the edge of a table. Initially, with the sheet flat, the reflected beam is clearly visible along the extended, flat portion of the paper. When the extended part of the sheet is **bent** downward along the table's edge, the reflected beam **disappears** from view on that bent portion; when the sheet is **flattened again**, the reflected beam **reappears**. This shows that the reflected beam lies in the same flat plane as the incident beam — bending the sheet creates a new, different plane, which breaks this alignment and makes the beam no longer visible along it.

### Q15: A shiny metallic spoon is used as an impromptu mirror. Explain what type of image is seen when looking into (a) the inner curved surface, and (b) the outer curved surface of the spoon.
(a) The **inner curved surface** of the spoon curves inward, acting like a **concave mirror**; the image seen in it is **inverted**.
(b) The **outer curved surface** of the spoon bulges outward, acting like a **convex mirror**; the image seen in it is **erect but smaller in size** than the object.

### Q16: Why are convex mirrors, and not plane or concave mirrors, preferred as side-view mirrors on vehicles?
Convex mirrors are preferred as side-view mirrors because their outward-curved reflecting surface **diverges** light, allowing them to form an image of a much **wider area** of the road behind compared to a plane mirror of the same size. They also **always produce an erect image**, unlike a concave mirror (whose image can become inverted at larger distances), making the reflected traffic easy to interpret at a glance. However, because the image formed is **diminished (smaller than actual size)**, objects seen in convex mirrors appear farther away than they actually are — which is why vehicles carry the warning "Objects in mirror are closer than they appear."

### Q17: A water drop placed on a flat glass/plastic surface is used to view printed text underneath it. Explain why the text appears magnified, connecting this to the concept of a lens.
The surface of the water drop is **curved outward** (like a convex lens), since the oil/wax coating on the flat glass causes the water to form a rounded drop rather than spreading flat. When text is viewed through this curved water drop, the curvature of its surface bends the light passing through it in such a way that the letters directly beneath the drop appear **larger** than the letters nearby that are not under the drop. This shows that the curved water drop is acting like a simple **converging (convex) lens**, similar in principle to how a magnifying glass makes small print appear bigger.

### Q18: Explain how you would use a beam of light, a comb, and a torch to experimentally verify the first law of reflection, describing the setup and expected result.
Using a plane mirror with stand, a torch, a comb with a single thin slit (formed by covering all other openings with black paper), a sheet of white paper, and a strip of black paper: place the plane mirror upright on the white paper, and use the torch and the slit in the comb to obtain a thin beam of light that falls on the mirror. Trace the position of the mirror, the incident ray, and the reflected ray on the paper. Remove the mirror and draw the **normal** — a line making a 90° angle to the mirror's position at the point where the incident ray struck it. Measure the **angle of incidence (i)** between the normal and incident ray, and the **angle of reflection (r)** between the normal and reflected ray, and record them. Repeating this for several different angles of incidence, the measured angle of incidence and angle of reflection are found to be **nearly equal each time**, verifying that **the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection**.

### Q19: How do the images formed by a convex lens compare to those formed by a concave mirror, in terms of how they change with object distance? Explain the similarity.
Both a **convex lens** and a **concave mirror** show a similar pattern in how their images change with object distance: when the object is placed close, the image is **erect and enlarged**; as the object is moved farther away, the image becomes **inverted**, first remaining enlarged and then gradually diminishing in size as the distance increases further. This is in contrast to a **concave lens** and a **convex mirror**, both of which always produce an **erect, diminished** image regardless of the object's distance, with only minor changes in the exact size as distance changes.

### Q20: In a science museum, a large concave mirror is observed by a person walking towards it from a distance. Describe the changes she would observe in her image as she approaches the mirror.
As the person is initially far from the large concave mirror, her image would appear **inverted**, and as she walks closer, this inverted image would keep **increasing in size**. As she continues to approach and gets sufficiently close to the mirror, her image would eventually **become erect and appear magnified (enlarged)**, consistent with the general behaviour of concave mirrors: images are inverted (and change in size) at larger object distances, but become erect and enlarged when the object is close to the mirror.

### Q21: Multiple parallel beams of light are made to fall separately on a plane mirror, a concave mirror, and a convex mirror. Describe and explain what happens to the reflected beams in each case.
When multiple parallel beams of light fall on a **plane mirror**, the reflected beams remain **parallel** to each other. When they fall on a **concave mirror**, the reflected beams **converge**, that is, they come closer together and meet. When they fall on a **convex mirror**, the reflected beams **diverge**, that is, they spread apart from each other. This happens because, even though each individual ray still obeys the laws of reflection at the point it strikes the mirror, the **curved surface** of a spherical mirror changes the direction of each ray differently depending on where it strikes, causing the overall bundle of parallel rays to either converge (concave) or diverge (convex), unlike a flat plane mirror which keeps them parallel.

### Q22: Explain, using the concept of convergence of light, how a solar concentrator can be used for large-scale cooking or generating steam for electricity.
A **solar concentrator** uses mirrors and/or lenses (such as a large concave mirror or convex lens) to converge a wide beam of sunlight into a small, concentrated area. When multiple parallel rays of sunlight are converged to a single point or small region, the light energy becomes concentrated there, producing significant **heat** at that point. This concentrated heat can be used to heat a liquid until it produces **steam**, which can then be used to **generate electricity** via a turbine, or the concentrated heat itself can be directly used for purposes like **large-scale cooking** or in **solar furnaces**, which are powerful enough to even melt steel.

### Q23: Describe how you would set up an experiment to demonstrate that a convex lens converges light while a concave lens diverges it, using multiple parallel light beams.
Using a thin transparent glass plate, a convex lens, a concave lens, a torch, and a comb (with multiple openings uncovered to produce several parallel beams of light) fixed upright between two identical books with paper sheets spread on both books: let the multiple parallel beams fall separately upon the thin glass plate, the convex lens, and the concave lens, and observe what happens to the beams after passing through each. It is observed that the light beam **passes through the thin glass plate as it is**, without any change in direction. The **convex lens converges** the parallel light beams passing through it, bringing them closer together to a point, while the **concave lens diverges** the parallel light beams, causing them to spread further apart after passing through.

### Q24: Compare the characteristics and typical use of a concave mirror with those of a convex lens, in terms of the effect each has on light.
A **concave mirror** reflects light and causes multiple parallel rays falling on it to **converge**; it is commonly used as reflectors in torches/headlights, as dental mirrors for an enlarged close-up view, and as the main mirror in reflecting telescopes. A **convex lens** allows light to pass through it and also causes multiple parallel rays to **converge** (which is why it is called a converging lens); it is used in eyeglasses, cameras, telescopes, microscopes, and the human eye. While both a concave mirror and a convex lens converge parallel light and can even concentrate sunlight enough to burn paper, the key difference is that a **mirror reflects** light (we see an image "in" it) while a **lens transmits** light through it (we see objects "through" it).

### Q25: A person notices that the image of a distant object seen through a magnifying glass appears larger and erect, but if the magnifying glass (a convex lens) is used in a different way, at a much greater working distance, the same object might appear differently. Using the chapter's concepts, explain why the image characteristics through a convex lens change with object distance.
A magnifying glass is a **convex lens**, and as established through Activity 10.9, the image formed through a convex lens depends heavily on the **distance of the object from the lens**. When the object is close to the lens, the image formed is **erect and enlarged**, which is why a magnifying glass held close to text makes the letters appear bigger and right-side-up. However, as the distance between the object and the convex lens **increases**, the nature of the image changes: it becomes **inverted**, initially remaining enlarged and then progressively **diminishing** in size as the distance grows further. This distance-dependent behaviour explains why the same convex lens can serve different purposes — as a magnifier when the object is close, but showing a different (inverted, and eventually smaller) image when the object is placed farther away.

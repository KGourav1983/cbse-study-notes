# Light: Mirrors and Lenses

Have you ever looked into the back of a shiny spoon and seen your face upside down, then flipped it over and seen yourself tiny and right-side up? That's not magic — that's the science of curved mirrors, and by the end of this chapter, you'll know exactly why it happens.

## 10.1 What Are Spherical Mirrors?

Imagine visiting a science centre where one mirror makes your face huge, another flips your friend upside down, and a third shrinks you down to a tiny version of yourself. All of these are variations on one idea: mirrors that aren't flat.

**Activity 10.1 (the spoon experiment):**
- Looking at the **inner curved surface** of a shiny spoon (held close to your face) → the image appears **inverted**.
- Looking at the **outer curved surface** of the spoon → the image appears **erect but smaller**.

> **Spherical mirrors** are a common type of curved mirror, shaped like a part of a hollow glass sphere. Mirrors whose reflecting surfaces are spherical are called spherical mirrors.

There are two types, based on which way the reflecting surface curves:

| Mirror type | Curves | 
|---|---|
| **Concave mirror** | Reflecting surface curves **inwards** |
| **Convex mirror** | Reflecting surface curves **outwards** |

> **Exam trap:** Students often think spherical mirrors are literally cut from a hollow glass sphere. They aren't! They're made by **grinding and polishing a flat piece of glass into a curved surface**, then coating it. If the reflective coating (like a thin layer of aluminium) is applied on the **outer curved surface**, it forms a **concave mirror**. If applied on the **inner curved surface**, it forms a **convex mirror**.

**Activity 10.2 (identifying concave vs convex):** Place both mirrors reflecting-surface-up on a table and view them from the side, at their level — you can directly see whether the surface curves inward (concave) or outward (convex).

## 10.2 What Are the Characteristics of Images Formed by Spherical Mirrors?

**Activity 10.3** compares images formed by a concave mirror and a convex mirror as an object's distance from each mirror changes:

| Mirror | Object close | Object far away |
|---|---|---|
| **Concave mirror** | Erect, **enlarged** | **Inverted**; initially enlarged, then gets smaller |
| **Convex mirror** | Erect, **diminished** (smaller) | Erect, diminished; size decreases slightly further |

- A **plane mirror** always forms an erect image of the **same size** as the object — this doesn't change with distance.
- **Spherical mirrors behave very differently**: image size changes with object distance, and a concave mirror's image can even flip from erect to inverted.
- **Lateral inversion** (left-right flip) is seen in the images formed by **all three types** of mirrors — plane, concave, and convex.

> **Exam trap:** Don't assume a concave mirror always gives an enlarged image or a convex mirror always gives a diminished one regardless of distance. For a **concave mirror**, the image is enlarged only when the object is close; move the object far enough and the image becomes inverted and shrinks. A **convex mirror**, however, is more predictable: its image is **always erect and diminished**, no matter the object's distance — only the exact amount of diminishing changes slightly.

### Everyday uses of concave and convex mirrors

- **Concave mirrors**: used as reflectors in torches and headlights of cars and scooters (to concentrate light); used by dentists as a mirror that gives an **enlarged view** of teeth when held close; the main mirror in most modern **reflecting telescopes** is a large concave mirror.
- **Convex mirrors**: used as **side-view mirrors** on vehicles (form an erect, smaller image of traffic behind, giving a wider field of view); installed at **road intersections/sharp bends** to help drivers see around blind corners and avoid collisions; installed in **big stores** to monitor a large area and deter theft.

## 10.3 What Are the Laws of Reflection?

Before diving into the laws, let's get the vocabulary straight, since examiners expect these exact terms:

- **Ray**: a straight line with an arrow representing the path along which light travels.
- **Incident ray**: the ray of light that falls on the mirror.
- **Reflected ray**: the ray of light that comes back from the mirror.
- **Normal**: a line drawn at the point of incidence, making an angle of 90° to the mirror's surface.
- **Angle of incidence (i)**: the angle between the normal and the incident ray.
- **Angle of reflection (r)**: the angle between the normal and the reflected ray.

**Activity 10.4 (measuring angles):** Using a torch, comb (with a single slit), and a plane mirror, a thin beam of light is directed onto the mirror at various angles, and the angle of incidence and angle of reflection are measured and recorded each time.

**Result — First Law of Reflection:**
> **The angle of incidence (i) is equal to the angle of reflection (r).**

Special case: when the incident ray falls **along the normal**, both the angle of incidence and the angle of reflection are **zero**.

**Activity 10.5 (checking the plane of reflection):** A sheet of chart paper is placed flat, extending beyond a table edge, with the mirror on it. The reflected beam is visible along the flat extended portion — but when that portion is **bent**, the reflected beam disappears; flattening it again makes the beam reappear.

**Result — Second Law of Reflection:**
> **The incident ray, the normal to the mirror at the point of incidence, and the reflected ray, all lie in the same plane.**

Both these laws of reflection are **valid for all kinds of mirrors — plane and spherical (concave and convex)**.

### Convergence and divergence in spherical mirrors

**Activity 10.6:** Multiple parallel beams of light (using a comb with many openings uncovered) are made to fall on a plane mirror, a concave mirror, and a convex mirror.

| Mirror | Effect on multiple parallel reflected beams |
|---|---|
| Plane mirror | Reflected beams remain **parallel** |
| Concave mirror | Reflected beams **converge** (come closer together) |
| Convex mirror | Reflected beams **diverge** (spread apart) |

Even though each individual ray still obeys the laws of reflection, the curved surface of a spherical mirror bends the overall bundle of parallel rays to either converge or diverge, depending on its shape.

**Activity 10.7 (burning paper with a concave mirror):** Holding a concave mirror's reflecting surface toward the Sun and directing reflected sunlight onto thin paper, the light converges to a sharp bright spot — producing enough heat at that point to make the paper start to burn/smoke.

> **A step further:** Devices that concentrate sunlight into a small area using mirrors and lenses are called **solar concentrators**. The concentrated sunlight can heat a liquid to produce steam, which is used to generate electricity or provide heat for large-scale cooking or solar furnaces (which can even melt steel).

## 10.4 What Is a Lens?

A flat glass window pane doesn't distort what you see through it — but what if the transparent surface itself is curved?

**Activity 10.8 (water drop as a lens):** A thin oil/wax coating is used to form a rounded water drop on a flat glass/plastic strip, placed over printed text. The water drop's surface is **curved outward**, and the letters seen through the drop appear **larger** than the surrounding letters. This shows the curved drop of water is acting like a simple **lens** — the same principle behind a **magnifying glass**.

> **Lens**: a piece of transparent material, usually made of glass or plastic, which has curved surfaces. Like mirrors, lenses can also be convex or concave. Unlike mirrors, **lenses allow light to pass through them** — we see things *through* a lens, not *in* it (as we do with a mirror).

| Lens type | Shape |
|---|---|
| **Convex lens** | Thicker at the **middle** compared to the edges |
| **Concave lens** | Thicker at the **edges** compared to the middle |

**Activity 10.9 (images through lenses):**

| Lens | Object close | Object far away |
|---|---|---|
| **Convex lens** | Erect, **enlarged** | Inverted; initially enlarged, then diminishes |
| **Concave lens** | Erect, **diminished** — always | Remains erect and diminished; size changes with distance |

> **Exam trap:** Notice the parallel: a **convex lens behaves like a concave mirror** in terms of image characteristics (enlarged when close, inverted when far), while a **concave lens behaves like a convex mirror** (always erect and diminished). Students often mix up which lens pairs with which mirror — remember, convex lens ≈ concave mirror behaviour, concave lens ≈ convex mirror behaviour, purely in terms of the *image* they produce.

### Convergence and divergence in lenses

**Activity 10.10:** Multiple parallel beams of light are passed through a thin transparent glass plate, a convex lens, and a concave lens.

| Optical element | Effect on parallel light beam |
|---|---|
| Thin glass plate | Passes through **as it is** (no change) |
| Convex lens | **Converges** the light |
| Concave lens | **Diverges** the light |

> A **convex lens** is also called a **converging lens**, while a **concave lens** is called a **diverging lens**.

**Activity 10.11 (burning paper with a convex lens):** Repeating the sunlight-focusing experiment (as in Activity 10.7) but using a convex lens instead of a concave mirror — the converged sunlight can similarly burn the paper.

### Where lenses are used

Lenses are everywhere: **eyeglasses** that help people see clearly are lenses; **cameras**, **telescopes**, and **microscopes** all use lenses to work. Even our own **eye has a convex lens inside it** — an amazing lens that can change its shape, allowing us to read a book up close or see something far away.

## Quick Recap

- A **spherical mirror** has a reflecting surface shaped like part of a hollow sphere: **concave** (curves inward) or **convex** (curves outward). Made by grinding/polishing flat glass, then coating the outer surface (→ concave) or inner surface (→ convex).
- **Concave mirror image**: erect and enlarged when object is close; becomes inverted (initially enlarged, then smaller) as object moves farther away.
- **Convex mirror image**: always erect and diminished, regardless of object distance (size decreases slightly further with distance).
- A **plane mirror** always gives an erect, same-size image — spherical mirrors don't.
- **Lateral inversion** occurs in images from all three mirror types (plane, concave, convex).
- **Concave mirrors** are used in torch/headlight reflectors, dental mirrors, and reflecting telescopes. **Convex mirrors** are used as vehicle side-view mirrors, road safety mirrors at intersections, and store surveillance mirrors.
- Key terms: **incident ray**, **reflected ray**, **normal** (90° to mirror surface at point of incidence), **angle of incidence (i)**, **angle of reflection (r)**.
- **First law of reflection**: angle of incidence = angle of reflection. When the ray falls along the normal, both angles are 0°.
- **Second law of reflection**: the incident ray, the normal, and the reflected ray all lie in the same plane.
- Both laws of reflection apply to **all mirrors** — plane, concave, and convex.
- Multiple parallel rays: **plane mirror** → reflected rays stay parallel; **concave mirror** → rays **converge**; **convex mirror** → rays **diverge**.
- A concave mirror can concentrate sunlight to a bright, hot spot capable of burning paper — the basis of **solar concentrators** (used for steam generation, cooking, solar furnaces).
- A **lens** is a transparent curved material that light passes *through* (unlike a mirror, which reflects light). **Convex lens**: thicker at the middle. **Concave lens**: thicker at the edges.
- **Convex lens image**: erect and enlarged when object is close; inverted (initially enlarged, then diminishes) as object moves farther.
- **Concave lens image**: always erect and diminished, changing size with distance.
- A **convex lens converges** light (also called a **converging lens**); a **concave lens diverges** light (also called a **diverging lens**). A plain glass plate lets parallel light pass through unchanged.
- A convex lens, like a concave mirror, can converge sunlight to burn paper.
- Lenses are used in eyeglasses, cameras, telescopes, microscopes, and even the human eye (which has a convex lens that can change shape).

## Answer Key

1. A **spherical mirror** is a mirror whose reflecting surface is shaped like a part of a hollow sphere.

2. The **normal** is a line drawn at the point of incidence on the mirror, making an angle of 90° to the reflecting surface of the mirror.

3. The **concave lens** is thicker at the edges than at the middle.

4. **False.** A convex mirror always forms an **erect** image, which is diminished in size; it does not form an inverted image.

5. Such a device is called a **solar concentrator**.

6. The two laws of reflection are:
   1. The **angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection**.
   2. The **incident ray, the normal to the mirror at the point of incidence, and the reflected ray all lie in the same plane**.

7. A **concave lens** is thicker at the edges than the middle and **diverges** a parallel beam of light passing through it (spreading the rays apart), which is why it is also called a diverging lens. A **convex lens** is thicker at the middle than the edges and **converges** a parallel beam of light passing through it (bringing the rays together to a point), which is why it is also called a converging lens.

8. Convex mirrors are preferred as side-view mirrors because their outward-curved surface **diverges** light, allowing them to show a **much wider area** of the road behind compared to a plane mirror of the same size, giving the driver a broader view of traffic.

9. Since the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection (first law of reflection), if the angle of incidence is **40°**, the **angle of reflection is also 40°**. The angle the reflected ray makes with the mirror surface itself would be 90° − 40° = **50°** (since the normal is at 90° to the mirror surface).

10. 

    ```
    Incident ray  \    | Normal    /  Reflected ray
                   \   |   /
                    \  |  /
                 i    \| /    r
                  __________________
                    Mirror surface
    ```

    - The **incident ray** is the ray of light that falls on the mirror.
    - The **reflected ray** is the ray of light that comes back from the mirror after striking it.
    - The **normal** is the line drawn perpendicular (at 90°) to the mirror's surface at the point where the incident ray strikes it (the point of incidence).
    - The **angle of incidence (i)** is the angle measured between the normal and the incident ray.
    - The **angle of reflection (r)** is the angle measured between the normal and the reflected ray.
    According to the first law of reflection, the angle of incidence is always equal to the angle of reflection (i = r), and according to the second law, the incident ray, the normal, and the reflected ray all lie in the same plane.

11. When the object is placed **close** to a concave mirror, the image formed is **erect and enlarged** (larger than the object). As the object is gradually **moved farther away** from the mirror, the nature of the image changes — the image becomes **inverted**. Initially, as the object moves away, this inverted image remains **enlarged**, but as the distance increases further, the image progressively **gets smaller (diminishes)** in size. This behaviour is very different from a plane mirror, which always produces an erect image of the same size as the object regardless of distance, showing that the concave mirror's image characteristics depend strongly on the object's distance from it.

12. 

    | Basis of comparison | Concave mirror | Convex mirror |
    |---|---|---|
    | Shape of reflecting surface | Curves **inwards** | Curves **outwards** |
    | Effect on parallel rays of light | Rays **converge** (come closer together) after reflection | Rays **diverge** (spread apart) after reflection |
    | Nature of image formed | Can be **enlarged, diminished, or same size**; may be **erect or inverted**, depending on the object's distance from the mirror | Always **erect** and **diminished** in size, regardless of the object's distance |

13. When the reflecting surface of a **concave mirror** is pointed towards the Sun, the parallel rays of sunlight falling on the mirror get reflected in such a way that they **converge** to a single small point, since a concave mirror converges parallel beams of light on reflection. Because sunlight energy from a wide area of the mirror gets concentrated into this one small point, sufficient **heat** is produced there. If a piece of thin paper is placed at exactly this point (where the sharp, bright spot forms), the concentrated heat can be enough to **ignite the paper**, causing it to smoke or burn — demonstrating the principle used in solar concentrators and solar furnaces.
